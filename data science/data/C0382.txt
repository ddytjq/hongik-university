<TD><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1"><B>Year End Tax Strategy</B></FONT></TD>
<TD ALIGN=RIGHT><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1">15/2/2001</FONT></TD></TR>
<TR><TD COLSPAN=2><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1">Winning Moves For Your End Strategy<BR>&nbsp;</FONT></TD></TR>
<TR><TD COLSPAN=2><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1"><p>
             With the calendar year over, it is time to focus on the next tax 
              year end. The 2000/01 tax year closes on Thursday 5 April. A few 
              weeks earlier, there will be the Budget, seen by many commentators 
              as the last before the next general election.</p>
            <p>This year, the main areas of tax planning to review are: </p>
            <p> 
       <b> Individual Savings Accounts (ISAs)<br>
       </b> 
       The ISA investment limit for 2000/01 is £7,000. This ceiling will 
              remain the same until April 2006, following a change announced in 
              last November's pre-Budget report. Time to act is running out, if 
              you have not yet put the maximum into an ISA for this year. If you 
              have already contributed to an ISA, but not up to the £7,000 limit, 
              you will need to take care because the complex ISA rules could restrict 
              your top-up options. We strongly recommend you seek advice before 
              investing, both to ensure you are making the right investment choice 
              and to check that you are not breaking the ISA rules. 
            <p> 
         <b>
              Personal Pension Contributions<br>
         </b>The personal pension rules are being drastically revamped from 
              6 April this year (see Radical New Rules for Pension Plans). The 
              changes present both opportunities and threats and make it all the 
              more important that you review your personal pension contributions 
              before the year end.
            <p>If you are self-employed or you are not a member of an employer's 
             pension scheme and have not made the maximum personal pension contribution 
              in any tax year since 1993/94, the first thing to consider is carry 
              forward relief. This allows you to catch up on contributions you 
              could have made to your personal pension plan in previous tax years 
              and reduce this year's tax bill. 
            <p>Carry forward is being abolished from 6 April 2001, although it 
              will still be possible to take advantage of it until 31 January 
              2002 if a contribution is carried back under the new rules. This 
              basically means asking the Inland Revenue to treat a contribution 
              as if it had been paid before 6 April 2001. For most people, the 
              replacement for carry forward will be much less beneficial. 
            <p>If you want to arrange personal pension life cover or top up your 
              existing pension-based insurance, then action before 6 April is 
              also important. The new rules, which take effect from that date, 
              will generally mean the amount of cover you can buy will be substantially 
              reduced.  
            <p> 
         <b>
              Additional Voluntary Contributions<br>
         </b>Additional voluntary contributions (AVCs) do not benefit from any 
              carry forward or carry back provisions, so if your contribution 
              in any year is not the maximum, there is no way to catch up in future 
              years. The general principle is that you can pay into AVCs as much 
              as 15% of your annual earnings, less whatever compulsory contributions 
              are required by your pension scheme. Taxable fringe benefits, such 
              as a company car or 'free' fuel, count towards the earnings total. 
              From 6 April you may be able to make alternative arrangements to 
              top up your employer's pension scheme. 
            <p> 
         <b>
              Contracting Out<br>
         </b>If you are contracted out of the State Earnings Related Pension 
              Scheme (SERPS) through a personal pension, 5 April marks the deadline 
              for deciding whether to stay out of SERPS for 2000/2001. These days 
              there is no general rule for making this decision, so you will need 
              to take advice<b>.</b> 
            <p> 
         <b>
              Capital Gains Tax<br>
         </b> 
         The reforms to capital gains tax (CGT) of the past few years have 
              created new pitfalls, as well as new ways to cut your tax bill. 
              One general rule remains: use your annual exemption, which could 
              be worth up to £2,880 if you are a higher rate taxpayer. However, 
              if you are disposing of business assets or investments held on 5 
              April 1998, the CGT taper relief rules mean it could be wiser to 
              delay your disposal to gain extra taper relief. For example, for 
              business assets owned on 5 April 1998, taper relief could double 
              overnight from 25% to 50% between 5 April 2001 and 6 April 2001.  
            <p> 
         <b>
              Inheritance Tax Planning<br>
         </b>This close to an election, the Chancellor is unlikely to start 
              strengthening the inheritance tax (IHT) framework. Nevertheless, 
              as the year end approaches, it is a wise precaution to check that 
              you have not wasted your annual exemptions. <BR>&nbsp;</FONT></TD></TR>
