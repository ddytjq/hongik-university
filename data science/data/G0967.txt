<span class="xldarkbluebold">	
Black hole's spin lets energy escape
</span>
 </font><br>
            <font size="2"><i>Last Updated 
Tue Oct 23 16:10:15 2001
 </i></font>
            <p> <font size=2> 
BOULDER, COLO. - 
 </font><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica" size=2> 
An international team of astrophysicists has observed what they say is 
the first evidence of energy being extracted from a supermassive black 
hole. <p>

<TABLE ALIGN="Right" width=162 CELLPADDING=0 cellspacing=0 
hspace=4><TR><TD><IMG src="/gfx/photos/blackholespin011022.jpg" 
width=160 height=120 hspace=3 border=1><BR> <FONT SIZE="1" 
FACE="verdana,arial"><I>Illustration of a black hole: twisted space and 
distorted magnetic fields. </I></FONT> </TD></TR></TABLE>The black hole, 
at the centre of a distant galaxy, is spinning and pumping out energy, 
heating the surrounding gas, which is already unimaginably hot from the 
force of its gravity. <p>

Black holes are objects, usually collapsed stars, that are so massive 
even light cannot escape their gravity. Astronomers say most galaxies 
have black holes at their centres. <p>

This black hole, at the centre of galaxy MCG-6&#150;30&#150;15, more 
than 100 million light-years away, has a mass of between one million and 
10 million suns. <p>

Astronomers have already shown that smaller black holes spin, but this 
is the first time they've seen the same behaviour in supermassive black 
holes. And it's the combination of the black hole's spin and its gravity 
that causes it to lose energy. <p>

Joern Wilms of Tuebingen University, Germany, says the black hole is so 
massive it twists the fabric of space around it. This distorts the 
surrounding magnetic fields, causing them to tighten around the black 
hole, slowing its spin. <p>

But, like a car's brake drums heating up when you step on the brakes, 
the lost energy has to go somewhere. The "friction" between the black 
hole and the surrounding space heats up the nearby gases, causing then 
to emit X-rays. <p>

It's this X-ray glow that the scientists observed using the European 
Space Agency's XXM Newton satellite, launched in 1999. By calculating 
the spin and other characteristics of supermassive black holes, they 
hope to learn how the galaxies themselves were formed. <p>

The possibility of energy tranferring from a black hole to its 
surroundings was first predicted mathematically 25 years ago, using the 
laws of conservation of energy. <p>

A paper on the group's findings will appear in the Monthly Notices of 
the Royal Astronomical Society in London. <p>
