They noticed that the inequality of the size of the <a href="MG1131.html">nuclei</a> of egg and sperm disappears.						<li>Before or during their union, each nucleus is transformed into a definite number of rodlike structures which appear to be of the same shapes, sizes, and number in the two sexes.						<li>These structures were soon recognized also in the division processes of <a href="MG1361.html">mitosis</a> and <a href="MG1361.html">meiosis</a>. In 1888, Wilhelm Waldeyer coined the term chromosome to describe these structures obviously important in nuclear division (<a href="(Empty Reference!)">ref</a>).				</ul>				</td>				<td rowspan="2" valign="bottom"><img src="i113212.gif" width="136" height="267"></td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td colspan="2">					<ul>						<li><a href="http://www.tokyo-med.ac.jp/genet/picts/chm1.jpg">Magnified</a>, the rod-like chromosomes within one cell vary considerably in size.						<li>Many chromosomes have clear constrictions in their rods. The primary constrictions are called <a href="MG1363.html">centromeres</a>. The ends are called <a href="MG135.html">telomeres</a>.						<li>The positions of the centromeres are used to broadly classify chromosomes into three morphological <a href="http://arbl.cvmbs.colostate.edu/hbooks/genetics/medgen/chromo/chromosomes.html">groups</a>: acrocentric (constriction near the top), telocentric (no obvious constriction and thus thought to be at the end), and metacentric (constriction in the middle).						<li>Secondary constrictions are present on some chromosomes. In maize, there is 1 on chromosome #6. In humans there are five, on #s 13, 14, 15, 21, 22 and in <i>Drosophila</i> there is only 1 on each the X and Y. The number of secondary constrictions agrees closely with the number of <a href="MG112285.html">nucleoli</a>.						<li>Size and shape are used to number and identify chromosomes.					</ul>				</td>			</tr>		</table>		</p>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Interp"></a>Interpretations</h4>		<ul type="circle">			<li>Chromosomes are morphologically variable. They condense and decondense.			<li>The condensed form occurs during genetic events such as fertilization and cell division.			<li>Special structural features of condensed chromosomes suggest special functions.			<li>The secondary constrictions are nucleolar organizer regions and must contain <a href="MG112285.html">rDNA</a> tandem repeats.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a><br>			</p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Info"></a>Further information<img src="nav4.gif" width="69" height="71" align="right" usemap="#nav4B1F889C9"><map name=""></map> </h4>		<ul type="square">			<li>Chromosome size and centromere position are, in many species, insufficient to unambiguously identify each of the chromosomes. Centromeres are not clearly recognizable on all chromosomes.			<li>No substructure beyond secondary constrictions were visible in chromosome arms with the early staining regimes. <a href="http://cellbio.utmb.edu/cellbio/nucleus2.htm#metaphase chromosomes">Electron micrographs</a> reveal substructure.			<li>Chromosomes also condense in preparation for cell <a href="MG1361.html">division</a>.  The chromosomes at metaphase are particularly distinctive.			<li>An arrangement of metaphase chromosomes according to size and type is called a <a href="http://www.tokyo-med.ac.jp/genet/cki-e.htm">karyotype</a>.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a name="bottom"></a><a href="MG1131.html">Last</a> | <a href="MG1139.html">Vocabulary</a> | <a href="MG113.html">Overview</a> | <a href="#Facts">Top</a> | <a href="MG11322.html">Next</a><br>			<br>			</p>		</center>		<p>		<hr>		</p>		<p>This is page 11321 of <a href="../MG01.html">Molecular Genetics</a> by <a href="http://opbs.okstate.edu/~melcher/UM.html">Ulrich Melcher</a>, &copy; 1997-8, 2000-1<br>		<hr>		</p>		<address>E-mail inquiries to <a href="mailto:umelcher@biochem.okstate.edu">U.
