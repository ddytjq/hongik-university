				<td width="100%" class="tableTitleRow"><p class="indent25X10"><font class="news"><b>Hussain makes welcome return</b></font></p></td>
				<td rowspan="2" valign="top" width="196">
				<img src="/images/news/1580.jpg" width="196" height="262"><!--  --><br><img src="/1.gif" width="1" height="5"><br>

				<font class="newsdate"> Wednesday, January 23, 2002 06:35 (GMT)</font><br>
				<img src="/1.gif" width="1" height="18"><br>
				<font class="news">The cricket loving people of Chennai are waiting to welcome the city&#8217;s most famous cricketing son. After all, it is not everyday that a Chennai-born lad comes back to the city as the captain of the England cricket team.<br><br>And yet this unlikely script is going to be played down to its dream finish when Nasser Hussain lands in Chennai, which is the venue of the third one-day international between India and England on 25 January. <br><br>Nasser was born in Chennai (then Madras) in 1968. His father Jawad Hussain was a popular figure in cricketing circles in the city from the late 50s to the early 70s and was good enough to represent the State in one Ranji Trophy match.<br><br>Like cricketers of that period, he played the game for the fun of it and for the love of it. On the field he was a striking personality. Of medium height, he was very fair, rather stockily built with a mop of curly hair that never failed to attract attention.<br><br>Off the field, he was an engaging personality. Fun loving and erudite, he was the life of any party or dressing room banter as he kept the conversation going whether the subject was cricket or anything else. <br><br>Then one day in 1973, Jawad Hussain migrated with his family to England. Now, almost 30 years later, he is expected back in India as the proud father of the England cricket captain.<br><br>The honor could not have gone to a nicer guy. Besides being friendly and gregarious by nature, Jawad Hussain has always been passionate about the game. A middle-order batsman and a good off-spinner, he played in the lower division league and then graduated to the senior division league in which he represented the prestigious Madras Cricket Club.<br><br>With his utility qualities as a cricketer, he proved to be extremely valuable to the side and he even captained the club, which had a number of fine players in those days, for a couple of years.<br><br>More than his tactical acumen, it was his ability as a motivator that saw Jawad Hussain emerge as a successful leader. Some of these qualities can be seen in his son, who has already earned rave reviews for his captaincy on the tour of India. <br><br>`Joe&#8217; Hussain he may be known as now, but in those days he was Jawad to everyone. And his many relatives in Chennai have vivid memories of both him and young Nasser Hussain.<br><br>The present England captain had studied for a brief period in a Madras school before the family migrated. With Jawad being an MCC member, it was only natural that young Nasser&#8217;s first steps towards cricketing fame were taken on the hallowed Chepauk ground. <br><br>Nasser&#8217;s cousin Dr Safinaaz recalls today that he was a pleasant child.<br><br>&#8220;He was also very determined. And I am not surprised at his cricketing talent. It is in his blood,&#8221; he said.<br><br>Incidentally Jawad runs a cricket training school in Essex, the county which Nasser captains. And speaking of cricket running in the family&#8217;s blood, Nasser&#8217;s elder brother Mel Hussain was also a good club cricketer who turned out for MCC for a season in the early 80s.<br><br>Nasser&#8217;s other brother Abbas is a good football and rugby player. He works for a pharmaceutical firm and visits Delhi often. Nasser&#8217;s only sister is well settled in Australia. <br><br>Dr Safinaaz is the daughter of the late Nawaz Hussain, the elder brother of Jawad Hussain.<br><br>&#8220;My father too was passionate about the game and wanted one among us to play for the country,&#8221; he added. &#8220;It is sad that he is not alive today. For he would be the happiest person to see his nephew lead a national side.&#8221;<br><br>Nasser&#8217;s aunt Mehjabeel Nawaz bubbles with enthusiasm as she talks about her famous nephew.<br><br>&#8220;He loves India and Chennai,&#8221; she said. &#8220;And he loves Indian food like biryani and kebabs. Although his mother is British, she makes excellent Indian food. In fact, Nasser always goes home every Sunday to eat Indian food.&#8221;<br><br>She and the family are waiting to welcome the England captain. &#8220;We have made a lot of plans for his visit. We aim to organize a function as all the relatives want to meet him. He is a Chennai boy after all.&#8221;<br><br>But she has no doubt where her alignment lies for the match. &#8220;I will be rooting for India,&#8221; she said.<br><br>Dr Safinaaz shares Mehjabeel&#8217;s enthusiasm. &#8220;We are looking forward to Nasser visiting our home. We are in close and constant touch with Jawad for he keeps calling us and visits us once a year.&#8221;<br><br>As for Nasser, he will be visiting his birthplace after 17 years. It will certainly be an occasion to savor not only for him and his relatives, but also his friends and old teammates. And with father and son together, it will be an occasion not to be missed. <br><br>- <i>Partab Ramchand</i></font><br>
