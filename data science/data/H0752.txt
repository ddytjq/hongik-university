        to nanomoles (nmol) to picomoles (pmol) to nanomolar to milimolar can be
        confusing.&nbsp; The goal of this tutorial is to show you a way to
        easily inter-convert these values while standing in front of your mass
        spectrometer talking to a client.</font></td>

      <td width="658" height="6" colspan="5"><font color="#000080" size="4"><b>1.)
        Quantities: An easy method to convert nmols to µgs&nbsp;</b></font></td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="658" height="6" colspan="5"></td>
    </tr>
  </center>
    <tr>
      <td width="658" height="11" colspan="5">
        <p align="left"><font color="#000080"><b>Easy trick number one.</b></font></td>
    </tr>
  <center>
    <tr>
      <td width="658" height="11" colspan="5">
        <font color="#000080">1 nmol of a compound is equal to the kDa mass of
        the compound converted to µgs.&nbsp;&nbsp;<br>
 For example 1 nmol of a 50 kDA
        protein is 50 µg.</font></td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="658" height="11" colspan="5">
        <p align="center"><font color="#FFFFFF">.</font></p>
      </td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="658" height="39" colspan="5"><font color="#000080">While this
        is not a stunning revelation it is easy to become flustered when you are
        calculating on your feet (boy that sounds funny).&nbsp; Immediately I
        instinctively fumble for a calculator or look for a pencil but it is not
        necessary.&nbsp; Since most of our business
        is done in nmols
        and µgs the trick above works well. &nbsp;
        This makes it easy to estimate on the fly.&nbsp; For example if someone comes into
        the mass spec. lab and says, &quot;I have 10 </font><font color="#000080">µ</font><font color="#000080">g
        of a 50kDa protein&quot;,&nbsp; I immediately&nbsp; think, &quot;Hey,
        that is 1/5 th the kDa weight and immediately I can say,
        &quot;Oh, you have 1/5th of a nmol or 200 pmol, that should be enough for the
        analysis!&quot;&nbsp; The stupid ones will be amazed!&nbsp; I was always
        amazed by the star molecular biologists that could ID a restriction site just
        by looking at unprocessed&nbsp; DNA sequencing data, I am sure they had a similar
        trick.</font></td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="658" height="12" colspan="5">
        <p align="center"><font size="2" color="#FFFFFF">.</font></td>
    </tr>

        another example using insulin</font></b>:<b><font color="#000080"> </font></b><font color="#000080" size="3">The
        mass of insulin is 5.808 kDa</font></td>

  <tr>
      <td width="658" height="23" colspan="5">
        <font color="#000080">In the above problem 10 µLs of a 1 mM solution
        would contain 10 µL X 5.808 µg/µL = <u>58.080 </u>µ<u>g</u> of insulin,
        which is a considerable amount, 10 nmol.</font>
        <p><font color="#000080">An easy way to do this conversion &quot;on your
        feet&quot; is to remember that a 1 mM solution is the same as the mass
        of the compound in kDa converted to µg per µL.&nbsp; Therefore if the
        molecule is insulin a 1mM solution is equal to 5.808 µg/ul.&nbsp; (or
        if you have a 50mM solution just multiply this number by 50)</font></td>
  </tr>
