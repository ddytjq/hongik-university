<h1 align="center"><u>A Map of the Milky Way</u></h1>
<p>This is a drawing of the Milky Way looking down from above.  The evidence
for this picture is provided below.  The Sun is just one of 200 billion stars
in this typical barred spiral galaxy that is about 90 000 light years in diameter.
</p>
<center>
<img src="milkyway.jpg" width="800" height="800" />
</center>
<p>It should be emphasized that there are almost as many stars between the spiral
arms as in the spiral arms.  The reason why the arms of spiral galaxies are so 
prominant is that the brightest stars are found in the spiral arms.  Spiral arms 
are the major regions of star formation in spiral galaxies and this is where most 
of the major nebulae are found.
</p>
<hr />
<center><u><b>The Shape of the Milky Way - The Evidence</b></u></center>
<p>
The first good map of the spiral form of the Milky Way was produced by
  <a href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=1958MNRAS.118..379O&db_key=AST&high=3bb0b6dfeb07806" target="_blank">
Oort, Kerr and Westerhout</a> in 1958 (an early version of their map exists 
  <a href="http://adsbit.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-iarticle_query?journal=MNRAS&year=1958&volume=.118&letter=.&db_key=AST&page_ind=651&plate_select=PLATES&data_type=GIF&type=SCREEN_GIF" target="_blank">
here</a>.)  They mapped the neutral hydrogen (HI) in the plane of the galaxy.  Later, in 1976, 
  <a href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=1976A%26A....49...57G&db_key=AST&high=3bb0b6dfeb08072" target="_blank">
Yvonne and Yvon Georgelin</a> produced a 
  <a href="http://adsbit.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-iarticle_query?bibcode=1976A%26A....49...57G&db_key=AST&page_ind=17&plate_select=NO&data_type=GIF&type=SCREEN_GIF" target="_blank">
map of many of the major HII regions</a> (bright nebulae of ionised hydrogen) showing 
how they are distributed along the spiral arms.  In 1993, 
  <a href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=1993ApJ...411..674T&db_key=AST&high=3bb0b6dfeb08611" target="_blank">
Taylor and Cordes</a> produced
  <a href="http://adsbit.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-iarticle_query?bibcode=1993ApJ...411..674T&db_key=AST&page_ind=3&plate_select=NO&data_type=GIF&type=SCREEN_GIF" target="_blank">
an updated version</a>.  Finally in 1995 
  <a href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=1995ApJ...454..119V&db_key=AST&high=3bb0b6dfeb07537" target="_blank">
J Vallee</a> examined the evidence and concluded that the galaxy was a four-arm 
logarithmic spiral depicted
  <a href="http://adsbit.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-iarticle_query?bibcode=1995ApJ...454..119V&db_key=AST&page_ind=3&plate_select=NO&data_type=GIF&type=SCREEN_GIF" target="_blank">
here</a>.
</p>
<p>
It has long been suspected that the middle of the Milky Way may have a bar, but 
the first conclusive evidence was produced by 
  <a href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=1991ApJ...379..631B&db_key=AST&high=3bb0b6dfeb08695" target="_blank">
Blitz and Spergel</a> in 1991.  Information on the exact shape of the central 
bar was published recently in July 2001 by 
  <a href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=2001A%26A...373..139L&db_key=AST&high=3bb0b6dfeb09583" target="_blank">
López-Corredoira, Hammersley, Garzón, Cabrera-Lavers, Castro-Rodríguez, Schultheis
and Mahoney</a>, (available as a postscript file <a href="http://arXiv.org/abs/astro-ph/0104307" target="_blank">here</a>.)
They conclude that the central bar of the Milky Way looks much like the bar in
the spiral galaxy <a href="http://www.noao.edu/outreach/aop/observers/m95.html" target="_blank">M95</a>.
</p>
<p>
If we put all this data together then we get a map like this one below.  There 
is very little data available about the far side of the galaxy but spiral
galaxies are usually fairly symmetrical.  Features on one side of a galaxy are
often repeated on the other side.
</p>
<center>
<img src="spiralmap.gif" width="440" height="400" />
</center>

Below - four galaxies which look like the Milky Way.  NGC 3953 (top left) is
55 million light years away and 95000 light years in diameter.  NGC5970 (top right)
is 105 million light years away and 85000 light years in diameter.  NGC 7329
(bottom right) is even further at a distance of 140 million light years but it is 
larger with a diameter of 140000 light years.  NGC 7723 (bottom right) is 80 
million light years away with a diameter of 90000 light years.
</p>
