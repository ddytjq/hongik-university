<h4>  A Budget for Business?</h4><p><i>
Gordon Brown’s recent Budget measures were widely hailed as an attempt to put something in everyone’s pocket. But what has the Chancellor done for the business community and in particular, how has Northern Ireland fared? Philip McDonagh, Chief Economist with PricewaterhouseCoopers in Belfast, provides his exclusive analysis for this month’s Ulster Business. CLICK FOR FULL STORY
</i></p><p>A BUDGET FOR VOTERS</p><p>- BUT WHAT ABOUT BUSINESS</p><p><br>Writing exclusively for Ulster Business, Philip McDonagh, Chief Economist with PricewaterhouseCoopers in Belfast, analyses the impact that the Chancellor’s recent Budget is likely to have, particularly on the business community.</p><p>Gordon Brown rose to the despatch box at 3:32 pm.  When he sat down a mere 52 minutes later, he had launched Labour’s election campaign with a budget intended to put something into every voter’s pocket. On closer examination, most people found something in their pockets- but not what they had expected.</p><p>In opening pre-election hostilities, the Chancellor opted for the role of defender of the working family and custodian of fiscal prudence, in preference to that of profligate tax cutter. He judged his audience shrewdly. </p><p>The following morning, even the most conservative of newspapers acknowledged that he had favoured many, while offending few. Some of the tabloids simply encouraged readers to go out and vote labour. As a political strategy, the budget was a winner.</p><p>The economic reality is not quite as clear. Small firms, single parents, film-makers, pensioners, drinkers, punters, drivers and even taxpayers, all felt the touch of the Chancellor’s giving hand.  However, few got as much they thought.</p><p>In overall terms Gordon Brown has spent little. His projections suggest that government tax revenues will be about £4 billion a year more than he forecast back in his Pre Budget report, last November. He effectively used that ‘windfall’ money to fund his main budget tax cuts and dipped into his contingency fund to find the rest. </p><p>His primary tax cuts fall into three key areas with one new spending pledge:</p><p>·	First, he confirmed that he would implement the cuts in fuel duty and other measures for road hauliers and motorists that he had already announced for consultation in November.  That will cost around £2 billion a year;</p><p>·	Second, he announced a widening of the 10p tax band from £1520 to £1880.  That will cost around £1 billion a year;</p><p>·	Third, he announced a package of measures to support families including an increased Children’s Tax Credit, enhanced maternity pay and the introduction of paid maternity leave.  This will cost around £1 billion a year from 2002/03.</p><p>·	Finally, he announced a package of measures to increase spending on schools, the health service and to fight crime.  Total value of the health and education measures are £2 billion while the package is worth around £700 million a year over three years.</p><p>Overall, this was a budget for the family, for local communities, for the City- and for the electorate.  Gordon Brown touched on every key issue a Chancellor should in an election year, but his intended audience was clearly on the other side of the Irish Sea.  </p><p>In the final seconds of his speech, the Chancellor pulled his by now traditional rabbit and hat trick- £2 billion of new spending for schools and hospitals. The audible gasp from the Chamber masked the reality for Northern Ireland. Even though it is extra money- and the more welcome for that- less of it will be coming our way than is needed to address some of our most pressing needs.</p><p>The Barnett Formula determines how much the regions get of total UK spending. While it is too early to be precise, one estimate is that applying the Barnett formula to  £2 billion of gross new money, will ultimately deliver about £18 million per annum to Northern Ireland- 2.7 per cent of the total.</p><p>In addition, if it turns out that only £54 million is coming our way, it may not end up where the Chancellor intended. The Northern Ireland Assembly has absolute discretion in how it spends this devolved money. That means it can be spent anywhere from schools and hospitals to road widening or agriculture, depending on local priorities at the time.</p><p></p><p>Speaking of agriculture, there was little rejoicing behind the disinfectant-soaked lanes of Ulster’s fortress farms. In Northern Ireland agriculture alone employs 35,000 people and accounts for 2.4 per cent of GDP, as compared with 0.8 per cent in the UK and 3.9 per cent in RoI. </p><p>In addition there are an additional 19,000 downstream jobs in processing alone accounting for a further 2.3 per cent of GDP. And that is before you get to employment and economic contribution from the marts, road haulage, feed merchants and the rest of the supply chain.</p><p>The industry is currently holding its breath in the hope that there will be no further outbreaks of Foot and Mouth. It certainly did not need to hold its breath for a lifeline from the Chancellor.  What it got was abolition of tractor duty and share in the overall reduction  in fuel prices, in addition to the already-announced agrimoney compensation.</p><p>Better news for the province’s beleaguered road haulage sector with heavy goods vehicle tax falling by £1,500-£2,100.  While that is certainly a welcome move to close the taxation gap between the North and the Republic, the cross-border fuel differential will still be somewhere between 23-27 pence and that will be little comfort to the industry.</p><p>Reaction to the budget by Northern Ireland’s businesses will be muted . These budget proposals have to be measured against the raft of compliance obligations introduced by the Chancellor since 1997,  which the British Chambers of Commerce estimate have cost UK business £10 billion over four years.</p><p>Simplified accounting measures for VAT will become effective from next month.  These will double the threshold of the annual accounting limit to £600,000 and increase the cash accounting limit from £350,000 to £600,000. In both cases the ceiling for these measure will increase to £750,000. </p><p></p><p><br>The Chancellor has also announced consultation on a new flat-rate scheme of VAT accounting for businesses with sales of up to £100,000. However, set against the growing burden of administration faced by businesses, there were few compliments for the Chancellor when it came to the morning-after interviews.</p><p>Greatest praise for the budget came from voluntary organisations and low-income families.  Ironically, it is this area of his £1 billion tax cuts that may have the greatest long-term impact on business and could actually increase the employer’s compliance burden and drive up operational costs. </p><p>More than 100,000 working families in Northern Ireland will benefit from his family and personal finance package. For the lowest-paid, up goes child benefit and Working Family Credit, while down comes taxable income as the 10p rate is extended. But the real tax benefit may still surprise many.</p><p>While the Chancellor extended the 10 pence tax rate, he only shifted it to £1,880, at the bottom of the expectation scale.  The maximum gain from that benefit alone will be £36 a year- about 70 pence a week.</p><p>But taken together these will have a worrying impact on employers’ compliance burden. The increased tax threshold, paid maternity leave increasing from 18 to 26 weeks, a fortnight’s paternity leave worth £200 and the increase in the minimum wage to £4.10 are already being calculated. One of Northern Ireland’ leading employers told me at the PricewaterhouseCoopers’ Budget Breakfast, the morning after the budget, that his overheads will go up by £1million a year as a direct result of these measures.</p><p>To add to the problems, the Chancellor announced that he would accept the Myners Report [a review of institutional investment with an emphasis on pension funds].  While this may help re-focus pension fund investment policies on smaller firms, the associated increase in compliance costs will not be so welcome by local employers providing company pension schemes.</p><p></p><p></p><p></p><p></p><p></p><p>Overall, the business community had hoped for more specific incentives for regional business and the private sector, particularly when the Chancellor announced a government surplus of £23 billion, compared with earlier forecasts of £14 billion.  The reality has been a prudent, carefully targeted budget, high on pre-election rhetoric, but with and his proposals are coming out at the lower end of expectations. For Northern Ireland, this has been more than a little disappointing.<br> <br></p></p>
