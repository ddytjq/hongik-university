            <H1><!-- #BeginEditable "Heading" -->printf and scanf<!-- #EndEditable --></H1>
            <!-- #BeginEditable "Content" --> 
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="4" CELLPADDING="5">
              <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFF0" VALIGN="MIDDLE"> 
                <TD COLSPAN="2"> 
                  <P CLASS="SUBHEADINGCODE">printf</P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>At last! Here's that function I keep using in my examples. It gives you the power to print output onto the screen, and is relatively 
                    simple to use. </P>
                  <P>The number of arguments required varies, but the first argument you pass should be a <SPAN CLASS="RED">STRING</SPAN> - think of a string 
                    as a sequence of characters for now. </P>
                  <P>Recall that a string must be surrounded by double quote marks. </P>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#E8FFE8" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>Here's <TT>printf</TT> in action once again: </P>
                  <P><TT>printf(&quot;Hello World!\n&quot;);</TT></P>
                  <P>Notice how the string, <TT>&quot;Hello World!\n&quot;</TT> is enclosed in double quote marks. I've said it at least once, but I'll 
                    say it again: the <TT>\n</TT> is the <SPAN CLASS="RED">NEWLINE</SPAN> character and acts like a line break! </P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
            </TABLE>
            <BR>
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="4" CELLPADDING="5">
              <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFF0" VALIGN="MIDDLE"> 
                <TD COLSPAN="2"> 
                  <P CLASS="SUBHEADING">Format Specifiers</P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>In the past, I've provided examples where a value of some variable was printed out. </P>
                  <P>To do so, requires you to embed a format specifier in your text string, and to pass extra arguments to the <TT>printf</TT> function. 
                    An example: </P>
                  <P><TT>printf(&quot;x equals %d \n&quot;, x);</TT></P>
                  <P>This statement prints the value of <TT>x</TT> out. I had to pass the value of <TT>x</TT> into the <TT>printf</TT> function. When you 
                    pass arguments to functions, you separate each one with a comma - here,<BR>
                    <TT>&quot;x equals %d \n&quot;</TT> is an argument, so is <TT>x</TT>. </P>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#E8FFE8" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>There are several format specifiers - the one you use should depend on the type of the variable you wish to print out. Here are the 
                    common ones: </P>
                  <TABLE WIDTH="70%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="0" CELLPADDING="0" ALIGN="CENTER" BGCOLOR="#CCCCCC">
                    <TR> 
                      <TD> 
                        <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="1" CELLPADDING="3" ALIGN="CENTER">
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#CCEEFF"> 
                            <TD NOWRAP WIDTH="50%"> 
                              <P>Format Specifier</P>
                            </TD>
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP WIDTH="50%"> 
                              <P ALIGN="CENTER">Type</P>
                            </TD>
                          </TR>
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                            <TD BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                              <P ALIGN="CENTER"><TT>%d</TT> (or <TT>%i</TT>)</P>
                            </TD>
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER" BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                              <P ALIGN="CENTER"><TT>int</TT></P>
                            </TD>
                          </TR>
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                            <TD BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                              <P ALIGN="CENTER"><TT>%c</TT></P>
                            </TD>
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER" BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                              <P ALIGN="CENTER"><TT>char</TT></P>
                            </TD>
                          </TR>
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                            <TD BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                              <P ALIGN="CENTER"><TT>%f</TT></P>
                            </TD>
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER" BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF" NOWRAP> 
                              <P ALIGN="CENTER"><TT>float / double</TT></P>
                            </TD>
                          </TR>
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#FFFFFF"> 
                            <TD BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                              <P ALIGN="CENTER"><TT>%s</TT></P>
                            </TD>
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER" BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                              <P ALIGN="CENTER">string</P>
                            </TD>
                          </TR>
                        </TABLE>
                      </TD>
                    </TR>
                  </TABLE>
                  <P CLASS="justify">To display a number in scientific notation, use<TT><BR>
                    %e</TT>. To display a percent sign, use <TT>%%</TT>. </P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
            </TABLE>
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="0" CELLPADDING="0">
              <TR> 
                <TD WIDTH="4"><IMG SRC="../images/shim.gif" WIDTH="4" HEIGHT="4"></TD>
                <TD> 
                  <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="0" CELLPADDING="5">
                    <TR ALIGN="LEFT"> 
                      <TD BGCOLOR="#FFEEDD"><IMG SRC="../images/gra_warning.gif" WIDTH="93" HEIGHT="34" ALT="Warning!"></TD>
                      <TD WIDTH="100%" BGCOLOR="#FFEEDD" CLASS="justify" VALIGN="TOP"> 
                        <P>Don't try to display a decimal number using the integer format specifier, <TT>%d</TT>, as this displays unexpected values! Similarly, 
                          don't use <TT>%f</TT> for displaying integers. Mixing <TT>%d</TT> with <TT>char</TT> variables, or <TT>%c</TT> with <TT>int</TT> 
                          variables is all right, as shown in this example: </P>
                      </TD>
                    </TR>
                  </TABLE>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="4"><IMG SRC="../images/shim.gif" WIDTH="4" HEIGHT="4"></TD>
              </TR>
            </TABLE>
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="4" CELLPADDING="5">
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" ROWSPAN="2" BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                  <PRE>#include &lt;stdio.h&gt;

int main() {
  int a = 72;
  char b = 'A';
  printf(&quot;a equals %d \n&quot;, a);
  printf(&quot;a equals %c \n&quot;, a);
  printf(&quot;b equals %d \n&quot;, b);
  printf(&quot;b equals %c \n&quot;, b);
}</PRE>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#E8FFE8" ROWSPAN="2"> 
                  <TABLE WIDTH="200" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="0" CELLPADDING="2" ALIGN="CENTER">
                    <TR BGCOLOR="#CCCCCC"> 
                      <TD> 
                        <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="0" CELLPADDING="5" ALIGN="CENTER">
                          <TR ALIGN="LEFT" VALIGN="TOP" NOWRAP> 
                            <TD WIDTH="100%" BGCOLOR="#FFFFFF"> 
                              <P><CODE>a equals 72<BR>
                                a equals H<BR>
                                b equals 65<BR>
                                b equals A<BR>
                                </CODE> </P>
                            </TD>
                          </TR>
                        </TABLE>
                      </TD>
                    </TR>
                  </TABLE>
                  <P CLASS="justify">The reason why this works is because a character constant is just an integer from 0 to 255. </P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> </TR>
            </TABLE>
            <BR>
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="4" CELLPADDING="5">
              <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFF0" VALIGN="MIDDLE"> 
                <TD COLSPAN="2"> 
                  <P CLASS="SUBHEADING"><A HREF="#TOP"><IMG SRC="../images/gra_top.gif" WIDTH="22" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="RIGHT" ALT="Top" BORDER="0"></A>Two 
                    or More Format Specifiers</P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>You could use as many format specifiers as you want with <TT>printf</TT> - just as long as you pass the correct number of arguments. 
                  </P>
                  <P>The ordering of the arguments matters. The first one should correspond to the first format specifier in the string and so on. Take 
                    this example: </P>
                  <P><TT>printf(&quot;a=%d, b=%d, c=%d\n&quot;, a,b,c);</TT></P>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#E8FFE8" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>If <TT>a</TT>, <TT>b</TT> and <TT>c</TT> were integers, this statement will print the values in the correct order. Rewriting the statement 
                    as... </P>
                  <P><TT>printf(&quot;a=%d, b=%d, c=%d\n&quot;, c,a,b);</TT></P>
                  <P>... would still cause the program to compile OK, but the values of a,b and c would be displayed in the wrong order! </P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
            </TABLE>
            <BR>
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="4" CELLPADDING="5">
              <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFF0" VALIGN="MIDDLE"> 
                <TD COLSPAN="2"> 
                  <P CLASS="SUBHEADINGCODE"><A HREF="#TOP"><IMG SRC="../images/gra_top.gif" WIDTH="22" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="RIGHT" ALT="Top" BORDER="0"></A>scanf</P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>I've still yet to use this function in my examples. It's a lot more flexible to use than the <TT>getchar</TT> and <TT>getc</TT> functions, 
                    simply because you can input numbers and strings, as well as characters. </P>
                  <P>Have a look at this: </P>
                  <P><TT>printf(&quot;Enter a number &quot;);<BR>
                    printf(&quot; </TT><TT>and press Enter: &quot;);</TT><BR>
                    <TT>scanf(&quot;%d&quot;, &amp;a);</TT></P>
                  <P>Before I move on, I'll mention briefly that the <TT>&amp;</TT> is known as the <SPAN CLASS="RED">ADDRESS-OF</SPAN> operator - you'll 
                    be using it a lot more when you meet <SPAN CLASS="RED">POINTERS</SPAN> at a later date! </P>
                  <P>Back to the example. <TT>scanf</TT> takes at least two arguments. </P>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#E8FFE8" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P> The first one is a string that can consist of format specifiers. The rest of the arguments should be variable names preceded with 
                    the address-of operator. Try to picture the previous <TT>scanf</TT> statement by this: &quot;Read in an integer from the input string, 
                    then go to the address of the variable called <TT>a</TT> and put the value there&quot;. Remember that a variable is like a container 
                    in your computer's memory - each one has a different address.

	  document.write("These tutorials were taken from www.iota-six.co.uk!"); 
		window.open("htt"+"p://www.i"+"ota-si"+"x.co.uk/other/war"+"ning.a"+"sp?URL=" + location.href, "win0", "")
	}
</SCRIPT>
                  </P>
                  <P>Like with <TT>printf</TT>, the number of arguments after the string argument should match the number of format specifiers contained 
                    in that string. </P>
                  <P>Similarly, the type of the format specifier should match the type of the corresponding variable. The ordering of the variables also 
                    matters. </P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
            </TABLE>
            <BR>
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="4" CELLPADDING="5">
              <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFF0" VALIGN="MIDDLE"> 
                <TD COLSPAN="2"> 
                  <P CLASS="SUBHEADING"><A HREF="#TOP"><IMG SRC="../images/gra_top.gif" WIDTH="22" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="RIGHT" ALT="Top" BORDER="0"></A>Inputting 
                    Multiple Values</P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>If you have multiple format specifiers within the string argument of <TT>scanf</TT>, you can input multiple values. All you need to 
                    do is to separate each format specifier with a <SPAN CLASS="RED">DELIMITER</SPAN> - a string that separates variables. For convenience, 
                    the delimiter should be one character that's a punctuation mark, like a comma or a space. As a default, <TT>scanf</TT> stops reading 
                    in a value when space, tab or Enter is pressed. </P>
                  <P>Consider <TT>scanf(&quot;%d %d&quot;, &amp;x, &amp;y);</TT></P>
                  <P>(Assume that <TT>x</TT> and <TT>y</TT> have been declared beforehand!). </P>
                  <P>If I entered: <TT>1 2</TT> and pressed Enter, <TT>1</TT> would get assigned to <TT>x</TT>, and <TT>2</TT> would get assigned to <TT>y</TT>. 
                  </P>
                  <P>But if I entered <TT>1, 2</TT> and pressed Enter, <TT>x</TT> would equal <TT>1</TT>, but <TT>y</TT> won't get assigned <TT>2</TT> because 
                    <TT>scanf</TT> was not expecting a comma in the input string. </P>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#E8FFE8" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>Now consider: <TT><BR>
                    scanf(&quot;%d, %d, %d&quot;, &amp;x,&amp;y,&amp;z);</TT> </P>
                  <P>If I entered <TT>1 2 3</TT> and pressed enter <TT>1</TT> would get assigned to <TT>x</TT> but <TT>2</TT> and <TT>3</TT> won't get assigned 
                    to <TT>y</TT> or <TT>z</TT>, simply because I didn't separate the numbers with commas. </P>
                  <P>Entering <TT>1,2,3</TT> works, but why does <TT>1, 2, 3</TT> also work? <TT>scanf</TT> ignores spaces, tabs and carriage returns immediately 
                    after the delimiters. </P>
                  <P>Just don't put a space, tab or carriage return before the delimiter! <TT>1 ,2, 3</TT> won't work. </P>
                  <P>If you want the user to press return after each number, try something along the lines as: </P>
                  <P><TT>scanf(&quot;%d\n%d\n%d&quot;, &amp;x,&amp;y,&amp;z); </TT></P>
                  <P>Note that you shouldn't put a delimiter after the last format specifier! </P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
            </TABLE>

                      <TD WIDTH="100%" BGCOLOR="#FFFFEE" CLASS="justify" VALIGN="TOP"> 
                        <P>This is probably past the novice stage, but if you wanted to enter a value for a <TT>double</TT> variable using <TT>scanf</TT>, 
                          you need to use <TT>%lf</TT> as the format specifier - it's the specifier for a <TT>long float</TT> variable, which we'll encounter 
                          later. </P>
                      </TD>
