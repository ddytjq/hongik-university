            <H1><!-- #BeginEditable "Heading" -->Binary Numbers<!-- #EndEditable --></H1>
            <!-- #BeginEditable "Content" --> 
                  
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="4" CELLPADDING="5">
              <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFF0"> 
                <TD COLSPAN="2"> 
                  <P CLASS="SUBHEADING">Zeros and Ones</P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>In a nutshell: <SPAN CLASS="RED">BINARY IS BASE 2</SPAN>. </P>
                  <P>So the only two digits you use to form binary numbers are 0 and 1. </P>
                  <P>I mentioned earlier that binary code is the lowest level language and that it's sometimes referred to as &quot;machine code&quot;. 
                    This is because that the computer interprets a string of zeros and ones as a sequence of instructions: zero meaning an &quot;off&quot; 
                    state, one meaning an &quot;on&quot; state. </P>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>Anyway, back to binary numbers themselves, base 2 implies that you should know the powers of 2: </P>
                  <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="0" CELLPADDING="0">
                    <TR VALIGN="TOP"> 
                      <TD WIDTH="33%"> 
                        <P>2<SUP>0</SUP> = 1<BR>
                          2<SUP>1</SUP> = 2<BR>
                          2<SUP>2</SUP> = 4<BR>
                          2<SUP>3</SUP> = 8<BR>
                          2<SUP>4</SUP> = 16<BR>
                          2<SUP>5</SUP> = 32 </P>
                      </TD>
                      <TD WIDTH="34%"> 
                        <P>2<SUP>6</SUP> = 64<BR>
                          2<SUP>7</SUP> = 128<BR>
                          2<SUP>8</SUP> = 256<BR>
                          2<SUP>9</SUP> = 512<BR>
                          2<SUP>10</SUP> = 1024<BR>
                          2<SUP>11</SUP> = 2048 </P>
                      </TD>
                      <TD WIDTH="33%"> 
                        <P>2<SUP>12</SUP> = 4096<BR>
                          2<SUP>13</SUP> = 8192<BR>
                          2<SUP>14</SUP> = 16384<BR>
                          2<SUP>15</SUP> = 32768<BR>
                          2<SUP>16</SUP> = 65536<BR>
                        </P>
                      </TD>
                    </TR>
                  </TABLE>
                  <P>- Any higher, and I must get my calculator :) </P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
            </TABLE>
                  <BR>
                  
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="4" CELLPADDING="5">
              <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFF0"> 
                <TD COLSPAN="2"> 
                  <P CLASS="SUBHEADING">Converting From Decimal to Binary</P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>The procedure is very similar to the method used for hexadecimal numbers - the only difference is that you're using powers of 2 as 
                    opposed to 16. </P>
                  <P>Take the number 70 in decimal. </P>
                  <P>Find the highest power of 2 that goes into 70. Obviously, it's 64, so the highest power of 2 is 6, therefore you should expect 7 binary 
                    digits (or 7 <SPAN CLASS="RED">BITS</SPAN> for short). </P>
                  <P>64 goes into 70 so the 1st bit is 1. </P>
                  <P>Now work out the remainder: 70 - 64 = 6. </P>
                  <P>Decreasing the power... how many times does 32 go into 6? Zero times, so the 2nd bit is 0. </P>
                  <P>Similarly, 16 and 8 don't go into 6, to the 3rd and 4th bits are both zeros. </P>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>4 goes into 6 once, so the 5th bit is 1. The remainder is 6 - 4 = 2. </P>
                  <P>2 goes into 2, so the 6th bit is also 1. </P>
                  <P>Now, there is no remainder, so the rest of the bits are all zeros - the 7th bit in this case. </P>
                  <P>Then we can conclude that 70 in decimal is expressed as 1000110 in binary. </P>
                  <P>Notice how you much of this you can do in your head, compared to hexadecimal. Because you're only working with zeros and ones - either 
                    a power of 2 goes into a number or not. </P>
                  <P>Converting negative decimals into binary will be covered in the bitwise operators section!

                  </P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
            </TABLE>
                  <BR>
                  
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="4" CELLPADDING="5">
              <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFF0"> 
                <TD COLSPAN="2"> 
                  <P CLASS="SUBHEADING"><SPAN CLASS="SUBHEADINGCODE"><A HREF="#TOP"><IMG SRC="../images/gra_top.gif" WIDTH="22" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="RIGHT" ALT="Top" BORDER="0"></A></SPAN>Converting 
                    From Binary to Decimal</P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>The reverse is easier. Take the binary number 100011011. </P>
                  <P>First write the bits in a column with the first bit at the top: </P>
                  <P>1<BR>
                    0<BR>
                    0<BR>
                    0<BR>
                    1<BR>
                    1<BR>
                    0<BR>
                    1<BR>
                    1</P>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>Then multiply the bottom with 2<SUP>0</SUP>, the next one up with 2<SUP>1</SUP> and so on... </P>
                  <P>1*2<SUP>8</SUP> = 256<BR>
                    0*2<SUP>7</SUP> = 0<BR>
                    0*2<SUP>6</SUP> = 0<BR>
                    0*2<SUP>5</SUP> = 0<BR>
                    1*2<SUP>4</SUP> = 16<BR>
                    1*2<SUP>3</SUP> = 8<BR>
                    0*2<SUP>2</SUP> = 0<BR>
                    1*2<SUP>1</SUP> = 2<BR>
                    1*2<SUP>0</SUP> = 1</P>
                  <P>Add up the right hand side to get your answer: </P>
                  <P>256 + 16 + 8 + 2 + 1 = 283. </P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
            </TABLE>
                  <BR>
                  
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="4" CELLPADDING="5">
              <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFF0"> 
                <TD COLSPAN="2"> 
                  <P CLASS="SUBHEADING"><SPAN CLASS="SUBHEADINGCODE"><A HREF="#TOP"><IMG SRC="../images/gra_top.gif" WIDTH="22" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="RIGHT" ALT="Top" BORDER="0"></A></SPAN>Converting 
                    From Hexadecimal to Binary</P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>This sounds scary but it's not that bad. </P>
                  <P>Isn't it a pure coincidence that 16 is 2<SUP>4</SUP>?! We can expand on this by saying that any number from 0 to F (0 to 15) can be 
                    expressed using 4 bits: </P>
                  <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="0" CELLPADDING="0">
                    <TR> 
                      <TD> 
                        <P>0 = 0000<BR>
                          1 = 0001<BR>
                          2 = 0010<BR>
                          3 = 0011</P>
                      </TD>
                      <TD> 
                        <P>4 = 0100<BR>
                          5 = 0101<BR>
                          6 = 0110<BR>
                          7 = 0111</P>
                      </TD>
                      <TD> 
                        <P>8 = 1000<BR>
                          9 = 1001<BR>
                          A = 1010<BR>
                          B = 1011</P>
                      </TD>
                      <TD> 
                        <P>C = 1100<BR>
                          D = 1101<BR>
                          E = 1110<BR>
                          F = 1111</P>
                      </TD>
                    </TR>
                  </TABLE>
                  <P>Then take any hexadecimal number: AD say. </P>
                  <P>Replace each hex digit with the 4 bit equivalent: </P>
                  <P>A = 1010<BR>
                    D = 1101</P>
                  <P>So AD in hex is 10101101 in binary. </P>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>Check: 10*16 + 13*1 = 160 + 13 = 173. </P>
                  <P>1*2<SUP>7</SUP> = 128<BR>
                    0*2<SUP>6</SUP> = 0<BR>
                    1*2<SUP>5</SUP> = 32<BR>
                    0*2<SUP>4</SUP> = 0<BR>
                  </P>
                  <P>128 + 32 = 160. </P>
                  <P> 1*2<SUP>3</SUP> = 8<BR>
                    1*2<SUP>2</SUP> = 4<BR>
                    0*2<SUP>1</SUP> = 0<BR>
                    1*2<SUP>0</SUP> = 1</P>
                  <P>8 + 4 + 1 = 13. </P>
                  <P>160 + 13 = 173. </P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
            </TABLE>
                  <BR>
                  
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="4" CELLPADDING="5">
              <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFF0"> 
                <TD COLSPAN="2"> 
                  <P CLASS="SUBHEADING"><SPAN CLASS="SUBHEADINGCODE"><A HREF="#TOP"><IMG SRC="../images/gra_top.gif" WIDTH="22" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="RIGHT" ALT="Top" BORDER="0"></A></SPAN>Converting 
                    From Binary to Hexadecimal</P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>This is just as easy. </P>
                  <P>Take a binary number, 1101010 say, and starting from the right, group the bits into four bits: </P>
                  <P>0110 1010</P>
                  <P>- Notice how I added an extra zero as the first bit to make it look a little nicer. </P>
                  <P>Now simply replace each group of 4 with the hex value: </P>
                  <P>0110 = 0 + 4 + 2 + 0 = 6. <BR>
                    1010 = 8 + 0 + 2 + 0 = 10 = A.</P>
                  <P>So 1101010 = 6A. </P>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="50%" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>Check: 6*16 + 10*1 = 96 + 10 = 106. </P>
                  <P>0*2<SUP>7</SUP> = 0<BR>
                    1*2<SUP>6</SUP> = 64<BR>
                    1*2<SUP>5</SUP> = 32<BR>
                    0*2<SUP>4</SUP> = 0<BR>
                  </P>
                  <P>64 + 32 = 96. </P>
                  <P> 1*2<SUP>3</SUP> = 8<BR>
                    0*2<SUP>2</SUP> = 0<BR>
                    1*2<SUP>1</SUP> = 2<BR>
                    0*2<SUP>0</SUP> = 0</P>
                  <P>8 + 2 = 10. </P>
                  <P>96 + 10 = 106. </P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
            </TABLE>
