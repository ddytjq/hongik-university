<center><H3>Appendix 11: The Greek Alphabet</H3></center>
<center><strong>(on Internet Explorer and Netscape)</strong></center>
</font>
<pre>

</pre>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Since the two main browsers on the market today - Microsoft's Internet Explorer and Netscape - have different ways of programming to get Greek letters, the coding for one browser frequently won't be displayed correctly on the other browser (though I have found at least one site - the Harvard Abstract Service - where the characters are properly shown on both browsers).
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Let's look at Internet Explorer (IE) first.   You get a Greek letter in HTML coding by using this format:
<p></pre>
<center><strong>
                         <font color=navy>&#60;font face=symbol&#62;</font><font color=red>letter</font><font color=navy>&#60;/font&#62;</font> 
</strong></center>
<p>
If you use a small <font color=red>a</font> where I have <font color=red>letter</font> you will get the Greek letter for small alpha.   And if you use capital <font color=red>A</font> instead of small <font color=red>a</font>, you get the capital Greek letter for alpha.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>A table follows shortly which tells you what English equivalents are necessary to get the large and small Greek letters using the font coding.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The problem is that is you view the "font" coding with Netscape you won't see the Greek letter; you'll see only the English letter used in the font command.   Therefore if you're using Netscape to view Essay #1, instead of the Greek letter for "pi" (3.14159) used in the formulas of Essay #1, you'll see "p" instead.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The coding to get the Greek letters in Netscape is much simpler.   All you do is add (without any space and without the quotation marks) "&" to the spelling of the Greek letter.   If you use a small letter to begin the spelling, you get the small letter in Greek.   A large letter at the start of the spelling gives you the large Greek letter.   For example, if you want a small alpha in Greek, you add & to alpha - remember, no space - and you'll get small alpha.   Similarly if you prefix Alpha with &, you'll get the capital Greek letter for alpha.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The problem is that if you use Netscape coding and view it with IE, you're <em>not</em> going to see the intended Greek letter; instead you're going to see the spelling of the Greek letter preceded by &.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>As a general rule, if the Greek letter in a given essay or appendix is mainly "pi", I prefer the IE coding because it's not too hard for the reader to understand "p" really means "pi" if he or she is viewing it with Netscape.   But if the Greek letter is less familiar - such as omega, rho, and lambda - I generally use Netscape.   At least you'll see the spelling (preceded by &) on IE, and you can always check the table below to see how the less familiar letter looks.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Which brings me to the table<strong>...</strong>
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The English equivalent (Eng Equiv) below is the letter you should use with the font command on IE.   (If you want to get the <em>capital</em> Greek letter, remember to use the <em>capital</em> letter in the font coding.)   The IE browser will properly illustrate the Greek letter in the cyan columns, but will probably show only the awkward spelling in the brown Netscape columns.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>But if you view this table with Netscape, you will probably see the Greek letters displayed in the brown columns, but only the English equivalents in the IE cyan columns.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>If you see the Greek displayed properly in <em>both</em> columns on the same browser, consider yourself lucky because it means you'll be able to see the Greek in the text as it was meant to be viewed, even though I switched back and forth between the two systems.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>In any event, you should be able to see the proper Greek letters displayed in the following table at least <em>once</em> via one of the two main browsers on the market today.

<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>There's a good chance you're interested in astronomy if you found yourself this far into the series of essays.   And you are probably already familiar with the Greek letters through the Bayer system, a method of naming the stars in each constellation that (usually) gives the brightest star the name "alpha", second brightest "beta", and so on.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Of course, our English "alphabet" comes from simply pronouncing the first two letters of the Greek alphabet together.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>And if you check how the capital Greek "delta" looks, you will understand the origins of "delta wing" and "Nile delta".
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Capital sigma is used frequently in mathematics as a summation sign, and the small sigma usually means "standard deviation" (a measure of the dispersion of data).
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Several Greek letters require two English letters to pronounce them properly such as theta (th), phi (ph), chi (ch), and psi (ps).   Many English words - such as theology, philosophy, chronology, and psychiatry - beginning with these <em>two</em> consonants usually have their origins in an ancient Greek word that began with these <em>single</em> Greek letters.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The placement of the "z" (zeta) as the sixth Greek letter has perhaps the most intriguing history.   You'll notice that the sequence of the Greek letter is quite similar to their English counterparts, with this letter being a major exception.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The Romans conquered the Greeks at the battle of Pynda in 168 BC.   Among the items of Greek culture they assumed were certain letters of the Greek alphabet, except the sixth letter zeta, which the Romans thought they could do without.   On second thought after a few years, they decided z was a necessary sound to have in the alphabet and appended it at the end.   Hence z, the sixth letter of the Greek alphabet, wound up as the last letter in the Roman alphabet, and that's the way it came down to us in the English alphabet. 
<p>
