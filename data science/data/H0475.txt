  <tr>
      <td width="732" height="7" colspan="2" align="center">&nbsp;
        <p><font color="#0000FF" face="Comic Sans MS" size="4">&quot;What
        I normally look for in a spectrum&quot;</font><font color="#0000FF" face="Comic Sans MS" size="5"><br>
        </font>
        </p>
      </td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
      <td width="24" height="7" align="left">
        <p align="left"></td>
      <td width="587" height="7" align="left"></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
      <td width="24" height="7" align="left" valign="top"><font color="#000080">1)</font></td>
      <td width="587" height="7" align="left"><font color="#000080"><b>Look for the
        tallest peak in the spectrum</b> above 200 mass units. (The low end of a
        spectrum can often be confounded with solvent noise.)&nbsp; Then look
        for peaks that are roughly double or half the mass. This may tell you whether
        there are multiply charged species present which can help with mass
        determination.</font>
        <p><font color="#000080">&nbsp;&nbsp;</font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
      <td width="24" height="7" align="left" valign="top"><font color="#000080">2)</font></td>
      <td width="587" height="7" align="left"><font color="#000080"><b>Counts.&nbsp; </b>Resist
        interpreting meager spectra.&nbsp; If you know that on a particular day
        that 1.0X10<sup>6 </sup>is a respectable, reliable signal then you will know that a
        spectrum that tops out at 1.0X10<sup>4</sup> counts (or at background)
        may not be a reliable spectrum to interpret.&nbsp;&nbsp;</font>
        <p>&nbsp;</td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
      <td width="24" height="7" align="left" valign="top"><font color="#000080">3)</font></td>
      <td width="587" height="7" align="left"><font color="#000080"><b>The quality</b> <b>of
        the spectrum is important.</b> You will waste valuable time interpreting
        a low quality spectrum.&nbsp; If you are not happy with the quality of
        the spectrum try averaging several or many low level spectra to obtain a
        better quality &quot;averaged mass spectrum.&quot;&nbsp; Compare this to a
        background spectrum to see if the peaks really stand out.</font>
        <p>&nbsp;</td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
      <td width="24" height="7" align="left" valign="top"><font color="#000080">4)</font></td>
      <td width="587" height="7" align="left"><font color="#000080"><b>Reproducibility</b>
        <b>is important.&nbsp; </b>The peak must be consistent to be considered
        a relevant peak. You should not lend credence to what I call &quot;one scan wonders.&quot;</font>
        <p>&nbsp;</td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
      <td width="24" height="7" align="left" valign="top"><font color="#000080">5)</font></td>
      <td width="587" height="7" align="left"><font color="#000080"><b>Determining the
        charge state of a peak when only one peak is obvious</b><br>
        A molecule will often have adduct ions associated with it other than
        hydrogen .&nbsp; Look for sodium or ammonium adducts.&nbsp; These
        adducts can often give you a hint as to the charge state of a
        peak.&nbsp; For example if there is only one major species in a spectrum
        look for the sodium adduct following that peak.&nbsp; If it is a singly charged species the
        sodium adduct will be found at +22 mass units.&nbsp; If the peak is
        doubly charged the adduct will appear at + 11 mass units.</font>
        <p><font color="#000080"><b>Isotopes</b><br>
        If the mass spectrometer you are working with has sufficient resolution look at the
        isotopes, a singly charged ion will show isotopic peaks that differ by 1
        mass unit, a doubly charged ion will show peaks that differ by 0.5 mass
        units and so on. This is another way to deduce the charge state of a
        peak and thus the mass.&nbsp;</font></td>
  </tr>

  <tr>
      <td width="24" height="14" align="left" valign="top"><font color="#0000FF"><b>Q</b></font></td>
      <td width="587" height="14" colspan="2" align="left"><font color="#000080">&nbsp;How can I tell
        if a peak is real?</font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
      <td width="24" height="14" align="left" valign="top"></td>
      <td width="587" height="14" colspan="2" align="left"></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
      <td width="24" height="14" align="left" valign="top"><font color="#0000FF"><b>A</b></font></td>
      <td width="587" height="14" colspan="2" align="left"><font color="#000080">Wow, this is some
        question.&nbsp; All peaks are real.&nbsp; In an LC/MS run we look for
        peaks that reoccur in multiple adjacent scans (spectrums) but not in
        every scan.&nbsp; If the peak occurs in every scan it may be a
        background peak. It is
        possible to get system noise or spikes that only occur in one scan or
        sporadically these are most likely electronic or some other form of system noise.&nbsp;</font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
      <td width="24" height="14" align="left" valign="top"></td>
      <td width="587" height="14" colspan="2" align="left"></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
      <td width="24" height="14" align="left" valign="top"><font color="#0000FF"><b>Q</b></font></td>
      <td width="587" height="14" colspan="2" align="left"><font color="#000080">&nbsp;How can I be
        sure of the identity of a peak.</font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
      <td width="24" height="14" align="left" valign="top"></td>
      <td width="587" height="14" colspan="2" align="left"></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
      <td width="24" height="14" align="left" valign="top"><font color="#0000FF"><b>A</b></font></td>
      <td width="587" height="14" colspan="2" align="left"><font color="#000080">Well, a mass is just
        a mass and many compounds have isobaric mass so you can't be sure from
        just a mass.&nbsp; In the old days we would perform an enzymatic digest
        on a protein, run an LC/MS peptide map and match up the mass with the
        theoretical fragments. Today the bar is rightly higher and we go one
        step further in the identification, we take the peak through a
        fragmentation and match up the fragment masses with the theoretical CID
        fragment masses for that peptide. This gives us a positive ID.</font>
        <p><font color="#000080">Another overlooked component in LC/MS is part
        of what makes LC/MS so powerful and that is the correlation of mass and
        LC retention time.&nbsp; If the retention time of a molecule has been
        previously characterized this information can be linked with the mass
        information for a positive ID.</font></p>
        <p><font color="#000080">If you are characterizing a new molecule try
        modifying the molecule to see if you can modify the mass.&nbsp; Try an
        enzyme digest if the unknown is a protein or try chemical
        modification if it is a small molecule and see if the mass of the
        unknown changes as predicted by the mass ID.</font></p>
      </td>
  </tr>

      <td width="24" height="21" align="left" valign="top"><font color="#0000FF"><b>Q</b></font></td>
      <td width="594" height="21" colspan="2"><font color="#000080">How can I differentiate a
        compound at one mass from another at twice the mass?&nbsp; For example a
        compound with mass 1000 will display peaks at m/z </font> <font color="#FF00FF">1001</font>
        <font color="#000080">
        and</font> <font color="#FF00FF">501</font>,<font color="#000080"> and a compound with mass 2000 may display peaks at m/z 2001,</font> <font color="#FF00FF">1001</font><font color="#000080">, 666.7 and</font> <font color="#FF00FF">501</font><font color="#000080">.&nbsp;
        The mass determination can further be confounded if the peptide at 1000
        forms dimers during the electrospray process.</font></td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="24" height="21" align="left" valign="top"></td>
      <td width="171" height="21"></td>
      <td width="423" height="21" align="left"></td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="24" height="21" align="left" valign="top"><font color="#0000FF"><b>A</b></font></td>
      <td width="594" height="21" colspan="2"><font color="#000080">1) The peak envelope does not skip
        peaks, for example the 2000 mass even if it does not have an obvious
        peak at 2001 it should have the 666.7 peak between the 1001 and 501
        peaks.</font>
        <p><font color="#000080">2) Also try to determine the charge state of the ions from the
        adducts or from the isotopes.&nbsp; This will tell you what the mass of
        the compound is.</font></p>
        <p><font color="#000080">3) Dimer formation can be a major problem in some analyses. Try to
        reduce the concentration of the analyte. Often if the concentration is
        too high dimers will be observed in the spectrum.&nbsp; Also dimers can
        be reduced by changing some of the setting on the mass spectrometer.</font></p>
        <p><font color="#000080">4) With the peak envelope of larger molecules (10kDa+) look for
        smooth peak distributions.&nbsp; The peak distribution should have a
        smooth bell shaped curve appearance, sometimes trailing off to the
        right. The peak to peak relationship should be predictable, if one observes
        an alternating pattern of peak intensities this may be a clue to a
        coeluting dimer.&nbsp;</font></td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="24" height="21" align="left" valign="top"></td>

      <td width="24" height="21" align="left" valign="top"><font color="#0000FF"><b>Q</b></font></td>
      <td width="594" height="21" colspan="2"><font color="#000080">I believe that I am observing
        artifctual fragmentation and do not know which peaks are real.</font></td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="24" height="21" align="left" valign="top"></td>
      <td width="171" height="21"></td>
      <td width="423" height="21" align="left"></td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="24" height="21" align="left" valign="top"><font color="#0000FF"><b>A</b></font></td>
      <td width="594" height="21" colspan="2"><font color="#000080">Again all peaks are real. Sometimes on can induce
        fragmentation before the first mass filter (in-source CID, source CID).
        While this effect can be a valuable characterization strategy for those possessing
        single quadrupole instruments it is often a distraction when fragmentation
        is not the goal.&nbsp; My suggestion is to try and slow the ions down so
        that they will not collide and fragment with the residual atmosphere in
        the source.&nbsp;&nbsp;</font></td>
    </tr>
