            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;One 
              of the most important things to remember about physics is that Nature 
              looks different depending on the distance or energy scale being 
              looked at. <b>Physical constants</b> 
              like the speed of light and Planck's constant have values that tell 
              us the size or energy where the the observable physics begins to 
              change substantially, and a different way of describing the physics 
              mathematically is called for.</font></p>
            <h4 align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3" color="#000099">Relativity</font></h4>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The 
              first example is the <b>speed of light</b></font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="../equations/SpeedOfLight.gif" width="106" height="48" alt="Speed of light"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">In 
              a physical system where all the velocities that matter are much 
              much less than the speed of light, the system can usually be described 
              very well by ordinary <b>Newtonian</b> 
              physics. When some important velocity in the system begins to approach 
              the speed of light, the Newtonian description doesn't fit so well 
              any more, and the system has to be described in fully <b>relativistic</b> 
              terms using the mathematics of Einstein's theory of special relativity. 
              </font></p>
            <h4 align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3" color="#000099">Quantum 
              physics</font></h4>
            <p> <font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The 
              most important physical constant uncovered in the 20th century is 
              <b>Planck's constant</b></font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="../equations/PlancksConstant.gif" width="298" height="50" alt="Planck's constant"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"> 
              which tells us about the distance or momentum scale where <b>classical</b> 
              physics stops making sense and a <b>quantum</b> 
              description becomes necessary. (The unit labeled eV is called an 
              electron-volt, it is the amount of work one needs to do to move 
              one electron across one volt of electric potential. This is a standard 
              unit for measuring particle physics energy scales. You'll see more 
              of it on this web site.) <br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Planck's constant, in the form of the de Broglie 
              wavelength, tells us when the wave/particle duality of quantum physics 
              becomes measurable in a physical object. The de Broglie wavelength 
              of a particle of momentum p is</font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="../equations/deBroglie.gif" width="57" height="50" alt="debroglie wavelength"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">In 
              quantum particle-wave duality, if the size of an object of momentum 
              p is smaller than its de Broglie wavelength, then quantum wave interference 
              will be measurable enough so that classical physics will fail to 
              be a good description of the object's behavior. <br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp; Planck's constant is a very very small number. The 
              de Broglie wavelength of something like a piece of cheese in the 
              fridge would very very tiny. The cheese would have to be subatomic 
              size before <b>quantum cheese effects</b> 
              would take over -- and then it wouldn't be identifiable as cheese 
              any longer.</font></p>
            <h4 align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3" color="#000099">Atomic 
              physics</font></h4>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp; 
              <b>Planck's constant</b> combined 
              with the <b>mass</b> and <b>electric 
              charge</b> of an electron gives us another important number 
              in physics called the <b>Bohr radius</b></font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="../equations/BohrRadius.gif" width="246" height="54" alt="Bohr radius"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">which 
              tells us the <b>average size of a hydrogen 
              atom</b>. The Bohr radius describes the distance scale where 
              atomic physics, using <b>nonrelativistic 
              quantum mechanics</b> in a classical electromagnetic field, 
              is the best way to describe the physics.</font></p>
            <h4 align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3" color="#000099">Elementary 
              particle physics</font></h4>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp; 
              In elementary particle physics, electromagnetism, the strong and 
              weak nuclear forces are described by a combination of relativity 
              and quantum mechanics, called relativistic quantum field theory. 
              The important numbers for comparing these three forces are called 
              coupling constants, whose size measures the strength of the respective 
              force. For electromagnetism, the coupling constant is called the 
              <b>fine structure constant</b> <font face="Symbol, symbol" size="3">a</font> 
              and is formed by the electron charge, Planck's constant and the 
              speed of light.</font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="../equations/FineStructure.gif" width="229" height="50" alt="Fine structure constant"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp; 
              Notice this constant ends up being just a plain number, with no 
              units associated with it. That is what is meant by a <b>dimensionless</b> 
              coupling constant. <br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp; The combination of <b>Planck's 
              constant</b> with the <b>speed of 
              light</b> and the <b>electron charge</b> 
              means this coupling constant is telling us about the <b>quantum 
              relativistic physics of electromagnetism</b>, that is, it 
              tells us something about electromagnetism at distance scales where 
              quantum mechanics and special relativity are both important to the 
              physics at the same time. <br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp; There are also dimensionless coupling constants for 
              the strong and weak nuclear interactions. The table below compares 
              their relative strengths and ranges.</font></p>

            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp; 
              The weak nuclear force isn't actually that weak when measured by 
              <font face="Symbol, symbol" size="3">a</font><sub>W</sub>, 
              but it has the shortest range, because the gauge bosons are very 
              heavy and have short lifetimes, so they can't travel very far without 
              decaying into lighter particles. The strong nuclear force binds 
              quarks into neutrons, protons and other hadrons, and binds protons 
              and neutrons into the nuclei of atoms, but because of quark confinement, 
              the strong force has a very small range as well.</font></p>
            <h4 align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3" color="#000099">Spontaneous 
              symmetry breaking</font></h4>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp; 
              Physicists originally had a lot of trouble reconciling relativistic 
              quantum field theory with the <b>weak interaction</b> 
              that is responsible, for example, for what is called <b>beta 
              decay</b> of neutron into proton, electron and neutrino. 
              The very short range of weak interaction suggested that the gauge 
              boson the mediated the force must be very heavy. But quantum relativistic 
              gauge field theories only seemed to make sense if the gauge boson 
              had zero mass. <br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp; The paradox was resolved by the discovery that a special 
              type of coupling to a particle called the <b>Higgs</b> 
              could make the weak interaction gauge bosons able to become very 
              heavy without destroying the symmetries that make the quantum theory 
              mathematically consistent. This interaction between the gauge bosons 
              and Higgs particles is called <b>spontaneous 
              symmetry breaking</b>, which is a misnomer because the symmetry 
              of the theory is still there, it's just hidden in the interactions 
              of the theory.<br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp; Spontaneous symmetry occurs at some energy scale determined 
              by the quantum interactions of the Higgs particle with itself, and 
              this scale in turn determines the mass of the gauge boson. If the 
              scale of symmetry breaking is very large, then it takes a very large 
              particle accelerator to find evidence of the effect. In 1983, this 
              theory was confirmed by the direct observation of the heavy gauge 
              bosons in the powerful particle accelerator at <b>CERN</b> 
              in Geneva. The masses of the three weak interaction gauge bosons 
              are now known to be</font></p>

            <h4 align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3" color="#000099">Running 
              coupling constants</font></h4>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp; 
              Assigning a coupling strength to each force is tricky, however, 
              because of the quantum relativistic effect known as the <b>running 
              coupling constant</b>. In quantum relativistic field theory, 
              the calculation of a simple particle interaction, say, an electron 
              interacting with another electron by exchanging a photon, is complicated 
              by an infinite series of possible virtual particle interactions 
              in a <b>cloud of quantum relativistic fluctuations</b>. 
              <br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp; That cloud of quantum relativistic fluctuations changes 
              the measured value of the coupling constant and makes it depend 
              on the energy scale at which one is trying to measure it.<br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp; The electromagnetic coupling constant <b>increases</b> 
              with energy. The strong and weak nuclear coupling constants <b>decrease</b> 
              with energy. The strong force in particular exhibits a property 
              called asymptotic freedom. The force that binds quarks together 
              into a proton gets stronger at lower energy but becomes negligible 
              at very high energies. That's why, in very high energy scattering 
              experiments, the quarks inside a proton scatter almost like free 
              particles.</font></p>
            <h4 align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3" color="#000099">A 
              unified theory</font></h4>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp; 
              The three running coupling constants wind up having the same strength 
              at some very high energy scale, much higher than the weak interaction 
              scale of about 80 GeV. That fact, and some <b>nifty 
              mathematics</b> concerning <b>particle 
              multiplets in group theory</b> convinced physicists that 
              there should be some energy scale where these three forces all have 
              the <b>same strength</b> and where 
              all the different types of particles fit into the same mathematical 
              theory in one unified group. This type of elementary particle theory 
              is called a <b>Grand Unified Theory</b> 
              or <b>GUT</b> for short.<br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp; The three gauge groups of the Standard Model of known 
              elementary particle physics are <b>SU(3)xSU(2)xU(1)</b>. 
              In a Grand Unified Theory, these three groups all fit into a single 
              unified group with a unified set of gauge bosons whose number is 
              determined by the properties of the unified group. The most studied 
              theories have been <b>SU(5)</b> 
              and <b>SO(10)</b>. The same process 
              of spontaneous symmetry breaking that makes the weak bosons massive 
              at a scale of 80 GeV is invoked by physicists to make most of the 
              unified gauge bosons massive at a much higher scale.<br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp; When physicists look for a <b>mass 
              scale</b> where the three known running coupling constants 
              run into a single value necessary for a Grand Unified Theory, they 
              find a very high mass scale of </font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="../equations/MGUT.gif" width="129" height="28"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">This 
              mass scale is far too high to be reached by particle accelerators 
              in the near or distant future. <br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp; But there is a way to test Grand Unified Theories without 
              probing that mass scale directly. The weak interaction was discovered 
              because of beta decay, where a neutron decays into a proton, and 
              electron and a neutrino. In a Grand Unified Theory, something like 
              beta decay can happen to a proton. Even a small rate of <b>proton 
              decay</b> would be disastrous and very noticeable, because 
              the stability of the proton is the basis for the stability of all 
              known matter in the Universe. So far, no evidence of proton decay 
              has been observed in experiments set up to detect it.</font></p>
            <h4 align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3" color="#000099">What 
              about gravity?</font></h4>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp; 
              The natural constant that describes the measured strength of the 
              gravitational force is called Newton's constant. This is the constant 
              that appears in Newton's law for the gravitation force between two 
              objects (written here in its generalization to higher dimensions, 
              where d is the dimension of spacetime)</font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="../equations/NewtonForce.gif" width="108" height="56" alt="Newton's law of gravity"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Newton's 
              constant is very different from the speed of light and Planck's 
              constant, because the units depend on the number of spacetime dimensions 
              </font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="../equations/NewtonsConstant.gif" width="226" height="105" alt="Newton's constant"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp; 
              Gravity feels like a strong force at the macroscopic distance scales 
              where humans experience it, but gravity is a very very weak force 
              from a microscopic point of view. For example, the equivalent of 
              the fine structure constant for an electron and a proton interacting 
              according to Newton's law is</font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="../equations/FineNewton.gif" width="209" height="49"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp; 
              The <b>gravitational radius</b> 
              of an object of mass M is a distance scale made from <b>Newton's 
              constant</b> and the <b>speed of 
              light</b></font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="../equations/GravRadius.gif" width="90" height="48" alt="Gravitational radius"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">When 
              the size of an object approaches its gravitational radius, the object 
              can collapse into a black hole. This gives a natural length scale 
              where we expect the system to be described by the Einstein equations 
              rather than by Newtonian physics.<br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp; The natural length scale at which <b>quantum 
              gravity</b> should become important is called the <b>Planck 
              length</b>, made from Newton's constant (in four dimensions), 
              the speed of light and Planck's constant. </font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="../equations/PlanckLength.gif" width="221" height="53" alt="Planck length"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp; 
              In string theory, the set of physical quantum states usually contains 
              a graviton that gives rise to gravitational interactions. Therefore, 
              it has been widely assumed that the natural distance scale of string 
              theory should be the Planck scale. However string theories contain 
              many <b>duality symmetries</b> that 
              connect a string theory at one distance scale to a different string 
              theory at a different scale. So the idea of a distance scale itself 
              is not as firm and reliable in string theory as it it normally is 
              in quantum field theory.<br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp; String theory is supposed to contain the physics of 
              the quantum behavior of gravity. This implies a very subtle and 
              rich structure where the idea of distance itself is shifting and 
              obscure.</font></p>
          </td>
