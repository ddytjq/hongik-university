<h1>Patience pays off as Marlin reels Talladega victory</h1>


TALLADEGA, Ala. -- Sterling Marlin held off Dale Jarrett to capture his first
victory of the season at Sunday's NASCAR Winston Select 500 at
Talladega Superspeedway.
<p>
Marlin credited his victory his fifth career victory -- all on superspeedways -- to uncharacteristic patience.
<p>
&quot;I'm not usually the most patient guy,&quot; said Marlin, who now
owns two victories at both Talladega and Daytona,  NASCAR's biggest
and fastest tracks. &quot;But Tony (crew chief Tony Glover) kept reminding me of it about every five laps. He said, 'I promise if you don't wreck the thing, we'll win the race.
<img src="0428car1.jpg" height="180" width="240" ALIGN="RIGHT">
&quot;Everything just fell in our hands today.&quot;
<p>
Marlin has had some magic in
restrictor-plate races the past two years and now has 11 Top-10 finishes
in 26 starts at Talladega. 
<p>
Jarrett, who also finished second at Talladega in the fall race,
continued his run of Top-10 finishes, winning at Daytona and
finishing second at Darlington and Rockingham. 
<p>
Dale Earnhardt, the winningest driver ever at Talladega with
seven victories, finished third, followed by Terry Labonte and Michael Waltrip.
<p>
Three different times, Marlin made pit stops that left him far back in the 43-car field. Each time, he carved his way quickly through the thick traffic and put the car back out front.
<p>
&quot;I went to the rear three times, but they (his crew) told me
just to be patient and you can get back up front,&quot; said Marlin, who also won at
Talladega last July and last year's Daytona 500 with the same car. &quot;They were right on.
<p>
&quot;The car ran flawless all day. I could pass at will, almost, and it's hard to pass these cars
one-on-one. You need four or five cars to help you most of the
time.&quot;
<p>
Marlin pitted the last time on Lap 140 under caution to top off
his tank after crew chief Tony Glover told him he would not be able
to finish without it. That stop dropped Marlin
from the lead all the way to 16th place for the restart on Lap 141
of the 188-lap race.
<p>
The yellow Chevy sliced through the crowd, moving to seventh by
Lap 146, third by Lap 152, bypassing Earnhardt for second on Lap 153 and passing Terry Labonte for the
lead on his 167th trip around the 2.66-mile oval.
<p>
&quot;I needed some help. Sterling was just too strong,&quot; said
Jarrett, whose Ford Thunderbird finished about a car length behind Marlin.
<p>
Marlin, a second-generation driving star, averaged 149.998 mph, winning
$109,845.
<p>
&quot;Runt (engine-builder Sheldon 'Runt' Pittman) stayed home from
Martinsville last week to work on the engine and the guys in the
shop did a great job. This is probably the best race car I ever
had.&quot;
<p>
There were six caution flags in the race, two of them brought
out by spectacular accidents.
<p>
The first crash involved 10-time Most
Popular Driver Bill Elliott, who spun coming off turn two, slid into the infield grass and became
airborne. His Ford soared about 15 feet into the air, came down
hard on its rear, bounced back into the air and came down just as
hard on four wheels.
<p>
Safety workers had to cut part of the car's roof away to remove
Elliott, who came away with a fractured left thigh.
<p>
The second major crash came on Lap 130 when defending series champion Jeff Gordon was
squeezed into the wall at Turn 1, hit Mark Martin and ignited a
14-car melee.
<p>
As the cars slipped and slid all over turns one and two, Ricky
Craven's Chevrolet flipped into the air, slammed into the retaining
fence atop the concrete wall and bounded back onto the track.
<p>
Craven complained of back pain and was also airlifted to
Birmingham, where a CAT scan was negative and doctors found no
serious injuries. However, Craven, who was the 1995 Rookie of the
Year, was to be kept overnight for observation.
<p>
