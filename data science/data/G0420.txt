<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Even at low red shifts, there is noticeable increase in magnitude that cannot be explained cosmologically, although most astronomers assume there is a reason in astrophysics for the increase.
<A NAME="sigma">  </A>
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The standard deviation <font face=symbol>s</font> is usually about 1 at higher red shifts and  the quasars begin to act like they do statistically at higher red shifts when we move toward the right side of the table (at RD = 0.37, z = 0.492).   The fact that the variance is less than one consistently at the lower red shifts leads one to wonder whether some of the quasars are eliminated by the arbitrary cutoff at absolute magnitude -23.   Strictly from the statistics viewpoint, it looks like the -23 borderline between quasars and Seyfert galaxies (the next less luminous category of objects) is a fuzzy one.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>If you check the difference in the number counts between the Veron 9th and Veron 10th editions at the bottom of the respective tables toward the right side, you see that most of the difference is accounted for by the 2QZ quasars.
