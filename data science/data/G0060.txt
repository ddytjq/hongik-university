<center><H3><font color=navy>QSO Appendix 5: Maxima and Minima of Other Distributions</font></H3></center>
<pre>

</pre>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>When I set up the spreadsheet to examine the statistical behavior of quasars in the Veron 10th catalogue, I decided to use all the quasars greater than or equal to z = 3.35 and less than 3.65 (because my best guess was that the antipode was in the middle of this red shift ragne).   There were 85 quasars in this sample.   So the following table also has 85 quasars, but the red shifts go from z = 3.068 to 3.817.   Obviously the wider range of red shifts indicates that the SDSS on line catalogue does not have as many quasars as the Veron catalogue does at the red shifts I'm most interested in.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>A few entries on the list below are listed in red to show when there is a change in the relative distance values used in my <A HREF="qta.html#table">quasar tables</A>.   The red shift of the low end of the relative distance interval for 0.85 is 3.119, and so the copy in Cell #5 is red to show that Cell #6 is the first quasar in the SDSS catalogue in this 0.85 interval.   The red shift at which the RD for 0.86 starts is 3.314, and Cell #36 is the first quasar here.   And RD 0.86 ends at z = 3.535, where RD 0.87 starts.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>So there are 30 quasars (Cell #35 - Cell #5) at RD 0.85, 9 quasars (#44 = #35) at RD 0.86, and 38 quasars at RD 0.87 (#82 - #44).   As expected, the SDSS quasars are passing though a minimum in the number counts in the mid three range of red shifts.

<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>There is even a little synergy between the two tables.  <A href="#tableVIII">Table VIII</A> showed a noticeable "hole" in the quasar population between cells 43 (z = 3.457) and 45 (z = 3.561).   The only quasar between them at cell 46 has z = 3.498.   A comparable "hole" occurs in Table X above, with only one quasar at cell 47 (z = 3.469) between cell 46 (z = 3.428) and cell 48 (z = 3.502).   The "hole" just below z = 3.5 occurs in both tables, and so our best guess for the red shift of the antipode (if there is one) is slightly less than z = 3.5.
