<strong><center><font color=navy SIZE=4>Comments on Tables 5 and 2A</font></center></strong>
</strong>
<pre>

<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The very first thing I suggest you do is establish a link to Table 2 (by clicking on "Table 2A" in the lower Summary Table).   Then set the relative position of each quasar table so that they appear at the same latitude on your screen, so that when you press the "back" and "forward" icons it will seem as if one table is overlaying the other,
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Obviously, not too many quasars in the 2QZ survey are in the yellow (brighter) area; those are the quasars brighter than visual magnitude 18.25 (the brightest quasars selected in the 2QZ survey).   But there are obviously plenty of quasars brighter than visual magnitude 18.25 in the Veron catalogue which have been found over the years, and a "back and forth" between tables 2 and 5 gives you a very good idea of what part of the overall quasar population the 2QZ researchers are concentrating on.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>And if the visual magnitudes of the 2QZ quasars are always in the <em>same</em> range - 18.25 to 20.85 visual magnitude at a certain wavelength - while the absolute magnitude of that range constantly <em>increases</em>, then it's obvious that something is <em>globally</em> affecting the quasar population (which is not obvious if we confine the examination <A HREF="QSOTable2.html">to Table 2</A>
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The absolute magnitudes of the quasars in the 10th Veron catalogue (which obviously includes a substantial number of 2QZ quasars) is still about 0.5 to 0.7 magnitudes brighter than the 2QZ quasars at the same red shift/distance.    Ergo, the average magnitudes of quasars <em>without</em> the 2QZ additions would have been roughly one absolute magnitude brighter.

If you haven't, check out the link first.]

</blockquote>
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>As I mentioned in the discussion <A HREF="QSOTable1.html#sigma">of Table 1</A>, notice at the left of the table that <font face=symbol>s</font> is significantly less than the usual 0.6.    The absolute magnitude of -23.0 has been accepted for years as the "floor" for quasars.   But the <em>statistical</em> behavior of 2QZ quasars in Table 5 leads one to wonder if this cutoff is too low.   The arithmetic average has got to be at least 1.5<font face=symbol>s</font> above the "-23 floor" before we no longer have to be concerned about losing a significant number of the quasars.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Since <font face=symbol>s</font> is usually in the 0.55 to 0.60 range, then 1.5<font face=symbol>s</font> indicates we neeed to get about 0.8 to 0.9 magnitudes above the "-23 floor" before we can be assured of their behaving the way they usually do (at higher red shifts).   This happens at RD = 0.50, when z = 0.781.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>For <em>all</em> quasars, the "normal" standard deviation is reached at RD =.37 and z = 0.492, as explained in Table 1.   This probably indicates that many quasars are being eliminated at the lower red shifts in the 2QZ survey due to the way they are selected (again indicating that the -23 cutoff in Mabs may be too low).
