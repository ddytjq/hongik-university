				<td rowspan="2" valign="top" width="196">

				<font class="newsdate"> Thursday, January 24, 2002 13:30 (GMT)</font><br>
				<img src="/1.gif" width="1" height="18"><br>
				<font class="news">With the Board of Control for Cricket in India (BCCI) all set to boycott the International Cricket Council (ICC) referees&#8217; commission set-up to review the Mike Denness controversy, it is clear that the on-going row between the two bodies is bound to intensify.<br><br>BCCI chief Jagmohan Dalmiya&#8217;s attitude remains confrontationist. He has made it clear he is not going to come down from the uncompromising stance he adopted at the start of the controversy in November last year after Denness, a former England captain, had taken a hard line against six Indian players, including icon Sachin Tendulkar.   <br><br>It was always on the cards that the BCCI would be at loggerheads with the world body after their suggested list of names on the commission was turned down by the ICC, which has stood by their three nominees on the panel.<br><br>Adopting a confrontationist attitude, Dalmiya informed the ICC that their three-member panel should either not meet until the ICC's executive board meeting in March, or a separate 10-member commission, with one nomination from each of the Test-playing nations, can be formed to review the unhappy episode, which has snowballed into a major international row. <br><br>Mincing no words, Dalmiya said in a statement: "If these alternatives are still not acceptable to the ICC, the BCCI will be reluctantly compelled to refrain from participation in the referees' commission in any form or manner."<br><br>The ICC confirmed the personnel making up the commission last week despite Indian protests. The three member commission is due to hold their first meeting on 23 February. <br><br>Dalmiya has written to ICC president Malcolm Gray to inform him that the BCCI would boycott the commission if their suggestions were not accepted. According to him, the BCCI wanted South African judge Albie Sachs, who will chair the panel, to be replaced, arguing no one from the three countries involved in the controversy should be on the body.<br><br>Emphasizing his point, Dalmiya is quoted to have said: "This is why we did not recommend a single name from India, including that of Sunil Gavaskar, who is in fact the chairman of the ICC cricket committee.&#8221;<br><br>India is also opposed to the other two members in the panel &#8211; former Australian Test opener Andrew Hilditch and former Pakistan captain Majid Khan.<br><br>The BCCI had opposed Majid Khan because they did not want to "embarrass" him after he was drawn into making comments over match-fixing allegations following Pakistan's defeat against Bangladesh in the 1999 World Cup. Hilditch&#8217;s name is frowned upon for not being a familiar name in present times.  <br><br>The BCCI has suggested such names as Justice Ahmed Ebrahim of Zimbabwe, who is an ICC match referee, Australia's Richie Benaud and Ian Chappell, Gary Sobers and Clive Lloyd of the West Indies and Imran Khan of Pakistan, all former Test captains. <br><br>The ICC&#8217;s stand, as spelt out by Gray, is clear. According to Gray, the three nominees &#8220;are highly-reputable individuals who are available and prepared to serve on the commission, which is one of quality and integrity and one that combines outstanding cricket pedigree with a high degree of legal and administrative expertise. I am confident that it will fulfill its brief to the satisfaction of all parties."<br><br>Striking an emotional note, Dalmiya, who has enjoyed considerable support in India as a result of his tough stance on the issue, even as he has received brickbats from abroad, said: "The ICC commands BCCI's respect as a parent body. But it is a pity that although BCCI is a part of the ICC cricket family, the Indian sentiments were not respected." <br><br>Last week in Kolkata, when asked what his next course of action would be in case the ICC rejected the BCCI&#8217;s request for reconstituting the panel, Dalmiya said: &#8220;We will cross that bridge when we come to it.&#8221;<br><br>Now that he has made it clear that he has no intention of coming down from his tough stance on the issue, there is every chance that the controversy is far from over.<br><br>Indeed, another unhappy chapter is almost certain to unfold. <br><br>- <i>Partab Ramchand</i></font><br>
