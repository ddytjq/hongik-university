				<td width="100%" class="tableTitleRow"><p class="indent25X10"><font class="news"><b>Comment: It's just not cricket</b></font></p></td>
				<td rowspan="2" valign="top" width="196">
				<img src="/images/news/1505.jpg" width="196" height="262"><!--  --><br><img src="/1.gif" width="1" height="5"><br>

				<font class="newsdate"> Sunday, December 23, 2001 06:53 (GMT)</font><br>
				<img src="/1.gif" width="1" height="18"><br>
				<font class="news">A few decades ago, England skipper Douglas Jardine came up with the idea of leg-theory, or Bodyline as it came to be called. It was all to contain the single most prolific batsman of his time and of times to come too - Don Bradman.<br><br>His contemporaries, and even history, have judged Jardine quite harshly, and with some justification too. The current tactics of the England skipper Nasser Hussain to counter the menace of Sachin Tendulkar must surely be the highest form of praise for Tendulkar as a batsman for, make no mistake, the tactic is meant primarily for Tendulkar and Tendulkar alone.<br><br>There has been a raging debate everywhere about Hussain's tactics. Actually, his tactics aren't really that new. A few years back, Sri Lankan skipper Arjuna Ranatunga employed the same tactics against the Indian batsmen, having Jayasuriya and Dharmasena bowl a foot and a half outside the leg-stump in order to stifle the Indians from scoring runs.<br><br>His tactics were soundly condemned by one and all, but if you take a look at it, his argument wasn't that different from what Hussain has advanced in support of his own tactics. "With the resources we have at our disposal, we can't really hope to get the batsmen out bowling at them" seems to be the theory behind it.<br><br>Firstly, this tactic in itself is rather negative, because it indicates a defensive mindset. The captain has basically decided that rather than get the batsman out by bowling good balls at him, he is going to wait for the batsman to get so frustrated, that he gets out due to an error induced by sheer boredom.<br><br>Is this how cricket is supposed to be played? Surely, the game is supposed to be a contest between bat and ball, where the bowler tries his hardest to get the batsman out, while the batsman scores runs and at the same time tries to safeguard his own wicket. If all the bowler does is bowl defensively, then the only way the batsman can get out is through his own folly.<br><br>Some have advanced the theory that if the tactic works, as it has in this case against the Indians, where they have got themselves out in trying to counter these negative tactics, then surely it is almost an attacking tactic. It is difficult to buy this argument, because if the batsmen had just shut up shop, as they did on the final day of the Second Test, then it kills the game and disappoints the spectators. And surely the fans deserve to see two teams battling it out, rather than two teams just sparring lightly at each other with an eye on the final bell. It would surely kill Test cricket.<br><br>Most of the successful Test teams in cricket history have been successful because of their aggressive tactics. Take, for example, the West Indians and the Aussies. Not only have they been successful because of their aggressiveness, they have also been real crowd-pullers. After all, why would the spectators stream into the stands to watch a team like England play the way it did?<br><br>Let us now ask a few honest questions, with respect to these tactics. Did the tactics go against the 'spirit' of the game? With the words "spirit of the game" being bandied about so often these days, one has to mention that the game of cricket being played these days is a far cry from the game as it was originally meant to be played, both in terms of tactics as well as 'spirit'. A game where the bowlers snarl at the batsmen, where batsmen stand their ground after knowing they are out, where bowlers and fielders appeal for catches that even they know aren't legal is surely not the same game which was played a century ago.<br><br>Times have changed to such an extent that the words "It's just not cricket" doesn't have any meaning any more. Everything these days is cricket. Having said that, then no, Hussain was perfectly within his rights to use any tactics within his means to win or at least avoid a loss in the Test matches as he saw fit.<br><br>"But," the knowledgeable enthusiast says, "the newly changed laws have been formulated precisely to deal with this menace of negative bowling. So haven't his actions been in contravention of the laws?"<br><br>It is true that, in that sense, his actions could have been penalized by the upholders of the law - the umpires. However, since they didn&#8217;t enforce the laws, the fault, one would say, lies with them. There is a rather thin line between bowling attackingly outside the leg stump, like Shane Warne does, and between defensive, run-denying, mind-numbingly boring, game-killing tactics. Well, in this writer's opinion, while bowling to Tendulkar, Giles and hence Hussain did cross that line and ventured into the less palatable region of negative bowling. <br><br>Critics of Tendulkar are also quick to point out that he could have adopted a more aggressive policy. "Look at Sehwag," they say. "Surely if Sehwag could adopt aggressive tactics against Giles and thus nullify him, the vastly more experienced Tendulkar could have done the same."<br><br>Unfortunately for Tendulkar, the responsibilities on his shoulder are way more than on Sehwag. Sehwag could get away with these tactics because, even if he had got out, people wouldn't really have bayed for his blood.<br><br>The same critics who accuse Tendulkar of not being aggressive enough were quite scathing in the way he finally got out, trying to attack Giles. What they forget is that in attacking there is always an element of risk involved, especially when the batsman does all the attacking and the bowler just waits for things to happen.<br><br>Moreover, Tendulkar realizes (and has realized it for more than 10 years) that he really can't depend on any of his teammates to bail his side out (except for one odd Test or two like the Kolkata Test against the Aussies). In that sense he has to be more circumspect and doggedly defensive than his natural inclination dictates. <br><br>Hence, the debate over whether Hussain's tactics, even though they were successful, constitute negative tactics or not would go on for quite some time. Then again, in these days, where anything goes, it really is no surprise that teams try any tactic to win.<br><br>One wonders though, if getting Tendulkar out for 88, 103 and 90, would count as a triumph for this strategy. If it does, then Tendulkar ought to be flattered.<br><br>- <i><a href="mailto:the_crumpet@hotmail.com">Arun Krishnan</a></i></font><br>
