<a name="about"></a>
<h3>About MICA</h3>

<p>The Multiyear Interactive Computer Almanac, MICA, is
a software system that provides high-precision astronomical data
in tabular form for a wide variety of celestial objects. MICA calculates,
in real-time, much of the information tabulated in the benchmark
annual publication, <cite>The Astronomical Almanac</cite> (see
<a href="./publications/docs/almanacs.html">Annual Astronomical and
Navigational Almanacs</a> in <strong>Publications</strong>).  However, MICA
goes beyond traditional almanacs by enabling the user to calculate
data for user-specified locations at user-specified times within
a sixteen-year interval (1990-2005). Designed primarily for professional
applications, MICA is intended for intermediate-to-advanced users. The MICA manual
provides a chapter on astronomical background, but basic knowledge of
astronomical terminology and positional astronomy is assumed.</p>

</div>
<div class="med">
<p>MICA provides essential data for use in:</p>

<center>
<table width=75% border=0 bgcolor="#B0D0FF" cellspacing=0 cellpadding=10>
  <tr><td> Astronomy and astrophysics             </td>
      <td> Space science                          </td>
      <td> Geodesy and surveying                  </td></tr>
  <tr><td> Geophysics                             </td>
      <td> Meteorology                            </td>
      <td> Environmental science                  </td></tr>
  <tr><td> Operations planning                    </td>
      <td> Accident reconstruction and litigation </td>
      <td> Illumination engineering               </td></tr>
  <tr><td> Architecture                           </td>
      <td> Photography                            </td>
      <td> &nbsp;                                 </td></tr>
</table>
</center>
</div>
<div class="med">

<p>Specifically, MICA provides the following information:</p>
<ul>
   <li>nine types of positions of celestial objects, including geocentric
and topocentric apparent places, astrometric places, and topocentric
horizon coordinates
   <li>time and Earth orientation data, including sidereal time, nutation
components, and obliquity of the ecliptic
   <li>times of rise, set, and transit of celestial objects, and times
of civil, nautical, and astronomical twilight
   <li>data on the apparent size, illumination, and orientation of the
Sun, Moon, and major planets for telescopic observations
   <li>a summary of the instantaneous configuration of the Sun, Moon,
and major planets at user-specified times
</ul>

<p>The data provided by MICA match the high precision and
benchmark accuracy of <i>The Astronomical Almanac</i>.  MICA
features a sixteen-year solar system ephemeris (the Jet Propulsion
Laboratory's DE200/LE200), an internal catalog of the 21 brightest stars
and Polaris, and five additional catalogs of astronomical objects.
Furthermore, MICA has the ability to access specialized catalogs
created by a competent user.  MICA tables can be generated in
either of two different time scales, with either civil or Julian
dates.  Rise, set, transit, and twilight times can be tabulated
for any time zone.  The MICA software is accompanied by a
comprehensive, hardcover manual.</p>

<p>Reviews of MICA version 1.0 have appeared in:</p>
<ul>
   <li> <i>Sky &amp; Telescope</i>, August 1993, pp. 54-55.
   <li> <i>Sky &amp; Telescope</i>, August 1995, pp. 58-61.
</ul>

<a name="new"></a>
<h3>What's New in Version 1.5</h3>

<p>MICA 1.5 retains the same interface as, and similar system requirements
to, the previous version (1.0).  Version 1.5 differs from Version 1.0 in the
following ways:
<ul>
   <li> the solar system ephemerides have been extended through 2005
   <li> the external catalog of bright stars has been updated using
improved data from the preliminary Yale Bright Star Catalog, 5th
edition
   <li> the lunar and planetary constants have been updated
   <li> the DOS edition features several new batch control parameters
   <li> both DOS and Macintosh editions are now provided on a single,
hybrid CD-ROM
   <li> the user's guides have been unified into a single, hardcover
book
   <li> a number of minor bug fixes and improvements
</ul>

<a name="test"></a>
<h3>Test Drive MICA Now!</h3>

<p>A web version of MICA is available that allows you to get an idea of
some of MICA's capabilities.  However, the web version is not a full
MICA implementation and differs from the PC and Mac editions in several
important ways:

<ul>
   <li> the date range is limited: it begins on 1 January of last
        year and ends 30 days after today's date
   <li> only selected MICA capabilities are available
   <li> the menus and forms for entering data are web-specific and
        not as user-friendly as those in the PC and Mac editions
</ul>
However, the output data is the same as
that from the PC and Mac editions.  If you'd like to give the web
version of MICA a try, click
<a href="./data/docs/WebMICA_2.html"><b>here</b></a>.<p>

<a name="sysreq"></a>
<h3>System Requirements</h3>

<p>MICA is provided in editions for PCs (Intel processors) and
Apple Macintosh systems.  In both cases the system requirements for
MICA are quite modest.  The PC (DOS) edition features an easy-to-use
menu-driven interface. The Macintosh edition offers a full-featured
Macintosh graphical user interface.</p>

<p>The <b>DOS edition</b> requires a PC with an Intel processor running DOS
version 2.0 or higher and at least 512K of RAM.  Instructions are provided
for running MICA under Windows 3.x or Windows 95.  A hard drive with 3
megabytes of free space is also required. A processor with a clock speed of at
least 8 MHz and a math coprocessor are highly recommended.</p>

<p>The <b>Macintosh edition</b> requires a Macintosh Plus or higher
running System 6.0.2 or higher.  It is compatible with System 7 and
MacOS 8, requiring at least 1 megabyte of memory in addition to the
system requirements.  A hard drive with at least 3 megabytes of free
space is also required.  Power Macintosh systems, or Macintosh systems
with 68020 or higher processors and math coprocessors, are highly
recommended.</p>
