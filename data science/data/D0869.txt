		<h4>A Note to Developers from JavaOne</h4>
		
			<em>by Andy Sullivan</em><br><font class="author"> -06/21/2001</font>
		<p>
		<P>J2ME technologies played a major role in the 2001 JavaOne, Sun's Worldwide Java Developer Conference, signaling the mainstream acceptance of Java on mobile devices, smart cards, and in embedded solutions. The message the conference sent to developers was loud and clear: develop for what you have today.</P>
<P>"If you want to build (J2ME) products to ship this year, focus on CLDC/MIDP and PersonalJava," said Eric Chu, Group Manager of Industry Marketing for the Sun Microsystems' Software Systems Group, strongly stating that application developers can be profitable with existing technologies. "MIDP 1.0 is here to stay," Chu proclaimed as he displayed device after device (phones, PDAs, and strange combinations of the two) running MIDP-compliant applications, and a few PersonalJava-compliant devices.</P>
<P>The message seems to have already resonated within the corporate development community, and the J2ME battle lines have been drawn between MIDP and PersonalJava. Companies working with MIDP are targeting the lower end PDAs and cell phones, in an attempt to seize the mass market created by lower cost devices. On the other side are companies focusing on PersonalJava, targeting their offerings to the high-priced PDAs and the next generation cell phone/PDA amalgamations.</P>
<H5>In this Corner: MIDP</H5>
<P>Leading the way for MIDP are, of course, Motorola and Nextel. As the pioneers of the MIDP community, Motorola/Nextel's early adoption has established them as the standard for MIDP implementations. In an effort to stay ahead of the curve, Motorola/Nextel continued their outreach to the developer community at JavaOne by announcing three new J2ME toolkits:</P>
<UL>
<LI>Developer's Guide: provides hints and guidance for application structure as well as code samples for open use.</LI>
<LI>User Interface Toolkit: the first 3rd party UI API for J2ME handsets, permitting the developer to customize menus, scroll bars, etc., using a core set of open source UI elements. "A lot of the apps (seen today) use the high level API because the low level API isn't easy to work with." - Nextel Director of Wireless Data Services, Bob Ewald, on Nextel's reason for releasing code in UI toolkit.</LI>
<LI>Record Management System and Networking Toolkit: provides added functionality to help developers easily complete common Data storage and networking tasks.</LI></UL>
<P>The new toolkits were put to immediate use in the Motorola/Nextel Developer Challenge by winner, and MJN Committee member, David Fox. David developed a cool network-aware application that enables the Motorola phone to control home air conditioning and lighting and to stream surveillance video to the phone from home web cams. Fox was one of the only contestants to fully utilize the graphic capabilities of J2ME, with others showing mainly menu-driven applications.</P>
<P>In other Motorola/Nextel news, the companies announced new capabilities for their Java-enabled handsets (four new phones to be released by the end of the year). In addition to new HTTPS capability, which ensures end-to-end secure connections between handset and internet or server, all Motorola handsets released after August 2001 will have built-in over-the-air application delivery capabilities for the Nextel network; all previously released phones will be easily upgraded. This will set the stage for the wirelessly enabled Motorola/Nextel application distribution website, which will debut in 4Q 2001 with over 2-dozen network aware apps. It's not very clear whether this is a true consumer play by Nextel, but they do plan on bringing in revenues from the site. The companies are working closely with developers to ensure fresh applications are available, and a fee will be implemented for most applications. "The large majority of any revenue generated from the download (of apps from Nextel) will go to the developer," ensures Ewald.</P>
<P>The Japanese community also has an eye on MIDP, realizing that it may help them move away from the games/consumer area into more business-appropriate usage. Applix, the leading Japanese provider of embedded Java solutions, demonstrated the capabilities of their MIDP-compatible microJBlend embedded technology. microJBlend, which has been adopted as the standard platform for the next-generation J-Phone Java cellular phones in Japan, includes the Aplix-original acceleration technology KFTT, which boosts KVM operation on the phone, performing 4X faster than Sun's KVM.</P>
<P>The only applications Aplix showed running on microJBlend were games, but CEO Ryu Koriyama believes that will soon be changing. Koriyama feels that the consumer adoption of Java phones in Japan can be attributed to the color display, which takes much of the processing power. Now that the networks are in place and the phones are a little more powerful (J-Phone will be allowing 30K apps to be downloaded onto their next-generation phones), the server-side developers will see mobile application deployment as a viable option. Since microJBlend is optimized for the color display, and is MIDP compliant, it will become the platform of choice for enterprise application development, Ryu explained. Look for Aplix to make a strong play in the North America and Europe as soon as the color LCD screens are available.</P>
<P>Phone manufacturers are not alone in their adoption of the MIDP. At JavaOne, Research in Motion showcased their J2ME-based Blackberry device, with the entire OS written in Java. MIDP really opens up the Blackberry for application development, extending the device beyond its current email and personal information management mold. Many people were initially angry at RIM for releasing their JDE for Blackberry without supporting it. "The JDE Early Access version was a preview, intended to show our commitment to Java," said RIM Application Development Engineer Sacha Meckler, explaining that RIM wanted to have everything working perfect with the device before releasing the new JDK to developers. The new GPRS version of the Blackberry will be first released in the UK.</P>
<P>This seems to be the logical approach, but unconventional in today's industry. RIM should be commended for placing greater importance on the quality of their product than on the speed-to-market. Once GPRS networks are implemented, RIM will release the J2ME device in North America and should be poised to extend their market reach beyond finance into other industries.</P>
<P>Zucotto Wireless showed its support for MIDP with its Xpresso family of Java technology-based processors targeted at CLDC devices "The great thing about technology is that it scales up much better than it scales down," explained Zucotto Technology Evangelist, Mark Sears, justifying their focus on CLDC, "previous attempts of hacking and squeezing J2SE into PersonalJava was the opposite approach and was much less successful." Zucotto built their Xpresso technology with this ground-up approach, to realize the vision of wireless devices running Java applications with great performance and great power efficiency. "The result is an exciting technology allowing developers, carriers, and device manufacturers to greatly increase the user experience," said Sears.</P>
<P>MIDP application development started to show some life at JavaOne, with many practical business applications being displayed for the phone and PDA. Some of the highlights included; Agea and Bonita both showing PIM applications and remote file manipulations; HillCast's application for delivering secure financial information; MapInfo's application for downloading customized maps and driving directions; Flashline.com's12-inch robot controlled using a Motorola i50sx; iAnywhere's sales force application and back-end data base synching capabilities; and LogicChain's server-side engine for easily creating and deploying interactive J2ME applications using a non-technical interface.</P> 
		
		</P>
