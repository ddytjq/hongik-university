			<a name="a1"></a> 
			<p><b>ANNUAL MANAGEMENT CHARGE:</b> : This is the 
			yearly fee taken off the value of an investment fund to cover the fund manager's pay and expenses including the costs of dealing in shares. <a name="a2"></a> 
			</p>
			<p><b>ASSET ALLOCATION:</b> Asset allocation refers 
			to the way in which you weight different investments 
			in your portfolio to try to meet a specific investment 
			objective. <a name="a3"></a></p>
			<p><b>ASSET CLASSES:</b> These are different types 
			of assets that you can invest in. Three of the 
			most commonly used are shares, bonds and money 
			market instruments. </p>
                              <p> 
                              <ul>
                                <li> Bonds - In general, these securities have 
                                  less severe short-term price fluctuations than 
                                  shares and therefore offer lower market risk. 
                                  Government bonds are felt to be safer than those 
                                  offered by companies as the investor must bear 
                                  the risk of defaults. <br>
                                  <br>
                                </li>
                                <li> Money market instruments - Among the most 
                                  stable of all asset classes in terms of returns, 
                                  money market instruments carry very low market 
                                  risk. At the same time, these securities don't 
                                  have the potential to outpace inflation by as 
                                  wide a margin through the years as shares. <br>
                                  <br>
                                </li>
                                <li>Shares - Well known for fluctuating frequently 
                                  in value, shares carry a high level of market 
                                  risk (the risk that your investments' value 
                                  will decrease with any drop in the overall market) 
                                  over the short term. However, historically, 
                                  shares have earned much higher returns than 
                                  other asset classes, although past performance 
                                  is no predictor of future results. Shares have 
                                  also outpaced inflation - the rising prices 
                                  of goods and services - at the highest rate 
                                  through the years and therefore carry very low 
                                  inflation risk. </li>
                              </ul>

                              <p><b>BID-OFFER SPREAD:</b> Many investment funds 
                                have a dual pricing system. The bid-offer spread 
                                is the difference between the buying and selling 
                                price of units in a unit trust. It can range from 
                                3 to 6 per cent according to the fund. <a name="b2"></a></p>
                              <p><b>BID PRICE:</b> This is the price than an investor 
                                can get for selling units. The offer price is 
                                what an investor has to pay to buy the units. 
                              </p>

                          <a name="c1"></a> 
                              <p><b>CAT:</b> The word CAT stands for <b>Cost</b>, 
                                <b>Access</b> and <b>Terms</b>. Providers of CAT 
                                standard ISAs must treat customers fairly, use 
                                plain English and avoid complex or misleading 
                                features in product design. </p>
                              <p> <b>Cost:</b> There should be no one-off or regular 
                                charges and this means no charge for withdrawals 
                                or for services like using ATMs. But there can 
                                be charges for replacements such as lost cards. 
                                <br>
                                <b>Access</b>: Providers should allow transactions 
                                for sums as little as £10 and facilitate withdrawals 
                                within seven working days or less. There should 
                                be no limits on frequency of withdrawals. <br>
                                <b>Terms:</b> The interest rate should be no lower 
                                than 2 percentage points below the bank base rate. 
                                Upward interest rate changes should reflect base 
                                rate movements within a calendar month although 
                                downward changes may be slower. <a name="c2"></a> 
                              <p></p>
                              <p><b>CORPORATE BOND:</b> Corporate bonds promise 
                                to pay a fixed amount of interest each year until 
                                they mature. The issuer is then scheduled to pay 
                                back the capital to the bondholder. The issuers 
                                are companies that wish to raise money for expansion 
                                or development. </p>

                              <p><b>EXCHANGE TRADED FUNDS (EFTS): </b>These are 
                                investments funds that can be traded on the stock 
                                exchange like shares. </p>

                              <p><b>INDEPENDENT FINANCIAL ADVISER: </b>An IFA 
                                is a regulated professional that advises on investments 
                                and savings and is not tied to any single product 
                                provider. Tied advisers can only recommend the 
                                range of products offered by one company. <a name="i2"></a></p>
                              <p><b>INDEX TRACKERS:</b> Index tracking funds have 
                                no initial charge and lower-than-average annual 
                                management fees if you do not mind performance 
                                in line with the stock market. <a name="i3"></a> 
                              </p>
                              <p><b>INHERITANCE TAX:</b> This is a charge that 
                                can be levied on the assets that a person leaves 
                                when they die. Such assets might include a home 
                                and investments and are called a person's estate. 
                                The government levies a tax of 40% at a threshold 
                                which has been traditionally raised regularly 
                                to allow for factors such as inflation. <a name="i4"></a> 
                              </p>
                              <p><b>INITIAL CHARGE:</b> This usually ranges from 
                                around 1 per cent to 6 per cent and is levied 
                                when units are bought in a fund. It is also called 
                                a front-end load. It is levied to remunerate the 
                                manager and cover costs such as marketing and 
                                commission to financial advisers that recommend 
                                the product. <a name="i5"></a></p>
                              <p><b>ISA:</b> The initials ISA stand for Individual 
                                Savings Account. The ISA was launched on 6 April 
                                1999 to offer straightforward and tax-efficient 
                                saving. It took over from where the earlier Personal 
                                Equity Plan (PEP) and Tax Exempt Special Savings 
                                Accounts (TESSAs) left off. You can invest up 
                                to £7,000 in an ISA in the 2000-2001 tax year 
                                free of income or capital gains taxes. </p>

                              <p><b>MAXI ISAS: </b>Maxi ISAs (investment limit 
                                up to £7,000 per tax year) allow you to invest 
                                in all three cash, equity and life insurance elements 
                                of an ISA. However there are limits of £3,000 
                                for the cash element and £1,000 for the life insurance 
                                element. The stocks and shares element can make 
                                up the remainder of the allowance or it can take 
                                up the entire allowance. However, if you choose 
                                to use the allowance up on equities you can't 
                                invest in the other two elements. And you are 
                                only allowed to choose one company to provide 
                                your Maxi ISA in any tax year. <a name="m1"></a></p>
                              <p><b>MINI ISAs:</b> Mini ISAs allow you to invest 
                                in any of the three investment elements and with 
                                more than one company. In theory, you can have 
                                a cash Mini ISA with your Building Society, (limit 
                                up to £3,000 per tax year), your life assurance 
                                Mini ISA with a life company (limit up to £1,000 
                                per tax year) and a stocks and shares Mini ISA 
                                with a fund provider (limit up to £3,000 per tax 
                                year). <a name="m2"></a></p>

                                them an annual limit free of income and capital 
                                gains taxes. The PEP was superseded by the stocks

                              <p><b>RISK:</b> For investors risk can be defined 
                                as the likelihood of losing capital through investments 
                                turning bad. Risks can include stock market slumps 
                                and companies going broke. <br>
                                Self-select ISAs are suitable only for the confident 
                                or professional investor that knows enough about 
                                stock markets to make an informed choice regarding 
                                individual stocks and shares. But however confident 
                                an investor is, the first task is to limit costs 
                                because every pound sterling saved contributes 
                                to the bottom line. The self-select title can 
                                be misleading as providers tend to differ in the 
                                limitations they place on your freedom to invest 
                                and costs vary. </p>

                          <a name="s1"></a> 
                              <p> <b>SHARE GLOSSARY OF TERMS</b> <b>Dividend yield</b> 
                                - This is arrived at through dividing the amount 
                                of dividend by the share price. It gives a measure 
                                of the percentage return a company is paying its 
                                investors. The Financial Times London Share Price 
                                lists the yields for a large number of companies. 
                              <p></p>
                              <p> <b>Payout ratio</b> - This figure represents 
                                the percentage of earnings that a company is paying 
                                out to its investors. It is an indication of whether 
                                most of a company's earnings are being paid to 
                                its investors or whether they are being reinvested 
                                in the growth of the company. 
                              <p></p>
                              <p> <b>Price range</b> - The price of a share is 
                                determined according to the rules of supply and 
                                demand. Tracking the price over time can give 
                                you a partial picture of the company and its recent 
                                performance. Sources include daily information 
                                in the Financial Times on world stock markets 
                                and in the London Share Service. 
                              <p></p>
                              <p> <b>Price to earnings ratio</b> - This figure 
                                found through dividing the share price by the 
                                company's earning per share. It illustrates how 
                                much of a company's return is being used to make 
                                payments to shareholders. The figure can be used 
                                to compare returns to shareholders made by all 
                                kinds of different companies. 
                              <p></p>

                                to share dividends that can be reclaimed by ISAs 
                                and PEPs until April 2004. The UK government has 
                                pledged to phase them out. <a name="t2"></a> </p>
                              <p><b>TAX YEAR:</b> This runs from 6 April to 5 
                                April the next calendar year. Your liability to 
                                tax is assessed for that period. <a name="t3"></a></p>
                              <p><b>TESSAs:</b> The initials TESSA stand for Tax 
                                Exempt Special Savings Accounts. TESSAs were launched 
                                in January 1991 to encourage cash savings by exempting 
                                the interest from income tax. But new TESSAs ceased 
                                to be available on 5 April 1999 when they were 
                                replaced by the cash ISA. <a name="t4"></a></p>
                              <p><b>TESSA-ONLY ISA:</b> If you have a TESSA that 
                                is still running, you can transfer the capital, 
                                but not the interest, when it reaches the end 
                                of its five-year term into a TESSA-only ISA. This 
                                means the capital you originally invested in the 
                                TESSA can continue to earn interest free of tax 
                                in the ISA. The transfer can be made in addition 
                                to your annual ISA allowance. <a name="t5"></a></p>
