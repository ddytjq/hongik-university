    <p><font SIZE="2" face="Impact" color="#804040"><a name="oxfordhitrocks">Monday 9th
    November 98</a><br>
    </font><font face="Arial Black" size="5">Oxford United Hit the Rocks</font></p>
    <p><font SIZE="2">Oxford United, next to bottom in the Nationwide Division One, have hit
    crisis point this week as their financial problems are threatening their very existance. </font></p>
    <p><font SIZE="2">They have accumulated debts of over £12m and in an effort to stave off
    the administrators a little longer, have put their entire squad and player manager,
    Malcolm Shotton, up for sale. The players were not paid last month and are threatening to
    strike. Shotton is not being paid at the moment:</font></p>
    <p><font SIZE="2">&quot;My wife is not happy about me not being paid and I will have to
    draw the line somewhere, although I have not reached that stage yet.&quot;</font></p>
    <p><font SIZE="2">The latest bid to take control of the club, that involving local
    businessman John Gunn has not fallen apart as reported elsewhere. Indeed only Gunn has
    pulled out, the remaining investors (Grenoble Investments) are continuing to talk to the
    present owners.</font></p>

    <p align="left"><font SIZE="2" face="Impact" color="#804040"><a name="david lloyd">Thursday
    5th November 98</a><br>
    </font><font face="Arial Black" size="5">David Lloyd finally sells Hull </font>After
    months of wrangling, Hull City owner David Lloyd has sold the Nationwide League's bottom
    club to a Tom Belton's Sheffield-based consortium. In a strange twist to the anger of
    supporters Lloyd has retained ownership rights to Boothferry Park.</p>
    <p>The Sheffield businessman Tim Buchanan is among the group taking charge, with Lloyd
    agreeing to invest a further £950,000 to wipe out debts.</p>
    <p>&quot;This is the best deal for everyone concerned, particularly as no one in Hull
    seems to have the money to buy the club,&quot; Lloyd said.</p>
    <p>The new owners may face problems if they proceed with plans, considered by Lloyd, to
    share The Boulevard rugby league ground with Hull Sharks while a new stadium is built.
    There are legal complications regarding Boothferry Park, quite apart from fans being
    angered because there were no firm plans for a new ground.</p>
    <p>This is considered to be the main reason that the Westferry deal stalled so badly. The
    successful consortium, who will rent Boothferry Park from Lloyd, face the problem of a £1
    million-plus wages bill with the manager Mark Hateley's package said to be worth up to
    £250,000.</p>
    <p>&nbsp;</p>
    <p align="left"><font SIZE="2" face="Impact" color="#804040">S<a name="keeganplead">aturday
    31st October 98</a><br>
    </font><font face="Arial Black" size="5">Keegan pleads for big boys to not forget smaller
    clubs<br>
    </font>Fulham manager Kevin Keegan this week launched a plea for football's big clubs to
    not forget the smaller clubs in their desire to grab more millions from the game. After
    witnessing his team give Liverpool a hard<img src="images/people/keegan.jpg" width="117"
    height="173" alt="keegan.jpg (9032 bytes)" align="right" border="0"> time before finally
    losing 3-1 at Anfield in the Worthington Cup, Keegan said: </p>
    <p>&quot;In football generally, there has to be a feeling - especially now when it is
    probably in its strongest ever position - that the big boys do have to look after the
    little boys a bit. If the big clubs are all going to go off in their own massive world way
    above us, like the supermarket did with the corner shop, then smaller clubs will
    suffer.&quot;</p>
    <p>Keegan's side matched Liverpool for an hour before class finally told. But he said:
    &quot;I know a lot of people think this competition is second rate, but for teams like us
    and the Hartlepools, Northamptons and Cambridges of this world, it gives you a lot of
    things.</p>
    <p>&quot;The chance to pit your wits against the very best, the chance to go into great
    stadiums and play, the chance to play against great players on great surfaces. It's
    wonderful for us, and our fans, who thoroughly enjoyed the night.&quot;</p>
    <p>&quot;It needs the big clubs in it, the romance of football is teams like Fulham at
    Anfield with a chance of winning the game. We had that chance and didn't take it. But it's
    a good competition for the small clubs and it's one that the big clubs should look on
    favourably despite bigger prizes elsewhere.</p>
    <p>It was estimated this week that Bury, who lost at Old Trafford on Tuesday evening,
    could gain around £500k in shared gate money. </p>
    <p align="right">&nbsp;</p>
    <p align="left"></font><font SIZE="2" face="Impact" color="#804040">S<a name="manutddeal">aturday
    31s</a>t October 98<br>
    </font><font face="Arial Black" size="5">Man Utd's BSkyB deal referred to MMC<br>
    </font><font SIZE="2">BSkyB's bid to takeover Manchester United was referred to the
    Monopolies and Mergers Commission (MMC) by Trade and Industry Secretary Peter Mandelson.</p>
    <p>Mandelson was acting on the advice of the Director General of Fair Trading, who said
    the acquisition raised issues of competition over the TV rights to football. The Director
    General also noted the wider concerns of public interest related to the proposed deal.</p>
    <p>The BSkyB offer will now be subject to a full investigation by the MMC which will
    report its findings next March. In its statement issued on Thursday, the Department of
    Trade and Industry was careful not to take sides over the deal.</p>
    <p>&quot;The decision to a make a reference does not in any way prejudge the question of
    whether or not the merger would be against the public interest. It is for the MMC to
    report on this after its investigation,&quot; the statement said.</p>
    <p>BSkyB has bid £623 million for the club or 240p per share. Earlier this week the BSkyB
    said it had received valid acceptances from shareholders representing 33.5% of the club's
    shares. Combined with shares it has been buying directly from the company, the broadcaster
    has a total ownership just short of 45%. For a takeover, the company has to have more than
    50% to gain a controlling stake in the club, at which point most other investors would
    sell their shares.</p>
    <p>The news was greeted with euphoria by the Manchester United Independent Supporters'
    Association. Spokesman Andy Walsh said: </p>
    <p>&quot;I am absolutely delighted. This is what we've been working for over the last two
    months and it's exactly what we wanted. The referral to the MMC will give football fans
    from Manchester United and other clubs the opportunity to discuss for the first time how
    football should be structured in this country.&quot;</p>
    <p>He said the campaign against the deal - which so far has included public meetings, mass
    letter-writing and lobbying of parliament - would now be about &quot;gathering
    thoughts&quot; for a complete investigation of how the British game should be run.</p>
    <p>&quot;Our concern has been that somebody spending £2.40 on a share in Manchester
    United Plc has a greater share in the way the club is run than somebody who spends £300
    to £400 on a season ticket. That disparity needs to be addressed. We expect the MMC to
    look at all aspects of the game, not just the BSkyB-Manchester United takeover, and
    hopefully they will ensure the situation caused by the Sky attempt to take over our club
    is a thing of the past.&quot;</p>
    <p>&nbsp;</p>
    <p align="left"><font SIZE="2" face="Impact" color="#804040"><a name="euro200pledge">Friday
    30th O</a>ctober 98<br>
    </font></font><font face="Arial Black" size="5">Euro 2000 pledge to distribute tickets
    fairly<br>
    </font><font SIZE="2">This week, Euro 2000 organisers insisted that they've learned from
    the France 98 ticketing fiasco and will make a genuine effort to sell more tickets to
    genuine fans. It pledged a fairer system for the tournament to be held in Belgium and
    Holland, free of package deals that left thousands without seats in France. The first of
    the 1.23 million tickets for the championships will go on sale next spring.</p>
    <p>&quot;We are doing it differently than others. We are giving everyone an honest and
    fair chance,&quot; said Harry Been, Euro 2000 board secretary. </p>
    <p>For the World Cup in France, some 65 percent of the 2.5 million tickets were sold on
    the French market, with only eight percent of tickets to each team involved in a match,
    creating uproar from other European nations.</p>
    <p>Euro 2000 organizers want to avoid such problems, so 37 percent of tickets will be sold
    to the general public and 32 percent to the two nations involved in the match concerned.
    Euro 2000 has also done away with package deals to travel agencies that offered hotels,
    transport and tickets in one contract - the system caused many problems at the World Cup.</p>
    <p>Thousands of fans, including many from Japan, arrived with package tours only to find
    out the tickets were not available on match day.</p>
    <p>&nbsp;</p>
    <p align="left"><font SIZE="2" face="Impact" color="#804040">M<a name="wimbledontomerge">onday
    26th O</a>ctober 98<br>
    </font><font face="Arial Black" size="5">Wimbledon to Merge with anyone who'll have them<br>
    </font>Wimbledon FC's owners are once again trying to take Wimbledon away from its roots
    by trying to merge with another club, this time either landlords Palace, Man City or even
    QPR. Multi-millionaire Kjell Inge Roekke's company, Wyndmore, own's 80% of Wimbledon's
    shares but he has so far been baulked in his attempts to transform the homeless club.</p>
    <p>Jan Petter Storatvedt, Managing Director of Wyndmore, said last night <br>
    that a decision will be made on the next plan by January 1. He said: &quot;We have four
    options. The first is to stay as we are but that, clearly, is not satisfactory. The second
    would be to go to Dublin and into a new stadium and be called FA Dublin.&quot;</p>
    <p>&quot;The third would be to build a new stadium in London, while the fourth <br>
    would be to go into partnership with a club from a lower division, and <br>
    the three clubs we are looking at are Manchester City, QPR and Crystal <br>
    Palace.&quot;</p>
    <p>Unless Roekke starts seeing a return on his Wimbledon investment, he <br>
    may consider a fifth option - selling and switching his attention to <br>
    Manchester City, who yesterday announced pre-tax losses of £6.3m and <br>
    need a major cash injection.</p>
