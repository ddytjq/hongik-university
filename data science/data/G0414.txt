<h1>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Cosmic Background Explorer</font></h1></center>

<center>
<h1>
<A HREF="cobe_poster.gif"><img SRC="home_page_logo.jpg" ALT="COBE home page" height=181 width=500></A></h1></center>

<p><br>
<p><b><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Students, educators and the general
public are encouraged to discover the
<a href="ed_resources.html">COBE
Educational Resources</a>.</font></b>
<p><b><font face="Arial,Helvetica">The COBE satellite was developed by
NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center to measure the diffuse infrared and
microwave radiation from the early universe to the limits set by our astrophysical
environment. It was launched November 18, 1989 and carried three instruments,
a <a href="#firas">Far Infrared Absolute Spectrophotometer (FIRAS)</a>
to compare the spectrum of the cosmic microwave background radiation with
a precise blackbody, a <a href="#dmr">Differential Microwave Radiometer
(DMR)</a> to map the cosmic radiation sensitively, and a <a href="#dirbe">Diffuse
Infrared Background Experiment (DIRBE)</a> to search for the cosmic infrared
background radiation. Each COBE instrument yielded a major cosmological
discovery:</font></b>
<ul>
<li>
<b><font face="Arial,Helvetica"><font color="#CC0000">FIRAS</font> - The
cosmic microwave background (CMB) spectrum is that of a nearly perfect
blackbody with a temperature of 2.725 +/- 0.002 K. This observation matches
the predictions of the hot Big Bang theory extraordinarily well, and indicates
that nearly all of the radiant energy of the Universe was released within
the first year after the Big Bang.</font></b></li>

<br>&nbsp;
<li>
<b><font face="Arial,Helvetica"><font color="#CC0000">DMR</font> - The
CMB was found to have intrinsic "anisotropy" for the first time, at a level
of a part in 100,000. These tiny variations in the intensity of the CMB
over the sky show how matter and energy was distributed when the Universe
was still very young. Later, through a process still poorly understood,
the early structures seen by DMR developed into galaxies, galaxy clusters,
and the large scale structure that we see in the Universe today.</font></b></li>

<br>&nbsp;
<li>
<b><font face="Arial,Helvetica"><font color="#CC0000">DIRBE</font> - Infrared
absolute sky brightness maps in the wavelength range 1.25 to 240 microns
were obtained to carry out a search for the cosmic <i>infrared</i> background
(CIB). The CIB was originally detected in the two longest DIRBE wavelength
bands, 140 and 240 microns, and in the short-wavelength end of the FIRAS
spectrum. Subsequent analyses have yielded detections of the CIB in the
near-infrared DIRBE sky maps. The CIB represents a "core sample" of the
Universe; it contains the cumulative emissions of stars and galaxies dating
back to the epoch when these objects first began to form. The COBE CIB
measurements constrain models of the cosmological history of star formation
and the buildup over time of dust and elements heavier than hydrogen, including
those of which living organisms are composed. Dust has played an important
role in star formation throughout much of cosmic history.</font></b></li>
</ul>
