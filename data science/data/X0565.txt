	<td width="75%">
		<b>Rural Liberal MPs oppose 'urban' bill                                                               </b><br>
		<font size=2>Author: Sarah Schmidt                                      <br>
		Source: National Post                                                                                                                                         </font>
	</td>

	<td align=right VALIGN=TOP>		
				Tues.          April 30, 2002
	</td>
	</tr>
	<tr>
		<td colspan=2><br>
		<br>
				<P align=justify><EM>Backbenchers fear animal-cruelty law could affect farms</EM> </P>
<P align=justify>Members of the Liberal Rural Caucus are demanding changes to the proposed animal-cruelty legislation, the second government bill yesterday to raise the ire of backbenchers. </P>
<P align=justify>Murray Calder, chairman of the 45-member rural caucus, wants the government to add a key clause he says will protect livestock producers from possible prosecution or frivolous lawsuits related to the way they raise and kill farm animals, a central concern of the Canadian Alliance. </P>
<P align=justify>"Like I told the Prime Minister last week, the real opposition sits behind him," Mr. Calder said in an interview yesterday. </P>
<P align=justify>Bill C-15B, which amends the Criminal Code and firearms act, proposes to strengthen the code's animal-cruelty provisions. They have not undergone significant changes since 1892. </P>
<P align=justify>The bill is in third reading. The Alliance has already delayed its passage in the House of Commons. </P>
<P align=justify>In a letter to members of the Liberal rural caucus, Mr. Calder proposes supporting an Alliance amendment to send the bill back to committee if Martin Cauchon, the Minister of Justice, fails to signal to the Senate his support for his proposed amendment; amending the bill in the House is highly improbable at such a late stage. </P>
<P align=justify>"I believe that unless this portion is amended, there will be a perception in rural Canada that once again a law tailored to urban interests is being thrust upon the rural community. Those of us representing rural ridings know all too well the divisiveness and distrust that remains from our government's passage of C-68, the gun registration law," Mr. Calder wrote. </P>
<P align=justify>His ultimatum comes at a difficult time for the Liberal government, which yesterday suddenly abandoned its plan to shut down debate and force a vote on its controversial endangered species bill, fearing a potential revolt from its frustrated backbench. </P>
<P align=justify>Many Liberal backbenchers are furious with the way the government has handled this legislation, undoing months of work by the all-party Commons committee and putting forth its own set of amendments. </P>
<P align=justify>Mr. Calder said he has spoken to Mr. Cauchon about his amendment to the proposed animal-cruelty bill. He has yet to receive a response. </P>
<P align=justify>In the meantime, Vic Toews, the Justice critic for the Alliance, welcomes the growing number of disgruntled Liberal backbenchers. </P>
<P align=justify>"What I think we've seeing on a number of bills, starting with gun <REGISTRY>, we're seeing a failure of this government to understand the values and needs of rural Canada. There just doesn't seem to be that understanding. Traditionally, Liberal backbenchers have just swallowed what's been given to them by a minister." </P>
<P align=justify>Animal-welfare groups, which have long advocated for strengthened animal-cruelty provisions in the criminal code, are surprised by this unrest in the Liberal caucus. They have spent most of their time attacking the Alliance's opposition to the bill. </P>
<P align=justify>Yesterday, for example, the Canadian branch of the International Fund for Animal Welfare ran an ad in the Hill Times, a Parliamentary publication. The ad was titled 'The Harper's Index to animal cruelty,' directed at Stephen Harper, the Alliance leader. </P>
<P align=justify>The ad listed 20 facts, including days since the Canadian Alliance's first argument against the animal-cruelty provisions of Bill C-15 (410), the percentage of animal cruelty complaints in Canada that result in charges (0.003) and convictions (0.00138) and number of hours the Alliance has been filibustering the animal-cruelty provisions (15). </P>
<P align=justify>Rick Smith, the fund's Canadian chairman, said the changes floated within the Liberal rural caucus "would make the law worse, not better. It would continue to allow animal abusers to get away scot-free and would create new loopholes in the law, not close existing loopholes. I fear that the Alliance misinformation campaign of the last few months is starting to have some effect." </P>
<P align=justify>The government's rural caucus is meeting today. Mr. Calder said he will lobby his colleagues to side with the Alliance. "As far as I'm concerned, the [proposed amendment] is very fair. We want to make sure that farming practices and hunting practices that are acceptable today will be acceptable tomorrow." </P>
<P align=justify>Peter Adams, Liberal MP for Peterborough and member of the rural caucus, disagrees with the chairman. He said the government has already incorporated suggestions of the rural caucus. "Normal farming practices were never, in my view, at risk, so I'm surprised to see this." </P>
<P align=justify><EM>Article was published in the </EM><A href="http://www.nationalpost.com/" target=_blank><EM>National Post</EM></A><EM> on April 30, 2002.</EM></P>
