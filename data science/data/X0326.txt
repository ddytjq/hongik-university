				<font class="newsdate"> Saturday, December 22, 2001 07:27 (GMT)</font><br>
				<img src="/1.gif" width="1" height="18"><br>
				<font class="news">The appointment of John Wright as the first foreign coach of the Indian team last year was met with mixed reactions.<br><br>There were certain disgruntled elements in Indian cricket who openly questioned the need of a foreign coach when, according to them, there were suitable candidates among former Indian players.<br><br>But he did have his supporters who reckoned that perhaps the time had come for a foreign coach, with the team not going exactly anywhere with greats like Bishen Bedi, Ajit Wadekar and Kapil Dev among others in charge throughout the 90s. <br><br>No one expected miracles from the affable former New Zealand captain, who had achieved some success with Kent, but it was hoped that he would make some kind of difference. Last month, Wright completed a year in his post and it was time for some stocktaking. <br><br>The victorious series against Zimbabwe in November last year was the first with Wright in charge. Predictably enough, there was no overnight transformation in Indian cricket. Yet, during the past year, there is little doubt that he has injected a certain professionalism in the approach.<br><br>Greater emphasis has been laid on matters of fielding and physical fitness, and while Wright has also touched upon some of the basics, aspects like running between the wickets and fielding remain a problem. But he has been responsible for making the players mentally tougher, not sparing anyone, and his frank appraisals have gone down well with team members and administrators. His honesty is patent and his observations are analytical and to the point. <br><br>At the time he took over, Wright made it clear that his main target was the World Cup in South Africa.<br><br>"I will be building up the team keeping in mind the 2003 World Cup. I firmly believe that India has the talent to match any other side in international cricket," he said. <br><br>There was another positive aspect to Wright's appointment. Board circles felt that he would not be biased towards any of the factions that normally exist in the Indian set-up.<br><br>From this viewpoint, it has been a healthy sign since the players have been encouraged to perform better and not expect any favoritism whatsoever from the coach. Indeed, in all his roles, Wright has been a quiet achiever. Never one for the grandiose stance or plans, his motto has been simple and straightforward - knuckle down and do your job well. <br><br>Wright's professionalism was seen immediately in the use of the latest computer technology to formulate crucial strategies. This was an aspect that no Indian coach had considered.<br><br>On landing in India, he went straight to Bangalore to purchase the right software. He also insisted that instilling the pride of playing for the country would be the topmost priority, a happy theory, for what is a coach if not a motivator? <br><br>By having the knack of saying the right thing at the right time, there were indications that he was the right man for Indian cricket. During the past year, he has developed a close rapport with the captain, the players and the physio. And even if the results have not exactly been spectacular, they have not been poor either.<br><br>Any balance sheet that displays arguably the greatest series triumph at home has to have a fairly healthy look, overall.<br><br>Unfortunately, the poor record of the Indian team abroad has not improved under Wright's tenure and this attracted not only adverse comment, but also the concern of the BCCI president Jagmohan Dalmiya, giving rise to rumors last month that the termination of his contract was only a matter of time.<br><br>Indeed, his detractors felt that Dalmiya&#8217;s election as BCCI chief in September would spell the end of Wright&#8217;s tenure. The BCCI president questioning the team coach on cricketing matters was something new. In the past, the board has not adopted such a tough line.<br><br>But Wright, unfazed as ever, met the problem head on. A few days ago, he met Dalmiya and as usual came out with the kind of candid comments that have won him a lot of friends in India.<br><br>"I had a constructive discussion with Mr Dalmiya," Wright added. "It's always good to talk to the man in charge of Indian cricket. Accountability is close to the president's heart and I welcome that.<br><br>"Everyone is accountable and that is a very good thing. We owe it to the people of India who are the greatest cricket fans in the world. No one is bigger than the game and that includes the coach." <br><br>Wright's open attitude is a welcome and path-breaking trend in Indian cricket. Coaches before him frequently distanced themselves from the team's failures by making excuses that were unpalatable.<br><br>Wright's approach is refreshingly different. Readily acknowledging his accountability and taking Dalmiya's probing in the right spirit, Wright has continued to concentrate on his job.<br><br>"I do the best job I can and hope it transmits through the performance of the team," the 47-year-old former New Zealand opening batsman said earlier this week.   <br><br>Indian cricket followers have generally been impatient. Expecting fast results, they have been too quick to hire and fire, when what is really needed is understanding, patience and perseverance.<br><br>There are certain inherent weaknesses in Indian cricket that Wright is working on. These will not go away overnight. He himself made it clear at the beginning of his tenure that he was building a team for the 2003 World Cup. He should be given time till then.<br><br>- <i>Partab Ramchand</i><br><br><b>COMMENT</b><br>Do you agree with Partab's comments?
