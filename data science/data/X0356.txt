				<font class="news">On Christmas Eve, the England cricket team left for home following their three-Test series against India, defeated but far from disgraced. Will the scenario be any different on 3 February when the six-match one-day international series that commences at Kolkata on Saturday, concludes? <br><br>For a start, it must be said that all the indications point to a close contest. And in this regard, the comment of the England coach Duncan Fletcher that his side is &#8220;under-prepared&#8221; for the series need not be taken too seriously.<br><br>The visitors did express their unhappiness over the practice facilities initially, but since then they have moved to the Eden Gardens where the net facilities are excellent and also had the benefit of a practice game against a Cricket Association of Bengal XI which they won comfortably by eight wickets. <br><br>All the same, it would be tempting to install India as favorites based both on home advantage and an admirable record in limited overs cricket in this country. In the last couple of years, India has registered series victories over New Zealand, South Africa and Zimbabwe and even against Australia, they ran the World Cup holders close before losing 3-2.<br><br>The immense strength of their batting covers up for the obvious weaknesses in the bowling and this factor, more than anything else, has enabled India to more than hold their own in their backyard. In India, high totals are the rule rather than the exception and we have had recent cases when teams scoring 300 plus have finished on the losing side.<br><br>But England too have put their disastrous World Cup campaign of 1999 behind them and have built a resilient side. While it is a fact that over the last year, they lost eleven one-day games in a row, they recently bounced back by winning all five in Zimbabwe.<br><br>And if anything, they can take heart that they have more than held their own in contests in India. In 1981-82, India, yet to come to terms with the intricacies of the one-day game, emerged surprising victors by 2-1. <br><br>Three years later, England triumphed by an emphatic 4-1 margin. And in 1992-93, the teams shared the six match series 3-3. Also to be placed on record is England&#8217;s victory in the Reliance Cup semifinal at Bombay in 1987 and India&#8217;s win in the MRF World Series game at Kanpur two years later. <br><br>Indeed, on both past record and current form, there is very little to choose between the contestants. And even when it comes to analyzing the strengths and weaknesses of the two teams, the sides appear evenly matched. If India have, on paper, the stronger batting, England would seem to have the edge in bowling.<br><br>In seam bowling, England are much better served. India have nothing to match the tried and tested duo of Darren Gough and Andrew Caddick and their experience of Indian conditions last month should stand Andrew Flintoff and Matthew Hoggard in good stead.<br><br>Considering their limited experience, both showed enough promise of performing better in future. But in spin bowling, England cannot match the Indian trio of Anil Kumble, Harbhajan Singh and Sarandeep Singh. Two will almost certainly take the field, for a four-pronged Indian seam attack, with only one spinner will only serve as meat and drink to the opposition.<br><br>India&#8217;s hopes will rest on the spinners pulling off a trick or two, for there is no doubt that the Englishmen would prefer to face Javagal Srinath, Ajit Agarkar and Zaheer Khan rather than the spin trio.<br><br>Ultimately, then we have a scenario where England are better served in seam bowling and India have more than an edge in the spin department, despite the presence of the persistent Ashley Giles. The onus then would seem to rest on the batsmen and we could frequently have a situation where the side that bats better wins the match.<br><br>On paper, as I said, India would seem to have the stronger batting. A line-up that starts with Sourav Ganguly and Sachin Tendulkar and continues with Venkatsai Laxman, Hemang Badani, Dinesh Mongia and Virender Shewag inspires more confidence than the England line-up of Nick Knight, Nasser Hussain, Michael Vaughn, Marcus Trescothick, Graham Thorpe, Craig White and Andrew Flintoff.<br><br>The problem however is that the much vaunted Indian batting has not always performed in keeping with their lofty reputation. Also, the absence of an all-rounder in the Indian side is all too apparent and it is hoped that Agarkar, a gifted cricketer who has not made full use of his many opportunities, will finally come good.<br><br>- <i>Partab Ramchand</i><br><br><b>COMMENT</b><br>Do you agree with Partab's comments?
