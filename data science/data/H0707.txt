<td valign="top"><p class="articleheadline">Bt Corn, Cotton Win Continued Approval</p></td>
<td rowspan="2"><img src="html/images/BtCorn_lrg.jpg" width=190 height=152 align=right></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td valign="bottom">
<p class="articlesubheadline">EPA says Bt crops show "no signs of any adverse effects to human health" and show "significant benefits" for growers.</p>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<br>

<p>Farmers and consumers can
continue to benefit from Bt corn and Bt cotton, thanks to recent actions by the
Environmental Protection Agency (EPA).  </p>

<p>In October 2001, following a
comprehensive <a href="http://www.epa.gov/pesticides/biopesticides/reds/brad_bt_pip2.htm" onclick="javascript:window.open('index.asp?redirect=http%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Eepa%2Egov%2Fpesticides%2Fbiopesticides%2Freds%2Fbrad%5Fbt%5Fpip2%2Ehtm','','').focus();return false;">study<ximg src="html/images/spacer.gif" border="0" width="4"><ximg src="html/images/ealink.gif" border="0" width="10"></a> of the
science and six-year track record of Bt crops, the EPA renewed its registration
of Bt corn for seven years, and Bt cotton for five years. The agency concluded
that Bt crops are an “effective, low-risk pest control alternative” that show
“no signs of any adverse effects to human health” and instead offer
“significant benefits … to growers, the public, and the environment.”<sup>1</sup></p>

<p>Bt stands for <i>Bacillus thuringiensis</i> &mdash; a common soil
bacterium that’s been used for years as a natural pesticide, particularly on
organic farms.<sup>2</sup> 

<p>Bt corn protects against the European corn borer, an insect that’s difficult to 
control because soon after hatching it tunnels into the corn stalk, where it’s 
protected from conventional pesticides. Prior to the introduction of Bt corn, 
the European corn borer caused production losses in the United States ranging from 33 
million bushels to more than 300 million bushels per year.<sup>3</sup></p>

<p>Bt cotton controls three
destructive insects: tobacco budworm, cotton bollworm and pink bollworm. While
other insecticides are available, in many areas the targeted pests have become
resistant. In 1995, for example, a year with high tobacco budworm infestation,
Alabama growers sustained yield losses of nearly 30 percent.<sup>4</sup></p>

<p>Bt crops have become a favorite
with many farmers, and a boon to consumers: 
</p>

<ul>

<li><p>Bt corn alone currently grows on about 30 million acres in
the United States<sup>5</sup>
(as well as in Argentina, Canada, and South Africa<sup>6</sup>),
a figure expected to rise by 13 percent in 2002.<sup>7</sup></p></li>

<li><p>In 1999, it’s estimated that Bt corn saved about 66
million bushels, the equivalent of production on nearly 500,000 acres, from
destruction by pests.<sup>8</sup>
</p></li>

<li><p>Bt cotton boosts yields over unprotected crops by about 7
percent. For a farmer working 300 acres of cotton, that translates to an extra
$10,000 annually.<sup>9</sup> </p></li>

<li><p>Because they’re naturally protected against pests, Bt corn
and cotton typically require less spraying. Corn growers, for example, have
reduced pesticide treatments for the European corn borer by about a third.<sup>10</sup></p></li>

</ul>

<p class="articlepullquote">No impact on the monarch butterfly</p>

<p>But it is the monarch butterfly
&mdash; not destructive insects &mdash; that garnered the most headlines for Bt crops
following a 1999 article in the journal <i>Nature.</i>
The article highlighted a fact already known to biotech scientists and
regulators: that at artificially high doses in the laboratory, pollen from Bt
corn eaten off milkweed leaves could be fatal to some butterfly larvae.</p>

<p>The study’s lead author said “it
would be inappropriate to draw any conclusions about risk to monarch
populations in the field based solely on these initial results.”<sup>11</sup>
Experts noted that results from a lab test in which monarch larvae were
force-fed Bt pollen were not necessarily reflective of actual conditions in the
field. However, alarm bells were rung claiming that Bt crops could pose a risk
to monarch butterflies that migrate through the
corn-growing central United States.</p>

<p>Follow-up studies, however,
showed that because corn pollen is relatively heavy, it moves at most a short
distance from cultivated fields, where it’s unlikely to encounter the wild
milkweed plants on which larvae feed. In addition, only larvae feeding during a
pollen shed could potentially be affected. The risk of exposure is thus minimal
at most, the studies showed.<sup>12</sup>
</p>

<p>After evaluating the evidence,
the EPA concluded that “the scientific evidence demonstrates that Bt corn does
not impact monarch butterfly populations.”<sup>13</sup>
A hazard in the laboratory does not translate into a risk in nature, the EPA
said. </p>

<p>The EPA’s report dispels other
concerns raised about Bt crops, including the possibility of cross-breeding
with weedy relatives to create highly resilient “<a href="index.asp?id=1241">superweeds</a>,” and
potential harm to nontarget organisms such as
earthworms.<sup>14</sup> </p>

<p>In evaluating these issues, the
EPA factored in scientific data and other input from peer review groups,
university researchers, farmers and the public. Since they were initially approved as safe in the mid-1990s,
biotech crops have been carefully evaluated on an ongoing basis by experts from
many different disciplines. </p>

<p>“For the consumer, my customer,
they should feel at ease, because this [review] really reaffirms the safety of
this product,” says Leon Corzine, an Illinois farmer who also serves as a
spokesman for the National Corn Growers Association.<sup>15</sup></p>

<p class="articlepullquote">Proven benefits</p>

<p>Bt crops provide an important
economic benefit to growers, estimated at about $350 million in 2000.<sup>16</sup>
They have also reduced spraying.</p>

<p>Cotton growers, for example,
have reduced pesticide use by an estimated 2.7 million pounds and made 15
million fewer insecticide applications per year due to the introduction of Bt
cotton.<sup>17</sup>
Anecdotally, many farmers say they see more small animals and other wildlife
near Bt-planted fields.<sup>18</sup>
</p>

<p>Bt corn has also been shown to
reduce the incidence of some <a href="index.asp?id=1238">mycotoxins</a> &mdash; fungi
that can cause health problems in animals and humans if there’s a high enough
concentration in the corn.<sup>19</sup>
</p>

<p>In renewing the registration of
Bt corn and cotton, the EPA required seed companies, growers and others to
continue to carefully monitor any possible environmental and human impacts of
these crops. Farmers must adhere to certain planting requirements, for example,
and researchers will continue to collect extensive data about the crops.<sup>20</sup>
</p>
