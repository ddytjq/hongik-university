Delays and difficulties in the dissemination of these DNAs among laboratories occurred.			<li>Pairs of oligonucleotides complementary to opposite strands of a DNA template will prime the amplification by <a href="../MGW4/MG426.html">polymerase chain reaction</a> (PCR) of the bracketed template DNA.			<li>Oligonucleotides of defined sequence can be obtained inexpensively and rapidly by any laboratory. Oligonucleotide availability and PCR have made classical <a href="MG11124.html">RFLP</a> analysis more accessible.			<li>PCR amplification using readily obtainable oligonucleotides also simplified the analysis of single-locus <a href="MG11125.html">VNTR</a>s.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Interp"></a>Interpretations</h4>		<h4>		<table border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0">			<tr>				<td>					<ul type="circle">						<li>The lab that discovers an RFLP determines the nucleotide sequence at the two ends of the probe. The sequences are made public as &quot;sequence tagged sites&quot;. Any lab can then have oligonucleotides for the end sequences made and, by PCR, generate the probe.					</ul>				</td>				<td><img src="i111262.gif" align="right"></td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td>					<ul type="circle">						<li>A pair of oligonucleotides complementary to conserved regions flanking a VNTR region is made. The size of PCR products can be analyzed directly.					</ul>				</td>				<td><img src="i111263.gif" align="right"></td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td>					<ul type="circle">						<li>If the region between primers A and B contains a restriction site that is polymorphic, the PCR product can be digested with the enzyme and the size of the digestion products analyzed by gel electrophoresis. This procedure by-passes the need for Southern blotting and hybridization. It differs from classical RFLP, in which the length of the fragment is determined by two restriction sites, in that one end of the assayed fragment is determined by a primer site.					</ul>				</td>				<td><img src="i111264.gif" width="289" height="69"></td>			</tr>		</table>		</h4>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Info"></a>Further information</h4>		<ul type="square">			<li>The VNTR approach is used in prediction of risk of hereditary forms of breast cancer, such as those caused by mutations in the <i>BRCA1</i> gene. A pair of VNTR loci, one on either side of the <i>BRCA1</i> gene are probed using the primed PCR procedure diagrammed above. DNA of affected and not affected family members is tested. Examination of the data reveals which VNTR alleles flank the mutant <i>BRCA1</i> gene and are thus linked to it.
