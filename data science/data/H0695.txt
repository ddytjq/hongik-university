<FONT SIZE="+1"><B>Understanding the Log Function.</B></FONT>
In the mathematical operation of addition we take two numbers
and join them to create a third:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
1 + 1 = 2.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP>
1 + 1 = 2.
</TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(21)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
We can repeat this operation:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
1 + 1 + 1 = 3.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP>
1 + 1 + 1 = 3.
</TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(22)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
Multiplication is the mathematical operation that extends this:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
3 \times 1 = 3.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG

 SRC="img39.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}3 \times 1 = 3.
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(23)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
In the same way, we can repeat multiplication:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
2 \times 2 = 4.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="79" HEIGHT="30"
 SRC="img40.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}2 \times 2 = 4.
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(24)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
and
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG

\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(25)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
The extension of multiplication is exponentiation:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
2 \times 2 = 2^2 = 4.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="122" HEIGHT="30"

 ALT="\begin{displaymath}2 \times 2 = 2^2 = 4.
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(26)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
and
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
2 \times 2 \times 2 = 2^3 = 8.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="154" HEIGHT="30"
 SRC="img43.gif"

\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(27)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
This is read ``two raised to the third is eight''.
Because exponentiation simply counts the number of multiplications,
the exponents add:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
2^2 \times 2^3 = 2^{2+3} = 2^5.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><A NAME="eq.sum2.3.5">&#160;</A><IMG

<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(28)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
The number `2' is called the base of the exponentiation.
If we raise an exponent to another exponent, the values multiply:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">

<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(29)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>

<P>
Now consider that we have a number and we want to know how many 2's
must be multiplied together to get that number.
For example, given that we are using `2' as the base, how many
2's must be multiplied together to get 32?  That is, we want to solve this
equation:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
2^B = 32.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP>
2<SUP><I>B</I></SUP> = 32.
</TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(30)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
Of course, 2<SUP>5</SUP> = 32,
so <I>B</I> = 5.
To be able to get a hold of this,
mathematicians made up a new function called the logarithm:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\log_{2}32 = 5.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="92" HEIGHT="33"
 SRC="img47.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}\log_{2}32 = 5.
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(31)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
We pronounce this as ``the logarithm to the base 2 of 32 is 5''.
It is the ``inverse function'' for exponentiation:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
2^{\log_{2}a} = a
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><A NAME="eqn.2log2aa">&#160;</A><IMG
 WIDTH="77" HEIGHT="28"
 SRC="img48.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}2^{\log_{2}a} = a
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(32)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
and
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\log_2(2^a) = a.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><A NAME="eqn.log22aa">&#160;</A><IMG
 WIDTH="102" HEIGHT="33"

 ALT="\begin{displaymath}\log_2(2^a) = a.
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(33)</TD></TR>

<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>

<FONT SIZE="+1"><B>The Addition Law.</B></FONT>
Consider this equation:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\fbox{
$2^{a + b} = 2^a \times 2^b$ }
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="139" HEIGHT="35"
 SRC="img55.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}\fbox{
$2^{a + b} = 2^a \times 2^b$ }\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(34)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
which is just a generalization of equation (<A HREF="node2.html#eq.sum2.3.5">28</A>).
Take the logarithm of both sides:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\log_2{2^{a + b}} = \log_2 \left( {2^a \times 2^b} \right)
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="201" HEIGHT="38"
 SRC="img56.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}\log_2{2^{a + b}} = \log_2 \left( {2^a \times 2^b} \right)
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(35)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
Exponentiation and the logarithm are inverse operations, so
we can collapse the left side:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
a + b = \log_2 \left( {2^a \times 2^b} \right)
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="174" HEIGHT="38"
 SRC="img57.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}a + b = \log_2 \left( {2^a \times 2^b} \right)
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(36)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
Now let's be tricky and substitute:

<!-- MATH: $\log_2 x = a$ -->
<IMG
 WIDTH="85" HEIGHT="34" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
 SRC="img58.gif"
 ALT="$\log_2 x = a$">and

<!-- MATH: $\log_2 y = b$ -->
<IMG
 WIDTH="83" HEIGHT="34" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
 SRC="img59.gif"
 ALT="$\log_2 y = b$">:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\log_2 x + \log_2 y = \log_2 \left( {2^{\log_2 x} \times 2^{\log_2 y}} \right)
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="301" HEIGHT="38"
 SRC="img60.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}\log_2 x + \log_2 y = \log_2 \left( {2^{\log_2 x} \times 2^{\log_2 y}} \right)
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(37)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
Again, exponentiation and the logarithm are inverse operations, so
we can collapse the two cases on the right side:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\fbox{
$\log_2 x + \log_2 y = \log_2 \left( x \times y \right)$ }
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="256" HEIGHT="39"
 SRC="img61.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}\fbox{
$\log_2 x + \log_2 y = \log_2 \left( x \times y \right)$ }\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(38)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
This is the additive property that Shannon was interested in.

<FONT SIZE="+1"><B>The ``Pull Forward'' Rule.</B></FONT>
From equation (<A HREF="node2.html#eqn.2log2aa">32</A>):
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
a = 2^{\log_{2}a}.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="82" HEIGHT="28"
 SRC="img62.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}a = 2^{\log_{2}a}.
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(39)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
Raise both sides to the <I>u</I>:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
a^u = \left(2^{\log_{2}a}\right)^u.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="122" HEIGHT="38"
 SRC="img63.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}a^u = \left(2^{\log_{2}a}\right)^u.
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(40)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
Now,
we can combine the exponents by multiplying, as in
(<A HREF="node2.html#eq.exponent.exponent">29</A>):
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
a^u = 2^{u\log_{2}a}.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="102" HEIGHT="28"
 SRC="img64.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}a^u = 2^{u\log_{2}a}.
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(41)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
Finally, take the log base 2 of both sides and collapse the right side:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\fbox{
$\log_2 a^u = u\log_{2}a$ }
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="161" HEIGHT="38"
 SRC="img65.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}\fbox{
$\log_2 a^u = u\log_{2}a$ }\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(42)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
This can be remembered as a rule that allows one to ``pull'' the 
exponent forward from inside the logarithm.

<FONT SIZE="+1"><B>How to Convert Between Different Bases.</B></FONT>
Calculators and computers generally don't calculate the
logarithm to the base 2, but we can use a trick to make this easy.
Start by letting:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
x = \log_{z} a / \log_{z} b
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><A NAME="eqn.x-convert">&#160;</A><IMG
 WIDTH="136" HEIGHT="33"
 SRC="img66.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}x = \log_{z} a / \log_{z} b
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(43)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
Rearrange it as:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\log_{z} a = x \log_{z} b.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="130" HEIGHT="33"
 SRC="img67.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}\log_{z} a = x \log_{z} b.
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(44)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
Now use a ``reverse pull forward'' (!):
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\log_{z} a = \log_{z} b^x
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="120" HEIGHT="33"
 SRC="img68.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}\log_{z} a = \log_{z} b^x
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(45)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
and drop the logs:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
a = b^x.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP>
<I>a</I> = <I>b</I><SUP><I>x</I></SUP>.
</TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(46)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
Now take the log base <I>b</I>:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\log_b a = \log_b b^x.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="123" HEIGHT="33"
 SRC="img69.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}\log_b a = \log_b b^x.
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(47)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
This simplifies to:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\log_b a = x.
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="84" HEIGHT="33"
 SRC="img70.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}\log_b a = x.
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(48)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
But we know what <I>x</I> is from equation (<A HREF="node2.html#eqn.x-convert">43</A>):
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\log_b a = \log_{z} a / \log_{z} b
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="169" HEIGHT="33"

\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(49)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
The conversion rule to get logarithms base 2
from any base z is:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\fbox{
$\log_2(a) = \log_z(a)/\log_z(2)$ }
\end{equation} -->

<TABLE WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD ALIGN="CENTER" NOWRAP><IMG
 WIDTH="229" HEIGHT="39"
 SRC="img72.gif"
 ALT="\begin{displaymath}\fbox{
$\log_2(a) = \log_z(a)/\log_z(2)$ }\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(50)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
Notice that since the <I>z</I> does not appear on the left hand
side it doesn't matter what kind of logarithm
you have available, because you can always get to another base
using this equation!
Try this example on your calculator:
<BR><P></P>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<!-- MATH: \begin{equation}
\log_2(32) =

\end{equation} -->

\frac{\log_{ \mbox{\scriptsize whatever!} }(32)}
{\log_{ \mbox{\scriptsize whatever!} }(2)}.
\end{displaymath}"></TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(51)</TD></TR>
</TABLE>
</DIV>

You should get `5'.
