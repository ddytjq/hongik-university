					<h1>Some Important Insights from Biotechnology</h1>
					
				</td>
			</tr>

			<tr>
				<td width="25%" valign="top" class="side"> &nbsp;</td>
				<td width="75%" valign="top">
			
			<p>Living beings show three distinct features: they 
			<strong>nourish</strong> themselves, they <strong>reproduce</strong> 
			and they can <strong>keep themselves alive</strong>.</p>
			
			<p><img src="10.jpg" /><br /><br /></p>
						
		</td>
			</tr>
			
			<tr>
				<td width="25%" valign="top" class="side"> &nbsp;</td>
				<td width="75%" valign="top">
			
			<p>Plants and animals have different organs, which in turn are made of 
			<strong>cells</strong>. A cell is the smallest unit capable of displaying 
			the three characteristics of life. 
			A <strong>cell</strong> could be compared to a miniature town:</p>
			
			<ul>
			
				<li><p>It communicates with its surroundings, while protecting itself with a cell wall.</p></li>

				<li><p>Maschines and tools (organelles and <a href="/en/glossary/1391">molecules</a> <img src="/glossary.gif">), 
				as well as regulators for their production, are produced by the cell itself.</p></li>
			
			</ul>
			
			<p>The necessary processes are handled by <a href="/en/glossary/1394">proteins</a> <img src="/glossary.gif">. 
			Everything has to be coordinated carefully, and is therefore <strong>regulated and 
			controlled</strong> from within the cell core by the <strong>genetic material</strong>.</p>
			
			<p><img src="12.jpg" /><br /><br /></p>
						
		</td>
			</tr>
			
			<tr>
				<td width="25%" valign="top" class="side"> &nbsp;</td>
				<td width="75%" valign="top">
			
			<p>In the cell core lies the <strong>genetic material</strong>, also known as 
			<a href="/en/glossary/1379">DNA</a> <img src="/glossary.gif">, well guarded like in a safe. Each human cell  
			contains approximatly 1.6 meters of DNA, only a millionth of a millimeter thick. 
			Enlarging the thickness to 1 millimeter would make each cell the size of a room of 
			5 to 10 meters and human beings would stand over 1600 kilometers high...</p>
			
			<p>DNA is built from only <strong>four different building blocks</strong>.
			Nevertheless a single strand of DNA can contain as much information 
			as a gigantic library.</p>
			
			<p><img src="14.jpg" /><br /><br /></p>
						
		</td>
			</tr>
			
			<tr>
				<td width="25%" valign="top" class="side"> &nbsp;</td>
				<td width="75%" valign="top">
			
			<p>The genetic material usually doesn't move around inside the cell, 
			but controls the cells activity from within the cell's core. 
			Messages are sent out into the space between the cell's core and 
			its membrane, where they are read and executed. 
			Each section of the genetic material which codes for one 
			<strong>hereditary trait</strong> is called a 
			<a href="/en/glossary/1382">gene</a> <img src="/glossary.gif">. The cell uses the information provided by 
			the genes to manufacture specific proteines. 
			<strong>Each protein has a special function.</strong> 
			Therefore each gene is responsible for one function.</p>
			
			<p><img src="15.jpg" /><br /><br /></p>
						
		</td>
			</tr>
			
			<tr>
				<td width="25%" valign="top" class="side"> &nbsp;</td>
				<td width="75%" valign="top">
			
			<p>Scientists discovered that the <strong>genetic code</strong> is 
			<strong>identical in all living beings</strong>. 
			A gene from one <a href="/en/glossary/1392">organism</a> <img src="/glossary.gif"> can therefore be read by a different one. 
			This fact helps explain <strong>evolution</strong> and <strong>biodiversity</strong>. 
			It is also one of the most important <strong>key concepts of biotechnology</strong>.</p>
			
			<p><img src="19.jpg" /><br /><br /></p>
						
		</td>
			</tr>
			
			<tr>
				<td width="25%" valign="top" class="side"> &nbsp;</td>
				<td width="75%" valign="top">
			
			<p>Genetic engineering makes use of <strong>natural, enzymatic processes</strong> 
			which are able to recognize certain pieces of genetic material, cut it in 
			certain places and glue pieces together again with high precision, 
			thereby changing the original message encoded in the genetic material. 
			Not quite unlike editing a text document on a computer, cutting and pasting text...</p>
			
			<p>By cutting out the gene coding for <a href="/en/glossary/1387">insulin</a> <img src="/glossary.gif"> 
			from a piece of human genetic material, and then having it inserted into the 
			genetic material of a specific kind of bacteria, you can get it to produce 
			exactly this substance. Such modified organisms are called <strong>recombinant</strong> 
			or <strong>transgenic</strong>.</p>
			
			<p><img src="20.jpg" /><br /><br /></p>
						
		</td>
			</tr>
			
			<tr>
				<td width="25%" valign="top" class="side"> &nbsp;</td>
				<td width="75%" valign="top">
			
			<p>The technique of producing insulin in large fermentors is a good example for 
			the use of classical <a href="/en/glossary/1376">biotechnology</a> <img src="/glossary.gif">.</p>
			
			<p><img src="21.jpg" /><br /><br /></p>
						
		</td>
			</tr>
			
			<tr>
				<td width="25%" valign="top" class="side"> &nbsp;</td>
				<td width="75%" valign="top">
			
			<p><strong>Classical biotechnology</strong> makes use of  
			knowledge and discoveries dating back to the time of Pasteur. 
			New discoveries and breeds of microorganisms allow for an 
			increasing level of control and efficiency in the production 
			(<a href="/en/glossary/1381">fermentation</a> <img src="/glossary.gif">) 
			of antibiotics and other chemical substances. 
			Genetic engineering adds many new possibilities to what can be 
			accomplished with biotechnology.</p>
			
			<p><img src="24.jpg" /><br /><br /></p>
						
		</td>
			</tr>
