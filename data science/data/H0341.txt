The balance between mRNA degradation and <a href="MG22.html">mRNA synthesis</a> determines the level of individual mRNAs in cells.							<li>Replacement of the 3' non-translated region (NTR) of the stable <a href="../MGW1/MG112286.html">globin</a> mRNA with the 3' NTR of the unstable c-fos mRNA made the globin mRNA unstable.							<li>51 nt of A and U rich RNA from the 3' NTR of the rapidly turning over mRNA for granulocyte-macrophage colony stimulating factor (GM-CSF), when placed in the 3' NTR of globin mRNA, resulted in an unstable globin mRNA (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=1717077&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>).							<li>No globin 3' NTR sequences when added to GM-CSF mRNA made that RNA stable.						</ul>					</td>					<td><img align="left" src="i2371.gif" usemap="#"><map name=""></map></td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td><img align="right" src="i2372.gif"></td>					<td colspan="2">						<ul>							<li>Two kinds of AU-rich elements can be distinguished. <a href="MG234.html">PolyA</a> tails with <a href="MG2371.html">class I elements</a> (c-fos mRNA) are degraded synchronously, apparently by a <a href="../MGW4/MG424.html">distributive</a> ribonuclease (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=7565731&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>).							<li><a href="MG2371.html">Class II AU-rich elements</a> cause the poly A tails to be removed asynchronously, probably by a <a href="../MGW4/MG424.html">processive</a> ribonuclease.						</ul>					</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td colspan="3">						<ul>							<li><a href="../MGW4/MG427.html">In vitro transcripts</a> with <a href="MG233.html">5' caps</a> <a href="../MGW4/MG431.html">electroporated</a> into tobacco protoplasts are 2-4 times more stable than transcripts without caps. Similarly, transcripts with <a href="MG234.html">poly(A) tails</a> are 2-3 times more stable than transcripts without tails (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=1682219&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>)						</ul>					</td>				</tr>			</table>			</p>		</ul>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Interp"></a>Interpretations</h4>		<ul type="circle">			<li>Stability or instability of mRNA is due to identifiable sequences in the 3' NTR.			<li>5' caps and 3' tails contribute to mRNA stability.			<li>mRNAs are inherently stable. Sequence elements present in some mRNAs cause those RNAs to be unstable			<li>At least two distinct mechanisms for RNA degradation appear to exist.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a><br>			</p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Info"></a>Further information<img src="navigator.gif" width="71" height="98" align="right" usemap="#navigatorB1E7A4D0"><map name=""></map></h4>		<ul type="square">			<li>Signaling by class I and class II elements depends on the presence of a <a href="MG2371.html">regulatory factor</a>.			<li>3' UTR instability elements have also been identified in plant mRNAs. These contain AUAGAU and GAU motifs (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=8329900&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>).			<li>Recent evidence indicates that globin mRNAs may contain stability elements (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=9528789&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>). Thus, the conclusion that mRNAs are inherently stable may not apply to all mRNAs.			<li>In yeast, the predominant mRNA degradation pathway begins with shortening of the poly(A) tail. The shorter tail allows Dcp1p protein to remove the 5' cap (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=9482745&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>). The capless RNA is degraded by a 5' to 3' exonuclease (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=1398123&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>).  In plants, endonucleases appear to be important initiators of degradation (<a href="http://www.biomednet.com/library/trends/display.exe?jcode=plants&action=jump_to_article&jta_volume=4&jta_page=429&x=9&y=9">ref</a>).			<li>mRNA stability is of importance to biotechnology. Unaltered Bt toxin genes produce unstable mRNAs in plants (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/abstract/117/4/1445?maxtoshow=&HITS=10&hits=10&RESULTFORMAT=&searchid=QID_NOT_SET&FIRSTINDEX=&volume=117&firstpage=1445&fdate=1/1/1998&tdate=12/31/1998">ref</a>).		</ul>		<center>			<p><a name="bottom"></a><a href="MG2362.html">Last</a> | <a href="MG239.html">Vocabulary</a> | <a href="MG23.html">Overview</a> | <a href="#Facts">Top</a> | <a href="MG2371.html">Next</a><br>			<br>			</p>		</center>		<p>		<hr size="2">		</p>		<p>This is page 237 of <a href="../MG01.html">Molecular Genetics</a> by <a href="http://opbs.okstate.edu/~melcher/UM.html">Ulrich Melcher</a>, &copy; 1997, 1998, 1999, 2001<br>		<hr size="2">		<img height="72" width="108" src="divlogo2.gif" align="right"></p>		<address>E-mail inquiries to <a href="mailto:umelcher@biochem.okstate.edu">U.
