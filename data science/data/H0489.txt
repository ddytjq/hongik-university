The chromosomes line up in the middle of the cell. The two sister chromatids are then evenly distributed to opposite halves of the cell undergoing division (<strong>mitosis</strong>).					</ul>				</td>			</tr>		</table>		</p>		<ul>			<li><img src="i13612.gif" align="right">At about the same time, it was also discovered that during the formation of egg and sperm cells, a cell division occurs without prior doubling of the number of chromosomes. In meiosis (right), two cell divisions follow a single round of chromosome replication. In the first division, homologous chromosomes pair and segregate to daughter cells. In the, second division, sister chromatids segregate.			<li>In the <a href="MG111111.html">segregation</a> of chromosomes to daughter cells, the chromosomes appear to be pulled via a structure attached to chromosome <a href="MG1364.html">centromeres</a>. This structure is now called the <a href="MG1365.html">kinetochore</a> and centromeres are formally defined as the kinetochore attachment sites.  			<li>In <a href="MG1132.html">metaphase chromosomes</a>, the homologous arms of sister chromatids almost always are apposed to one another.			<li>The <a href="MG11221.html">double-helical</a> nature of DNA results in intertwining of daughter double helices unless the intertwining is destroyed by <a href="MG1328.html">topoisomerases</a>.			<li><a href="MG1132.html">Sister chromatid exchanges</a> are routinely observed after exposure of cells to DNA damaging agents.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Interp"></a>Interpretations</h4>		<ul type="circle">			<li>The consistent equal segregation of chromosomes to daughter cells suggested that the chromosomes were the carriers of heredity.			<li>The gametes contain a single complement of a species' chromosomes (n, haploid) and are formed by reductive division of a 2n (diploid) cell.			<li>Attachment of chromosomes at centromeres to kinetochores is essential for segregation.			<li>In meiosis I, sister chromatids must remain attached to one another. A protein factor may be required to prevent their premature dissociation.			<li>Sister chromatids remain connected to one another through replication and chromatin condensation.  The cohesion must be destroyed at anaphase.			<li>Daughter DNA strand intertwining can be one explanation of <a href="MG13611.html">sister chromatid cohesion</a>.  Other factors may also be responsible for cohesion.  <a href="MG1328.html">Topoisomerases</a> play important roles in chromosome dynamics.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a><br>			</p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Info"></a>Further information</h4>		<ul type="square">			<li><a href="http://www.tokyo-med.ac.jp/genet/anm/mimov.gif">Animation</a> of <a href="http://biog-101-104.bio.cornell.edu/BioG101_104/tutorials/cell_division.html">meiosis</a> illustrates the integration of replication, crossing over and segregation.
