        <td bgcolor="#FFFFEE" valign="middle" align="center" width="640" height="107">
            <p style="margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 0" align="center"><b><font face="Arial" size="4">Comparing
            CART and Formula One</font></b>
            </p>
            <p style="margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 0" align="center">&nbsp;
            </p>
            <p style="margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 6" align="center"><b><font size="2" color="#000000">
            &nbsp;</font><font color="#FF0000" face="Arial" size="2">by</font></b><font size="2" face="Arial"><b><font color="#FF0000">
            </font></b><font color="#FF0000"><b>Mark Cipolloni</b></font></font><b><font color="#0066FF"><br>
            </font><font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">October 29, 2001</font></b></p>
        </td>
          </tr>
  </center>
          <tr>
        <td bgcolor="#000000" valign="middle" align="center" width="480" height="21">
            <p align="left"><font color="#FFFFFF" size="3" face="Arial"><b>Go to
            our <a href="http://autoracing1.motorsportforum.com">forums</a>
            to discuss this article</b></font>
        </td>
          </tr>
          <tr>
          <TD align=right bgcolor=#FFFFEE vAlign=top width=640>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">For over 50 years the top level
    of international motorsports has been Formula One.&nbsp; Although the Indy
    500 has always been the biggest race (since 1911), long ago Formula One
    surpassed the popularity of Indy Car racing.&nbsp; Until CART made a
    concerted effort to break out into the international racing scene, the world
    was always looked upon as F1 territory and Indy Car racing viewed purely a domestic
    series.&nbsp; However, race fans around the world have begun to take notice
    of CART, and naturally the comparisons started.</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">The F1 crowd has always looked
    down their noses at Champ Car/Indy Car racing, perhaps they felt
    threatened.&nbsp; Maybe it was because Indy Car racing grew up out of the most powerful nation in the world, the USA.&nbsp; Maybe it's just
    human nature, but people like to take pot shots at the leader.&nbsp; Perhaps it is
    because F1 drivers have enjoyed better success in Indy Car racing than the
    reverse.&nbsp;</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">For years F1 drivers would come
    to America and cherry pick its greatest race, the Indy 500.&nbsp; However,
    it never was possible for American's to cherry pick a 'big' race
    in F1 because there isn't just one big race; you had to win the entire series and the world championship, a
    difficult feat indeed.&nbsp; Very few drivers have successfully competed in both CART and F1.&nbsp;</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial"> Generally drivers have enjoyed more success moving to CART after a successful career in F1, rather than the other way around.
    Three exceptions in recent years are 4-time CART/IndyCar champion (65, 66,
    69, 84), Indy 500 winner,
    and&nbsp; Daytona 500 winner Mario Andretti, who won the F1 World Driving
    Championship in 1978 driving for Lotus; 1995 CART champion Jacques Villeneuve, who won the 1997 F1 World Championship driving for
    Williams F1; and 1999 CART champion, and 2000 Indy 500 winner, Juan Pablo Montoya who has already achieved one win and
    numerous poles in his rookie F1 season.</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">Are F1 drivers better than Indy
    Car/Champ Car drivers?&nbsp; Are F1 cars better than Champ Cars?&nbsp;
    Perhaps that question will never be answered as long as CART and F1 are two separate
    series played before a worldwide arena, but I decided to do a comparison of
    the two series, one the pinnacle of open wheel racing in Europe, the other
    the pinnacle of open wheel racing in North America, and let you decide which
    is better.</font></p>
    <table border="1" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" width="100%" bordercolor="#CCCCCC">
      <tr>
        <td width="50%" align="center" bgcolor="#FF0000"><b><font size="2" face="Arial" color="#FFFFFF">Similarities</font></b></td>
        <td width="50%" align="center" bgcolor="#FF0000"><b><font size="2" face="Arial" color="#FFFFFF">Differences</font></b></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="100%" valign="top" colspan="2" align="center"><b><font size="2" face="Arial">General
          Philosophy of Competition</font></b></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="50%" valign="top" align="left">
          <p align="center"><a href="../../Images/2001Misc/ChampCar.jpg"><img border="0" src="../../Images/2001Misc/ChampCar_300.jpg" width="300" height="145"><br>
          </a><b><font size="2" face="Arial">Champ Car</font></b></p>
          <p align="center"><a href="../../Images/2001/F1/JaguarR2_02.jpg"><img border="0" src="../../Images/2001/F1/JaguarR2_02_300.jpg" width="300" height="120"><br>
          </a><b><font size="2" face="Arial">F1 Car</font></b></p>
          <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">F1 is there to show what is possible of a car manufacturer or a car builder. How far they can go with it,&nbsp;
          how much technology you can put into a car.&nbsp;
          CART is more about the sport of racing.&nbsp; There's less emphasis on
          technology and more emphasis on driver ability and teamwork</font></p>
        </td>
        <td width="50%" valign="top" align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">The
          regulations in F1 are more geared toward producing the fastest car man
          can create.&nbsp;&nbsp; In CART, the philosophy is more about getting the
          most out of a far more restrictive rule book, where races are won and
          lost on the race track and not in the design shop.&nbsp; In F1, the teams do everything in their power to find performance gains, constantly pushing the
          performance envelope higher, almost at an exponential rate. CART's regulations are relatively static and competing CART teams purchase engines and chassis from third party
          manufacturers where there are escalation caps for costs.&nbsp; There is less research and
          development in CART.
          However, each F1 team is required by the rulebook to design and development all aspects of their car. Very few elements of the car are purchased from third
          parties.&nbsp;&nbsp; This leads to greater innovation and diversity in the technology applied in
          F1.&nbsp; F1 has a total of 12 teams/car manufacturers competitively working on different solutions, compared with the 2 chassis and 3 engine suppliers in CART. This
          higher level of technology development in F1 requires larger teams with a
          wider range of diverse skills (to design, manufacturer, test and race
          the cars), and much larger budgets.&nbsp; Whereas a top 2-car CART
          team spends no more than $20 to 30 million per year, a F1 team can
          spend upwards of $200 million.</font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="100%" colspan="2" align="center"><font size="2" face="Arial"><b>The
          Chassis/Suspension/Brakes/Bodywork</b></font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">Appearance
          wise, modern F1 and Champ Cars are defined by their chassis. Both share the following characteristics:&nbsp;</font>
          <ul>
            <li><font size="2" face="Arial">They are single-seat cars&nbsp;</font></li>
            <li><font size="2" face="Arial">They have an open cockpit&nbsp;</font></li>
            <li><font size="2" face="Arial">They have open wheels -- there are no fenders covering the the wheels&nbsp;</font></li>
            <li><font size="2" face="Arial">They have wings at the front and rear of the car to provide downforce&nbsp;</font></li>
          </ul>
          <p><font size="2" face="Arial">They position the engine behind the driver's compartment
          (called the cockpit), and drives the two rear wheels only.&nbsp; The
          transmission sits behind the engine and directly forward of the rear
          wheels in a transverse arrangement.&nbsp; Both have front and rear
          wings for downforce that can't be adjusted by the driver during the
          race.&nbsp; In fact the driver can't adjust any surface of the car
          from the cockpit.&nbsp; Electronic measurement and data telemetry
          between the car and the pits is used to monitor and control car
          performance.&nbsp; Both use similar suspension geometries, i.e.
          pushrod actuated shock/spring combinations that lay horizontal in the
          car.&nbsp; Both use 4-wheel disc brakes with a set of calipers and
          brake pads per wheel</font></p>
          <p align="center"><a href="../../Images/2001Misc/ChampvsF1.jpg"><img border="0" src="../../Images/2001Misc/ChampvsF1_300.jpg" width="300" height="116"><br>
          </a><b><font size="2" face="Arial">In a sport where designers go
          through great pains to shave 5 or 10 lbs of weight from a car, an
          overweight Champ Car with driver weighs 1750 Lbs, some 400 Lbs more
          than a F1 car with driver</font></b></p>
        </td>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">Because Indy
          Car racing started out as strictly an oval racing series, the cars
          were constructed to deal with the higher speeds and G-forces that are generated on the banked oval
          circuits.&nbsp; With driver and fuel, Champ cars weigh approximately 800kg (1750 Lbs), some 200kg (400+ Lbs)
          heavier than F1 cars.&nbsp; F1 cars race only on
          road circuits, and don't crash into concrete walls too often, hence do
          not have to be as beefy so-to-speak.&nbsp;
          Champ cars are slightly longer and wider but have close to the same wheelbase.&nbsp;
          Because they are lighter, F1 cars are generally more nimble - quicker
          around corners, especially tight ones. This is primarily due to lower weight,
          but also to the fact their engines are V-10's that rev up faster, which helps
          them get up and out of a corner quicker.&nbsp; Champ Cars excel on the fast ovals. They have the advantage of additional traction and downforce because they employ ground effect aerodynamics in their underbodies and run on slick
          tires, both currently banned in F1.&nbsp; Champ cars use slick tires,
          F1 cars grooved tires (to limit cornering speeds).&nbsp; F1 allows
          expensive carbon fiber rotors on all circuits, CART only on high speed
          ovals (to reduce un-sprung weight)&nbsp; for better braking performance coming
          into pitlane from a very high speed.&nbsp; In F1, both team cars must have the same
          sponsor and look the same.&nbsp; In CART, sometimes the cars are
          decked out in the same livery from the same sponsor, but many times
          each car carries a different sponsor.&nbsp; F1 is very conscious of
          appearance and promoting the 'team' concept (like in most sports where
          the entire team wears the same uniform).</font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="100%" valign="top" colspan="2" align="center"><font size="2" face="Arial"><b>The
          Engines</b></font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">Like most
          racing series, the engines used in both series are 4-cycle internal combustion power plants.&nbsp; Both engines are in a 'V'
          configuration and employ multiple intake and exhaust valves per
          cylinder.&nbsp; </font><font size="2" face="Arial">Both engines use
          high grades of Aluminum and other exotic metals, but F1 is a lot more
          lenient in that regard.&nbsp; The engines and transmissions are
          stressed members, meaning they are like an extension of the
          chassis/tub, with the rear suspension mounted to them and transmitting
          torsional, shear and bending forces from one corner of the car to the
          other.&nbsp; Fuel and air are
          delivered by means of fuel injection systems that are controlled by
          sophisticated computerized engine management systems.&nbsp; In F1,
          engine manufacturers are more aligned with specific teams, and in some
          cases, such as Ferrari and Toyota, the team produces both the car and
          the engine.&nbsp; In CART all engines are leased from the three
          (formerly four) engine manufacturers (or at least they
          will be leased up through 2002.&nbsp; The rules after that have yet to
          be announced).</font>
          <p align="center"><img border="0" src="../../Images/2001Misc/Radiator.jpg" width="315" height="242"><br>
          <font size="2" face="Arial"> Both engines are water cooled with the water and oil
          radiators mounted in the sidepods of the car.&nbsp;</font></p>
        </td>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">Champ car
          engines are limited to 2.65 Liter capacity, F1 3.0 Liter. Champ cars are
          limited to 4-valves per cylinder, F1 cars 5. F1 cars run on low-lead
          high-octane gasoline (petrol), whereas Champ Cars run on high-octane
          Methanol.&nbsp; Champ car engines produce up to 900HP, F1 engines
          currently up to 850 HP.&nbsp; However, because Champ Car engines are
          turbocharged , they would produce well over 1,000 HP if not for almost
          annual reductions in turbocharger boost pressure, i.e. the the force
          by which the air and fuel mixture is forced into the combustion
          chambers. F1 engines are naturally aspirated, meaning the air and fuel
          is not forced into the engines, but sucked into the cylinders by the
          vacuum that is created when a piston is in a downward stoke while the
          intake valves are open. </font><font size="2" face="Arial">The HP
          output is similar from both engines, but the Champ cars do it at a
          lower RPM (16,500 to 17,000 RPM) than F1 engines which peak at around
          18,500 RPM.&nbsp; Because F1 cars have 10 cylinders to a Champ Cars 8
          cylinders, the mass of the F1 engines pistons and pushrods are
          smaller, hence they can rev faster and higher (ask any engine
          designer, the lighter the pistons the higher they can rev them w/o the
          pistons/rods/crank self destructing). In CART the fuel tank can hold a maximum of 35 US gallons, while in F1 onboard fuel capacity is theoretically
          unlimited.&nbsp; It's up to the design team to determine which is more
          important, the added weight of carrying more fuel, or the reduction in
          the number of pit stops afforded by a bigger fuel tank.</font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="100%" valign="top" colspan="2" align="center"><font size="2" face="Arial"><b>Transmissions</b></font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">The
          transmissions in both cars sit just in front of the rear wheel;
          centerline in a transverse configuration.&nbsp; There is no
          limit to the number of forward gears on either car and both must have
          a reverse gear.&nbsp; The exception to this being that CART does not
          require a reverse gear on oval tracks.&nbsp; F1 transmissions
          typically have 7 forward gears and Champ cars 6 or 7.&nbsp; The driver
          never uses the clutch on upshifts in either series, but in CART some
          drivers do use it for downshifts.&nbsp; Both cars are capable of
          standing starts, but a F1 car has what is called launch control, to
          help get the car off the line with a minimum of wheel spin.</font></td>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">Gear shifts in
          CART are done semi-manually.&nbsp; The gearbox is a sequential unit
          where the driver shifts by pulling back on the gear shift lever each
          time he wants to go up a gear, and forward on the lever to shift down
          a gear.&nbsp; Champ cars do employ shift-without-lift technology
          meaning a driver never has to lift off the accelerator while changing
          gears while up-shifting. A sensor on the gear shift mechanism knows
          when the driver is pushing or pulling on the lever and it sends a
          signal to the cars engine management system to reduce power for a
          split second between gears.&nbsp; Did you know that F1 transmissions
          are so sophisticated they shift themselves? It's more than just your
          regular automatic transmission though.&nbsp; The cars go out on
          Friday, pass a beacon transponder at the S/F line (which synchronizes
          everything) and do a few laps in anger with the driver shifting the
          transmission with the paddle levers.&nbsp; From the data collected
          from those few laps, the computers, with the map of the circuit in
          memory, knowing the speed of the car, the throttle percentage, brake
          pedal pressure, etc, will automatically shift the transmission the
          rest of the weekend.&nbsp; The driver just sits back, turns the wheel
          and brakes and accelerates.&nbsp; Fascinating technology, but how long
          before we no longer need the driver?</font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="100%" valign="top" colspan="2" align="center"><font size="2" face="Arial"><b>Fuel</b></font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">Both cars carries fuel in a fuel cell located behind the driver. This cell is made of a flexible Kevlar and polymer material -- it is more like a bag than a tank. Inside the bag is a sponge-like substance that gives the bag its shape. The bag is designed to withstand a crash without rupturing -- rather than rupturing, it flexes and changes its shape. The idea behind the sponge is to hold the fuel so that, in a severe crash, it does not spray over the driver, other cars or the track.&nbsp;
          Whereas a Champ Cars fuel cell can hold no more than 35 gallons, there
          is no such restriction in F1.&nbsp; The exact size of a F1 fuel cell
          is a highly guarded secret.</font></td>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">Champ Cars burn methanol fuel. Methanol is a form of alcohol and has several advantages over gasoline in an engine.  Methanol can run at much higher compression ratios, meaning that you can get more power from the engine on each piston stroke.  Methanol provides significant cooling when it evaporates in the cylinder, helping to keep the high-revving, high-compression engine from overheating. Methanol, unlike gasoline, can be extinguished with water if there is a fire. This provides a nice safety feature. The ignition temperature for methanol (the temperature at which it starts burning) is much higher than it is for gasoline, so the risk of an accidental fire is lower.&nbsp;</font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="100%" valign="top" colspan="2" align="center"><font size="2" face="Arial"><b>Pitstops
          and Refueling</b></font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">Both series use
          pitstops to add fuel to the car and change tires if needed.&nbsp; Both
          use single probe refueling devices.&nbsp; The pitlane has a speed
          limit in both series for the safety of the crewman on pitlane
          servicing the cars.</font>
          <p align="center"><a href="../../Images/2001/CART/Mexico/P0002348.JPG"><img border="0" src="../../Images/2001/CART/Mexico/P0002348_200.JPG" width="200" height="134"><br>
          </a><b><font size="2" face="Arial">Both series use single-point
          refueling probe</font></b></p>
        </td>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">F1 allows an
          unlimited number of pit crew members over the wall during
          pitstops.&nbsp; CART allows just 6 (one at each corner of the car to
          change tires, one refueler and one air jack man).&nbsp; Champ cars
          have onboard air actuated jacks that lift the car with pressurized air
          during pitstops, whereas F1 cars are lifted with hand jacks , one
          front and one back&nbsp; Fuel is fed into Champ cars by gravity,
          whereas in F1 it is forced into the car under pressure.&nbsp; Whereas
          a Champ car must get 1.85 miles per gallon, a F1 car has no such fuel restrictions.&nbsp;
          Hence F1 pitstop strategy is usually made by how much weight you are
          willing to carry vs. the time you gain or lose with more or less
          pitstops; whereas in CART, conserving fuel is the name of the
          game.&nbsp; In F1 the driver usually drives 100% the whole race.&nbsp;
          In CART it's usually a fuel conservation contest until the last
          pitstop.&nbsp; Each car in a Champ Car race has its own pit crew,
          whereas in F1 the same crew usually services both team cars, though
          usually never on the same lap.</font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="100%" valign="top" colspan="2" align="center"><font size="2" face="Arial"><b>Tires</b></font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">Both series run
          wide, soft compound tires.&nbsp; The rear tires are wider than the
          front tires because they put the engine power down to the track
          surface.</font>
          <p align="center"><img border="0" src="../../Images/2001/F1/Tire_grooved.jpg" width="122" height="100">&nbsp;
          <img border="0" src="../../Images/2001Misc/Tire_slick.jpg" width="116" height="100"><br>
          <b><font size="2" face="Arial">Grooved F1 tire left, slick Champ Car
          tire right</font></b></p>
        </td>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">With slick tires even if the car is not perfect, you can get a really good lap out of the car. You can
          over push the car a little bit. With the grooved
          tires in the Formula One, if the setup is wrong, the tires grain
          quickly and you lose grip really quick, so you need to have a really nice balance in the car so it works together with you. It's a bit more complex.</font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="100%" valign="top" colspan="2" align="center"><font size="2" face="Arial"><b>Driver
          Skill</b></font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">The cars in
          both series are hard to drive because they are so fast.&nbsp; A good
          driver can do well in both series, but there are some subtle
          differences.&nbsp; Because CART races on four different types of
          circuits, it requires a bit more diversity to win consistently.&nbsp;
          Because F1 cars are much lighter, everything happens much faster than
          in a Champ Car.&nbsp; Hence F1 tends to be a young mans game, where by
          35 years old your career is probably done.&nbsp; In CART, you won't
          win on the road courses too often once you reach 35 years old, but
          your oval track successes can continue well into your 40's.&nbsp;
          Ovals are less busy, hence driver hand and foot movement takes a back
          seat to smooth driving, i.e. as little steering correction as possible.</font></td>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">Juan Montoya
          best described it recently - &quot;Mentally in F1
          a driver must be a bit stronger. You've got to be a lot stronger
          mentally in F1 than
          in CART because in CART the atmosphere is a lot more friendly.
          Driving the car itself is very similar. In the car you've got to push
          it to its limits.&nbsp; Both cars are very physical.&nbsp; Outside the
          car is very different.&nbsp; In CART everybody talks to everybody, everybody is friendly.
          In F1 you don't even cross a word with anybody. You're there by yourself, and you've got to work with the
          team around you.&quot;&nbsp; However, CART's oval races do tend to require a sort of
          mental toughness in their own right - the speeds are higher and the
          dangerous concrete walls are always there.&nbsp; Driving around in circles can
          almost be hypnotic at times, one lapse in concentration can result in
          a crash.</font></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="100%" valign="top" colspan="2" align="center"><b><font size="2" face="Arial">Races</font></b></td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">Except for
          CART's 500 mile events, races in both series last about 2-hours
          long.&nbsp; Both series start from 22 to 28 cars per race, and of
          course the object of the game is to get to the finish line first.&nbsp;
          Both series race in Europe, Japan, Australia, Canada, the USA and
          Brazil. F1 races are primarily based in Europe, CART in the USA.&nbsp;
          Both series are equally international, and both are recognized as World
          Championship Series by the FIA.&nbsp; While overtaking in both series
          on road courses is difficult, you tend to see more overtaking in CART
          than in F1.&nbsp; Part of this can be attributed to the longer braking
          distances in CART because the cars are much heavier and use steel
          brake rotors. Whereas the extra 400+ Lbs of weight works against a
          Champ Car on a road course in terms of sheer performance, it helps to
          make the races a bit more entertaining to the fans because longer
          braking distances increase the chance for overtaking.&nbsp; Since most
          of the passing on road courses is done under braking, the longer brake
          zones help.</font></td>
        <td width="50%" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Arial">F1 allows
          almost unlimited off-season testing.&nbsp; In CART, to save cost, each
          team gets a very limited number of test days, and they must pick and
          choose when and where they test wisely. F1 races 16 or 17 times per
          year, CART 20 to 22.&nbsp;&nbsp; F1 races start
          from a complete stop, sort of like a drag race, in a staggered 2-row
          formation.&nbsp; Champ car races
          begin from a rolling start in rows of 2.&nbsp; To qualify for a F1 race your time
          must be within 107% of pole position time.&nbsp; In CART it is 110%.&nbsp;
          Both series use Friday's of each weekend&nbsp; as purely a practice
          day. Drivers practice again on Saturday morning and qualify on
          Saturday afternoons. On road courses both series hold similar
          qualifying sessions, whereby a driver's best lap during that 30 minute
          (CART) or 1-hour (F1) session counts as their qualifying time.&nbsp;
          In CART, when they race on ovals, the drivers gets only two qualifying
          laps, the best one counts and it's done with no other cars on the
          track.&nbsp; On road courses, qualifying is done with many cars on the
          track at the same time.&nbsp; Getting a clear gap in traffic to turn a
          hot lap can sometimes be hit or miss.&nbsp; Drivers only earn F1 championship points for the top 6
          finishing positions 10-6-4-3-2-1 in the race.&nbsp; In CART the top 12 finishers
          score points 20-16-14-12-10-8-6-5-4-3-2-1, plus 1 point goes to the
          driver who wins the pole, and 1 point to the driver who leads the most
          laps during the race.&nbsp;&nbsp;</font></td>
      </tr>
    </table>
    <p align="left">
    <font size="2" face="Arial">With $200 million per year team budgets
    (compared to CART's $25 to 30 million), F1 represents the pinnacle of
    motorsports.&nbsp; However, when it comes to bang-for-the-buck, CART
    delivers more in the way of entertainment to the paying customer - the fans,
    by virtue of its better on-track presentation, i.e. passing and
    wheel-to-wheel, side-by-side action.&nbsp; Perhaps a British journalist put it best after this years
    inaugural CART race in Rockingham, England.&nbsp; Andrew Baker wrote in
      the London Daily Telegram
      &quot;YEARS from now, graying petrolheads will bore their grandchildren with tales of the day at Rockingham Motor Speedway that changed British motor racing for good.&quot;</font>
    </p>
