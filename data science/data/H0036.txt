  <td width=900 style='width:675.0pt;padding:.75pt .75pt .75pt .75pt'>
  <p class=MsoNormal style='mso-margin-top-alt:auto;mso-margin-bottom-alt:auto'><b><span
  lang=EN-GB style='font-size:24.0pt;font-family:Arial;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The
  Origin of Birds.<br>
  </span></b><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>A theory on
  the origin of birds, and how they obtained the ability to fly.</span></b><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='font-family:Arial;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>By
  Svend Palm. </span><span style='font-family:Arial'>Lindbjergvej 13, DK 2750
  Ballerup, Denmark.</span><br>
  <a href="..\..\..\..\MIDLERTIDIG\palm@post7.tele.dk"><span lang=DE
  style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:Arial;mso-ansi-language:DE'>E-mail:
  palm@post7, tele.dk</span></a><span lang=DE style='mso-ansi-language:DE'><o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The birds originated
  from small bipedal, ground-living archosaurs, which evolved the anatomical,
  physiological, and behavioural preconditions for flying as an adaptation to
  climb and descend from tree-trunks. The habit of flapping the forelimbs,
  which initiated the ability to fly, originated before the wings had arisen.</span></b><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:18.0pt;font-family:Arial;mso-ansi-language:
  EN-GB'>FOREWORD</span><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>.<br>
  In 1860 was in Solnhofen in the southern Germany discovered the most
  significant fossil of the vertebrate evolution. It was </span><a
  href="http://odur.let.rug.nl/~nieuwlnd/"><i><span lang=EN-GB
  style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Archaeopteryx</span></i></a><i><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>,</span></i><span lang=EN-GB
  style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> dated to Upper Jurassic and thus about 150
  million years old. It has until now been discovered 7 specimens of this fossil.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><i><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Archaeopteryx</span></i><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> was unmistakably a winged and
  feathered, two legged creature, and as its similarity to two-legged dinosaurs
  is apparent it is obvious that birds and dinosaurs are closely related. No
  doubt was <i>Archaeopteryx</i> able to fly, of which its well-developed wings
  were evidence. And as feathers and wings must have taken a very long time to
  evolve, and as <i>Archaeopteryx </i>is likely not to have been the only one
  of its kind, it can safely be concluded that at that time existed feathered,
  flying bird-like vertebrates, closely related to dinosaurs. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>In the following years
  were nevertheless a great number of theories</span><a
  href="http://odur.let.rug.nl/~nieuwlnd/palm"><span lang=EN-GB
  style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>*</span></a><span lang=EN-GB
  style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>) forwarded on the evolutionary origin of
  birds and on the relation of birds to dinosaurs, and for the time being is a popular
  theory that birds <i>are</i> dinosaurs and originate from two legged ones
  which ran on the ground and evolved wings and then began to fly. According to
  this theory feathers are not a unique bird trait, and it is claimed that the
  dinosaurs in general were feathered. The only support for this bizarre
  statement is that some dinosaur-like fossils of China, discovered in the
  1990-th, had feathers. </span><a
  href="http://www.peabody.yale.edu/exhibits/cfd/CFDclado.html"><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>**</span></a><span lang=EN-GB
  style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>)<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>A weakness &#8211; to put it
  gently &#8211; of that theory is that the Chinese fossils are from Cretaceous, more
  than 25 million years younger than <i>Archaeopteryx</i>, and were most
  probably ancestors of archaeopteryx-relatives from Jurassic or earlier, which
  have ceased to fly and reduced their wings and plumage just as ostriches,
  emus, kiwis and other modern ratites later on have done. They provide no
  evidence what so ever about the bird/dinosaur relation and nothing can be
  concluded from them about the dinosaurs being feathered.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Another weakness of the
  idea of the dinosaurs as feathered is that no fossils of dinosaurs have ever
  been discovered with even traces of feathers. The assertion that the
  dinosaurs generally were feathered is with no foundation whatsoever. Until
  furthered evidence feathers must be considered as an exclusive bird-quality
  not to be found in any other group of vertebrates. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Another point is how to
  explain how two legged; dinosaur-like bird-ancestors which lived on the
  ground could possibly have evolved wings and the ability to fly.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>In 1989 I published a
  theory in Danish &#8220;<i>Fuglene &#8211; sådan opstod de!</i>&#8221; and in 1997 in English &#8220;</span><a
  href="http://home13.inet.tele.dk./palm/birdweb.htm"><i><span lang=EN-GB
  style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The Origin of Flapping Flight in Birds</span></i></a><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>.&#8221; The latter is in full to be
  found at the Internet. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The following is a
  resumption of this theory.<o:p></o:p></span></p>

  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:18.0pt;font-family:Arial;mso-ansi-language:
  EN-GB'>THE PROBLEM.</span><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><br>
  </span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Evolution is by a
  step-by-step process, any step more advantageous than the previous one. But
  birds&#8217; origin and evolution presents a problem: What<span
  style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>adaptive purpose have undeveloped
  wings in a non-flying pre-bird? And how could a pre-bird with underdeveloped
  wings achieve the ability to fly? <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>If pre-birds lived in
  trees they might have evolved wings to parachute and glide like &#8220;flying&#8221;
  frogs, squirrels and lizards. But in this case it is hard to comprehend why
  such an uncomplicated airborne locomotion is replaced by the complicated
  flapping flight of real birds! <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Moreover it is an even
  greater puzzle how a bipedal, long-legged bird-ancestor, which obviously was
  not a tree-dweller, possibly could have evolved wings and ability of flight.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><i><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The only logical answer
  to that is that the bird-ancestors were not really tree-dwellers and evolved
  the requisites of flight for flying, for another purpose than flying.</span></i><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>In the following it set
  up a comprehensive theory based on known facts and accepted principles of
  evolution. The soundness of such a theory depends on its ability to explain
  the origin of the prerequisites mentioned. And it should serve as an
  acceptable explanation of other birds&#8217; characteristics.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:18.0pt;font-family:Arial;mso-ansi-language:
  EN-GB'>THE THEORY.</span><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><br>
  </span></b><b><i><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:13.5pt;mso-ansi-language:
  EN-GB'>The prerequisites of flapping flight</span></i></b><b><i><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>.</span></i></b><span lang=EN-GB
  style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Before they could
  initiate flight the bird-ancestors must have acquired the necessary
  fundamental anatomy, behaviour, and aerodynamic qualities to perform at least
  some degree of airborne locomotion: <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>1.</span></b><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> <b>Anatomical prerequisites:</b>
  A breast-skeleton and a musculature that enabled them to stretch the
  forelimbs sideways out and to move them in rotating up- and downswings. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>2.</span></b><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> <b>Behavioral prerequisites:</b>
  A habit to <i>stretch</i> the forelimbs and to move them <i>simultaneously</i>
  in a flapping manner. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>3.</span></b><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> <b>The aerodynamic prerequisites:
  </b>An<b> </b>airfoil fit for aerodynamic purpose.<o:p></o:p></span></p>

  <p><b><i><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:13.5pt;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Anatomical

  EN-GB'><br>
  </span></i></b><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The breast
  girdle.</span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The fundamental structure
  of birds shows that they descend from four legged vertebrates, and the bird&#8217;s
  eggs is the evidence that these must have been reptiles, most likely some that
  resemble the old-fashioned lizard from isles ashore of New Zealand, the
  tuatara, <i>Sphenodon.</i> Hence this reptile can represent ancient
  four-legged bird-ancestors, as well for the general appearance as for its
  manner of locomotion.<o:p></o:p></span></p>

  blade is broad and fuses with the coracoid. The collarbones in <i>Sphenodon</i>
  fuse with the anchor-shaped middle-breastbone, (ic) into a Y-shaped
  structure.<b> </b><o:p></o:p></span></p>

  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The manner of walking
  of the four-footed bird-ancestors.<br>
  </span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The four-footed
  bird-ancestors moved in the same straddling manner as four-footed reptiles:
  They lift the forepart of body off the ground and move the limbs in
  horizontal swings, which they performed by three muscle movements: <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <ul type=disc>
   <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-margin-top-alt:auto;mso-margin-bottom-alt:
       auto;mso-list:l0 level1 lfo3;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span lang=EN-GB
       style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The body is lifted by the breast muscles
       (pm), which depress the sideway-projecting upper arms and press the
       forearms against the ground.<o:p></o:p></span></li>
   <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-margin-top-alt:auto;mso-margin-bottom-alt:
       auto;mso-list:l0 level1 lfo3;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span lang=EN-GB
       style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>This pair of muscles is attached to the
       breastbones and collarbones and insert on the near part of the upper arm
       bone.<o:p></o:p></span></li>
   <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-margin-top-alt:auto;mso-margin-bottom-alt:
       auto;mso-list:l0 level1 lfo3;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span lang=EN-GB
       style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The elbows are flexed by the upper arm
       muscles (bb), which originate from the tuber of the coracoid, beneath to
       the shoulder joint and the distal parts of the upper arm bones and
       insert on the lower arm bone, radius. <o:p></o:p></span></li>
   <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-margin-top-alt:auto;mso-margin-bottom-alt:
       auto;mso-list:l0 level1 lfo3;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span lang=EN-GB
       style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The forelimbs are moved forward in
       sideway swings by the small breast muscles. These muscles are attached
       to the coracoid in front of the tuber and insert on the near parts of
       the upper arm bone. <o:p></o:p></span></li>
  </ul>
  <p><i><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Thus the breastbone
  and the collarbones played an important part in the four-legged reptiles
  bird-ancestor's mode of locomotion</span></i><span lang=EN-GB
  style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:13.5pt;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The
  bipedal bird-ancestors.</span></b><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:
  EN-GB'><br>
  </span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The four-footed
  bird ancestors, which were common ancestors of birds and dinosaurs, evolved the
  bipedal stance, why their pectoral skeleton was generally reduced. But before
  the original breast-skeleton was reduced some small bipedal archosaurs, which
  had evolved digitigrady and long metatarsals, initiated the habit of climbing
  tree-trunks to stay in the tree-crowns as a supplement to their ground-living
  manner of life. Those were the birds&#8217; ancestors, <i>the pro-avians</i>. <b>(</b>See<b>
  </b></span><a href="http://home13.inet.tele.dk./palm/dinweb.htm"><b><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Dinosaurs Origin and Extinction.</span></b></a><b><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>)</span></b><span lang=EN-GB
  style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><i><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:13.5pt;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The
  scenario.</span></i></b><b><i><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><br>
  </span></i></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The bipedal
  pro-avians must have initiated the climbing activities in the Triassic when
  the trees were ferns and gymnosperms of which the 2 - 3 m tall un-branched,
  palm-like cycadoids were abundant. The evolution of the ground-living
  pre-birds into flying birds should therefore have taken place in a landscape
  with cycadoids in the flat crowns of which the pre-birds could reside.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The tree mounting
  pre-birds. <br>
  </span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The mounting of
  trees by was performed as the pre-birds pushed themselves upwards by the hind
  legs, walking by moving one leg at the time, while hooking to the tree-trunk
  with the claws of the forward toes. Not to tumble over in behind they clang
  to the tree with the forelimbs. Due to the long hind legs and the saggital
  stance, the body was held in an oblique angle to the tree-trunk.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>This forelimb clinging
  was performed by the claws of the hands that were outwardly rotated and
  pulled strongly towards each other, interlocking the claws into cracks. The
  forelimbs were moved <i>simultaneously</i> in lateral swings to take higher
  grips. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The descend from the
  trees must have taken place in a head-down manner, like that of modern
  tree-living reptiles and squirrels, and adherence to the tree was performed
  with claws of the reversed first toes. And in down descend the pre-birds
  should cling to the tree with the claws of the outstretched forelimbs and <i>posteriorly</i>
  rotated hands. </span>See fig 5. </p>

  <p><b><i><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:13.5pt;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The
  anatomical prerequisites.</span></i></b><b><i><span lang=EN-GB
  style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><br>
  </span></i></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The hooking
  grip of the forelimbs of a pre-bird which climbed a tree-trunk in this manner
  was performed by the pectoral muscles which were enforced as the ability of
  climbing was improved. An adaptation to the tree-mounting behaviour thus gave
  a particular function not even to the forelimbs, but also to these muscles.
  The reduction of the breastbone and the collarbones, which took place in the
  dinosaurs, was therefore prevented in these archosaurs. The broad breastbone
  and the well-developed collarbone, the &#8220;wish-bone&#8221;, was therefore maintained
  as a characteristic trait of the bird- skeleton. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The tree-mounting
  behaviour of the pre-birds was also responsible for the anatomical change of
  the wrist and hand as the particular manner of climbing required a flex of
  the wrist only in the lateral plane, posteriorly in the ulnar direction.
  Accordingly, the power of rotation of the hands, the supination, was
  restricted, and the carpals coalesced into a strong, simplified wrist-joint.
  This anatomical structure is also a characteristic of birds, and also to be
  recognized in the forelimbs of <i>Archaeopteryx</i> and in the coelurosaurs
  of Cretaceous.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Adaptation to the
  tree-climbing behaviour required an increased power of adhesion, which caused
  greater movability in the palmar plane of the first digit, the thumb. In the
  upward climbing this digit should be directed forwardly whereas the other
  fingers should be outwardly directed. In the downward climbing the first
  digit should be directed outwardly and the other fingers directed backwards.
  This manner of climbing caused that the second and the third metacarpals and
  digits were elongated and coalesced and shaped the hand into a long,
  double-pointed hook furnished with strong, hook-shaped claws. </span>See fig.
  5.</p>

  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>When the pre-birds
  descended in the head-down manner, it had to use the claw of the first toe as
  a prehensile hook. This toe, which was about to be reduced and reversed in
  the dinosaurs, therefore was retained and had renewed function, as the
  characteristic bird-hind toe. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The adaptation to the
  tree-mounting behaviour thus supplied the bipedal pre-birds with the
  anatomical prerequisites for evolution of powered flight. They had a pectoral
  skeleton with a broad sternum and an collar-bone structure to which the
  strong pectoral muscles adhere. These muscles perform in birds the
  wing-strokes in flapping flight. Besides, the tree-mounting behaviour
  transformed the coracoid with the biceps tubercle situated in front of the
  shoulder joint and the site of the small breast muscle (su) so that it
  performs the forward pull of the upper arm around the biceps tubercle as a
  pulley. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Due to the oblique
  orientation of the body axis of the down-jumping pre-bird the movement that
  the small breast muscle (su) performed became an almost upward directed
  movement, homologous with the upstroke in flying birds. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:18.0pt;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The
  physiological consequences of tree mounting.</span></b><b><span lang=EN-GB
  style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><br>
  The purpose of tree mounting.<br>
  </span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>It appears from
  the shape of <i>Archaeopteryx </i>and modern birds that the pre-birds
  retained the bipedal, long legged stance with the legs beneath the body. This
  indicates that the pre-birds did not live in the treetops, but stuck to a
  ground-living life style. Most likely as they lived on insects and other
  small prey, which they found on the ground, and only occasionally, climbed
  the cycadees, perhaps to escape from predators or to bask in the sunny
  treetops.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>As the tree-mounting
  behaviour must have lasted for millions of generations to cause the said
  anatomical changes, it must have served the most beneficial purpose: The
  survival of most offspring.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The reptilian bipedal,
  ground-living bird-ancestors may originally have laid their eggs on the
  ground in heaps of decayed vegetables as most reptiles do. If the pre-birds
  brought this breeding habit up to the flat crowns of cycadoids, their eggs
  and hatchlings were protected from ground-living egg-robbers and predators,
  and thus increased the chance of their offspring to obtain maturity greatly
  and thus increased their numbers much more than those of their fellows, which
  did not climb the trees and had to keep laying their legs on the ground.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Rise of
  body-temperature.<br>
  </span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>However
  advantageous the residence in the flat crowns of cycadoids were, it had the
  disadvantage that the pre-birds and their hatchlings were exposed to sun
  heating for longer periods with only little possibility to seek shade unless
  they left their safe place.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>A rather simple
  precaution to sustain this sun heating was to raise the temperature tolerance
  and the optimal body-temperature as much as possible, which explains the high
  internal temperature of about 40<sup>o</sup> - 42<sup>o</sup> C. of birds in
  general. Warm-bloodedness and an increased metabolism might have evolved subsequently
  as a means to maintain this optimal internal temperature when the pre-birds
  had to leave the treetops for food seeking in shady undergrowth. An increase
  of the metabolic rate also might have been evolutionarily enforced as an
  increase of the activity level of the pre-birds in general. (See<b><u> </u></b></span><a
  href="http://home13.inet.tele.dk./palm/warmweb.htm"><b><span lang=EN-GB
  style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Warm-blooded Dinosaurs?</span></b></a><b><span
  lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>) </span></b><span lang=EN-GB
  style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Respiratory system and
  air sacs.<br>
  </span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>As a means to
  obtain internal cooling by ventilation and to maintain the optimal internal
  temperature, the peculiar air sac- and respiratory system of birds evolved in
  the pre-birds. By this manner of cooling a possibility of temperature
  regulation, necessary for warm-bloodedness, was possible.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The high
  body-temperature, the high metabolic rate and the warm-bloodedness in birds
  can thus be explained as an adaptation to an obligatory residence of the
  pre-birds in the sun-exposed crowns of cycadoids for protection of broods. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><i><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:13.5pt;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The
  feathers.</span></i></b><b><i><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><br>
  </span></i></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Another means
  to stand the sun-exposure were the feathers, which evolved from knobbed or
  plated scales through stages correspondent to the development of feathers.
  The earliest proto-feathers might then have been protuberances, which gave
  the pre-birds at least <i>some</i> protection against sun-radiation and
  increased their tolerance to the sun-exposure. Gradually, the protection was
  greater as the protuberances evolved into quills and at last evolved into
  feathers. </span>See fig. 7.</p>

  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The special benefit of
  this kind of dermal protection - besides from its lightness and economy of material
  - is that it shades against the sunrays without retarding radiation from the
  skin. Depending on posture, the proto-feathers could cause shade or sun close
  to the animal&#8217;s skin, and this effect was refined by the evolution of the
  dermal musculature that deliberately erects or compresses the feathers. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>As plumage must be
  carefully tended to be effective for thermo-insulation, the pre-birds
  simultaneously evolved the habit of plumage care.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The evolution of the
  feathers in the first place was is thus an adaptation to obligatory residence
  in the sun-exposed crowns of cycadoids; later on the feathers were an
  indispensable pre-requisite of flight.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:18.0pt;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The
  behavioural prerequisites.</span></b><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:
  EN-GB'><br>
  The forelimb movements.<br>
  </span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>When the
  pre-birds mounted the trunk of the cycads and when they descended, step by
  step, they <i>simultaneously</i> made two movements by the forelimbs:<br>
  <br>
  1.An embracing grip-movement by which the outstretched forelimbs, retaining
  the hooking grip, simultaneously were carried towards each other and to the
  rear.<br>
  <br>
  2. A recovery movement by which the forelimbs released the hooking grip and
  in a fast-forward swing carried the forearms forward to take a new grip. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><i><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>These movements are
  completely the same as the flapping movements which flying birds perform and a
  prerequisite of flapping flight.</span></i><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:
  EN-GB'><o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The jump descent.<br>
  </span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The climbing
  pre-bird descended in the head-down manner, but the last part of the way down
  it might have jumped down from the side of the tree-trunk in such a manner
  that it landed on its feet. To do that it made a vertical onset by the hind
  limbs and as it released the hooking grip performed made a fierce downward
  pull by the of the forelimbs. The united effect of these movements was a
  rotation of the body so that the pre-bird hit the ground feet first, and
  could be buffer the impact of the jump by the outstretched hind limbs. </span>See
  fig. 8.</p>

  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:18.0pt;font-family:Arial;mso-ansi-language:
  EN-GB'>The flight.<br>
  </span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Birds&#8217; flight can
  be distinguished in two moments, <i>elevation</i> and <i>thrust </i>of which
  elevation is the ability to stay unsupported in the air, and thrust is the
  ability to move forward in the air with no connection to the ground. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The power of elevation in
  birds must have evolved gradually through the stages:<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <ul type=disc>
   <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-margin-top-alt:auto;mso-margin-bottom-alt:
       auto;mso-list:l1 level1 lfo6;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span lang=EN-GB
       style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>1. <i>No</i> means to reduce the
       velocity of a fall from a level.<br>
       2. <i>Some</i> means deliberately to reduce the velocity of a fall.<br>
       3. A means to <i>stay </i>at a level in the air.<br>
       4. An ability to <i>lift</i> from the ground.<o:p></o:p></span></li>
  </ul>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The power of thrust must
  have evolved as an increased remedy to move forward during the fall in the
  air.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Elevation.<br>
  </span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>When a pre-bird
  in jump-descent from the side of the tree-trunk made a down stroke by its
  outstretched forelimbs, an upward force of the air resistance which reduced
  the velocity of the fall met these. That means that the down stroke of the
  forelimbs had <i>some</i> elevating effect, and that the impact of the jump
  was reduced and lesser dangerous. The pre-birds were therefore motivated to
  maintain and develop the habit of performing the strokes. As this effect
  increases by square the velocity of the down strokes, the pre-birds were
  further motivated to make faster and more vigorous down-strokes during the
  jump.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Any enlargement of the
  area of the forelimbs, which means that even a rim of small feathers on the
  border of the forelimb increased the effect, also increases the elevating
  effect of the strokes. Therefore it was thus an evolutionary motivation on
  the down-jumping pre-birds to evolve enlarged feathers into remiges.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The thrust.<br>
  </span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Even if down
  strokes with the unfeathered forelimbs during the jump hardly had any propulsive
  effect, just a rim of small feathers on the rear border of the forelimbs
  should have some propulsive effect. In down-strokes the air pressure bent the
  feathers obliquely upwards and caused a forward air pressure that threw the
  animal forward just like a propeller of an airplane. In the upstrokes the
  feathers were bent obliquely downwards, and a similar forward, propelling,
  air pressure was created.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>This effect
  evolutionarily bridged the slope between pre-birds with no propulsive ability
  and those with some propulsive ability.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:18.0pt;font-family:Arial;mso-ansi-language:
  EN-GB'>CONCLUSION.</span><b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><br>
  </span></b><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The particular
  manner of flight in birds, the flapping of feathered forelimbs, is thus the
  end of a gradual evolution of small two-legged, ground-living archosaurs
  which at an early stage, when the pectoral girdle was unreduced, took to
  climb the trunks of the abundant trees of that times, the cycads, to seek
  protection for their broods in the tree-tops. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
  <p><span lang=EN-GB style='mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>The climbing behaviour
  maintained and developed the particular anatomical prerequisites, and the
  movements of the forelimbs the behavioural prerequisites. The aero dynamical
  prerequisites, the feathered wings, evolved as a means to safer down jumps
  from the feathers which originated as a means to sustain the sun heating in
  the tree-tops. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
