In contrast, the mobility of single strands can vary considerably as a result of only small changes in nucleotide sequence. This fact led to the development of single-stranded conformation polymorphism (<strong>SSCP</strong>) techniques.					</ul>				</td>				<td><img src="i111291.gif" width="233" height="76" align="right" usemap="#"><map name=""></map></td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td><img src="i111292.gif" width="238" height="71"></td>				<td>					<ul>						<li>Most SSCP protocols are designed to analyze the polymorphism at single loci. To this end a specific pair of PCR primers bracketing the target region is used to amplify DNA from individuals.						<li>Single-stranded DNA is produced by asymmetric PCR, in which one primer is present in excess over the other. After the low concentration primer is used up, the reaction continues, producing only the target strand.					</ul>				</td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td>					<ul>						<li>The mobilities of single strands are compared by gel electrophoresis.					</ul>				</td>				<td>					<center>						<img src="i111293.gif" width="182" height="75"></center>				</td>			</tr>		</table>		</h4>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other </a><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Interp"></a>Interpretations</h4>		<ul>			<li>Single-stranded DNA mobility is sequence dependent because of varying degrees of intrastrand base pairing and the resulting looping and compaction.			<li>SSCP's are <a href="MG11121.html">codominant</a> markers, like <a href="MG11124.html">RFLP</a>'s. Codominance results when the polymorphism is due to sequences within the amplified region. In this regard the technique differs from allele-specific PCR in which primers are designed to amplify from one allele but not from the other.			<li>Precise knowledge of the sequence polymorphism is not necessary. Indeed, polymorphisms at any of several sequence positions may be detectable with <a href="http://www.bioproducts.com/technical/sscpanalysiswithmdegelsolution.shtml">SSCP</a>.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Info"></a>Further information</h4>		<ul>			<li>Single strands may also be generated by denaturing double-stranded DNA.			<ul>				<li>If denaturation precedes electrophoresis, the mobility of each of the two complementary strands is observed. Strands can be distinguished by attaching different fluorescent labels to the two primers.				<li>Experimental conditions may be arranged to denature double-stranded DNA during electrophoresis. The major factor determining mobility in <strong>denaturing gradient gel electrophoresis</strong> is the melting behavior of individual duplexes. Sensitivity to denaturation is revealed practically by				<ul>					<li>incorporation of a gradient of denaturant into the gel (low concentration near origin);					<li>or gradually increasing the temperature of the gel during electrophoresis.				</ul>			</ul>			<li>Single-stranded DNA <a href="http://www.apbiotech.com/search/super_index.asp?section_dir=1&stage_url=%2Fproduct%2Fpublication%2Flsn%2Flsn%2D3%2Flsn%2D3%2D606%2Ehtml">mobilities</a> are dependent on temperature.			<li><a href="http://www.bioproducts.com/technical/heteroduplexanalysiswithmdegelsolution.shtml">Heteroduplex analysis</a> is a related technique. In this technique, the single stranded products are annealed with a reference complementary strand. Denaturing gradient gel electrophoresis is used to analyze the stability of the resulting duplexes. Stability is affected by mismatches.			<li>Ribonuclease protection may also be used. A labeled reference RNA is annealed to the DNA strands and the duplex treated with RNase, which cleaves the RNA only where it is not base-paired. Products are displayed by gel electrophoresis and revealed sites where the subject DNA is not identical to the reference sequence.<img src="nav6.gif" width="69" height="71" align="right" usemap="#nav4B1F889C9"><map name=""></map>			<li><a href="MG11120.html">SNP</a> (single nucleotide polymorphism) <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/SNP/">techniques</a> can be used if the nucleotide responsible for the mobility difference is known.<br>		</ul>		<center>			<p><a name="bottom"></a><a href="MG11127.html">Last</a> | <a href="MG1119.html">Vocabulary</a> | <a href="MG1112.html">Overview</a> | <a href="#Facts">Top</a> | <a href="MG112.html">Next</a> | <a href="../MG0.html#Disclaimer">Disclaimer</a><br>			</p>			<p>			<hr>			This is page 11129 of <a href="http://opbs.okstate.edu/~melcher/MG0/MG01.html">Molecular Genetics</a> by <a href="http://opbs.okstate.edu/~melcher/UM.html">Ulrich Melcher</a>, &copy; 1998, 1999, 2000<img height="72" width="108" src="divlogo2.gif" align="right"><br>			<br>			<hr>			</p>		</center>		<address>E-mail inquiries to <a href="mailto:umelcher@biochem.okstate.edu" to/cher@biochem.okstate.edu">U.
