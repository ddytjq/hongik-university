A frame shift near the end of the COAT gene leads to premature terminationin close proximity to the initiation codon of the lysis gene. Other frameshiftslead to COAT-LYSIS fusions and longer than normal synthetase polypeptides.</li></ul><center><img SRC="i24141.gif" ></center><ul><li>Translation of some retroviral (HIV-1, for example) mRNAs, requires a ribosomalframeshifting event to translate the pol ORF which follows gag.</li><ul><li>In MMTV, two frameshifting events occur, the first between gag and theprotease domain of pol, the second between the latter and the remainderof pol.</li><li>The frameshifts require a 7 nucleotide signal at the site of frameshiftingand a stem-loop structure. An RNA pseudoknot is involved.</li></ul><li>Frameshifting is due to slippage rather than reading of a dinucleotideas a codon, since mutation of UUAUA to UUUUA results in a change in <a href="MG241.html">coding</a>from Leu Ile to Phe Leu.</li></ul><center><img SRC="i24142.gif" ></center><ul><li>Phenotypic revertants of bacteria and yeast with frameshift mutations areoften due to extragenic <a href="MG2421.html">second-site</a> mutations.These mutations are often in tRNA genes. The tRNA mutation creates an anticodonthat recognizes a four base codon, restoring the reading frame of a onebase insertion mutation.<img SRC="i24232.gif"  align=RIGHT></li><li>tRNA Phe can act as a frameshifting tRNA without mutation if the UUU codonit recognizes is in a stretch of five or more U's.</li><li>The gene for the <i>E.coli </i>factor required for termination of proteinsynthesis at UGA codons, <a href="http://opbs.okstate.edu/~melcher/N4/N4151.html">RF2</a>,has an internal UGA stop codon.</li><li>In the presence of insufficient RF2, termination at this UGA is suppressedby frameshifting. In the presence of sufficient RF2, premature terminationof RF2 synthesis occurs.</li></ul><center><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a>| <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></center><h4><a NAME="Interp"></a>Interpretations</h4><ul TYPE="circle"><li>Most mRNAs are translated precisely three nucleotides at a time.</li><li>Yet, translational frameshifting is a frequent strategy used in expressionof genes of viruses, related pathogens, and related genetic elements (eg.transposable elements).</li><li>Shifts in reading frame due to transcriptional or RNA processing eventsalso occur.</li><li>Since translational frameshifting requires a complex signal consistingof a nucleotide sequence and a complex RNA secondary structure element,it is unlikely that frameshifting will occur accidentally during the translationof most mRNAs.</li><li>The efficiency of the frameshifting signal determines the stoichiometricratio of the two products.</li><li>In bacteria, termination suppression by frameshifting provides a homeostaticgene expression regulation mechanism.</li></ul><center><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a>| <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></center><h4><a NAME="Info"></a>Further information</h4><ul TYPE="square"><li>Reversion mutations at second sites are called <b>suppressor</b> mutations.The tRNAs responsible for such reversions are known as <a href="MG2421.html">suppressortRNAs</a>.</li><li>Some retroviruses use a different strategy, <a href="MG2421.html">terminationsuppression</a>, to produce gag-pol fusion polypeptides.</li><li>Frameshifting occurs during translation of some non-viral mRNAs. For <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/htbin-post/Entrez/query?uid=9382847&form=6&db=m&Dopt=b">example</a>,the active product of the <i>EST3</i> gene, required to maintain <a href="../MGW1/MG1353.html">telomere</a>length in <i>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</i>, is made by a +1 ribosomal frameshift.&nbsp;<img SRC="nav4.gif" USEMAP="#nav4B1F889C9" height=71 width=69 align=RIGHT>&nbsp;<map NAME></map></li></ul><center><a NAME="bottom"></a><a href="MG2413.html">Last</a> | <a href="MG259.html">Vocabulary</a>| <a href="MG241.html">Overview</a> | <a href="#Facts">Top</a> | <a href="MG2415.html">Next</a></center><hr SIZE="2"><p>This is page 2414 of <a href="../MG01.html">Molecular Genetics</a> by<a href="http://opbs.okstate.edu/~melcher/UM.html">UlrichMelcher</a>, &copy; 1997, 1998, 2000&nbsp;<hr SIZE="2"><address>E-mail inquiries to <a href="mailto:umelcher@biochem.okstate.edu">U.
