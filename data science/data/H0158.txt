<TD WIDTH="70%" VALIGN="TOP"><P><B>Two terms you might see has your progress through these links on speciation
are microevolution and macroevolution. Microevolution is adaptation and
change in individual species and the splitting of an ancestoral species
into two or more descendent species. Macroevolution deals with the evolutionary
appearance of major functional or morphological changes in the evolutionary
record. For example, a proper macroevolutionary study is the appearance
of birds from their dinosaur ancestors. A current seed of debate in evolutionary
biology is whether the same gradual selection mechanisms used to explain
microevolutionary events can account for macroevolutionary changes such
as the appearance of the major groups in the fossil record. Probably not
entirely and the next couple of decades promise to be an exciting time in
the development of evolutionary thought.</B></P>

<P><B>You can find more information on speciation and macroevolution in
many of the sites located on the <A HREF="genevol.htm">general evolution
page</A> of <I>Nearctica</I>.</B></P>

<P>&nbsp;</P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.sprl.umich.edu/GCL/paper_to_html/speciation.html">The
Process of Speciation</A>. A very nice introduction to the species concept
and speciation from a course entitled Global Change taught at the University
of Michigan. Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.biology.ucsc.edu/people/barrylab/public_html/classes/old_animal_behavior/Speciation_Mechanisms.html">Speciation
Mechanisms</A>. Barry Sinervo, University of California at Santa Cruz. A
nice, inclusive introduction to the mechanisms of speciation including allopatric,
parapatric, and sympatric speciation. Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.botany.hawaii.edu/faculty/carr/silversword.htm">Hawaiian
Silversword Alliance</A>. Gerlad D. Carr, University of Hawaii. Some of
the most spectacular examples of adaptive radiations are found in the Hawaiian
Islands. One of these radiations is the group of plants called silverswords.
The silverswords exhibit a wide variety of shapes, forms, and habitat types.
They may also be some of the most interesting plants on Earth. This site
has a detailed examination of the silversword species of Hawaii, the species,
their habitats, and their evolution and systematics. There is a special
section of adaptive radiation in the group. Highly recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.micro.utexas.edu/courses/levin/bio304/evolution/speciation.html">Macroevolution:
Species Formation</A>. Gabor Lendvai. A very nice page on speciation, very
well illustrated, and with lots of examples. Some of the topics covered
are allopatric speciation, sympatric species, speciation by polyploidy and
chromosomal rearrangement, and adapative radiation. Lecture notes from an
ecology and evolution course at the University of Texas. There are a lot
of pictures and they will take a while to download. Highly recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.micro.utexas.edu/courses/levin/bio304/evolution/macroevolution.html">Macroevolution:
Speciation and Phylogenesis</A>. Gabor Lendvai, University of Texas. Lecture
notes from an ecology evolution course at the University of Texas. This
particular page is about differentiation and adaptation among populations
of a species as a result of selective pressures. Isolating mechanisms and
the fundamentals of speciation are also covered here. Well illustrated.
Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.skeptic.com/01.3.prothero-punc-eq.html">Punctuated
Equilibrium at Twenty: A Paleontological Perspective</A>. Donald R. Prothero.
An excellent introduction to the concept of punctuated equilibria and its
implications for current thought about the mechanisms of speciation.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://clab.cecil.cc.md.us/faculty/biology1/speciation.htm">Speciation</A>.
Beth Kuser Olsen, Cecil Community College. Lectures notes on speciation
from an introductory ecology course.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.santarosa.edu/lifesciences/ensatina.htm">Ensatina
eschscholtzi</A>. Speciation in Progress. Charles W. Brown. A nice article
on speciation in the salamander superspecies <I>Ensatina eschscholtzi</I>.
The presentation includes photographs of the geographical isolates, distributions,
abstracts of mitochondrial and enzyme studies, taxonomy, references, and
other <I>Ensatina</I> sites on the web.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.scar.utoronto.ca/~thompson/course/Speciation/sld001.htm">Speciation</A>.
Paul Thompson, University of Toronto. A slide show covering the basic principles
of species. Part of a university course on ecology and evolutionary biology.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.hsu.edu/faculty/engmanj/bio2114/power/species/sld001.htm">Speciation</A>.
James Engman, Henderson State University. A slide show covering speciation
as part of a general zoology course taught at Henderson State University.
The set of slides also has material about the species concept.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.talkorigins.org/faqs/faq-speciation.html">Observed
Instances of Speciation</A>. Joseph Boxhorn, Talk.Origins. This article
from Talk.Origins begins with a discussion of the species concept and moves
on to cover several cases were speciation events have been observed in the
laboratory or the wild.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.tulane.edu/~guill/speciation.html">Speciation
and Species Concepts</A>. David C. Heins, Tulane University. A synoptic,
but extensive set of lecture notes on the principles of speciation and the
species concept.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.crc.losrios.cc.ca.us/~scotts/biology3/zspeciation_macroevolution.html">Speciation
and Macroevolution</A>. Susan M. Scott, Cosumnes River College. A short,
synoptic introduction to speciation and macroevolution with links to other
web sites related to speciation.</B></TD></TR>
