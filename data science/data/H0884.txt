<title>HXB2 Numbering</title>
<body bgcolor = "#FFFFFF">
<center>
<img src=../../IMAGES/newtoplogo2.gif><P>
<strong><font size="+3">Numbering Positions in HIV Relative to HXB2CG</font></strong>
<hr noshade> </center>
<br>Bette T. Korber<strong><sup>1</sup></strong>, Brian T. Foley<strong><sup>1</sup></strong>, Carla L. Kuiken,<strong><sup>1</sup></strong>, Satish K. Pillai,<strong><sup>1</sup></strong> and Joseph
G. Sodroski<strong><sup>2</sup></strong>, 
<br>
<br><strong><sup>1</sup></strong>Theoretical Biology and Biophysics, Group T-10, MS K710, Los Alamos National Laboratory, Los Alamos, NM 87545;
<br><strong><sup>2</sup></strong>Howard Hughes Medical Institute, Columbia University, New York, NY 10032
<p>
In this section we present a simple numbering scheme to facilitate the
identification of the position number or precise location of interest
in HIV DNA or proteins.
<p>
Inconsistent and inaccurate numbering of locations in HIV DNA and
protein sequences is a serious problem in the HIV literature.
Therefore we decided to provide a practical guide to help circumvent
these problems in the future, and to attempt to bring a common language
into discussions in the field.  We present a clearly numbered set of
proteins, and the full length genome, for HIV HXB2, GenBank accession number
K03455. HIV HXB2 is also known as: HXBc2, for HXB clone 2; HXB2R, in the Los Alamos HIV database, with the R for revised, as it was slightly revised relative to the 
original HXB2 sequence; and HXB2CG in GenBank, for HXB2 complete genome.  Our web site 
has an interactive program to further facilitate obtaining position numbers
relative to HXB2CG <TT><A HREF="http://virus.lanl.gov/NUM-HXB2/HXB2.MAIN.html" NAME="http://virus.lanl.gov/NUM-HXB2/HXB2.MAIN.html">http://hiv-web.lanl.gov/NUM-HXB2/HXB2.MAIN.html</A></TT>.
<p>
HXB2 was selected as the prototype, because this
virus is the most commonly used reference strain for many different
kinds of functional studies. Importantly, all of the envelope
structural data published to date translates residue numbers into the
HXB2 numbering scheme.  Now that a core HIV-1 gp120 structure is
solved (for review, see Wyatt et al, this compendium), it has become
apparent that conservation in core sequences, especially in hydrophobic
interior domains, exists to preserve similar folding in gp120
variants.  As the envelope protein is riddled with insertions and
deletions, it is particularly problematic for numbering.  The current
system, of sequentially numbering proteins from any strain, lacks a
common way to refer to specific locations in a protein. We propose the
following system to circumvent this problem:
<p>
1) <i><strong>Case of insertion in sequence relative to HXB2CG</i></strong>. Use residue
number/alphabet (e.g., 131a, 131b, 131c, etc.) to
  refer to residues in variable regions that are ``extra" compared
  to what HXB2 has.  A similar scheme has been used for
  immunoglobulin complementarity-determining region (CDR) loops (see
  Lucas et al., <I> J Immunol</I> 1998 <strong>161:</strong>3776--80 (1998) for an example). 
<P>
  Example: 
  If the region under study is LLLTRDGGSNRSEPEVEIFRP of ENVB, gp120, 
<pre>
  452          465    470 HXB2 amino acid position from start of gp160
   |            |      |
   LLLTRDGGNSNNES--EIFRP  
   LLLTRDGGSNRSEPEVEIFRP 
</pre>
  one could refer to it as corresponding to HXB2 gp160 position numbers 
  452--470 with a two base insertion (465a = E and 465b = V) 
<p>
2) <i><strong>Case of deletion in sequence relative to HXB2</i></strong>. Indicate the deleted
residues.
<p>
  Example: 
  If the region under study is LLLTRDGGNN of 92RW020.5,
<Pre>
  452        463 HXB2 amino acid position from start of gp120
   |          |
   LLLTRDGGNSNN
   LLLTRDGG..NN
</pre>
  one refers to it as corresponding to HXB2 gp160 position numbers
  452--463 with a two base deletion at positions 460--461.
  We suggest using the annotation 452-463(del 460-461) to make this explicit.
<p>
  The sequential numbering relative to either 92RW020.5
   or ENVB could also be provided in the above two examples, 
   but the HXB2 numbering should also be provided as a reference.
<p>
The benefit of this numbering strategy is that, for example, aspartate
368, which is involved in CD4 binding, or gp160 368 D, means the same thing to
everyone working on envelope glycoproteins, regardless of the reference
strain they used in their particular studies.
<p>
Also, when working with a short functional domain, epitope, or primer,
researchers should publish the precise amino acid or nucleotide string
that they are working with, as well as the HXB2 numbered positions, to
ensure that there is no confusion (for example, write out ENVB
LLLTRDGGSNRSEPEVEIFRP as well as give the boundary position numbers).
<p>
We intend to change the HIV Immunology Database to this system, through
the course of 1999. This year we have made the HXB2 strain the
reference strain in our alignments for the sequence compendium,
although the WEAU strain remains the reference strain for the
immunology compendium in 1998.
<P>
This numbering was based on previous HIV sequence database annotation, cross-checked with protein structure
databases, Tozser et al.,<i>FEBS letters</i> <strong>281:</strong>77-80 (1991), and R. J. Gorelick and L. E. Henderson, <i>Human
Retroviruses and AIDS 1994</i>, part III, pages 2-10.
