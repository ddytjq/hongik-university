      <p>Here are some of the ways you can arrange to pay the loan amount of an 
        interest only mortgage.</p>
      <p class="bodyBold"> with profit endowment assurance </p>
      <p>This combines an investment plan with life assurance. It should repay 
        the mortgage at the end of the agreed period, or in the event of your 
        death.</p>
      <p> The amount you receive at the end of the period is usually enhanced 
        by annual bonuses, so that there is money left over after repaying the 
        mortgage. This would be paid to you (or your dependents if you died).</p>
      <p> It can be more expensive than other schemes, but does mean that you 
        can pay the mortgage in full.</p>
      <p class="bodyBold"> unit linked endowment assurance</p>
      <p> Payments are used to buy 'units' in a range of funds such as fixed interest 
        rate deposits, property, or shares in public companies. You can choose 
        the funds, and they can be administered by fund managers. The different 
        investments offer stability against the sometimes unstable market conditions. 
        The choice of funds also means you can strike a balance between security 
        and growth potential to match your own circumstances and personal objectives. 
      </p>
      <p>The payments are designed to create a fund that will repay your mortgage, 
        but investments can fall as well as rise, and you may end up having to 
        pay a lump sum at the end of the agreed period. </p>
      <p>There is life assurance included in this plan, which means that your 
        mortgage could be repaid in full if you died before the mortgage ends. 
      </p>
      <p class="bodyBold">low cost endowment assurance </p>
      <p>This is like the 'with profit endowment plan'. The repayment calculations 
        assume a certain future rate of investment returns that support annual 
        bonuses. This should result in a fund big enough to pay off the full loan 
        amount. The annual bonuses cannot be guaranteed. You would receive any 
        surplus, but you would also have to pay any deficit.</p>
      <p> The life assurance in this plan means that your mortgage could be paid 
        in full if you died before the end of the mortgage period. This is often 
        the cheapest option, although you may have to pay a lump sum at the end 
        of the period. </p>
      <p class="bodyBold">open ended investment company (OEIC) </p>
      <p>This is a collective investment scheme. Your monthly payments are used 
        to purchase shares in various funds offered, and you can structure the 
        payments to repay the mortgage in a way that suits your needs. There is 
        no guarantee that the value of your investments will be enough to pay 
        the loan amount at the end of the mortgage period and you may have to 
        pay a lump sum. </p>
      <p>OEICs do not include life cover, and it is <span class="bodyBold">strongly recommended</span> that 
        you take out a separate policy to repay your mortgage if you were to die 
        before the mortgage ends. </p>
      <p class="bodyBold">individual savings account (ISA) </p>
      <p>This is a tax efficient investment introduced in 1999. Your money will 
        be invested in shares, unit and investment trusts and life assurance or 
        cash. The tax advantages are specific to your circumstances. Using ISAs 
        to repay your mortgage carries a high degree of risk, with tax levels 
        and reliefs subject to Governmental change. There is also no guarantee 
        that the funds will be sufficient to repay the full mortgage amount. You 
        could end up with a surplus, or you could have to pay the deficit. </p>
      <p>ISAs do not include life cover, and it is <span class="bodyBold">strongly recommended</span> that you take out a separate policy to repay your mortgage if you were to die before the mortgage ends.</p>
      <p class="bodyBold">pension plan policies </p>
      <p>These have traditionally been used to provide retirement income, but 
        the lump sum can also be used to repay a mortgage. At the moment, contributions 
        are treated as an allowable expense for Income Tax purposes, and the funds 
        invested grow free of UK Capital Gains Tax. The value of the relief depends 
        on your personal financial circumstances. Because of the tax concessions, 
        this is a popular choice for people who are not already in a company pension 
        scheme.</p>
      <p> There is no guarantee that the lump sum will be enough to repay the 
        mortgage amount, and you could end up paying the deficit. Pension policies 
        do not include life cover, and it is <span class="bodyBold">strongly recommended</span> that you take  out a life policy to repay your mortgage if you were to die before the mortgage ends.</p>
