<td valign="top"><p class="articleheadline">Plant Biotechnology: Solutions in a Seed</p></td>
<td rowspan="2"><img src="html/images/clip0002.jpg" width=190 height=152 align=right></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td valign="bottom">
<p class="articlesubheadline">An advertorial in The New York Times describes how biotechnology is benefiting the developing world.<br>&nbsp;<br><a href="html/pdf/PlantBioSeeds_Web_FNL.pdf" onclick="javascript:window.open('index.asp?redirect=pdf%2FPlantBioSeeds%5FWeb%5FFNL%2Epdf','','').focus();return false;">Advertisement<img src="html/images/spacer.gif" border="0" width="4"><img src="html/images/pdf.gif" border="0" width="20"></a></p>

<br>

<p>Papaya farmers in Hawaii, cotton
farmers in China and corn and soybean farmers in the United States and
Argentina: These and other growers around the world are benefiting from
agriculture made better by technology in a seed. Plant biotechnology is helping
them grow more food and crops while protecting their land. And it will help
them grow even better food and crops in the future.</p>

<p>No wonder that global plantings of
biotech crops increased nearly 20 percent last year.<sup>1</sup> While the lion’s share of biotech crops are produced in North America, more
farmers in the developing world are being introduced to the benefits of
genetically improved crops. Chinese farmers, for example, tripled their use of
biotech cotton in 2001.<sup>2</sup></p>

<p>Why the growth? Because these
farmers are discovering that biotech crops help them control weeds and insects
better, which allows more of their crop to be harvested. </p>

<p>While biotech cotton has led the
way in countries like China and South Africa, there’s even more excitement
about the benefits this new technology can bring to staple food crops grown for
survival in the developing world.</p>

<p>“Plant biotechnology is a science
that has more to offer to the developing world than the developed world,” says
C.S. Prakash, a native of India who
directs the Center for Plant Biotechnology Research at Tuskegee University in
Alabama. </p>

<p>Recently, the International Society
of African Scientists issued a statement calling agricultural biotechnology a
“major opportunity” to enhance food production in Africa, the Caribbean and
other developing areas.<sup>3</sup></p>

<p>“Africa and the Caribbean cannot
afford to be left further behind in acquiring the uses and benefits of this new
agricultural revolution,” wrote the researchers.<sup>4</sup></p>

<p>The reason is very simple: The
developing world has the most at stake &mdash; and potentially the most to gain through plant
biotechnology.</p>

<p class="articlepullquote">More food for more people</p>

<p>With the world population projected
to top 8 billion people by 2030, there will be another 2 billion mouths to feed<sup>5</sup> — most of them in developing regions. With income growth also fueling demand for better
diets, farmers will need to at least double their production over the next 25
years to feed these additional mouths.<sup>6</sup></p>

<p>But annual increases in
agricultural yields in recent years are holding at just 1.3 percent a year<sup>7</sup> — less than half of the gains of 30 years ago.</p>

<p>“Far from being a luxury add-on to
the conventional research scene, biotechnology provides us with a vital new set
of tools that offers us real hope for a better future,” says Florence Wambugu,
founder of A Harvest Biotech Foundation International and author of the recent
book, <i>Modifying Africa: How Biotechnology
Can Benefit the Poor and Hungry, A Case Study from Kenya</i>. “Agri-biotechnology matters to Kenya, as to
most other African countries, for the most basic of reasons: Our people do not
have enough to eat.”</p>

<p>Current research shows promise in
improving several crops important to the developing world.</p>

<p>New varieties of rice are being
developed that are expected to have 50 percent higher yields and higher protein
content, mature 30 to 50 days earlier, be more disease- and drought-tolerant
and resistant to certain insect pests, and be grown with less fertilizer or
pesticides.<sup>8</sup></p>

<p>“This initiative shows the enormous
potential of biotech to improve food security in Africa, Asia and Latin
America,” wrote the United Nations Development Program in its 2001 Human
Development Report.<sup>9</sup></p>

<p>Biotech banana tissue cultures have
been developed to cultivate clean, disease-free banana plantlets<sup>10</sup> so they can be distributed to small farmers in Kenya, where pests and diseases
have reduced average yields to less than a third of the crop’s potential.<sup>11</sup> The result will be higher yields. </p>

<p>Also in Kenya, researchers, led by
Wambugu, are continuing the development of a biotech sweet potato that is
resistant to feathery mottle virus, a pest which consumes (along with several
related virus strains) an estimated three-fourths of the annual harvest. The improved sweet potato, a staple in many
countries, is expected to deliver much higher yields. Chinese researchers have
also developed a virus-resistant sweet potato that has improved yields by as
much as 160 percent.<sup>12</sup></p>

<p class="articlepullquote">Better food for better nutrition</p>

<p>Research is also underway to
improve the nutritional and medicinal value of food in the developing world.</p>

<p>Researchers are currently working
to build a vaccine into bananas for hepatitis B, a deadly disease that afflicts
some 300 million people worldwide.<sup>13</sup> Traditional vaccines require refrigeration
and sterilization equipment that is expensive and scarce in the developing
world. The edible vaccine could be delivered for as little as 2 cents a dose
vs. $125 for conventional treatment.<sup>14</sup></p>

<p>Researchers are also working to
build a gene into rice that produces more beta carotene, a precursor to vitamin
A. Worldwide, an estimated 100 million to 140 million children suffer from
vitamin A deficiency, which can lead to illness and death.<sup>15</sup> Up to half a million children worldwide go blind each year due to vitamin A
deficiency, according to the World Health Organization.</p>

<p>New varieties of high-protein corn
are being developed that could help alleviate chronic protein deficiencies that
afflict still more children in parts of Africa, Asia and Latin America.</p>

<p>Those are just a few of the biotech
products that may enhance health and quality of life for people in the
developing world. Although the technology is new, the concept behind the
improvements is centuries old. As Hippocrates, the Greek physician who’s known
as the father of modern medicine, said in 400 B.C., “Let food be your medicine
and medicine be your food.”</p>

<p class="articlepullquote">Protecting the environment</p>

<p>Plant biotechnology is also helping
protect the environment in many regions of the developing world by improving
the productivity of existing agricultural land and by allowing the use of more
conservation tillage and less spraying.</p>

<p>Environmentalists fear that up to
half of the world’s remaining 6 billion acres of tropical forests will be lost
to agricultural expansion. And some biologists are warning that as many as 20
percent of all tropical forest species could be extinct within 30 years if
forests continue to disappear at the current rate.<sup>16</sup></p>

<p>Researchers are using plant
biotechnology to boost productivity from existing land in a number of ways.</p>

<ul>

<li>In Mexico, researchers are working to make rice and
corn more tolerant of aluminum — a common soil toxin.<sup>17</sup></li><br>&nbsp;

<li>In California, researchers have developed a genetically
enhanced tomato that can thrive in salty soils<sup>18</sup> — a problem in arid regions where irrigation is used. The hope is to make other
plants salt-tolerant as well.</li><br>&nbsp;

<li>In India, scientists are adding two genes to rice
plants so they can survive long periods under water.<sup>19</sup></li>

</ul>

<p>Getting the most production from
existing land is important because more than a fourth of the world’s 19.7
billion acres of agricultural land, pastures, forest and woodlands have already
been degraded from overuse or misuse, such as over-irrigation or erosion.<sup>20</sup> Another 12.3 million to 24.6 million acres are afflicted every year, according
to the Consultative Group on International Agricultural Research.</p>

<p class="articlepullquote">Enhancing lives through technology sharing</p>

<p>Research on plant biotechnology is
underway throughout the world by scientists from government, universities,
public institutions and private companies. Yes, there are issues to resolve,
some of them difficult. But that work must continue because it can benefit
people everywhere, no matter where they live.</p>

<p>The world has just begun to see the
benefits of this technology in a seed, but as more products of plant
biotechnology reach the marketplace, those benefits will grow. Prime among them
is the simple, but still elusive, benefit that comes when hungry people have
enough to eat.</p>
