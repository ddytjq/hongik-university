<H2><A NAME="Glossary">GLOSSARY</A> </H2>

<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Symbol">L</FONT><FONT SIZE=2>:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2>
The cosmological constant, which measures the energy density of
a vacuum. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Symbol">W</FONT><FONT SIZE=2>:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2>
The ratio of the average total density of matter to the critical
density required to close the universe and eventually stop its
expansion. can be broken down into its components according to
the type of matter involved. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Symbol">W</FONT><SUB><FONT SIZE=2>B</FONT></SUB><FONT SIZE=2>:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2>
The ratio of the mean density of baryons to the critical density
required to close the universe. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Anisotropy:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Variation
with direction. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Axion:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A hypothetical
elementary particle whose existence might explain certain particle
physics experiments. A candidate for cold dark matter.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Baryon:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A massive, strongly
interacting elementary particle, such as a proton or a neutron.
</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Baryonic matter:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Ordinary
matter as we know it consists largely of baryons, as opposed to
hypothetical matter that might theoretically exist. Both kinds
have mass, and either kind can be dark matter.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Big Bang theory:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The theory
that the universe began with all matter and energy concentrated
to very high density and temperature some 15 billion years ago.
The present universe expanded from that epoch and is still expanding.
In the hot Big Bang theory, the ratio of photons to atoms is large,
say ~10<SUP>9</SUP>, as astronomers now observe. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Black hole:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> An object
that has become so dense that, through the effects of general
relativity, its contents are no longer accessible to the outside
universe. No light from the surface can escape to the outside,
hence the term &quot;black.&quot;</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Brown dwarf:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A hypothetical
star not sufficiently massive to ignite hydrogen burning, with
mass less than 10 percent of the mass of the Sun.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>CCD:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Charge-coupled device.
An electronic image detector used in modern video cameras and
astronomical instruments. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>CERN:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The European Laboratory
for Particle Physics. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Closed universe:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A universe
expanding slowly enough to be braked by gravity. In this case,
</FONT><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Symbol">W</FONT><FONT SIZE=2> = 1. If
</FONT><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Symbol">W</FONT><FONT SIZE=2> &gt; 1
(above critical density), the universe will eventually recollapse.
If = </FONT><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Symbol">W</FONT><FONT SIZE=2> &lt;
1 (at critical density), the universe will expand forever, but
ever more slowly.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Cluster:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> An assemblage
of many galaxies. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>CMBR:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Cosmic microwave
background radiation.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>COBE:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The Cosmic Background
Explorer satellite.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Cosmic remnant:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A product
of a primordial physical process. The cosmic microwave background
radiation is a cosmic remnant. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Cosmic velocity flow:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2>
Alterations in the regular movement of celestial objects away
from each other caused by the gravitational attraction of other
nearby objects. These flows give an indication of total mass,
both luminous and nonluminous. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Cosmology:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The study of
the contents, structure, and evolution of the universe from the
beginning of time to the infinite future. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>CP:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Charge-parity conservation.
The symmetry of properties under a reflection in space and reversal
of charge.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Critical density:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The
density of matter that would just halt the expansion of the universe.
The dividing line between a collapsing and an ever-expanding universe.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Dark matter:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Matter that
does not emit enough light or other radiation to be observed directly.
Most of the matter in the universe is believed to be of this type.
Cold dark matter had a low velocity compared to the speed of light
during the epoch of recombination. An example would be elementary
particles with mass about equal to that of a proton or higher.
Hot dark matter had a high velocity (near the speed of light)
during the epoch of recombination. An example would be light elementary
particles. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Epoch:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A period characterized
by the dominance of a particular physical process, such as the
formation of the light elements from protons and neutrons. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Epoch of photon decoupling:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2>
See epoch of recombination. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Epoch of recombination:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2>
The time when electrons and nuclei were combining to form atoms
and the universe was 1,600 times smaller than its present size.
Also called the epoch of photon decoupling and the epoch of atom
formation. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>eV:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> An electron-volt,
a measure of energy equal to that gained by an electron passing
through a potential difference of 1 volt. Also a unit of particle
mass. Electrons have a mass of about 0.511 MeV (million electron-volts);
protons have a mass of about 938 GeV (billion electron-volts).</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Flat universe:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A universe
where space is euclidean (zero curvature). If </FONT><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Symbol">L</FONT><FONT SIZE=2>
= 0, a flat universe has </FONT><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Symbol">W</FONT><FONT SIZE=2>
= 1. If </FONT><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Symbol">L</FONT><FONT SIZE=2>
is non-zero, then </FONT><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Symbol">L</FONT><FONT SIZE=2>
+ </FONT><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Symbol">W</FONT><FONT SIZE=2> = 1.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Forces of nature:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The
four basic forces of physics: gravity, electromagnetism, and the
weak and strong interactions.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Freeze-out:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The disequilibrium
by which relics are formed in the universe.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Galaxy:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A large assemblage
of stars. Our own galaxy, the Milky Way, contains 10<SUP>11</SUP>
stars.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Grand unification era:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2>
The era when the universe cooled sufficiently for gravity to be
described by Einstein's general relativity theory, but where the
temperature was still sufficiently high that the other remaining
three forces of nature remained unified.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Grand unified theories:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2>
Theories that combine the strong, electromagnetic, and weak interactions
into one unified theory. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Gravitational instability:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2>
The process whereby a small lump in an expanding universe can
grow under gravity, pulling in surrounding matter and ultimately
collapsing to form an object like a galaxy or cluster of galaxies.
</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Gravitational lens:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A
celestial object that distorts the image of another object behind
it by virtue of the fact that its gravity affects the propagation
of the light from the background object. </FONT>
<P>
<I><B><FONT SIZE=2>H</FONT></B></I><B><SUB><FONT SIZE=1>0</FONT></SUB><FONT SIZE=2>:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2>
The Hubble constant. A measure of the expansion rate and age of
the universe. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Hadron:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A strongly interacting
particle such as a proton or neutron. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Halo:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The matter surrounding
a galaxy.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Horizon:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Edge of the portion
of the universe visible to us. Light signals from beyond this
point have not had time to reach Earth yet. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>HST:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Hubble Space Telescope.
</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Hubble's law:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The principle
that any two distant celestial objects (e.g., galaxies) move away
from each other at a speed that is proportional to the distance
between them, due to the homogenous expansion of space. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Inflation:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A rapid expansion
appearing as an early phase in some cosmological models, which
solves several problems of cosmology. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Infrared:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Light of wavelength
longer than the reddest part of the visible spectrum. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Intergalactic medium:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2>
The material between galaxies.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Ionized:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Under terrestrial
conditions, most matter has an equal amount of positive and negative
charge, so that its net charge is zero. At high temperatures,
the charges separate in a process called ionization. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>IRAS:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The NASA, British,
and Dutch Infrared Astronomy Satellite, which was flown in 1983.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Keck telescopes:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The two
new, state-of-the-art ground-based 10-meter optical telescopes
located on Mauna Kea, Hawaii. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>LEP:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The Large Electron-Positron
Collider. A particle accelerator at CERN. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Leptons:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A class of elementary
particles including electrons, muons, and tauons. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Microlensing:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> If a small,
dark body is directly in the line of sight to a bright background
star, the brightness of the background star may appear to increase
because of bending of the light rays by the dark body. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Neutrinos:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Very light
(possibly massless) particles that are emitted in the process
of radioactive decay. There are three species, associated with
electrons, muons, and tau-leptons. They interact with ordinary
matter through the weak force. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Nucleosynthesis:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The process
by which the elements are built up from protons and neutrons.
</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Open universe:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A universe
expanding faster than the retarding pull of gravity. It has less
than critical density, </FONT><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Symbol">W</FONT><FONT SIZE=2>
&lt; 1, and expands forever.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Planck time:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The period
of time immediately after the creation of the universe (10<SUP>-43</SUP>
s) during which quantum gravity is the principal phenomenon governing
the evolution of the universe. </FONT>
<P>
<I><B><FONT SIZE=2>q</FONT></B></I><B><SUB><FONT SIZE=1>0</FONT></SUB><FONT SIZE=2>:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2>
The deceleration rate for the expansion of the universe, which
is related to the curvature of space. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Quarks:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The elementary
constituents of hadrons or baryons (such as protons and neutrons).
</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Quasar:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> An object with
a large redshift and an inferred luminosity often hundreds of
times that of a normal galaxy. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Redshift:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The shifting
of light toward the red end of the spectrum that occurs when the
observed light source is receding from the observer. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>SIRTF:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Space Infrared
Telescope Facility. A proposed orbiting infrared telescope. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Standard candle:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A celestial
object whose intrinsic brightness is known or can be estimated
by some physical principle and whose observed brightness is therefore
useful as a tool to measure distance. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Supersymmetry:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> A space-time
symmetry that would imply the existence of partners to all elementary
particles, with quantum spins of one-half a unit higher or lower.
Often used in constructing theories that unify gravity with the
three other forces. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Thermal spectrum:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The
characteristic distribution of radiation as a function of frequency
that is emitted by a body at a well-defined temperature, also
called a black-body spectrum. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Tully-Fisher relation:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2>
A method to determine galactic distances. Big, luminous galaxies
rotate faster than small, faint ones. The connection between the
two is given by the Tully-Fisher relation. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Unification:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The concept
that two or more forces that seem distinct in today's universe
could, at higher energies (or temperature), merge to become one
force.</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Universe:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> All of space
and time taken together. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>VLA:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The Very Large Array.
An array of 27 radio telescopes in New Mexico, capable of adjustable
spacing along a Y-shaped track, up to a radius of 27 km. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>VLBA:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Very Long Baseline
Array. A newly completed radio interferometer operated by the
National Radio Astronomy Observatory. Capable of producing images
with angular resolution of one thousandth of a second of arc.
</FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>Weak interactions:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> The
interactions of elementary particles that are responsible for
radioactive decay. </FONT>
<P>
<B><FONT SIZE=2>WIMP:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2> Weakly Interacting
Massive Particle. A candidate for dark matter. </FONT>
