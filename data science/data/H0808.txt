Later generationsare the most severely affected.&nbsp;</li><li>The mutated genes for several such diseases (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/htbin-post/Omim/dispmim?309550">fragileX syndrome</a>, <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/htbin-post/Omim/dispmim?143100">Huntington'schorea</a>, <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/htbin-post/Omim/dispmim?160900">myotonicmuscular dystrophy</a>) have been isolated by <a href="../MGW4/MG412.html">map-basedcloning</a>, sequenced and compared with the parental and normal allelesof the gene.&nbsp;</li><li>Normal alleles of each of the genes contain a segment of 10 to 30 <a href="../MGW1/MG112284.html">tandemrepeats</a> of a trinucleotide. The allele in the parent of the first affectedindividual in a family has more such repeats (for some dieseases, 50 to80, for others into the thousands). The number of repeats increases withgeneration, as does disease severity.&nbsp;</li><li>In the characterized genes, the repeats are located at different placeswith respect to the coding regions. Some are in coding regions, othersare 5' of the transcription unit, 3' of the transciption unit or in <a href="../MGW2/MG231.html">introns</a>.(<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=9747913&dopt=Abstract">rev1,</a><a href="http://genom.AnnualReviews.org/cgi/content/abstract/1/1/281">rev2</a>)&nbsp;</li></ul></td></tr></table><center><p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> |<a href="#Info">FurtherInfo.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></center><h4><a NAME="Interp"></a>Interpretations</h4><ul type="circle"><li>Repeat expansion causes disease, the severity of the disease correlatingwith the degree of expansion.</li><li>Given their varying locations in genes, above threshold levels of tripletrepeats must cause disease by a variety of mechanisms<img SRC="nav6.gif" usemap="#nav4B1F889C9" height=69 width=69 align=RIGHT>.<map name=""></map></li></ul><center><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a>| <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></center><h4><a NAME="Info"></a>Further information</h4><ul type="square"><li>How expansion occurs is unknown, though a variety of models have been proposed(<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=9666328&dopt=Abstract">rev</a>).Some repeat expansions occur in <a href="../MGW2/MG2319.html">non-translatedregions</a> of transcripts and may act through their RNA.</li><li>Detailed explanations of the molecular pathogenesis of each gene's expansionhave not been agreed upon (<a href="http://genom.AnnualReviews.org/cgi/content/abstract/1/1/281">rev2</a>).</li><li>Other triplet repeat expansion diseases include<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/disease/SCA.html">spinocerebellar atrophy</a>, <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/htbin-post/Omim/dispmim?229300">Friedreichataxia</a>, <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/htbin-post/Omim/dispmim?125370">dentatorubraland pallidoluysian</a> atrophy, and <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/htbin-post/Omim/dispmim?109150">Machado-Joseph</a>disease.</li><li>The protein encoded by the locus affected by the fragile X syndrome, FMRP,binds to <a href="../MGW1/MG1352.html">G-quartet</a> structures on a subsetof mRNAs, including those important in neural development (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=11719189&dopt=Abstract">ref1</a>,<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=11719188&dopt=Abstract">ref2</a>,<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=11532944&dopt=Abstract">ref3</a>).</li></ul><center><a NAME="bottom"></a><a href="MG341.html">Last</a> | <a
