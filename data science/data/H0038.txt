<TD WIDTH="70%" VALIGN="TOP"><P><B>The page will take you to sites with introductions to evolutionary
biology</B>.</P>

<P>&nbsp;</P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.ucmp.berkeley.edu/history/evolution.html">Enter
Evolution: Theory and History</A>. University of California at Berkeley
Museum of Paleontology. The Museum of Paleontology at Berkeley probably
has the premier web site on paleontology and evolution. This introductory
pages will lead you into the rest of this massive site. A number of essays
on the people and history of biology and evolutionary thought from the ancient
past to the present may also be found here. Highly recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.talkorigins.org/faqs/faq-intro-to-biology.html">Introduction
to Evolutionary Biology</A>. Talk.Origins Archive. Chris Colby. An excellent
introduction to the principles of evolutionary biology including the history
of its development, population genetics, and natural selection.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.talkorigins.org/origins/faqs-evolution.html">Biology
and Evolutionary Theory</A>. Talk.Origins. This site has a number essays
covering background and basic information about evolutionary biology. Included
are the essay &quot;Introduction to Evolutionary Biology&quot; listed above.
Also included are essays on random genetic drift, macroevolution, observed
speciation events, punctuated equilibria, natural selection, and much more.
This site is highly recommended by us and forms an excellent introduction
to the whole field of evolutionary biology.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://sensorydrive.lscf.ucsb.edu/evolution/evolution.html">Principles
of Evolution</A>. Sam Sweet, University of California at Santa Barbara.
A very nice set of lecture notes on the principles of evolution covering
such topics as genetic variation, population genetics, selection and adaptation,
speciation, adaptive radiations, and macroevolution. Highly recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.sprl.umich.edu/GCL/notes1.html">Introduction
to Global Change, Fall Semester</A>. University of Michigan. This site represents
the lecture note for a global change course team taught at the University
of Michigan. Although the lecture notes for this course are not uniform
in quality, most of them are just plain outstanding. Just a few of the topics
covered are evolution, speciation, communities, competition and predation,
ecosystems, some biomes (coral reefs and tropical rain forest), nutrient
cycling, and energy flow in ecosystems. This is a great site for both evolution
and ecology. Highly recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.micro.utexas.edu/courses/levin/bio304/index.html">Biology
304: Ecology and Evolutionary Biology</A>. Donald A. Levin and Gabor Lendvai,
University of Texas. This site contains the lecture notes on an introductory
course in ecology and evolutionary biology. The notes are very well illustrated
and are not overly technical. The major topics in both fields are covered,
although the evolution section tends to be slightly better than the ecology
part. The site is very graphics intensive and the pages will take a while
to download. Highly recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://pespmc1.vub.ac.be/evolut.html">Evolutionary Theory</A>.
Principia Cybernetica. This site deals with the philosophy of evolution
and not necessarily only biological evolution. If you are interested in
philosophy, this is a good place to go. If, however, you are more interested
in the real-world principles of evolutionary biology, you may want to look
elsewhere.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.nyu.edu/projects/fitch/courses/evolution/index.html">Evolution</A>.
David H.A. Fitch, New York University. An excellent set of lecture notes
for a course in evolution taught at New York University. Some of the topics
covered are; adaptation, speciation, evolutionary genetics, systematics
and phylogeny, the fossil record, coevolution, biogeography, and molecular
evolution. Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://fig.cox.miami.edu/Faculty/Tom/bil160sp98/bil160sp98.html">Biology
160 - Evolution and Biodiversity</A>. Thomas J. Herbert. Lecture notes for
an introductory evolution and evolutionary ecology course at Miami University.
Some of the topics covered are the species concept, systematics, coevolution,
predation, competition, natural selection, and speciation. Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://marine.geol.sc.edu/BIOL/Courses/BIOL301/Wethey/index.html">Ecology
and Evolution: Biology 301</A>. David S. Wethey, University of South Carolina.
Synoptic lecture notes on evolution and ecology. Topics include morphological
and genetic variation, natural selection, fitness, population ecology and
life history strategies, competion models and examples, predation, succession,
and energy flow. The emphasis is on mathematical models.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.scar.utoronto.ca/~thompson/course/lecture.html">BGYB50Y:
Ecology and Evolutionary Biology</A>. Paul Thompson. This site represents
the teaching materials for an Ecology and Evolutionary Biology course taught
at the University of Toronto. The lecture material is extensive and consists
of a series of slide shows covering such topics as population genetics,
the species concept, adaptation and selection, speciation, sociobiology,
and phylogenetics.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://biology.uoregon.edu/Biology_www/Online_classes/Bi103s97/">Biology
103: General Biology Spring, 1997</A>. Biology 103 is a team taught course
on evolution and ecology taught at the University of Oregon. The quality
of the lecture notes is variable. Most is in the form of a series of slides,
although more complete material is also present. The section of coevolution
is particularly well done.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://gause.biology.ualberta.ca/bio108.hp/bio108hp.html">Biology
108, Organisms in their Environment</A>. University of Alberta. Some synoptic
lecture notes for an introductory biology course including notes on fitness,
natural selection, and speciation.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.spacelab.net/~catalj/">The World of Richard
Dawkins</A>. John Catalano. It is difficult to know just what to say about
this site. Richard Dawkins is one of the better known evolutionary biologists
of the day and this web site is in part about Dawkins, his books, and his
view of evolution. On the whole the site is a good place to keep abreast
of current thought and controversy in evolutionary thought. You'll also
find links to other web sites.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.panspermia.org/intro.htm">Cosmic Ancestry</A>.
Cosmic Ancestry is on evolution and the origin of life on Earth. It holds
that life on Earth was seeded by bacterial spores from space, and that the
genetic programs necessary for the evolution of life came from space. The
idea that life may have come in the form of spores from outer space has
been around a long time and is generally referred to as panspermia. The
inclusion of the hypothesis that these spores contained the genetic programming
for all future evolution and speciation is very radical. The site is extensive.
Readers should be aware that this theory is well off the main stream of
evolutionary thought.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.ed.uiuc.edu/facstaff/g-cziko/without_miracles/">Without
Miracles</A>. Gary Dziko. This web site contains an online book dedicated
to the principle of natural selection and its production of structure in
plant and animal species. The emphasis is on humans. On the whole the book
is philosophy oriented and is strongest on the history of evolutionary thought.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.geocities.com/CapeCanaveral/Lab/7111/">Evolution
Update</A>. Stanley C. Spencer. This web site is a listing of the current
literature related to evolutionary biology. The site features links to other
web sites where you can purchase of view the articles. In most cases you
will have to pay for the privilege.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.baz.com/litm/CERD/">Thomas Moore's Creation/Evolution
Reference Database</A>. An extremely large bibliography of references to
the Evolution-Creation debate.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://weber.u.washington.edu/~jahayes/evolution/index.html">Sci.Bio.Evolution</A>.
A compendium of links on evolution and paleontology.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://golgi.harvard.edu/biopages/evolution.html">Evolution</A>.
World Wide Web Virtual Library. A compendium of links to web sites related
to evolution and paleontology. The site is strongest on organizations.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://ccp.uchicago.edu/~jyin/evolution.html">Evolution
and Behavior</A>. Yie Yin. A collection of links to web sites concerned
with evolutionary biology topics.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://statgen.ncsu.edu/statgen/email.html">Population
Genetics/Evolution Email List</A>. Brian Golding. A list of e-mail addresses
for researchers in Populations Genetics and Evolution.</B></TD></TR>
