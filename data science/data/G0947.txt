                                            <P><FONT FACE="Arial,Helvetica,Univers,Zurich BT,sans-serif"></FONT><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial,Helvetica,Univers,Zurich BT,sans-serif"><B>Overheating bubble.</B>
                                                 An experiment on board the MIR spacecraft showed that a bubble of SF<SUB>6</SUB> gas (above, surrounded by SF<SUB>6</SUB> liquid and two temperature sensors) can become hotter than its heat source in the surrounding walls.

                    <FONT FACE="Arial,Helvetica,Univers,Zurich BT,sans-serif">When you heat soup on the stove, you don't expect it to get hotter than the burner because heat normally flows from hot regions to cold ones. But now, in the <nobr>1 May
                    </nobr><I>PRL</I>, a team using an experiment on the Russian space station MIR has shown otherwise: For a fluid consisting of liquid and gas phases on the verge of a transition, the gas temperature can exceed that of its 
                    surrounding heat source, a condition known as local overheating. This counterintuitive effect was predicted theoretically a decade ago and demonstrates the complex nature of heat transfer in fluids.</P>
                <P>For fluids confined to a fixed volume, such as a steel container, heat can be transferred inward in several ways: Warmer fluid can move into colder regions (convection), or the heat can conduct from 
                    hot to cold (thermal diffusion). Another heat transfer mechanism is &quot;adiabatic compression,&quot; also called the piston effect, where a hot, expanding outer layer acts like a piston and 
                    squeezes the interior of the fluid, heating it up. Near the liquid-gas critical point--a pressure and temperature where the two phases are 
                    indistinguishable--diffusion is limited, and in the absence of gravity, convection and other complicating effects are eliminated. So according to computer simulations, the piston 
                    effect should predominate if a near-critical fluid is warmed in space.</P>
                <P>A French and American experiment on board MIR has now demonstrated the piston effect and the existence of local overheating. The team, led by Yves Garrabos and Regis 
                    Wunenburger of the University of Bordeaux I in Pessac, France, subjected a small cylinder of sulfur hexafluoride (SF<SUB>6</SUB>) to periodic warmings. Looking something like the 
                    bubble chamber in a builder's leveling tool, the cylinder contained a sulfur hexafluoride gas bubble surrounded by its liquid state, all maintained just below the critical temperature of <nobr>45.5 degrees C</nobr>
                    . The researchers recorded temperatures from both phases while the cell walls were heated in increments ranging from 20 to <nobr>100 mK</nobr>.</P>
                <P>During each small warming, or &quot;quench,&quot; the liquid remained cooler than the walls. But before the end of each quench the gas temperature passed up to 23% above that of the 
                    walls because of the piston effect. &quot;The liquid surrounding the vapor squeezes and raises the temperature of the bubble faster than the heat can diffuse back out through the 
                    liquid,&quot; explains Allen Wilkinson of the NASA Glenn Research Center in Cleveland. Because the gas bubble is isolated from the heat source (the cell wall), and because gas 
                    has a larger thermal response to a pressure change than a liquid, the gas bubble becomes an isolated hot region. The overheating doesn't violate the laws of 
                    thermodynamics because the expanding liquid performs mechanical work; the phenomenon would be impossible in a purely diffusive system.</P>
                <P>&quot;This is very beautiful work by a well recognized team,&quot; says Horst Meyer of Duke University. He calls the work &quot;significant&quot; for gaining a deeper understanding of phenomena such as boiling in fluids.</P>
                <P>David Appell is a freelance science writer.</P>
