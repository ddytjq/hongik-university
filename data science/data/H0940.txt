        <p align="center"><font face="Comic Sans MS" color="#0000FF" size="5">Introduction</font><font face="Comic Sans MS" color="#0000FF" size="4"><br>
        </font><font color="#0000FF" face="Comic Sans MS" size="2">(flow rate, pressure and
        chromatography)</font></p>
        <p>&nbsp;The standard
        size HPLC column (4.6 X 250 mm) is run at a flow rate of 1 mL/min.&nbsp;
        4.6 refers to the internal diameter and 250 refers to the
        length of the HPLC column in millimeters.&nbsp; The limiting factors in
        choosing a flow rate are, instrument pressure limitations, the effect on
        the quality of the chromatography, and time.&nbsp; Most HPLC's operate
        in the pressure range between 30 and 200 bar.&nbsp; Maintaining linear
        velocity is the single most important factor when trying to reproduce a
        chromatographic separation on columns of differing diameters.&nbsp;
        Since flow rate directly impacts HPLC system pressure a discussion of
        parameters that affect system
        pressure are listed below.&nbsp;</p>
        <p align="center"><br>
        &nbsp;<br>
        <font size="4"><font face="Comic Sans MS" color="#0000FF">Factors that
        affect system pressure:</font></font></p>
  </center>
      <p align="left"><br>
      <font color="#0000FF" face="Comic Sans MS">Column Dimension</font><b><br>
      </b>Shorter, fatter columns produce lower system pressures, which allows them to be run at higher flow rates.&nbsp;Often
      the chromatographer will exploit&nbsp; this and run short fat columns at
      higher flow rates to speed up analysis times.&nbsp; HPLC peaks do not always
      get narrower with increased flow rate.&nbsp; At faster flow rates the
      analyte may have insufficient time to interact with the stationary phase.&nbsp;</p>
      <p align="left"><font color="#0000FF" face="Comic Sans MS">Stationary
      Phase Particle Size</font><b><br>
      </b>Stationary phase particles range in size from 2 to 25 microns.&nbsp;
      The most common particle size is 5 micron.&nbsp; In general the larger the
      particle the lower the system pressure for a given flow rate.&nbsp;
      Smaller particles generally provide greater surface area and better
      separation but produce higher system pressures.<br>
      <br>
      <font color="#0000FF" face="Comic Sans MS">Temperature</font><b><br>
      </b>System pressure is affected by temperature.&nbsp; The viscosity of the
      mobile phase will decrease with increasing temperature.&nbsp; For example
      if the HPLC system pressure is too high for a given solvent system the
      chromatographer may choose to raise the temperature of the column
      compartment to 40°C or even 60°C.&nbsp; In terms of trouble shooting high
      system pressure, adjusting temperature in not usually the first factor to
      consider.&nbsp; Often an increase in back pressure will be a sign of
      column plugging or fouling.</p>
      <p align="left"><font color="#0000FF" face="Comic Sans MS">Flow Rate</font><b><br>
      </b>It is not difficult to imagine that<b> </b>system pressure is affected by flow rate.&nbsp; Flow rate impacts HPLC
      system pressure, chromatographic quality, and analysis time.&nbsp; One
      must choose a flow rate that is appropriate for the HPLC system and column.&nbsp; A higher than usual
        flow rate may adversely affect the quality of the chromatography not
      giving the analyte sufficient time to interact with the stationary phase.&nbsp; Faster
        isn't always better.&nbsp; A lower than usual flow rate may leave the
        analyst waiting for the peak to appear at the detector.&nbsp;&nbsp;<br>
      <br>
      </p>

      <td width="677" height="24" colspan="2" align="center">
        <p align="left">Maintaining mobile phase linear velocity is important
        when attempting to reproduce chromatography obtained on columns of
        differing internal diameters.&nbsp; For example to maintain mobile phase
        linear velocity from the standard column mentioned above, 4.6 X 250 mm,
        to a small bore column with the dimensions 2.1 X 250 mm one must adjust
        the flow rate downward by a factor.&nbsp; When going from a 4.6 to a
        2.1mm ID or to any smaller ID column decrease the flow rate by the square of
        the ratios of the column diameters.&nbsp;&nbsp; As mentioned above the
        standard flow rate for a 4.6 mm column is 1 ml/min.</p>
      </td>
    </tr>

      <td width="677" height="1" colspan="2" align="center">
        <p align="left">In our example the new flow rate determined for the 2.1 mm column
        is 0.208 ml/min.&nbsp; Commonly 2.1 mm columns are run at a flow rate of
        200 µl/min.&nbsp;&nbsp;<p align="left">It is important to note that
        sometimes adjustments in mobile phase flow rates from the standard flow
        rate can have minor beneficial effects in chromatography.&nbsp;
        Sometimes flowing a column at one half the normal flow rate will have a beneficial
        effect on a chromatographic separation.</td>
