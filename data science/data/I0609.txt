  <p align="left" style="word-spacing: 0; line-height: 100%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 0; margin-right: 0; margin-top: 15; margin-bottom: 15"><font color="#FF0000" face="Impact" size="7">&nbsp;1996

  <p style="text-indent: 0; line-height: 100%; word-spacing: 0; margin: 0" align="left"><font face="Times New Roman" size="4"><img border="0" src="96scot.gif" width="200" height="110" align="left" hspace="10" vspace="10">Remember
  1996? The whole country was in a frenzy as football finally came home. England
  or course qualified as hosts, the downside of that was that for two years,
  Terry Venable’s team only played friendlies. Most of which were played at
  Wembley in front of meager crowds with a lack of any real atmosphere. The draw
  for the finals pitched us in the same group as the old enemy, Scotland, the
  much fancied Dutch team and Switzerland. The opening match of the tournament
  saw us take on the Switzerland, after getting off to a fine start with a
  Shearer goal, England were frustrated by the battling Swiss team and conceded
  a late penalty to only draw 1-1. Next came the match everybody had been
  talking about for the last six months, the return of England V Scotland. On a
  barmy summers afternoon in June, Paul Gascoigne scored the finest goal I have
  ever seen. Already 1-0 through Alan Shearer, David Seaman brilliantly saved
  McCalister penalty and whilst most of Wembley was still celebrating, Gazza
  touched the ball over Hendry’s head and whilst still sprinting forwards,
  smashed the ball in the back of the net before it had even bounced, brilliant.
  Three days later and with the team looking good for qualification, the mighty
  Dutch team arrived at the twin towers expecting to win. El-Tel’s team had
  other ideas and led 1-0 at half time after Shearer (who else) scored from the
  penalty spot. It was after the interval where the team came to life, in a 10
  minute period early in the second half, Holland barely touched the ball as
  England played some of the finest football ever. Shearer doubled his tally and
  Sherringham notched a brace himself and we were soon 4-0 up. Meanwhile at
  Villa Park, Ally McCoist had put the sweatys socks 1-0 up against the Swiss
  and as things stood, Scotland would join us in the next round!!!! This though
  is unacceptable, the men in white let Patrick Klivert stroll though our
  defence to score a consolation goal and put the Jock(e)s out on goals scored – what a shame. Winning the group was important for England
  because that meant we’d stay at Wembley for the duration of the tournament
  and it’s there where we met Spain in the quarter finals. The Spanish team
  played well and were unlucky as two of their goals were disallowed. After&nbsp;
  120
  minutes the deadlock had not been broken and was eventually settled on
  penalties. Only six years after the world cup semi-final in Turin, Stuart
  Pearce bravely stepped up and converted his spot-kick. The hero of the hour
  was undoubtedly David Seaman who saved the final Spanish kick to put us in the
  semi final. Eventual winners Germany were our opponents that day and were
  quickly 1-0 down when Shearer scored his fifth goal of Euro 96. Stephan Kuntz
  equalised and the game went to a &quot;golden goal&quot; situation. England
  had the better chances and after hitting the post, Gascoigne couldn’t quite
  reach the ball as it spun along the open goal and once more penalties were
  needed to separate the two teams. After eleven converted kicks, up stepped
  poor Gareth Southgate and the rest, as they say, is history…</font></p>
