<u>The Spiral Nebulae</u></font>
<p>
<table><tr><td><a href="./images/telescopes/leviathan.jpg">
<img align=left src="./images/telescopes/leviathan_sm.jpg"></a></td><td><b>
In 1755 Immanuel Kant first speculated on philosophical grounds that there 
might exist "<i>island universes</i>" of stars like the Milky Way.  In 1845,  
<a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/education/archive/local_heroes/biogparsons.shtml">William Parsons</a>, third Earl of Rosse, using a 72-inch home-built 
telescope at <a href="http://ireland.iol.ie/birr-castle/index.html">Birr 
Castle</a> in Ireland, nicknamed the 
<a href="http://ireland.iol.ie/~mcgibbon/birrno.html">Leviathan of Parsonstown
</a>, determined that some of the nebulae have 
Spiral Structure and adopted Kant's term for them.</td></tr>
<tr><td><font color="blue"><b>The Leviathan of Parsonstown</font></b></td>
<td>&nbsp;</td></tr></table>
<p>
The nature of the Spiral Nebulae was debated both literally and figuratively
for the next several decades.  A summation of the arguments was made in
the <a href="http://antwrp.gsfc.nasa.gov/diamond_jubilee/debate_1920.html">
Shapley-Curtis Debate</a> (1920), between two eminent Astronomers, Harlow 
Shapley, then at Mt. Wilson and H. D. Curtis of Lick Observatory.
Hubble detected Cepheid variables in 
<a href="http://www.mtwilson.edu/History/cal90/cal1090.html">Messier 31</a> - 
the Andromeda Galaxy - and other Local Group galaxies, establishing distances 
of hundreds of thousands to millions of light years.
<p>
<font size=+2 color="burgundy">
<u>Hubble Classification</u></font>
<p>
Hubble also developed a classification scheme for galaxies which, with minor 
revisions remains in use today.  Hubble divided galaxies into two principal 
catagories Elliptical and Spiral, with a third "Irregular" category left to
catch those galaxies which defied regulae classification.  Elliptical galaxies,
which essentially consist of only a <i>nuclear bulge</i> component are 
subdivided among seven ellipticity classes from 
<font color="burgundy"><i><u>E0</font></u></i> (circular) to 
<font color="burgundy"><i><u>E7</u></font></i> (cigar shaped).  Numerically 
the ellipticity is given by
<p><font color="burgundy" size=+1>
<center> 10(a-b)/a </center></font></p>
where a is the length of the major axis and b is the length of the minor axis.
Of course, the Hubble Classification does not tell us the <i>true shape</i> of 
the galaxy (<i>e.g.</i> an E0 could be a "cigar" seen down its barrell).
Statistical arguments suggest that the distribution of galaxies among the 
ellipticities is roughly uniform.
<p>
Spiral galaxies are subdivided among three classes <font color="burgundy"><i>
<u>Sa</u>, <u>Sb</u>, <u>Sc</u>,</font></i> with a parallel sequence for 
Barred Spirals <font color="burgundy"><i><u>SBa</u>, <u>SBb</u>, <u>SBc</u>
</font></i>.  (More modern classifications add a class <font color="burgundy">
<i>Sd</font></i> and subdivide among the classes <font color="burgundy"><i>Sab,
Sbc, Scd</font></i>.) The three criteria are:
<ul>
<li>Size of nuclear bulge (<font color="burgundy"><i>Sa</font></i>=large; 
<font color="burgundy"><i>Sc</font></i>=v. small)
<li>Openness of spiral pattern (<font color="burgundy"><i>Sa</font></i>=tightly
wound; <font color="burgundy"><i>Sc</font></i>=v. open)
<li>Resolution of arms into supergiant stars and HII regions 
(<font color="burgundy"><i>Sa</font></i>=smooth, few small HII regions; 
<font color="burgundy"><i>Sc</font></i>=clumpy, lots of bright supergiants 
&amp; HII regions).
</ul>
Upon completion of the scheme Hubble realized that he needed to allow for an 
intermediate classification between Elliptical &amp; Spiral
<font color="burgundy"><i><u>S0</u>, <u>SB0</u></font></i>
<p>

diagram"</i>:

Irregular galaxies come in two types:  
<ul>
<li><u><i><font color="burgundy">Irr I</font></u></i> which are in some sense 
a logical extension of the Hubble tuning fork, having characteristics "beyond" 
those of class Sc - high gas content, dominant presence of a young population. 
<font color="burgundy"><i>Irr I</font></i>  galaxies may show bar-like 
structures and incipient spiral structure like the Large Magellanic Cloud, 
below.  Such galaxies are sometimes referred to as "Magellanic Irregular" 
galaxies.
<p>
<li><u><i><font color="burgundy">Irr II</font></u></i> which are galaxies 
which defy classification because of some form of disturbance.  M82, shown 
below, is undergoing an intense period of star-formation.
</ul>

<p>
The unique relationship between the Hubble Class and other properties, most 
especially <i>stellar populations</i> suggests that the Hubble Class is 
fundamentally related to the way in which galaxies form and evolve.  Originally
Astronomers thought that the Hubble sequence might be an evolutionary sequence
with galaxies evolving from right-to-left (or <i>vice versa</i>) along the 
tuning fork.  This cannot be correct because to the best of our knowledge
<p>
<center><font size=+1 color="burgundy"><i>
All galaxies are about the same age ~ 15 billion years old</center></font></i>
<p>
It is clear, however that the Hubble sequence is a sequence in <i>evolutionary 
state</i>, however.  For some reason Elliptical galaxies formed all their 
stars a long time ago, using up all their gas, so that new stars are no longer 
forming, there is virtually no young stellar population nor gas nor dust.
Spirals, on the other hand, have retained much of their gas and are continuing 
to form stars.  The answer is reminiscent of the <i>Nature vs. Nurture</i>
debate occurring among biologists, psychologists &amp; sociologists about 
human behaviour:
<p>
<ul>
<li><i><u><font color="burgundy">Nature</font> - 
<font color="blue">Rotation/Angular Momentum</font></i></u>:  The rotational 
characteristics of Spiral &amp; Elliptical galaxies are different as shown 
below;  Spiral galaxy disks have well organized rotational dtructure whereas
elliptical galaxies have random orbits.  A rotating cloud of protogalactic gas 
would certainly contract more slowly than one without rotation and could only 
contract along the rotation axes, forming a disk as we see in spiral galaxies.
It is plausible to suppose that a non-rotating, freely collapsing protogalaxy 
would reach higher densities at earlier times, using up its gas rapidly in a 
time of order a billion years.  As the rotating protogalaxy contracts more 
slowly to a disk shape, it would not reach such high densities, star-formation 
would proceed more slowly, preserving gas for future epochs of star formation
<p>
<center>

<td><img src="./images/sp_orbits.gif"></td></tr>

Disk</font></td></tr>

<tr><td align="center"><font color="blue">Random Orbits in an Elliptical<br>
                       (or Spiral Bulge)</font></td></tr></table></center>
<li><i><u><font color="burgundy">Nurture</font> - 
<font color="blue">Physical Environment</font></i></u>: The environments of 
differing types of galaxies are also distinct.  Most rich clusters contain 
only E &amp; S0 galaxies, whereas Spirals and Irregular galaxies are found
principally in small groups.  It has become apparent that collisions, both 
between galaxies themselves and between the fragments that conglomerated 
together to build galaxies, are important in the formation/evolution of 
galaxies.  As we have discussed collisions between galaxies have a much more 
dramatic effect upon the gas than upon the stars because of the great 
interstellar distances.  Collisions and mergers of galaxies can stimulate 
intense bursts of star formation as molecular clouds collide.  Collisions can 
also strip the gas out of galaxies.  
</ul>
<p>
It is quite likely that both effects play a role in determining the nature of 
galaxies.  Certainly collisions and mergers must play a role in the formation
of cluster elliptical galaxies.  It seems almost certain, for example, that 
the giant elliptical galaxies seen at the centers of rich clusters formed as 
galaxies gradually accumulated at the center of the cluster's strong 
gravitational field.  While rotation probably also plays a role, especially 
for disk (spiral and S0) galaxies, the history of the Milky Way suggests that 
the contraction of the Galaxy to a disk was slower than had previously been 
thought with accumulation of 10<sup>6</sup> - 
10<sup>8</sup>M<sub><img src="./images/doodads/odot_sm.gif"></sub> fragments 
forming first the bulge, then the halo and gradually settling down into
a disk due to angular momentum.  The idea of this "bottom-up" accumulation 
of larger galaxies from smaller fragments is also supported by current 
calculations of the formation of structure in the early Universe.  Our picture 
of the formation of galaxies remains hazy, however, and much remains to be 
understood.
