They are effective regardless of their orientation relative to the gene and can often be placed at considerable distances (thousands of base pairs) from the transcribed gene without diminishing their effect. Enhancers can be stimulated by binding of auxiliary transcription factors.<img src="i22551.GIF" border="0" align="right">			<li>Several upstream enhancers act on the yellow gene of <i>Drosophila</i>. When the gypsy transposable element inserted between two yellow gene enhancers, the one upstream of the insertion ceased to stimulate yellow transcription. The stimulatory effect of the downstream enhancer didn't decrease. (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=1327958&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>)<img src="i22552.GIF" border="0" align="left">			<li>DNA segments are tested for insulating activity by measuring the activity of genes with segments inserted between enhancer and promoter elements of genes and of those genes with the segment inserted on the other side of the enhancer. The segment may contain an insulator if the gene's activity is much lower in the first configuration than it is in the second configuration (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=1327958&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>). 			<li><a href="MG2255.html"><img src="i22553.GIF" border="0" align="right"></a>DNA segments are also tested for insulating activity by comparing the variation in reporter gene expression in individual transformants, transformed with constructs that either contain or do not contain the segments at their ends (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=1848159&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>).		</ul>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></p>		</center>		<h4>Interpretations</h4>		<ul>			<li>Enhancers stimulate transcription independently of location and orientation, when examined in model systems.  In vivo, there must be limits on the realm of effect of an enhancer since some neighboring genes must experience differential regulation.			<li>The gypsy transposable element must contain an element that insulates the yellow gene from the effect of the upstream enhancer.			<li>Insulators have two testable properties:			<ul>				<li>They insulate promoters from the effects of enhancers when the insulator is between the promoter and enhancer.				<li>They substantially reduce the variability of transgene expression when added to transformation constructs.			</ul>			<li>Insulators demark chromatin regions that are coordinately regulated and allow neighboring regions to be regulated independently.			<li>Variation in expression among transformants is due to the transforming DNA integrating into different chromosomal locations and becoming controlled by the regulatory environment into which it is integrated.			<li>Insulators are of value to biotechnologists since they provide more predictable and uniform expression properties of transformants.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a name="Interp"></a><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a><br>			</p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Info"></a>Further information<img src="nav6.gif" width="69" height="71" align="right" usemap="#NavigatorB1D2AD80"><map name=""></map></h4>		<ul>			<li>Several distinct insulator elements have been identified (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=11228144&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>). Specific proteins recognize distinct insulator elements. For example, the insulator at the upstream end of the chicken <font face="Symbol">b</font>-globin locus, cHS4, and that at the downstream end, 3'HS, both contain binding sites for the CTCF protein. The CTCF binding sites are necessary and sufficient for enhancer blocking activities. However, the CTCF binding sites are not required for the integration position effect, suggesting that two distinct phenomena may be at play (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=10458613&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>). 			<li>Drosophila mutant in the <i>mod</i> gene fail to make the protein that binds the gypsy insulator. In the absence of mod, all yellow enhancers are silent. Only in the presence of mod is the downstream enhancer active.			<li>cHS4 and 3'HS are associated with DNAse I hypersensitivity.			<li>Though placing one insulator between an enhancer and a promoter blocks the enhancement of promoter activity, placement of two copies in tandem does not (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=11161205&dopt=Abstract">ref1</a>, <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=11161206&dopt=Abstract">ref2</a>). An interpretation consistent with the observations is that insulators work in pairs, forming loops. The small loop formed by tandem insulators is thought to be ineffectual.			<li>Insulators may play a role in parental <a href="../MGW1/MG1382.html">imprinting</a>.  In imprinting, insulator sequences may be differentially methylated depending on the sex of the parent from which the chromosome came.  Some methylated insulators do not bind CTCF while non-methylated ones do (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=10839546&dopt=Abstract">ref1</a>, <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=10839547&dopt=Abstract">ref2</a>, <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=10837224&dopt=Abstract">ref3</a>)		</ul>		<center>			<p><a name="bottom"></a><a href="MG2253.html">Last</a> | <a href="MG229.html">Vocabulary</a> | <a href="MG225.html">Overview</a> | <a href="#Facts">Top</a> | <a href="MG223.html">Next</a><br>			<br>			</p>		</center>		<p><img height="66" width="102" src="divlogo2.gif" align="right"><map name=""></map>		<hr>		</p>		<p>This is page 2255 of <a href="../MG01.html">Molecular Genetics</a> by <a href="http://opbs.okstate.edu/~melcher/UM.html">Ulrich Melcher</a>, &copy; 2001<br>		<hr>		</p>		<address>E-mail inquiries to <a href="mailto:umelcher@biochem.okstate.edu">U.
