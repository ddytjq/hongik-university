					<h1>The Genesis of Gene-Technology Legislation in the Non-Human Domain</h1>
					
				</td>
			</tr>

			<tr>
				<td width="25%" valign="top" class="side"> &nbsp;</td>
				<td width="75%" valign="top">
			<dl>
				<dt>2000</dt>
				<dd>On March 1, 2000 <a href="http://www.admin.ch/uvek/doku/presse/2000/d/00030106.pdf">the announcement and the draft for a change of the Federal Law on Environment</a> are released by the Bundesrat. </dd>
				<dt>1999</dt>
				<dd>The Gen-Lex Package proposes to extend the  				<a href="http://www.admin.ch/ch/d/sr/814_01/">Law on Environment</a> so that it forms the central law of the revision. Furthermore, seven other laws and a federal resolution are to be changed. With the release of the <a href="http://www.uvek.admin.ch/doku/presse/1999/d/99082507.pdf">Ordinance on Closed Systems</a>, of the <a href="http://www.uvek.admin.ch/doku/presse/1999/d/99082506.pdf">Ordinance on Dissemination</a> 
				[<a href="http://www.uvek.admin.ch/doku/presse/1999/d/99082505.htm">1</a>], of the 	<a href="http://www.uvek.admin.ch/doku/presse/1999/d/99082508.pdf">Ordinance on the Protection of Employees</a> (within the revision of the USG of 1995) and of the <a href="http://www.admin.ch/ch/d/sr/8/817.02.de.pdf"> Ordinance on Food</a>, the Swiss standards of biological safety correspond to the <a href="05">international</a> ones.</dd>
				<dt>1998</dt>
				<dd>The Federal Council creates the <a href="http://www.buwal.ch/stobobio/ekah/d/index.htm">Ethics Commission for the Non-Human Domain</a> [<a href="http://www.uvek.admin.ch/doku/presse/1998/d/98042701.htm">1</a>, 
				<a href="http://www.uvek.admin.ch/doku/presse/1999/d/99042302.htm">2</a>]. In the campaign against the Gene-Protection-Initiative (GSI) the Federal Council brings forth the Gen-Lex Package as an indirect counter-proposition. The GSI is rejected with 67% by the population and the cantons on June 7, 1998.</dd>
				<dt>1996/1997</dt>
				<dd>A motion of the Parliament, the Gen-Lex-Motion, charges the Federal Council to identify possible deficiencies in the legislation in the area of human and non-human gene-technology on the basis of the IDAGEN report. The deficiencies are to be corrected as fast as possible. The announcement for the changes in laws and ordinances judged necessary is to be handed in at latest in 1997. The draft of the changes plans the <a href="03">adaptation of eight laws and of one federal resolution</a>. At this point the supervising 
<a href="http://www.admin.ch/bvet/">BVET</a> charges Prof. Dr. Rainer. J. Schweizer from the University St. Gallen with the formulation of a "Report on the Realization of the Gen-Lex-Motion".
The newly created Federal Commission for Biological Safety [<a href="http://www.admin.ch/cp/d/32938F27.514A@gs-edi.admin.ch.html">1</a>, 
				<a href="http://www.admin.ch/ch/d/sr/814_01/a29h.html">2</a>, 
				<a href="http://www.admin.ch/ch/d/sr/818_101/a29e.html">3</a>]  takes up its work and therewith the role of the SKBS created in 1986.</dd>
				<dt>1993-1995</dt>
				<dd>The report prepared by the IDAGEN presents nine projects in which existing laws are modified to meet legislative needs. The Federal Council accepts the proposition of the IDAGEN and therewith definitively decides against an all-encompassing gene-technology law. 
1993 is also the year in which the Gene-Protection-Initiative is handed in. It demands the protection of all living beings especially the strict prohibition of the breeding of transgenic animals, the dissemination of transgenic plants and the patenting of "inventions" in the field of gene-technology. The initiative proposes a text to be incorporated in the BV as Article 24 decies. The Federal Council and the Parliament rejects the GSI without submitting an alternative proposition.</dd>
				<dt>1992</dt>
				<dd>The new Article 24 novies (since 1999 <a href="http://www.admin.ch/ch/d/sr/101/a119.html">Article 119</a> for Human and <a href="http://www.admin.ch/ch/d/sr/101/a120.html">Article 120</a> for Non-Human Gene-Technology) is anchored in the Federal Constitution "for the protection of man and his environment from abuse by reproduction- and gene-technology". The idea of elaborating an all-encompassing "Law on Gene-Technology" in the non-human domain is rejected on the basis of the KOBAGO report. An interdepartmental work-group for gene-technology (IDAGEN) is appointed to work out "a program for the middle- to long-term legislative measures necessary in the area of gene- and biotechnology as well as a concept for the cooperation of existing and potential new commissions and coordinating-bodies ".</dd>
				<dt>1987</dt>
				<dd>The "Schweizer Beobachter" initiates the "Beobachter-Initiative" for the regulation of gene-technology. This is followed by many other initiatives. All of them are withdrawn in 1992 when the Bundesrat establishes a basis for Swiss gene-technology legislation by an addendum to the <a href="http://www.admin.ch/ch/d/sr/101/">Federal Constitution (BV)</a>.</dd>
				<dt>1986</dt>
				<dd>For the first time gene-technology is addressed legally in Switzerland. A Commission for Biological Safety in Research and Technology is created by the academies (<a href="http://www.samw.ch/">SAMW</a>, 
				<a href="http://www.sanw.ch/">SANW</a>, and 				<a href="http://www.satw.ch/">SATW</a>). It formulates the first rules for the handling of genetically modified organisms. The Federal Council charges the 				<a href="http://www.edi.admin.ch/">EDI</a> and the 	<a href="http://www.evd.admin.ch/">EVD</a> with the appointment of an interdepartmental coordination office charged with the approval procedure for the use of organisms containing rDNA: the KOBAGO. This body is to be in charge of following up the practical application of gene-technology and of existing legislative mechanisms for the following six years.</dd>
				<dt>1985</dt>
				<dd>The Federal Office for Education and Science appoints an ad-hoc work-group to produce a report: "Reflections and Advice for the Development and Use of Organisms Whose DNA Has Been Recombined in vitro" under the supervision of Dr. Ernest Bovay.</dd>
				<dt>1975</dt>
				<dd>Scientists of the main industrial nations become aware of the potentials of gene-technology and create a committee to formulate regulations in this new field. These are taken over by the <a href="http://www.nih.gov/">National Institutes of Health</a> of the USA that lay down the first official rules for gene-technology. These rules are used as a foundation for the national gene-technology regulations in Great Britain, France and Germany. They are also analyzed and declared applicable to Switzerland by the Commission for Experimental Genetics of the SAMW under supervision of Prof. Werner Arber.</dd>
			</dl>
