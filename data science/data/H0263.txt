Others, <i>o</i><sup>o </sup>mutations, completely prevented enzyme synthesis.					<LI>Some mutations in another gene, the <I>i</I>gene, also resulted in constitutive expression (<I>i</I>- mutations). 				</UL></TD>			</TR>			<TR>				<TD WIDTH="120">o+ z+ </TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">&lt; 0.1</TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">100</TD>			</TR>			<TR>				<TD WIDTH="120">o<SUP>c</SUP>z+</TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">15</TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">90</TD>			</TR>			<TR>				<TD WIDTH="120">o+ z+/o<SUP>c</SUP> z-</TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">&lt; 0.1</TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">90</TD>			</TR>			<TR>				<TD WIDTH="120">o+ z-/o<SUP>c</SUP> z+</TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">90</TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">250</TD>			</TR>			<TR>				<TD WIDTH="120">i+ o<SUP>o</SUP> z+ </TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">&lt; 0.1</TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">&lt; 0.1</TD>			</TR>			<TR>				<TD WIDTH="120">i- o+ z+ </TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">200</TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">200</TD>			</TR>			<TR>				<TD WIDTH="120">i+ o<SUP>o</SUP> z+/i- o+ z+</TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">1 </TD>				<TD ALIGN="RIGHT">260</TD>			</TR>			<TR>				<TD COLSPAN="4">				<UL>					<LI>The two constituitive mutations <I>i</I>- and <I>o</I><sup>C </sup>mapped to different loci and behaved differently in partly diploid					bacteria. The <I>i</I>+ allele dominated over <I>i</I>- and acted in trans. The <I>o</I><sup>C </sup>mutations were not dominant and did not  act in trans.					<LI>Based on these and other results, Monod proposed his now famous					operon model. 				</UL></TD>			</TR>		</TABLE>		</P>		<CENTER>			<P><A HREF="#Facts">Facts</A> | <A HREF="#Interp">Interpretations</A> | <A HREF="#Info">Further Info.</A> | <A HREF="#bottom">Other Pages</A></P>		</CENTER>		<H4><A NAME="Interp"></A>Interpretations</H4>		<UL TYPE="circle">			<LI>Monod's approach is an example of the <STRONG>genetic approach</STRONG> to understanding the regulation of transcription. 			<LI>Beta-galactosidase synthesis is an example of induction. A small molecule, a <STRONG>co-inducer</STRONG>, induces enzyme synthesis. 			<LI><FONT FACE="Symbol">b</FONT>-galactosidase regulation is also an example of <STRONG>negative regulation</STRONG>, where binding of the protein inhibits transcription. 		</UL>		<CENTER>			<P><A HREF="#Facts">Facts</A> | <A HREF="#Interp">Interpretations</A> | <A HREF="#Info">Further Info.</A> | <A HREF="#bottom">Other Pages</A> <BR>			</P>		</CENTER>		<H4><A NAME="Info"></A>Further information</H4>		<UL TYPE="square">			<LI>Repression systems, in which the presence of a small molecule,			a <STRONG>co-repressor</STRONG>, turns gene expression off, also exist. 			<LI>Inhibition is in part due to occlusion of the <A HREF="MG2223.html">promoter</A>. In some other systems, examples of positive regulation, protein			binding stimulates transcription. <STRONG>Positive regulation</STRONG> of <FONT FACE="Symbol">b</FONT>-galactosidase synthesis also occurs: synthesis is stimulated			by binding of the cyclic AMP binding protein. Positive regulation			is thought to act by altering DNA conformation. 			<LI>In some cases, the inducing (or repressing signal) is extracellular.			In many such cases, the regulators consist of <A HREF="../MGW4/MG4372.html">two components</A>: a membrane sensor and a DNA-binding protein. The stimulated			sensor activates the DNA-binding protein. <IMG SRC="nav4.gif" WIDTH="69" HEIGHT="71" ALIGN="right" USEMAP="#nav4B1F889C9">			<MAP NAME="nav4B1F889C9">				<AREA COORDS="3,55,24,69" SHAPE="rect" HREF="MG2223.html">				<AREA COORDS="4,29,26,50" SHAPE="rect" HREF="MG229.html">				<AREA COORDS="24,3,44,27" SHAPE="rect" HREF="MG22.html">				<AREA COORDS="43,29,58,56" SHAPE="rect" HREF="MG223.html">				<AREA COORDS="26,30,40,56" SHAPE="rect" HREF="MG222.html">				<AREA COORDS="42,59,66,69" SHAPE="rect" HREF="MG2225.html">			</MAP>			<LI>The expression complex controlled by a single operator is called			an <STRONG><a href="http://www.ndsu.nodak.edu/instruct/mcclean/plsc431/prokaryo/prokaryo2.htm">operon</a></STRONG>. Multiple operons controlled by the same repressor or activator			are called a <STRONG>regulon</STRONG>. 			<li><a href="http://www2.kenyon.edu/depts/biology/HHMI/HHMImeeting2000/lac1/lac1c.htm">Quiz</a> yourself on <a href="http://www.orst.edu/instruction/bb492/lectures/Regulation.html">lac</a> operon <a href="http://www.blc.arizona.edu/marty/411/Modules/lacmut.html">genetics</a>.		</UL>		<CENTER>			<P><A NAME="bottom"></A><A HREF="MG2223.html">Last</A> | <A HREF="MG229.html">Vocabulary</A> | <A HREF="MG222.html">Overview</A> | <A HREF="#Facts">Top</A> | <A HREF="MG2225.html">Next</A> <BR>			<BR>			</P>		</CENTER>		<P>		<HR>		</P>		<P>This is page 2224 of <A HREF="../MG01.html">Molecular Genetics</A> by <A HREF="http://opbs.okstate.edu/~melcher/UM.html">Ulrich Melcher</A>, &copy; 1997, 1998, 2001<BR>		<HR>		<img height="72" width="108" src="divlogo2.gif" align="right"></P>		<ADDRESS>E-mail inquiries to <A HREF="mailto:umelcher@biochem.okstate.edu">U.
