<TD><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1"><B>Funding Higher Education</B></FONT></TD>
<TD ALIGN=RIGHT><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1">3/7/2000</FONT></TD></TR>
<TR><TD COLSPAN=2><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1">How To Avoid Student Debt<BR>&nbsp;</FONT></TD></TR>
<TR><TD COLSPAN=2><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1"><p>The new academic year will soon be upon us, and with it yet another change 
        in the financial support regime for students. For students (other than 
        Scots), the maximum fee contribution will increase to £1,050 per year. 
        For Scottish students, the fee will effectively be deferred until after 
        their course has ended.</p>
      <p> With very few exceptions, there are no grants for new students, but 
        the maximum level of student loans has been increased (see table below). 
        At the same time, the entitlement to loans has been expanded. For the 
        2000/01 academic year only, 25% of the loan will be means-tested (generally 
        based on parental taxable income): the balance is available to everyone.</p>
      <p> The greater availability of loans is something of a two-edged sword. 
        It should make life easier for those students who find that their parents 
        are not willing (or able) to provide the assistance that the Department 
        for Education and Employment deems appropriate. On the other hand, it 
        will mean students will almost certainly have accumulated even larger 
        debts by the time they graduate.</p>
      <p> For example, a student studying outside London could accumulate debts 
        of over £11,000 on a three- year course, assuming the retail prices index 
        and therefore maximum loan levels rise by 2.5% per year. Under current 
        rules, this debt is repayable at the rate of 9% of pre-tax earnings over 
        £10,000. So, for example, a graduate earning £20,000 a year would be expected 
        to make loan repayments of £75 per month, out of net income. Allowing 
        for loan repayment, basic rate tax and national insurance contributions, 
        a graduate with an outstanding loan will lose 41p in the pound, once he 
        or she breaks the £10,000 a year earnings barrier. That is more than the 
        higher rate of tax, which is currently payable when earnings exceed at 
        least £32,785.</p>
      <p> If your children <font face="symbol">-</font> or grandchildren <font face="symbol">-</font> 
        are likely to go to university, would you want them to start their working 
        life with a five-figure debt and a marginal 'tax' rate of 41%? If your 
        answer is no, then you need to start planning now to ensure the funds 
        are available as an alternative to the student loan. There are many ways 
        in which this can be done, both through regular savings and by a lump-sum 
        investment. In some instances, tax can be saved by taking advantage of 
        the student's likely non-taxpayer status. </p>
