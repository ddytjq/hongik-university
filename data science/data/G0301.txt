<td class="bodytext">
            <div class="linkstyle1"> 
              <p><br>
                The <a href="../m_mm.html">science goals</a> of MAP broadly dictate 
                that the relative CMB temperature be measured accurately over 
                the full sky with high angular resolution and sensitivity. The 
                overriding priority in the design was the need to control <a href="../m_help/h_glossary.html">systematic 
                errors</a> in the final maps. The specific goal of MAP is to <i>map</i> 
                the relative CMB temperature over the full sky with an angular 
                resolution of at least 0.3&deg;, a sensitivity of 20 &micro;K 
                per 0.3&deg; square pixel, with systematic artifacts limited to 
                5 &micro;K per pixel. </p>
              <p>To achieve these goals, MAP uses differential microwave radiometers 
                that measure temperature differences between two points on the 
                sky. MAP will observe the sky from an orbit about the <a href="ob_techorbit1.html">L2 
                Sun-Earth Lagrange point</a>, 1.5 million km from Earth. This 
                vantage point offers an exceptionally stable environment for observing 
                since the observatory can always point away from the Sun, Earth 
                and Moon while maintaining an unobstructed view to deep space. 
                MAP will scan the sky in such a way as to cover ~30% of the sky 
                each day and as the L2 point follows the Earth around the Sun 
                MAP will observe the full sky every six months. To facilitate 
                rejection of foreground signals from our own Galaxy, MAP will 
                observe in five separate frequency bands from 22 to 90 GHz.</p>
              <p><a name="anchor2"></a><span class="hdline3"><b>MAP Spacecraft Overview:</b></span></p>
              <p>The main features of the MAP spacecraft are shown in the following 
                collection of diagrams and photographs. The most prominent feature 
                is a pair of back-to-back telescopes that focus the microwave 
                radiation from two spots on the sky roughly 140&deg; apart and 
                feed it to 10 separate differential receievers that sit in an 
                assembly directly underneath the optics. Large &quot;elephant 
                ear&quot; radiators provide cooling for the sensitive amplifiers 
                in the receiver assembly. The bottom half of the spacecraft provides 
                the services necessary to carry out the mission including command 
                and data collection electronics, attitude (pointing) control and 
                determination, power services and a hydrazine propulsion system. 
                The entire observatory is kept in continuous shade by a large 
                deployable sun shield that also supports the solar panels.</p>
