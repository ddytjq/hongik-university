</H1>
</CENTER>
<P><FONT SIZE=3> Because some wavelengths of light do not penetrate the
Earth's atmosphere, observations at these wavelengths must be taken from
space. Each different waveband provides different information about the
object being observed, so it is useful to do as many types of observations 
as possible.  Below are links to web pages about the many satellite missions 
being conducted by NASA, The European Space Agency (ESO), and the Japanese 
Space Agency (ISAS).</FONT></P>

<TD> <A HREF="http://xrtpub.harvard.edu/">Chandra</A> X-Ray Observatory

It was renamed to honor 
<A HREF="http://www.nap.edu/readingroom/books/biomems/schandrasekhar.html">

<TABLE><TD><IMG SRC="./images/space/rosat.gif"></TD>
<TD> <A HREF="http://euve.gsfc.nasa.gov/docs/rosat/rosgof.html">ROSAT 
X-Ray Satellite </A> (Rontgensatellit) was named in honor of  
<A HREF="http://www.nobel.se/laureates/physics-1901-1-bio.html">Wilhelm 
Conrad Rontgen</A>, the German physicist who discovered x-rays. It was 
originally scheduled to be launched from the Space Shuttle, but was delayed
by the Challenger accident. The launch was rescheduled for June,1990 
using a Delta-II rocket.  <BR>
Roentgen took the first x-ray of a human 
<A HREF="http://legacy.gsfc.nasa.gov/docs/xte/learning_center/universe/x-ray.html"> hand </A></TD></TABLE>

<TD>

The Rossi X-RAY Timing Explorer, launched in 1996. High time-resolution 
satellite designed to study the x-ray variability of pulsars, black holes,
and quasars. <BR> UCSD's High Energy Astrophysics group, led by Dr. Rick 
Rothschild, constructed the <A HREF="http://mamacass.ucsd.edu:8080/hexte/hexte.html">HEXTE</A>

RXTE <A HREF="http://heasarc.gsfc.nasa.gov/docs/xte/xhp_image.html">
images</A> and 
<A HREF="http://heasarc.gsfc.nasa.gov/docs/xte/learning_center/">
Learning Center</A>.<BR> The satellite was named in honor of 
<A HREF="http://legacy.gsfc.nasa.gov/docs/xte/learning_center/name.html">
Bruno Rossi </A>, a pioneer in the field of X-ray Astronomy.</TD></TABLE>

<FONT SIZE=5><B><U>The Planets:</U></B></FONT>
<BR>
<BR>
<TABLE><TD><IMG SRC="./images/space/cassini.jpg"></TD>
<TD> <A HREF="http://www.jpl.nasa.gov/cassini/">Cassini</A> - NASA/JPL 
Mission to Saturn to study the planet,its rings and moons.The spacecraft is
composed of an 
<A HREF="http://www.jpl.nasa.gov/cassini/Spacecraft/orbiter.html">
orbiter</A> and the 
<A HREF="http://www.jpl.nasa.gov/cassini/Spacecraft/probe.html">
Huygens probe</A> that will be dropped through Titan's atmosphere.<BR>
Because Saturn is 7 x 10<SUP>8</SUP> kilometers from the Sun, solar power
could not be used by Cassini. Instead, the satellite runs on 
<A HREF="http://www.jpl.nasa.gov/cassini/MoreInfo/spacepwr.html">
Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generators</A> which convert the heat produced
by the radioactive decay of plutonium to electricity. This is the
same power source used by both Pioneers, both Voyagers, the Galileo,
and Ulysses missions.<BR>
<A HREF="http://www.jpl.nasa.gov/cassini/Images/">Cassini Images and Videos</A><BR>
Cassini carries a DVD with over 600,000  
<A HREF="http://www.jpl.nasa.gov/cassini/MoreInfo/dvd.html">signatures from Earth</A>
</TD></TABLE>
<BR>
<BR>
<TABLE><TD><IMG SRC="./images/space/galileo.gif"></TD>
<TD><A HREF="http://www.jpl.nasa.gov:80/galileo/">Galileo</A> - NASA/JPL Mission 
to Jupiter. The spacecraft was launched in 1989 and arrived at Jupiter on
December 7,1995. After orbiting Jupiter for two years, Galileo conducted
detailed observations of Europa for 14 months, then went on to Io.<BR><BR> 
<A HREF="http://www.jpl.nasa.gov:80/galileo/images.html">Galileo Image Gallery</A><BR>
<BR><A HREF="http://es.rice.edu/ES/humsoc/Galileo/">Learn about Galileo</A><BR><BR>
You've seen Galileo's middle finger, but have you seen his 
<A HREF="http://www.jpl.nasa.gov:80/galileo/vertebra.html">
fifth lumbar vertebra</A>? </TD></TABLE>
