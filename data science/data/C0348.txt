Pension Scheme Investments
</B></FONT></TD>

<font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="-1">

<P>
<TABLE CELLSPACING="3" CELLPADDING="0" BORDER="0">

<TR VALIGN=TOP>
<TD><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1"><B>Boots Pension Scheme announces the completion of a switch of its equity investments into bonds</B></FONT></TD>
<TD ALIGN=RIGHT><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1">19/12/2001</FONT></TD></TR>
<TR><TD COLSPAN=2><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1">Investment considerations for pensions schemes<BR>&nbsp;</FONT></TD></TR>
<TR><TD COLSPAN=2><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1">There has been considerable press comment about the decision by the Trustees
of the Boots Pension Scheme to switch its £2.3bn assets to long-dated
sterling bonds. "Another nail in the coffin for final salary pension
schemes" has been mentioned, together with speculation over what this
decision says about the state of investment markets worldwide. 
<p>As with all decisions taken by Trustees, what may be appropriate for one
scheme may be wholly inappropriate for another.  Each scheme has its own
special characteristics and circumstances, and decisions should be made
based on what is right for the scheme, rather than merely on what the market
is doing.  Nevertheless you may be interested in the following general
comments from PIFC's team of actuaries. </p>
<p><b>


The views of PIFC's Actuaries: </b></p>
<p>

This Scheme must have been in a 'special situation' to warrant such a
drastic change.  The following factors are relevant for most
schemes. </p>
<p><b>

1)	Long-Term Cost </b></p>
<p>

The long-term cost of a final salary type pension scheme depends on the
performance of its assets relative to the variation in its liabilities over
time.  At a time when yields on  gilts and corporate bonds are low, unless
there are some significant short-term liabilities or guarantees to worry
about, investment should be in whatever will produce the best return over
the long term, and not in just one asset class. </p>
<p>

Long-term historical evidence shows that equities have been a good match for
salary related liabilities.  Fixed Interest assets are likely to be the best
match for guaranteed pensions in payment.  So long as an economy produces
real returns mainly from its private enterprises, a broadly-based equity
portfolio should outperform any other investments in the long term.  This
means that for most pension schemes, investing mainly in equities should
enable many employers to continue to afford to provide their employees with
reasonable pensions in retirement, although inevitably at the risk of some
volatility along the way. </p>
<p><b>

2)	Investment Flexibility vs Pension Guarantees </b></p>
<p>

The incompatibility of investment flexibility and any short-term pension
guarantees is a well-known fact to most professionals who work in the
financial sector.  It appears, however, that the general lack of
understanding of this issue has caused some of the uninitiated to seek
absolute guarantees for final salary pension scheme benefits, even when no
such guarantee has been given in the past.  However the Government is not
forcing full guarantees of the promised accrual rates on final salary
pension schemes, so is there a need to worry about any guarantees?  The
answer depends on the replacement for the Minimum Funding Requirement
(MFR). </p>
<p>  

For example, for an ongoing final salary pension scheme, individual or bulk
transfer values on a 100% fixed interest basis as the MFR replacement would
be likely to cause extra expense.  If a discontinued pension scheme causes a
debt on the employer that is calculated by reference to 100% fixed interest
yields, again, there will be a mismatch between any equity investments and
the discontinuance liability.  This means that employers could face a
significant extra liability on discontinuance.  

If, on the other hand, the MFR replacement is partially linked to an equity
formula, employers will have much more freedom of investment to produce
affordable salary-linked pensions for their employees. </p>
<p><b>

3)	Matching Assets and Liabilities </b></p>
<p>

Trustees are required to consider the liabilities when selecting suitable
investments.  Some pension schemes have many people near to retirement age
or in retirement.  These liabilities are more closely linked to fixed
interest assets than equities, so there is a need to have a reasonably close
match to assets and liabilities.  Trustees should consider the wording of
their Statement of Investment Principles and take specialist advice from
their appointed independent advisers with this issue in mind. </p>
<p><b>

4)	Employer's Accounts </b></p>
<p> 

The new accounting standard FRS17 will be based on corporate bond yields, so
future corporate financial reporting will include much more variable final
salary pension scheme figures.  Increased investment in corporate bonds
should reduce accounting risks, but at the risk of increasing the long-term
cost of the pension scheme. </p>
<p><b>

5)	Conclusion </b></p>
<p>
 
Problems could arise in the future for employers with ongoing final salary
pension schemes, unless a sensible replacement can be found for the MFR.  In
our view Trustees should not rush to sell even part of their equity
portfolio unless they have significant pensioner liabilities, or members
near retirement, which they have not matched with fixed interest type
assets; even then, they should seek professional advice. </p>
<p>
 
If, on the other hand, employers are planning to discontinue their final
salary pension scheme, there is a need to continue to monitor the proposed
changes to the MFR, some of which could be introduced on an interim basis.
Again, professional advice could be important. </p>
<p><b>

In our opinion, sustained 100% investment in fixed interest assets is likely
to prove a very expensive way to fund a non-mature final salary pension
scheme. </b></p>
<p>
  
Needless to say, professional advice from practitioners such as PIFC's team
of actuaries should be taken at an early stage.  As a starting point, they
should be able to estimate the possible effects of future changes in
critical areas such as accounting and short-term funding
requirements. </p>
<p>
