and largely ignored by the United States, Asia's first World Cup finals have brought sharply diverging verdicts.<br />
<br />
            With the Brazil-Germany final still to come, attentions in western Europe, Germany apart, had already turned elsewhere - from Tim Henman's quest for glory at Wimbledon to French hopes for a Tour de France winner.<br />
<br />
            Italians and Spaniards were still fuming over what they perceived to be referee bias against their teams and in favour of co-hosts South Korea who accounted for both of them.<br />
<br />
            Americans cast a brief, amused eye over the quarter-final appearance of their side before switching back to the baseball season in full swing.<br />
<br />
            But in Asia, the finals have been greeted enthusiastically especially with South Korea breaking the mould in making it all the way through to the semi-finals.<br />
<br />
            &quot;South Korea, you are still the pride of Asia,&quot; said the Vietnamese daily Thanh Nien following the co-host's 1-0 loss to traditional powerhouse Germany in the last four.<br />
<br />
            Others spoke of a golden era about to dawn for Asian soccer and there was even anger at what was seen as sour grapes from Europe and South America over early exits for France, Argentina, Italy, Spain and Portugal.<br />
<br />
            Asked if Brazil and Germany meeting in the final had in some way salvaged the tournament's place in history, French great Michel Platini was taken aback.<br />
<br />
            &quot;That's a European slant on things!&quot; he complained.<br />
<br />
            &quot;The World Cup is not a competition where the France's or Italy's of this world have to win. It would not have been a scandal if the final had been between Turkey and South Korea.<br />
<br />
            &quot;It's true that there has been a lack of truly talented players, but you have to be pleased to see how football is developing everywhere.&quot;<br />
<br />
            Platini's words were echoed by the greatest player ever to grace the World Cup finals, Pele.<br />
<br />
            The Brazilian scoffed at suggestions that the level of play had been lower than at previous finals, allowing traditional minnows on the international stage to cause upset after upset.<br />
<br />
            &quot;The level has been good. This World Cup was fantastic, so many teams surprised.<br />
<br />
            &quot;It was good for the game. The USA, Japan, South Korea and Senegal doing well was good for the sport.<br />
<br />
            &quot;FIFA should support the under-developed countries more.&quot;<br />
<br />
            Pele, who starred in Brazil's winning campaigns of 1958 and 1962, said that Asia had proved that the continent deserved an extra berth at the finals.<br />
<br />
            FIFA President Sepp Blatter explained away the poor performances of European countries by saying that players were involved in too many club games and called on national associations to take immediate action.<br />
<br />
            &quot;Some top leagues in Europe have 20 teams. This is too much. If the leagues and associations will not stop it then one day FIFA must dictate to them,&quot; he warned, although how he would achieve that was never explained.<br />
<br />
            But for others it was all a question of planning, preparation and peaking at the right time, something that the South Koreans did to perfection under Dutch coach Guus Hiddink.<br />
<br />
            Aime Jacquet, who enjoyed full access to his players before he led France to the title in 1998 said the earlier than usual start to the finals had been significant.<br />
<br />
            &quot;The main thing you need to prosper at a World Cup is time to prepare and I think they could all have done with an extra week before the tournament started.<br />
<br />
            &quot;If they had had that, things might have been different. Certainly that was the difference between these finals and France 98 which began 10 days later.&quot;<br />
