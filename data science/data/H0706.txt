      <td width="607" height="4" align="center" colspan="2">
        <p align="left"><font color="#000080">This section of the tutorial
        explains how mass and resolution affects mass labeling and could
        possibly explain the delta masses observed between observed and expected
        mass.&nbsp; It all depends on what you expect and how your mass labeling
        software behaves.&nbsp; At low mass the &quot;A&quot; peak (monoisotopic
        peak), will dominate and at higher mass the largest isotope will be
        closer to the average mass of the compound.&nbsp; The spectra bellow
        will illustrate some of these general principles and we will attempt to
        explore some of the common mass labeling problems.</font></td>

      <td width="630" height="4" align="center" colspan="2">
        <p align="left"><font color="#000080">When operating a mass spectrometer
        at lower resolution (normal for LC/MS) an isotopic display as
        illustrated in Figure A may look like the spectrum shown in Figure
        B.&nbsp; The monoisotopic peak is still identifiable.&nbsp; Peak
        labeling software will most likely pick the peak top of the monoisotpoic
        peak in Figure B and label this as the mass.&nbsp; Most software will
        have difficulty centroiding the cluster in Figure B to deliver a mass
        that is near to the average mass.&nbsp; One may presume that the mass
        delivered by most mass labeling software will be closer to the
        monoisotopic mass when the peak is below ~1700 amu.&nbsp; Each operator
        should investigate how their software behaves.&nbsp; Some labeling
        software is quite flexible allowing the operator to modify centroiding
        parameters.&nbsp;</font></td>

        Figure D illustrates the difficulty encountered at what we like to call
        the crossover mass.&nbsp; The crossover mass occurs when the A+1 peak is
        competing with the &quot;A&quot; peak for base peak status. If the mass
        assignment software is just a peak top picking utility then the mass
        assignment for Figure D (solid line) may be significantly different than
        the peak centroid (dashed line).</font></td>

      <font color="#000080">Figure E shows an isotopic display where the A+1 peak
      is in greater abundance than the &quot;A&quot; peak.&nbsp; Figure F
      illustrates how the local maximum, (solid line), intensity moves closer to
      the centroid, (dashed line), of the merged cluster.&nbsp; At higher masses
      the peak top will essentially be very close to the centroid and average

    <tr>
      <td width="630" height="12" colspan="2">
      <font color="#000080">Labeling Conclusion:&nbsp; The easy take home points
      are, at low mass (&lt;700 amu) the local maximum in an isotopic display will be close to the
      monoisotopic mass. At masses above 2000 amu the
      local maximum will shift to be closer in agreement with the average mass
      of the compound.&nbsp; Mass labeling software may have difficulties with
      masses between 1000 and 2000 amu depending on the software centroiding
      capabilities.</font>
