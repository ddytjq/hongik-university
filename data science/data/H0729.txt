      <td width="560" height="9" align="center" colspan="2" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF">
        <h1 align="center"><b><font size="+3">The Truth About Splitting</font></b></h1>
      </td>
      <td width="121" height="9" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFCC">
        &nbsp;
      </td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="560" height="9" align="center" colspan="2" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF">&nbsp;</td>
      <td width="121" height="9" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFCC">&nbsp;</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="560" height="185" align="center" colspan="2" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        <p align="left">The majority of <b>commercial capillary HPLCs</b> split
        their flow down from conventional flow rates in order to drive capillary
        HPLC columns.&nbsp; Splitting is a fact of life for capillary
        chromatography.&nbsp; A few <b>commercial capillary HPLC </b>systems
        have the ability to perform gradient HPLC in the low µL/min
        range.&nbsp; A non-splitting capillary HPLC is preferable for a
        number of reasons which will be discussed in the <b>splitting issues</b>
        section below.&nbsp; However, when choosing a commercial capillary HPLC
        system be sure to compare the two types of systems with a strict set of
        analytical criteria.&nbsp; Compare retention time stability,&nbsp; peak
        area statistics and linearity of a quan curve when choosing a
        system.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

        <p align="left">All experts agree that currently a split will need to be
        performed to drive smaller capillary HPLC columns at sub-microliter flow
        rates.&nbsp; It is thought that a lower split ratio is preferable.&nbsp;
        For example, splitting down to 100 nL/min. from 200 µL/min. is a split
        ratio of 1:2000 and splitting down from 3 µL/min. is only a split ratio
        of 1:30.&nbsp; It would be our advice that systems with a high split
        ratio should be compared functionally in a demo with those that have a
        low split ratio.&nbsp; &quot;Prove it to me&quot; should always be the
        attitude when choosing a new analytical instrument.&nbsp;</p>

        <p align="left">&nbsp;</p>

      <td width="534" height="27" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        <p align="left">Flow splitting&nbsp; uses a simple inexpensive device,
        the tee, and it works.</td>
      <td width="121" height="329" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFCC" rowspan="6"><a href="art/simplecap.jpg" target="_blank"><img border="2" src="art/simplecap_small.jpg" alt="simplecap.jpg (102530 bytes)" width="100" height="127"><br>

        <p>&nbsp;</p>
        <p>&nbsp;</p>
      </td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="26" height="32" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        (-)&nbsp;&nbsp;</td>
      <td width="534" height="32" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        <p align="left">If the column starts to foul the split ratio will change
        as more mobile phase is forced through the restriction capillary.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="26" height="27" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        Sol.</td>
      <td width="534" height="27" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        <p align="left">When splitting, care must be taken to measure the flow
        rate periodically before starting a batch of analyses.&nbsp; If a column
        begins to foul the first clue is that retention times begin to lengthen.&nbsp;
        Capillary guard columns can be used to protect the analytical capillary
        column from fouling but this will not prevent a split ratio change in
        the case of fouling.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="26" height="28" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        &nbsp;</td>
      <td width="534" height="28" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        ------</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="26" height="34" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        (-)&nbsp;</td>
      <td width="534" height="34" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        <p align="left">When using a flow splitting tee the flow rate changes
        over the course of the organic gradient as the viscosity of the mobile
        phase changes.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="26" height="27" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        (+)</td>
      <td width="534" height="27" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        <p align="left">We have not been able to demonstrate this.&nbsp; See flow rate change experiment on
        the <a href="http://www.ionsource.com/tutorial/capillary/truth2.htm" target="_blank">following
        page</a>.</td>
    </tr>

      <td width="121" height="246" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFCC" rowspan="3"><a href="art/integrated.jpg" target="_blank"><img border="2" src="art/integrated_small.jpg" alt="integrated.jpg (110907 bytes)" width="100" height="74"></a>
        <p><font size="3">Figure 3</font></p>
        <p>&nbsp;</td>

    <tr>
      <td width="26" height="23" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        (-)&nbsp;</td>
      <td width="534" height="23" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        <p align="left">When using flow splitting most of the mobile phase goes
        to waste through the restriction capillary.&nbsp; HPLC grade solvent is
        expensive and it is even more expensive to dispose of as waste.&nbsp;
        The ideal situation is to have the capability not to split.&nbsp; Less
        waste means less environmental impact, less inspection solvent storage
        impact, and a healthier work environment.&nbsp;&nbsp;</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="26" height="31" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        (+)</td>
      <td width="534" height="31" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top">
        <p align="left">1) In isocratic mode the untouched waste exiting the tee
        can be fed right back into the solvent reservoir for recycle.<br>
        2) As mass spectromitrists we spend most of our time
        nebulizing&nbsp; acetonitrile and methanol at atmosphere at high flow
        rate, 1 to 0.2 mL / min.&nbsp; The reduced flow rate of capillary
        chromatography reduces this environmental hazard.</td>
