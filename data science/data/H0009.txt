<center><b><font color="#FF00FF"><font size=+1>Genetic Distances Among
Primates</font></font></b></center>

<p>To read, match the species on the left with the species you want to
compare it to along the top. For example, to see the distance between human
(Hsa) and gorilla (Ggo), first find Ggo on the left,then, since human is
the first species, read down the first column to where they intersect (0.01903317).
To see the distance between gorilla and rabbit (Ocu), go down to Ocu on
the left, go to the 4<sup>th</sup> column (since Ggo is the 4<sup>th</sup>
taxa). There you will find 0.33493841.&nbsp; You will find that rabbit
is essentially equidistant from everything else (between 28-33%).&nbsp;
Not surprising – it's the outgroup.&nbsp; The distance measure I used was
the uncorrected ‘P’, which is the default measurement in the program I
used (PAUP*4.0). You will also notice a general trend as you go down, say,
the first column.&nbsp; Human differs from the chimps by ~ 1.6 %; gorilla
by ~1.9%;then a jump to orang at ~3%.&nbsp; Gibbon (Hla) ~4%.&nbsp; Then
a jump to ~6% for all the Old World monkeys (with a few minor exceptions),
from Mmu (Rhesus) to Tob (type of langur).&nbsp; Then a jump at the New
World monkeys at ~11-12% (Cal to&nbsp; Ase – not sure of their common names,
but it is <i>Callicebus</i> to <i>Alouatta</i>). Then the Tarsiers (Tba,
Tsy) at ~24%.&nbsp; Then some prosimians (Ocr(galago) to Cme (bush baby)),
varying from 21-28%. Then rabbit (Ocu) at ~33%.&nbsp; The jumps do not
represent kinds or typological distances – they represent adaptive radiations.

<p><tt>Overview of primate taxonomy (with emphasis on the branches more
closely related to humans - I'm so darned anthropocentric).</tt>
<p><tt>Primates</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Haplorhini - the "single nosed" primates</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Anthropoidea - monkeys, apes, and
humans</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Catarrhini - Old World
monkeys, apes, and humans</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Hominoidea
- apes and humans</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
Hominini - great apes and humans</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
Hominina - gorillas, chimps, and humans</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
Homo? - chimps and humans</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
Homo - humans</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
Pan - chimps</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
Gorilla - gorillas</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
Pongina - orang-utans</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
Hylobatini - gibbons</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Cercopithecoidea
- Old World monkeys</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Platyrrhini - New World
monkeys</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Tarsiiformes - tarsiers</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Strepsirhini - the "wet nosed" primates</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Lemuriformes - lemurs</tt>
<br><tt>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Lorisiformes - lorises</tt>
<p>Note - Tarsiers were previously lumped in with lorises and lemurs as
prosimians, but most reasearchers now think they are closer to the monkeys,
apes, and humans.
