    color="#AB29B6">Coaching Box </font></h2>
    <h2 align="center">Feature Clinic</h2>
    <div align="center"><center><table border="0" cellpadding="3" width="672">
      <tr>
        <td width="162"><img border="0" src="../../../../images/Clinics/Teaching_Run_N_Jump.gif">
        </td>
        <td width="488"><h2 align="center"><font color="#000000" size="2">Teaching
          the Run and Jump Pressure Defense</font></h2>
        <h3 align="center"><font color="#000000"><small><small>by Alan Lambert</small></small><br>
        <small>President of The Basketball Highway</small></font></h3>
          <p>&nbsp;
        </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td width="660" colspan="2">
    </center>
        <h2 align="center"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman"><font size="1"><font color="#FF9933"><br>
        </font>
        </font></span><font size="2"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">Introduction&nbsp;</span></font></h2>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">Through
        the past thirty-five to forty years I have played and coached basketball
        I have seen the coming and passing of many different offenses and
        defenses.&nbsp; Some types of defenses seem to stand the test of time
        while others pass away as fads, trophies of defenses who's time has come
        and gone.&nbsp; The one constant that can be found all relatively
        successful defenses is the element of constant pressure.&nbsp; With the
        advent of the shot clock some 25 years ago, and now the reduction in the
        time required to advance the ball across the mid-court line from 10
        seconds to 8 seconds, pressure
        can extend the court away from size or individual player mismatches and
        turn the advantage back toward the favor of the defending team.&nbsp; I
        believe that the Run and Jump defense can be a very effective defense
        when properly taught and practiced.</span></font></p>
        <h3><font color="#808000" size="1">What Is the Run and Jump?</font></h3>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">The
        Run and Jump is a man-to-man pressure defense which can be extended full
        court to
        the baseline, 3/4 court or even half court with multiple variations as
        simple as 1 Jump and done
        scenarios. This
        defense is designed to either speed or slow the offenses tempo, and to
        create a large number of turnovers in a short period of time.&nbsp;It is
        a good defense if you are behind late in the game and need to create
        indecision on the part of an offensive team.&nbsp;&nbsp; It
        is also designed to help your team make up for physical limitations in
        your match-ups on the defensive end of the court.&nbsp; It is NOT a
        trapping defense, but a surprise jump switch and rotation defense
        involving either 2, 3, 4 or all 5 players on the defensive team, and designed to slow the
        basketballs progress and add confusion to a patterned
        offensive pressure attack.&nbsp;&nbsp;</span></font></p>
        <h3><font size="1" color="#808000"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">Can
        Your Team Play the Run and Jump?</span></font></h3>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">Prior
        to making a decision to commit the time and energy to teach the Run and
        Jump you must ask these questions:</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">1.
        Do you in your season planning have the time and patience to teach the
        Run and Jump defense properly?&nbsp; It may take more than one season
        for most of your players to adequately learn this defense, and it will
        take a substantial amount of your practice time to perfect the technique
        and judgment necessary to effectively make it work.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">2.
        Are the majority of your players going to be with your team for several
        seasons?&nbsp;&nbsp; The more familiar your players are with each other the
        better your chances of succeeding with the Run and Jump.&nbsp; If you
        are constantly changing personnel, you will find it difficult to have
        success with this defense and should consider playing other pressure
        defenses.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">3.
        Do you have the type of players (physical and mental traits) who can
        successfully execute the Run and Jump?&nbsp; Your players must be
        intelligent, with good quickness, and a willingness to work extra hard
        to be successful defensively.&nbsp; The greater the number of players on
        your team who possess these traits the better your chances of having
        success with the Run and Jump.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">4.
        Does your team talk a lot, on the court I mean?&nbsp; The Run and Jump
        is definitely NOT a defense for teams that don't communicate well.&nbsp;
        Talking, and telling your teammates where you are and your intentions
        are crucial to an effective Run and Jump.</span></font></p>
        <h3><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman"><font color="#808000">In
        What Situations Should You Use the Run and Jump Defense?</font></span></font></h3>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">The
        eloquent thing about a defense's ability to play the Run and Jump is that
        you can red light-green light the defense like a light switch without
        obviously tipping off the offensive team, either in full out pressure or given
        set backcourt defensive situations.&nbsp; Use these guidelines to help you decide
        <b><font color="#0077B0"> when to play the Run and Jump</font></b>.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">1.
        When you have size mismatches at one or more positions.&nbsp; The Run
        and Jump can help prevent a 1 on 1 mismatch in your half court
        defense.&nbsp; This is accomplished by forcing the offensive team to use
        as much of the shot clock as possible in advancing the ball over the
        mid-court line and in entering their normal offensive patterns, thereby
        reducing the time and opportunity to score of our their practiced sets.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">2.
        When your opponent has poorly skilled dribblers and passers.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">3.
        To slow down quick transition teams after made baskets.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">4.
        To increase or decrease the tempo of the game.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">5.
        When you want to increase the number of high percentage shots your team
        is getting by increasing the number of steal/turnover conversion
        opportunities.&nbsp; Teams that successfully play the Run and Jump can
        normally force 20-30 turnovers a game.&nbsp; If your team is having
        problems getting high percentage shots in their half court offense,
        maybe you need this defense to improve your scoring opportunities.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">6.
        When you want to surprise and panic your opponent.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">7.
        When you need to contain a very quick ball handling guard.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">8.
        Usually after made baskets.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">9.
        When your opponent has a very weak back up point guard.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">10.
        You can play various types of Run and Jump but they fall basically into
        two categories.&nbsp; One would be a high percentage/low gamble (for
        most game situations), and the other a high gamble/low percentage (all
        out Run and Jump all the time) best used when trailing late in games or
        wanting to quickly turn up the pressure on a physically inferior team to
        break open a close game.</span></font></p>
        <h3><font size="1" color="#808000"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">Where
        Should You Play the Run and Jump?</span></font></h3>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">It
        is best to play the Run and Jump primarily in the offensive teams
        backcourt.&nbsp; Occasionally, with very quick defenders, you can play
        this defense over the mid-court line but in these cases it should
        be a &quot;one and done&quot; run and jump, and when the dribbler can be
        funneled into the corner over the line and turned.&nbsp; Sidelines and
        mid-court corners in the offensive teams back court are excellent
        locations to execute the Run and Jump.&nbsp; Finally, a good general
        rule for the Run and Jump Defense is NOT to jump in the middle of the
        court.&nbsp; There are exceptions, especially when&nbsp; the dribbler
        attacker is totally out of control, but this is a very difficult spot on
        the court to recover defensively if the attacker can advance the ball
        quickly forward by the pass which you will learn through experience.&nbsp;
        Forcing the ball out of the middle enable your help/jump defenders have
        a clearer picture of whether or not they are ball side or help side in
        their defensive positioning.</span></font></p>
        <p><font size="1"><span style="mso-bidi-font-family: Times New Roman">Now
        that we have taken you into some elementary details of playing the Run
        and Jump let us know proceed to teaching you the basic rules of the Run
        and Jump. <a href="../../../../Talk/Coaching_Box/Clinics/RunNJump/rj_rules.asp">Click here to learn about the
        basic rules of the Run and Jump</a>.<br>
        </span></font></p>
