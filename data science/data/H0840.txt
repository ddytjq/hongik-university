<td valign="top"><p class="articleheadline">Environmental Benefits</p></td>
<td rowspan="2"><IMG height=152 src="html/images/image003.gif" width=190 align=right></td>

<tr>
<td valign="bottom">
<p class="articlesubheadline">Biotech crops an eco-efficient solution to global
food needs.</p>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<br>

<p>With about 200,000 people added to the global population
every day, one of <i>the</i> key issues
confronting the 21<sup>st</sup> century is how to feed a growing world
population without destroying the environment. </p>

<p>Increasingly, plant biotechnology is viewed as an important
tool in creating the eco-efficient world of the future, where more food can be
produced while at the same time reducing environmental impact and resource use.
</p>

<p>Experts fear that in the coming
decades, half of the world’s remaining 6 billion acres of forests will be lost
to agricultural expansion.<sup>1</sup>
If forests continue to disappear at the current rate, as many as 20 percent of
all tropical forest species of plants and animals could become extinct in 30
years.<sup>2</sup></p>

<p>That’s because rising
population, coupled with income growth, are placing more and more demands on
the world food supply.  According to a <a href="http://www.unfpa.org/swp/2001/english/ch02.html" onclick="javascript:window.open('index.asp?redirect=http%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Eunfpa%2Eorg%2Fswp%2F2001%2Fenglish%2Fch02%2Ehtml','','').focus();return false;">United Nations report<ximg src="html/images/spacer.gif" border="0" width="4"><ximg src="html/images/ealink.gif" border="0" width="10"></a>,
farmers will need to at least double their production over the next 25 years to
feed these additional, more affluent people.<sup>3</sup></p>

<p>Annual increases in agricultural
yields in recent years, however, are holding at just 1.3 percent a year<sup>4</sup>
&mdash; a fraction of the yield increases of 30 years ago when improved hybrid seeds
spurred an earlier revolution in agriculture. That’s why so many people,
including groups like the <a href="http://www.dca.net/isas/" onclick="javascript:window.open('index.asp?redirect=http%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Edca%2Enet%2Fisas%2F','','').focus();return false;">International
Society of African Scientists<ximg src="html/images/spacer.gif" border="0" width="4"><ximg src="html/images/ealink.gif" border="0" width="10"></a>, are looking to plant biotechnology to boost
production while preserving the environment.<sup>5</sup>
</p>

<p>They see plant biotechnology as
a tool to help preserve the Serengeti Plains, the Amazon rainforest and other
global treasures for generations to come.</o:p></p>

<p class="articlepullquote">Conserving water and topsoil</p>

<p>Already, plant biotechnology is
proving to be a valuable resource to help steward the environment.</p>

<p>“Reduced tillage practices in
soybeans saved 247 million tons of irreplaceable topsoil in 2000, and reduced
the number of times a farmer had to run equipment over the field, saving 234 million gallons of fuel,” according to an American Soybean Association
study in
November 2001.<sup>6</sup></p>

<p>That’s because biotech soybeans
&mdash; with built-in protection from weeds &mdash; require less spraying, which preserves
both topsoil and soil moisture. This is important because the earth loses
enough topsoil each year to cover the state of Missouri,<sup>7</sup>
and water tables in many areas of the world are falling (agriculture consumes
about 70 percent of the fresh water people use every year). <sup>8</sup>
</p>

<p class="articlepullquote">Getting the most from marginal land</p>

<p>New crop varieties being tested
will do even more to help preserve the environment. Several different crops are
being developed that can thrive in difficult conditions such as areas plagued
by drought, soil that is contaminated by salt from too much irrigation, or soil
laced with aluminum, which is toxic to plants.</p>

<p>Some examples include:</p>

<ul>

<li><p>Tomatoes designed to grow in overly irrigated saline soil.<sup>9</sup></p></li>

<li><p>Rice and corn more tolerant of aluminum, a common soil toxin.<sup>10</sup></p></li>

<li><p>Rice that can survive long periods under water in extremely wet climates.<sup>11</sup></p></li>

</ul>

<p class="articlepullquote">Adapting to the environment</p>

<p>Getting the most from
compromised soil is critical since, by one estimate, a fourth of the world’s
agricultural land, pastures, forests and woodlands have already been degraded
from misuse or overuse.</p>

<p>Bt corn is one example of how
biotech crops can take up the challenge of feeding more people.</p>

<p>According to the <a href="http://www.ncfap.org/" onclick="javascript:window.open('index.asp?redirect=http%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Encfap%2Eorg%2F','','').focus();return false;">National Center for Food and Agricultural Policy<ximg src="html/images/spacer.gif" border="0" width="4"><ximg src="html/images/ealink.gif" border="0" width="10"></a>,
the average yield for Bt corn in 1999 was 3.3 bushels per acre higher than for
conventional corn, enabling growers to produce an additional 66 million bushels
in total.<sup>12</sup> These gains
were achieved while spraying less.</p>

<p>Cotton farmers have also seen
yield increases and reduced spraying. In 1999, Bt cotton generated an additional $99 million in
revenue and 260 million pounds of increased production.<sup>13</sup> Cotton farmers used 2.7 million pounds
less insecticide in 1999 to combat the budworm, bollworm and pink bollworm than
in 1995 (the year before Bt cotton was introduced in the United States).<sup>14</sup> One <a href="index.asp?id=1711">Louisiana cotton farmer</a> said she reduced total
applications of insecticides per acre by 74 percent.<sup>15</sup></p>

<p class="articlepullquote">A sustainable solution</p>

<p>In the name of protecting
croplands and natural areas, some argue for a simpler, organic style of
farming. But others believe that that’s not a viable option because organic
farming yields are much lower than yields using modern agricultural practices.
At the same time, populations and food consumption are on the increase.</p>

<p>Dennis Avery, director of global
food issues for the Hudson Institute, says a global shift to organic farming
would require plowing down at least 6 million square miles of wildlife habitat
to make up for the lower organic yields. That is more than the total land area
of the United States. <sup>16</sup></p>

<p>Biotechnology offers a
sustainable middle ground, helping farmers grow more food while protecting
their farmland from degradation &mdash; and prairies, forests and other natural areas
from cultivation.</p>

<p>Patrick Moore, a former
president of Greenpeace, is among those who promote the importance of
biotechnology in providing more food in an environmentally friendly manner:
“[Biotechnology] will be good for the environment in reducing reliance on
chemicals and in using less land to grow the same food for our 6 billion people
in the world.”<sup>17</sup></p>
