        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1901</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Max 
            Planck makes his <i>quantum hypothesis</i> 
            -- that energy is carried by indistinguishable units called <i>quanta</i>, 
            rather than flowing in a pure continuum. This hypothesis leads to 
            a successful derivation of the black body radiation law, now called 
            Planck's Law, although in 1901 the quantum hypothesis as yet had no 
            experimental support. The unit of quantum action is now called Planck's 
            constant.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1905</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Swiss 
            patent clerk Albert Einstein proposes Planck's quantum hypothesis 
            as the physics underlying the photoelectric effect. Planck wins the 
            Nobel Prize in 1918, and Einstein in 1921, for developing quantum 
            theory, one of the two most important developments in 20th century 
            physics.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1905</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Einstein 
            publishes his simple, elegant Special Theory of Relativity, making 
            mincemeat of his competition by relying on only two ideas: 1. The 
            laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames, and 2. The speed 
            of light is the same for all inertial observers.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1905</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Poincar&eacute; 
            shows that Lorentz transformations in space and time plus rotations 
            in space form a group, which comes to be known as the Lorentz group. 
            The Lorentz group plus translations in space form a group called the 
            Poincar&eacute; group.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1907</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Minkowski 
            publishes Raum und Zeit (Space and Time), and establishes the idea 
            of a spacetime continuum </font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1909</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Hilbert's 
            work on integral equations later leads to the concept of a <b>Hilbert 
            space</b> in quantum mechanics</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1915</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Emmy 
            Noether publishes <b>Noether's Theorem</b>, 
            discovering the relationship between symmetries and conserved currents 
            that was crucial to the later development of quantum gauge field theory 
            and string theory</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1915</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Einstein, 
            with Hilbert in stiff competition, publishes his stunning General 
            Theory of Relativity, and is lucky enough to be able to find observational 
            support for his theory right away, in the perihelial advance of Mercury, 
            and the deflection of starlight by the Sun.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1916</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">German 
            astrophysicist Karl Schwarzschild, serving on the Russian front in 
            WWI, mails Einstein his paper on the Schwarzschild metric and Einstein 
            presents it at a meeting of the Prussian Academy of Sciences. Six 
            months and another major paper later, Schwarzschild dies of illness 
            on the front.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1921</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Theodor 
            Kaluza follows Einstein's advice and publishes his highly unorthodox 
            ideas about unifying gravity with electromagnetism by adding an extra 
            dimension of space that is compactified into a small circle. Kaluza-Klein 
            compactification will become a rich subject of exploration in particle 
            theory 60 years later.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1925</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Werner 
            Heisenberg shows that his quantized probability operators form a non-commutative 
            algebra. Born and Jordan point out to him that this is a matrix algebra, 
            and the matrix formulation of quantum mechanics is born. He gets the 
            Nobel Prize in 1932.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1924</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Louis 
            duc de Broglie proposes the particle-wave duality of the electron 
            in his doctoral thesis at the Sorbonne. He gets the Nobel Prize in 
            1929.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1926</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">After 
            learning of the work of de Broglie, Erwin Schrödinger develops his 
            wave equation version of quantum mechanics, and unravels its relationship 
            to the matrix formulation of quantum mechanics by Heisenberg. He shares 
            the Nobel Prize with Dirac in 1933.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1926</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Young 
            Cambridge math student Paul Dirac discovers the operator algebra behind 
            Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle for his doctoral thesis.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1927</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Heisenberg 
            discovers the Uncertainty Principle that bears his name.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1928</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Dirac 
            introduces a relativistic quantum equation for the electron, an equation 
            now known as the <b>Dirac equation</b>. 
            His equation predicts the discovery of the positron, and he shares 
            the Nobel Prize with Schrodinger in 1933.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1928</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Werner 
            Heisenberg, Hermann Weyl and Eugene Wigner begin an exploration of 
            symmetry groups in quantum mechanics that has far-reaching consequences.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1929</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Edwin 
            Hubble, with the help of his mule driver Humason, observes the redshift 
            of distant galaxies and concludes that the Universe is expanding.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1931</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Einstein 
            stops using the cosmological constant to keep the Universe from expanding.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1931</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Dirac 
            shows that the existence of magnetic monopoles would lead to electric 
            charge quantization.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1931</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Georges 
            De Rham goes to work on his famous theorem in cohomology and characteristic 
            classes, results that would become very important in string theory.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1935</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Young 
            physicist Subramahnyan Chandrasekhar is attacked by famous astronomer 
            Arthur Eddington for his report that there is a stellar mass limit 
            beyond which collapse to what we now call a black hole is inevitable. 
            Chandrasekhar wins the Nobel Prize in 1983 for his work on stellar 
            evolution.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1938</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Wigner 
            constructs a class of irreducible unitary representations of the Lorentz 
            group</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1939</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><i>Elements 
            de Mathematique,</i> by Nicholas Bourbaki, pseudonym for a 
            group of young mathematicians at the Ecole Normale in Paris, is begun. 
            This extended set of works aims to set down in writing what is no 
            longer in doubt, but rather on a boring and rigorous footing, in modern 
            mathematics.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1943</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Chinese 
            mathematician Shiing-Shen Chern begins his work on characteristic 
            classes and fiber bundles that will become an important tool for understanding 
            quantum gauge theories and string theory. </font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1948</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Richard 
            Feynman, Julian Schwinger and Tomonaga Shin'ichiro report that the 
            divergent integrals that plague the quantum gauge field theory of 
            electrodynamics (QED) can be sensibly dealt with through the process 
            of renormalization.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1953</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Based 
            on particle scattering data, Murray Gell-Mann suggests that there 
            is a new quantum number, called hypercharge, which we now call stangeness 
            and recognize as a part of the quark model coming from the strange 
            quark. Gell-Mann receives the Nobel Prize in 1969 for his work on 
            the quark model.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1954</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"> 
            Gell-Mann and Francis Low develop the idea that the physical content 
            of a quantum theory should be invariant under a change of scale in 
            the theory. This is called renormalization group, and it turns out 
            to constrain quantum field theories enough to make it a very powerful 
            tool for analyzing asymptotic behavior of quantum theories. </font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1954</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">C.N. 
            Yang and R. Mills develop non-Abelian gauge invariance, an idea that

            physics.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1954</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"> 
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Eugenio

              theory. </font></p>
          </td>
        </tr>

            physics ceases to make good sense.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1964</font></b></td>

        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1964</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"> 
            Peter Higgs, Francois Englert and R. Brout suggest a method of breaking

        </tr>

            his paper <i>A Model of Leptons</i>, 
            Steven Weinberg relies on Lie group theory combined with quantum field 
            theory to explain the weak nuclear and electromagnetic forces in a 
            single theory, using the Higgs mechanism to give mass to the weak 
            bosons. Adbus Salam and Sheldon Glashow share the Nobel Prize with 
            Weinberg in 1979 for Electroweak Theory.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999">

            Coleman and Jeffrey Mandula prove that well-behaved particle scattering 
            theories can't have symmetry algebras that relate particles of different 
            spin. But the strict consequences of the Coleman-Mandula Theorem were 
            avoided by the supersymmetry algebras that were discovered a few years 
            later.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1968</font></b></td>

            Atiyah and Isadore Singer begin their work on <i>The 
            Index of Elliptic Operators</i>. They prove the Atiyah-Singer 
            index theorem, a powerful mathematical result that will later be used 
            extensively in theoretical physics.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1968</font></b></td>

            Veneziano begins modern string theory with his paper on the dual resonance 
            model of the strong interactions.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999">

            Nambu, Leonard Susskind, and Holger Nielsen independently discover

            quantum mechanics of relativistic vibrating strings, and string theory 
            begins. </font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1971</font></b></td>

            't Hooft publishes his proof that the electroweak gauge theory of

            begins -- the age of quantum gauge field theory.</font></td>
        </tr>

            </font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1971</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Ken 
            Wilson publishes work using the renormalization group to understand 
            the quantum behavior of systems undergoing phase transitions, this 
            opens up the study of critical phenomena in particle physics and leads 
            to greater understading of quark confinement. Wilson wins the Nobel 
            Prize in 1981.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1971</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Soviet 
            physicists Yuri Gol'fand and E. Likhtman extend the Poincar&eacute; 
            algebra into a superalgebra and discover supersymmetry in four spacetime 
            dimensions.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1973</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">David 
            Gross, David Politzer, Frank Wilczek and Gerard 't Hooft arrive at 
            the conclusion that the coupling constant in non-abelian quantum gauge 
            theories vanishes at high energy. This is called asymptotic freedom 
            and is one of the major results in the history of quantum gauge field 
            theory.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1973</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Quantum 
            field theories with spacetime supersymmetry in four spacetime dimensions 
            are discovered by Julius Wess and Bruno Zumino. </font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1974</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Stephen 
            Hawking combines quantum field theory with classical general relativity 
            and predicts that black holes radiate through particle emission, behave 
            as thermodynamic objects, and decay with a finite lifetime into objects 
            that we don't yet understand.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1974</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Magnetic 
            monopole solutions of non-Abelian gauge field theories are found separately 
            by 't Hooft and Moscow physicist Alexander Polyakov.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1974</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Joel 
            Scherk and John Schwarz propose string theory as a theory of quantum 
            gravity, an idea that takes ten years to be widely appreciated.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1974</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Howard 
            Georgi and Sheldon Glashow propose SU(5) for a &quot;Grand Unified 
            Theory&quot; (GUT) of all forces except gravity, the theory predicts 
            that protons could decay. </font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1975</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Instanton 
            solutions of Yang-Mills equations are discovered by Belavin, Polyakov, 
            A. Schwarz and Tyupkin. This is exciting because instantons can tell 
            us about non-perturbative physics that is not approachable by other 
            means of calculation.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1976</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Shing-Tung 
            Yau proves the Calabi conjecture and discovers the Calabi-Yau space, 
            an important development for later progress in string theory.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1980</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Alan 
            Guth puts forward the idea of an inflationary phase of the early Universe, 
            before the Big Bang.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1981</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Michael 
            Green and John Schwarz develop superstring theory.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1981</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">After 
            Schoen and Yau do it in a more traditional manner, Ed Witten uses 
            supersymmetry to prove the positive mass conjecture.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1982</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Mathematician 
            Karen Uhlenbeck shows that Yang-Mills instantons discovered by physicists 
            can be used as a powerful analytical tool in abstract mathematics.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1983</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Witten 
            and Luis Alvarez-Gaum&eacute; derive general formulas for gauge and 
            gravitational anomalies in quantum field theories in any dimension. 
            They show that the gravitational anomalies cancel in type IIB superstring 
            theory.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1983</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Mathematics 
            graduate student Simon Donaldson discovers exotic 4-manifolds, using 
            instanton techniques learned in part from Uhlenbeck.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1984</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Michael 
            Green and John Schwarz show that superstring theory is free from quantum 
            anomalies if the spacetime dimension is 10 and the quantum gauge symmetry 
            is SO(32) or E8 times E8.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1984</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Gross, 
            Harvey, Martinec and Rohm find another class of anomaly-free superstring 
            theories, and call it the heterotic string theory.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1985</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Candelas, 
            Strominger, Horowitz and Witten propose the use of Calabi-Yau spaces 
            for the extra dimensions in heterotic string theory.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1991</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Connes 
            and Lott develop non-commutative geometry, which will find its way 
            into the heart of string theorists at the turn of the millennium. 
            </font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1993</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">In 
            search of an understanding of black hole entropy, <a href="http://arXiv.org/abs/gr-qc/9310026" target="_blank"><font color="#663399">'t&nbsp;Hooft</font></a> 
            suggests the idea that the information in a 3+1-dimensional system 
            cannot be greater than what is need to store it as an image in 2+1 
            dimensions. Susskind generalizes this idea and applies it to string 
            theory in his paper <a href="http://arXiv.org/abs/hep-th/9409089" target="_blank"><font color="#663399">The 
            World as a Hologram</font></a>, and the Holographic Principle 
            is born. </font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1994</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><a href="http://arXiv.org/abs/hep-th/9407087" target="_blank"><font color="#663399">Nathan 
            Seiberg and Ed Witten</font></a> discover electric-magnetic 
            duality in N=2 supersymmetric gauge theory in four spacetime dimensions, 
            with very important applications in both mathematics and string theory.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1995</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><a href="http://arXiv.org/abs/hep-th/9503124" target="_blank"><font color="#663399">Witten</font></a> 
            and <a href="http://arXiv.org/abs/hep-th/9501068" target="_blank"><font color="#663399">Townsend</font></a> 
            introduce the idea of Type IIA superstring theory as a special limit 
            of 11-dimensional supergravity theory with quantized membranes. This 
            begins the M-theory revolution in superstring theory, and leads people 
            to ponder the role of spacetime in string theory.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1995</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">Andrew 
            Wiles, with help from Richard Taylor, completes a rigorous proof of 
            Fermat's Last Theorem.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1995</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><a href="http://arXiv.org/abs/hep-th/9510017" target="_blank"><font color="#663399">Joseph 
            Polchinski</font></a> ignites the D-brane revolution in string 
            theory with his paper describing extended objects in string theory 
            formed by dual open strings with Dirichlet boundary conditions.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1996</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">In 
            their paper <a href="http://arXiv.org/abs/hep-th/9601029" target="_blank"><font color="#663399">Microscopic 
            Origin of Black Hole Entropy</font></a>, Andy Strominger 
            and Cumrun Vafa use D-branes to count the quantum states of an extreme 
            black hole and their result matches the Bekenstein-Hawking value. 
            This stimulates new respect for string theory from the relativity 
            community.</font></td>
        </tr>
        <tr bordercolor="#669999"> 
          <td width="49"><b><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#339966">1997</font></b></td>
          <td width="380"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><a href="http://arXiv.org/abs/hep-th/9711200" target="_blank"><font color="#663399">Juan 
            Maldacena</font></a> finds that string theory in a background 
            of five-dimensional anti-de Sitter space times a five-sphere obeys 
            a duality relationship with superconformal field theory in four spacetime 
            dimensions. The result, called AdS-CFT duality, opens up a new era 
            of exploration in string theory.</font></td>
        </tr>
      </table>
    </td>
    <td width="6" height="102" valign="top"></td>
