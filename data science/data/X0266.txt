				<font class="news">A close contest was predicted at the outset and, as events have unfolded, there is every reason to believe that the six-match one-day series between England and India could go down to the wire, bringing back memories of what happened nine years ago on England&#8217;s previous tour of India.<br><br>On that occasion, at the halfway mark, England led 2-1. They went on to increase the lead by winning the fourth game before India fought back to win the last two games and level the series 3-3. <br><br>At the halfway mark this time, the situation is reversed with India leading 2-1, but if the three games have proved anything it is that there is not much to choose between the teams and one cannot discount a similar shared series this time too. <br><br>The fluctuating fortunes &#8211; India winning the first and third games and England emerging triumphant in the second &#8211; have helped cover up for a lot of mediocre cricket dished out by both sides.<br><br>The contestants have displayed chinks in their armor and have been inconsistent in both the batting and bowling. England, for example, have been over dependent on two of three batsmen and a couple of bowlers, and that is not the kind of situation an international team should find themselves in.<br><br>But then India have not been very different in this regard, something proved by their loss in Cuttack. Also, one must admit they were a bit fortunate in winning at Kolkata. England has wasted little time in exposing their inherent weaknesses and may well do so again before the series is over.<br><br>Fortunately for India, there are certain loopholes in England&#8217;s batting and bowling. This probably cost the visitors victory in the first game. Batting without a sense of purpose and direction saw them lose the third match rather tamely, but it was a really fine performance that saw them close out the match at Cuttack. <br><br>India have stuck to their plan of playing six batsmen and four bowlers. Actually in the absence of a genuine all-rounder, they have few other options.<br><br>But it is good to see Ajit Agarkar finally come good. The slim utility player from Bombay has had his critics who feel that he has had more than a fair share of opportunities.<br><br>That he is talented no-one will deny. But he has not done his admirable gifts much justice. He has all the qualities to make him a genuine all rounder in one day cricket &#8211; he certainly cannot command a place in the Test side &#8211; and his form particularly with the ball, but also to some extent with the bat, has seen to it that India stays ahead at the halfway mark in the series. <br><br>Another plus point from India&#8217;s viewpoint is the encouraging showing of Ajay Ratra. The young wicketkeeper is a bundle of energy and has always been in the thick of action. His work behind the stumps has been exemplary and he is always goading the bowlers and other teammates along.<br><br>He is no rabbit with the bat too, and has verily proved to be a find. He has certainly lived up to the promise he held out while keeping for the victorious under-19 team in Sri Lanka two years ago and it is to be hoped that the selectors will persist with him.<br><br>Certainly the unhappy scenario of the last couple of years when the selectors tried out half a dozen stumpers should not be repeated. India has found an able young keeper and Ratra should be encouraged. <br><br>Sourav Ganguly&#8217;s injury was a blessing in disguise. His withdrawal proved two things &#8211; that there is a readymade opening pair in Tendulkar and Sehwag should Ganguly the batsman be unavailable; and also that there is excellent leadership material in Anil Kumble should he have to drop out for any reason.<br><br>Ganguly has been woefully out of touch, and even though he would have been disappointed at missing out on a vital encounter, perhaps the enforced rest will do him a world of good and he could even recover his form.<br><br>In the meantime, Sehwag showed, not for the first time, that he can be a match for Ganguly in stroke play and run production. And Kumble proved that he has the qualities to lead the country &#8211; perhaps even on a long-term basis.<br><br>He has probably not been given enough credit for Karnataka&#8217;s excellent record in the Ranji Trophy under his captaincy. There was something refreshingly different about his bustling, instinctive approach at Chennai on Friday. <br><br>England will have to tighten up their game if they aim to come back in the series and the inclusion of Graham Thorpe and Andrew Caddick may be a step in the right direction. Both the batting and bowling have to be strengthened and the experienced duo could do a lot in plugging the loopholes in the England ranks.<br><br>If England are to have a chance of winning, it has to be with seam bowling, for once again the spin bowlers have been exposed by quick footed Indian batsmen. <br><br>-<i> Partab Ramchand</i><br><br><b>COMMENT</b><br>Do you agree with Partab's comments?
