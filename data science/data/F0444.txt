	<font size=4><div class=cutline>Product Review:</div></font><BR>
	<font size='-1'><h4>Bar Hopping</h4>  Everyone is familiar with toolbars, and plenty of products can give you basic toolbar functionality.  But UltraToolBars really went above and beyond my expectations of what this type of product could be. In both the quality and the number of the controls in the package, UltraToolBars was a refreshing surprise. This is one bar I'll stay with until last call!  <h4>Let Me Freshen That Up For You...</h4>  Both UltraToolBars and the company that makes it, Infragistics, were new to me. Then I noticed that the product looked a lot like ActiveToolBars Plus from Sheridan Software. And I found that Infragistics is a new company formed by the merger of Sheridan and ProtoView Development, two regulars in the field of ActiveX development with solid reputations. <p> So what did Infragistics do to this product? Well, UltraToolBars is not just a rehash of ActiveToolBars Plus - it's a new package with far more controls! Infragistics mixed ActiveToolBars Plus with Sheridan's ActiveThreed Plus (another perennial favorite) to create this new product, all without a corresponding increase in price! It's like getting two products for the price of one.  <h4>Tools On Tap</h4>  The toolbars in the UltraToolBars package are quite sophisticated, yet easy to use. You have extensive design-time support for setting up an application's toolbar and a library of commonly used toolbar button icons. You can incorporate regular buttons, state buttons, drop-down state buttons, combo boxes, edit areas, and labels into your toolbar, plus there's special support for creating drop-down, pop-up, and fly-out menus. The toolbar setup is intuitive and consisted of creating each type of tool I wanted to use, then assigning it to one or more toolbars that would appear to the user. The design-time interface makes this mostly a drag-and-drop procedure.  <h4>Let Them Serve Themselves</h4>  UltraToolBars provided a complete interface for the end-user of my application to customize the toolbars. As with Office XP's toolbars, I could display uncluttered menus and toolbars containing only my program's most-used functions, yet still make everything easily available to my users, letting them customize the toolbar automatically over time to display the functions they use most.  The control automatically maintains a list of which controls are used most and uses the information to intelligently scale toolbars and menus when there's not enough room to display all they contain. Users can easily drag tool and menu bars to wherever they want (docked or floating) and access hidden options using the automatic "more tools" button.  <h4>Would You Like To Run A Tab?</h4>  UltraToolBars' two controls for creating tabbed dialogs go beyond straight toolbar functionality. Tabbed dialogs are standard features of most Windows apps and even Windows itself. I've often created them for my programs, using a variety of solutions. The tab control in UltraToolBars is stable, well implemented, and among the best controls I've used. To place controls on a tabbed dialog, you use the included lightweight TabPanel as a container; this makes setting up tabbed dialogs and working with controls much smoother. I could also create controls that appeared on every tab, a nice touch. The tab control supported some nice cosmetic effects, such as pictures in the tabs, background textures, and mouse-sensitivity.  <h4>A Meaningful Dialog</h4>  Tabbed dialogs are great, but it's what's on the dialog that counts. UltraToolBars contains plenty of controls for dressing up your dialogs and forms. Each solves a specific interface problem, and some add cool new features to standard controls. Included in this "enhanced" category are the Command and Ribbon button, the Check and Option controls, the Frame control, and the Panel, which functions like a picture box or a label. These controls all share such attributes as support for transparency, pictures, sound, and animation.  The button controls also support non-rectangular shapes, and the Check and Option controls let you define custom graphics, so you can cook up an interface as funky as your imagination will allow. <p> Speaking of funky, there's a Transition control that adds slide-show-style transitions to any form. I had fun with this one, setting up controls as they would appear before the transition and afterwards, then transitioning between the two "phases" of the program with a Broadway-style Uncover Up, a film-noir Horizontal Blinds, a splashy Checkerboard Across effect, or a classy Dissolve. For applications that have to work to keep the user's attention focused (like computer-based training), these transitions are not just flash, but an effective tool.  <h4>Find Your Space</h4>  The other UltraToolBars controls all address space - creating more of it, making the most of it, or changing the way you deal with it. The Splitter, Resizer, and Scroll controls change the way you use the space on a form; the Splash control changes the way the form itself uses space. <p> The Scroll control acts as a window into an area of virtual screen real estate that you can enlarge as needed. I could position and scroll controls at design time, or I could place them via code at any spot within the Scroll's virtual area. The Resizer control is in some ways the opposite of the Scroll - it resizes controls proportionally to occupy the same relative areas and positions within their container. So if the user doubles the height of the form, the height of the controls on the form also doubles, while maintaining the relative positions of the controls. It can automatically handle font sizing, too.  <h4>Add a Splash...</h4>  The Splash control changes the shape of normal VB forms to something other than a rectangle. The obvious use is to create splash screens, and the control even includes a built-in timer for just this purpose. But the uses go beyond that. The concept of "skins" or customizable user interfaces has been gaining ground steadily in the consumer space, led by a variety of media player and Internet applications. Splash is a perfect way to add a cutting-edge design to your apps.  <h4>Ready To Split?</h4>  The Splitter control divides an area of the form into multiple resizable regions separated by splitter bars. The user can resize these regions at runtime using the mouse. I placed a Splitter control on a form and quickly broke it up into the areas I wanted using its simple design-time interface. I easily locked the size of specific panes, and implementing custom resizing behavior was straightforward, thanks to the accessible object model and relevant example code. I could even re-parent entire forms to occupy Splitter panes - particularly handy if you're updating older applications. <p> Speaking of splitting, I have to go. Now that I've found the ultimate toolbar control in UltraToolBars, it's time to find a different kind of bar! <ul> <li>Provides Office-style docking toolbars and tabs, plus a range of other interface controls. <li>The perfect collection of tools for creating state-of-the-art user interface designs. <li>Provide sophisticated customization capabilities to your users without writing code. <li>Supports the latest Office XP and Internet Explorer conventions and styles. </ul></font>
