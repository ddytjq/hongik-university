            <h2><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" color="#6666CC"> 
              <!-- #BeginEditable "title" --><b>A Selected List of Web Sites for 
              Instructors of Introductory Astronomy Courses</b><!-- #EndEditable --></font></h2>
          </td>
          <td>&nbsp;</td>
        </tr>
        <tr> 
          <td valign="top" width="520"><!-- #BeginEditable "content" --> 
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#6666CC"><b><a name="5">5. 
              Laboratory and Observing Exercises</a> </b></font></p>
            <p> </p>
            <blockquote> 
              <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2"><a href="http://solar.physics.montana.edu/tslater/plunger/index.html" target="_blank">Active 
                Astronomy</a> <br>
                Tim Slater has put a curriculum of 21 hands-on activities on the 
                web, many of which take interesting or fresh approaches. Alas, 
                some are missing crucial elements, like charts or images, in the 
                web version. Still, the others are worth exploring. <br>
                <a href="http://www.eso.org/outreach/spec-prog/aol/market/experiments" target="_blank">Astronomy 
                On-Line: The Experiments Shop</a> <br>
                In 1996, astronomers in the countries belonging to ESO organized 
                a series of astronomy education and observing exercises at many 
                levels of difficulty. They vary widely in approach and style, 
                and many may not be to your liking, but you can browse and find 
                some gems. <br>
                <a href="http://www.uark.edu/misc/clacy/BinaryStars/" target="_blank">Binary 
                Stars</a> <br>
                A suite of computer-based activities about binary star astronomy, 
                by Claud Lacy of the U. of Arkansas. <br>
                <a href="http://www.gettysburg.edu/project/physics/clea/CLEAsoft.overview.html" target="_blank">CLEA 
                Software Overview</a> <br>
                Larry Marschall's Contemporary Lab Experiences in Astronomy project 
                has produced some terrific and sophisticated computer-based lab 
                exercises, which can be explored and downloaded from here. <br>
                <a href="http://annie.astro.nwu.edu/labs/m100/" target="_blank">Determining 
                the Extragalactic Distance Scale</a> <br>
                Diane Dutkevich of Northwestern has put a sophisticated series 
                of activities on the web that begin with a Cepheid hunt in M100 
                and finish with estimating the Hubble time. She hints that other 
                labs will follow and be downloadable from the top level of this 
                site. <br>
                <a href="http://www.astro.washington.edu/labs/" target="_blank">Lab 
                Archive: Univ. of Washington Astronomy Dept.</a> <br>
                A rich collection of labs and exercises by U. of Washington faculty 
                and graduate students, including Bruce Balick's clever "Suntanning" 
                lab and a number of good exercises on the distance scale. Note 
                that the site contains both homework assignments and labs, but 
                the descriptions clarify which is which. <br>
                <a href="http://astro4.ast.vill.edu/" target="_blank">Villanova 
                University Astronomy Experiments</a> <br>
                This is a series of 14 lab experiments using Distant Suns and 
                Dance of the Planets software, developed by Frank Maloney & David 
                Steelman, which can be retrieved in WordPerfect 5.1 format. <br>
                <a href="../activities/astroacts.html" target="_blank">Astronomy 
                Activities on the Web</a> <br>
                A listing of web-based K-12 astronomy activities, many of which 
                can be adapted to college labs or assignments. Organized by topic, 
                with capsule reviews. </font></p>
            </blockquote>
            <p> <font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="1"><a href="educsites.html#toc">table 
              of contents</a></font></p>
            <p> <font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#6666CC"><b><a name="6">6. 
              Applets and Other Web-based Exercises</a> </b></font></p>
            <p> </p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2"> Small 
              interactive applications or calculators are springing up all over 
              the web, so our listing is bound to be incomplete. We try to list 
              a few places where collections of aplets can be found: </font></p>
            <blockquote> 
              <p> <font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2"><a href="http://www.cvc.org/astronomy/freeware.htm" target="_blank">Astronomy 
                Freeware and Software</a> <br>
                High school teacher Bill Drennon reviews and tells you where to 
                download useful astronomy programs, including an H-R Diagram Calculator 
                and several planetarium programs; has links to other astronomy 
                shareware sites and a search program. <br>
                <a href="http://janus.astro.umd.edu/intro-astro.html" target="_blank">Astronomy 
                Programs for Beginners</a> <br>
                Several basic but cute "calculation exercises", including calculating 
                the results of an impact by a comet or asteroids, practicing scientific 
                notation, and getting a feel for cosmic distances (by Douglas 
                Hamilton and Mike Asbury at the U. of Maryland). <br>
                <a href="http://www-astro.phast.umass.edu/courseware/vrml/bb/" target="_blank">Blackbody 
                Radiation</a> <br>
                Karen Strom (U. of Massachusetts) has written a guide to the characteristics 
                of blackbody radiation, which includes several interactive VRML 
                and Java exercises. <br>
                <a href="http://webphysics.davidson.edu/Applets/Applets.html" target="_blank">Physlets</a> 
                <br>
                A series of aplets from Wolgang Christian & others at Davidson 
                on basic physics topics, including a number that are relevant 
                to astronomy. <br>
                <a href="http://solar.physics.montana.edu/tslater/real-time/" target="_blank">Real-Time 
                Science Data Access Page</a> <br>
                Tim Slater of Montana State has assembled a set of very useful 
                links to places where current astronomical data can be found on 
                the web, displayed in visual terms that students can usually deal 
                with. Includes solar activity, weather on the planets, etc. <br>
                <a href="http://jersey.uoregon.edu/vlab/" target="_blank">Virtual 
                Laboratory: University of Oregon </a> <br>
                Greg Bothun and his team have put together some powerful aplets 
                on astronomy, astrophysics, and physics at this site. There are 
                sections on galaxy spectra, photon emission/absorption, the Hubble 
                Law, and more. (If you are having trouble, try: <a href="http://jersey.uoregon.edu/vlab/selectj.html" target="_blank">jersey.uoregon.edu/vlab/selectj.html</a>) 
                <br>
                <a href="http://instruct1.cit.cornell.edu/courses/astro101/java/simulations.htm" target="_blank">Web 
                Simulations</a> <br>
                Three stellar applets by Terry Herter of Cornell, including evolution 
                on the H-R diagram. </font></p>
            </blockquote>
            <p> <font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="1"><a href="educsites.html#toc">table 
              of contents</a></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#6666CC"><b><a name="7">7. 
              Interdisciplinary Approaches to Astronomy Teaching</a> </b></font></p>
            <p> </p>
            <blockquote> 
              <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2"><a href="pseudobib.html" target="_blank">Astronomical 
                Pseudo-science: A Skeptic's Resource List</a> <br>
                An annotated list of written and web resources for dealing with 
                astrology, UFO's, the Face on Mars, ancient astronauts, and many 
                similar fringe topics. <br>
                <a href="http://www.harcourtcollege.com/astro/fraknoi/teaching/interdisc.html" target="_blank">Astronomy 
                Plus: Interdisciplinary Approaches to Astronomy</a> <br>
                A bibliography of readings organized by subject; includes astronomy 
                and art, music, poetry, psychology, the environment, law, stamp 
                collecting, historical events, etc. <br>
                <a href="http://www.psi.edu/projects/projects.html" target="_blank">Bill 
                Hartmann's Paintings and Astronomy Projects at Planetary Science 
                Institute</a> <br>
                A gallery of astronomer/artist Hartmann's superb (and accurate) 
                paintings, with some examples of how they can be used to illuminate 
                and explain current research in planetary science. <br>
                <a href="http://www.harcourtcollege.com/astro/fraknoi/stories/stories.html" target="_blank">Science 
                Fiction Stories with Good Astronomy and Physics:</a> <br>
                A listing of novels and stories organized by topic in astronomy, 
                with a brief descriptions. </font></p>
            </blockquote>
            <p> <font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="1"><a href="educsites.html#toc">table 
              of contents</a></font></p>
            <p> <font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#6666CC"><b><a name="8">8. 
              Miscellaneous Resources</a> </b></font></p>
            <p> </p>
            <blockquote> 
              <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2"><a href="educ_bib.html" target="_blank">Astronomy 
                Education: A Selective Bibliography </a> <br>
                A list of books and articles about astronomy education, collected 
                by the present author, not just for college, but many for levels 
                and arenas (including the media, museums, amateur clubs, etc.) 
                <br>
                <a href="useduc.html" target="_blank">Astronomy Education in the 
                U.S.</a> <br>
                A review, by the present author, of the many arenas in which astronomy 
                education takes place today, citing statistics, issues, and exemplary 
                programs in each. <br>
                <a href="http://www.firstmonday.dk/issues/issue3_1/noble/index.html" target="_blank">Digital 
                Diploma Mills</a> <br>
                (Just so we hear dissenting voices), this is an eloquent indictment 
                of the rush to put courses on the web, and the commercial/political 
                interests behind it, by an iconoclastic scholar. <br>
                <a href="http://physics.open.ac.uk/IAU46/newslet.html" target="_blank">International 
                Astronomical Union Commission 46 Newsletter</a> <br>
                Editor Barrie Jones keeps a back file of newsletters from the 
                Commission on Teaching of Astronomy of the IAU. This newsletter 
                varies tremendously in quality and utility, depending on who happens 
                to send in an article or tidbit, but does provide some insight 
                on the international picture in astronomy education. <br>
                <a href="http://spacescience.nasa.gov/education/ecosystem.htm" target="_blank">NASA 
                Office of Space Science Education Ecosystem </a> <br>
                This site explains the complex, intriguing system of topical forums 
                and regional brokers set up by NASA OSS to facilitate cooperation 
                among scientists and educators. <br>
                <a href="http://www.aas.org/%7Ewgae/wgae_index.html" target="_blank">Newsletter 
                of the AAS Working Group on Astronomical Education</a> <br>
                Steve Shawl of the U. of Kansas organizes this electronic bulletin 
                board of announcements and brief reports on many aspects of astronomy 
                & physics education. <br>
                <a href="http://www.aip.org/enews/pen/" target="_blank">Physics 
                Education News (PEN):</a> <br>
                A semi-monthly collection of news, opportunity announcements, 
                and brief resource reviews in physics education; includes a number 
                of astronomy items. <br>
                <a href="http://www.aas.org/%7Eeducation/asp2.html" target="_blank">Survey 
                of AAS Members' Interest in Education</a> <br>
                Over 1300 AAS members returned a survey about their work, interests, 
                and needs in astronomy education. The site contains the analysis 
                of 600 randomly selected surveys. <br>
                <a href="../survey.html" target="_blank">Survey of College Astronomy 
                Instructors</a> <br>
                The Astronomical Society of the Pacific is actively conducting 
                a survey of instructors who teach astronomy at the non-research 
                oriented institutions, including state colleges, liberal arts 
                colleges, community colleges, adult and extension schools, etc. 
                The form can be printed out. <br>
                <a href="http://www.aip.org/statistics/trends/undtrends.htm" target="_blank">Trends 
                in Undergraduate & Graduate Education: Physics & Astronomy</a> 
                <br>
                Selected reports on some of the extensive work of the Statistics 
                division of the American Institute of Physics, which gathers information 
                about all aspects of physics (and sometimes astronomy) education. 
                </font></p>
            </blockquote>
