      <td width="534" height="1" align="center" rowspan="4" valign="top">
        <p align="left"><u><font size="4" color="#0000FF" face="Comic Sans MS">Method
        Notes:</font></u><b><br>
        <br>
        Step 1</b>&nbsp;&nbsp; Some proteins may require a denaturant to expose buried
        disulfides.&nbsp; If this is the case Urea (1-8M) or Guanidine HCl
        (1-6M) may be used in this RCM buffer.&nbsp; If urea is used, avoid
        heating the reduction reaction because this can promote <a href="../carbam/carbam.htm" target="_blank">carbamylation</a>.</p>
        <p align="left"><b>Step 2</b> Some resistant proteins benefit from; higher
        concentrations of the reducing agent, addition of a chaotrope and increased
        temperature.&nbsp; During the reduction of a very resistant protein we have used up to
        80 mM
        dithiothreitol , 6 M guanidne HCl, at 60°C for 60 min..&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
        <p align="left"><b>Step 4</b>&nbsp; Make the iodoacetic acid up in 1M NaOH.&nbsp;
        The iodoacetic
        acid, dry reagent, should be a white flaky crystalline material.&nbsp; It
        should not be brown but it can be slightly yellow.&nbsp; The color in
        the reagent is free iodine which can oxidize your protein or
        peptide.&nbsp; If the reagent is slightly yellow the excess DTT in the
        reduction will quench this effect.&nbsp; Always make and use
        the iodoacetic acid reagent fresh.&nbsp; Be careful with this reagent
        it's dangerous, don't alkylate yourself !&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;<br>
        This is how we weigh out our IAA:&nbsp;&nbsp;<br>
        (A)&nbsp; Tare the
        receiving container (1.5 ml capped tube) on the balance.&nbsp;&nbsp;<br>
 (B) Add a
        small chip of&nbsp; iodoacetic acid in a fume hood to the tared tube. (C) Weigh the capped tube on the
        balance.&nbsp;<br>
        (D) Add the appropriate amount of 1 N NaOH to the tube in
        the fume hood.&nbsp;<br>
 This may be over cautious but better safe than sorry.&nbsp; Always wear
        gloves, safety glasses and a lab coat.&nbsp; Do not let the
        carboxymethylation go for an extended period of time, it is possible to
        over alkylate.&nbsp; There are reports of histidine, methionine and
        tryptophan being alkylated admittedly, at lower efficiencies.&nbsp; The reaction is almost
        instantaneous, therefore 30 min in the dark, should be way more than enough
        time.&nbsp; Again, if you have the time method development is in order.</p>
        <p align="left"><b>Steps 5</b> <b>and 6&nbsp; </b>Steps 5 and 6 can be bypassed with
        a quick size exclusion or reverse phase purification. The down side of
        dialysis is that it can take as long as 24hrs.&nbsp; The advantage of
        size exclusion or dialysis is that the protein can be exchanged in to
        the enzyme digest buffer.<br>
        <br>
        <b><font color="#000000">Deamidation Risk</font><font color="#0000FF">&nbsp;
        </font></b>The RCM reaction is carried out at high pH and the reduction
        is often carried out at elevated temperature and pH which makes <a href="../Deamidation/deamidation.htm" target="_blank">deamidation
        of proteins</a> during the procedure likely.&nbsp; To reduce the likelihood
        of deamidation keep the procedure as short as possible and avoid or
        reduce the heating time on the reduction.&nbsp; If artifctual deamidation is a real concern for you the pH of the RCM procedure can be
        reduced but some method development and validation will need to be done
        on your part. Most likely the method you develop will be specific to your
        particular protein.&nbsp;<b><br>
        <br>
        </b></p>
