      <p class="orange-subhead"><a name="what"></a>What are mutual funds?</p>
      <p>There are a number of different kinds of mutual funds, some of which 
        you may have heard of, namely unit trusts, investment trusts and OEICs 
        (open-ended investment companies). These are covered later on under "which 
        type of fund suits you?" section. <br>
        <br>
        When you invest in a mutual fund, you pool your money with money from 
        other people who share the same investment goals. You own shares in the 
        fund. The pool of money is invested by professional money managers, who 
        choose securities they believe will help achieve the fund's financial 
        objectives. <br>
        <br>
        You can profit from a mutual fund in two ways: from dividends earned by 
        the fund's investments and by the gains made when the fund sells a security 
        at a profit. <br>
        <br>
        Mutual funds are not insured, and the value of your shares may fluctuate 
        as market conditions change. So when you redeem your shares, they may 
        be worth more or less than you paid for them. <br>
        <br>
        Nevertheless, mutual funds offer many attractive benefits. Because they 
        invest your money in a variety of securities, you enjoy diversification 
        of your investment, which helps manage risk. And unlike many other investments, 
        they provide liquidity, which means you can redeem your shares at any 
        time. <br>
        <br>
        Mutual funds can be a smart investment for nearly every investor's portfolio. 
        With thousands of different funds available, there are funds suitable 
        for virtually every investment goal and style. Funds range from very conservative 
        to very aggressive, and strive to provide steady income, growth of capital, 
        or tax-exempt income. </p>

      <p class="orange-subhead"><a name="adv"></a>Unique advantages of mutual 
        funds? 
      <p>Mutual funds offer many special advantages some other investments might 
        not have. They include: <br>
        <br>
      <p><b>Diversification</b> <br>
        Diversification is a powerful investment principle that can reduce risk 
        by spreading your money among different investments so you don't put your 
        eggs in just one basket. <br>
        <br>
        Different securities may perform differently under the same market conditions. 
        By investing in a number of different securities, you may be able to blunt 
        the effect of a decline in the value of just one. Investing in a mutual 
        fund is a way to diversify in a single investment. The portfolio managers 
        of a fund may invest in as many as 200 or more different securities. </p>
      <p><b>Potential Growth</b> <br>
        By investing in mutual funds, you have the potential for higher long-term 
        earnings than if you invested in conventional savings. The potential growth 
        is largely determined by the type of mutual fund you choose, its performance, 
        the performance of the underlying markets, whether or not you reinvest 
        your earnings, your investment time frame and the tax consequences to 
        you. </p>
      <p><b>Liquidity</b> <br>
        Most mutual funds are "liquid." That means you can redeem your shares 
        at any time. You'll receive the current value of your investment, which 
        may be higher or lower than the price you paid. You don't have to tie 
        up your money until a pre-determined date. <br>
        <br>
        Many mutual funds even offer cheque-writing privileges that allow you 
        to write a cheque against your investment at any time. The money is withdrawn 
        from your account when the cheque clears. Some types of mutual funds may 
        charge a fee at liquidation. </p>
      <p><b>Variety</b> <br>
        Mutual funds invest in nearly every type of investment instrument you 
        can think of. So if there's a particular type of investment you'd like 
        to make, chances are you can find a mutual fund to match. </p>
      <p><b>Automatic Reinvestment</b> <br>
        With most mutual funds, you can automatically reinvest your dividends 
        and capital gains to purchase additional shares in the fund at no additional 
        cost. This kind of compounding can significantly increase the value of 
        your assets over time. </p>

      <p class="orange-subhead"><a name="type"></a>Many different types of fund 
        are available to meet your needs 
      <p>Because the choice of mutual funds mirrors the financial marketplace, 
        you can find virtually any kind of investment. Here are brief descriptions 
        of the major fund categories: <br>
        <br>
        <a name="Unit"></a>
      <p><b>Unit Trust</b> <br>
        A unit trust is a pooled fund of investors' money which is used to buy 
        shares. If you buy into a unit trust, you become a unitholder. It is open-ended 
        because the number of 'units' in each trust will vary according to supply 
        and demand. The unit trust is managed by a fund manager whose job it is 
        to make sure the money is invested properly and investors receive the 
        best returns. Some unit trusts are very general and hold a large number 
        of shares in different companies, spreading investments into overseas 
        companies in some cases. </p>
      <p><b>Investment Trust</b> <br>
        An investment trust is also a pooled fund of investors' money in the form 
        of shares. If you buy into an investment trust, you become a shareholder. 
        The number of shares in the trust is fixed i.e. closed-ended. Unlike a 
        unit trust, an Investment Trust is a public limited company whose shares 
        are quoted on the Stock Exchange. As a consequence, the price can go up 
        and down in relation to the value of the underlying assets. Because it 
        is a company, you have a say in how the trust is managed as a shareholder 
        and how the company is run. Investment trusts invest in different companies 
        across various regions and specialisations. </p>
      <p><b>OEICs</b> <br>
        OEICs are a hybrid of unit trusts and investment trusts. They are like 
        unit trusts in that the size of the fund is dependent on people buying 
        and selling shares i.e. they are open-ended. They are unlike unit trusts 
        in that OEICs have one pricing structure so the price that you buy or 
        sell at will be the same price. OEICs are like investment trusts in that 
        they are quoted companies, but different in that the shares will only 
        reflect the value of the underlying assets and the price will not go up 
        and down in relation to the real value as opposed to market value. </p>
      <p><b>Stock Funds</b> <br>
        Stock Funds invest in the stocks of various types of companies. Traditionally, 
        stocks have kept pace with the economy and represent your best protection 
        against inflation. <br>
        <br>
        Some stock funds, such as growth, aggressive growth and value funds are 
        based on a specific investment strategy. Others, such as small cap, mid 
        cap, large cap and blue chip, choose securities determined by the size 
        or financial status of the selected companies. </p>
      <p><b>Bond Funds</b> <br>
        Bond Funds seek to deliver steady income from a portfolio of bonds. Because 
        of the added risk from price fluctuation inherent in longer-term bonds, 
        yields vary based on the term of the bonds in the fund's portfolio. </p>
      <p><b>Hybrid Funds</b> <br>
        Hybrid Funds combine different types of securities in one fund to meet 
        varying objectives. For instance, a growth and income fund might invest 
        in both stocks and bonds. </p>
      <p><b>Asset Allocation Funds</b> <br>
        Asset Allocation Funds combine securities from different asset classes 
        in varying proportions to help manage different levels of risk and return. 
        <br>
        <br>
        Each portfolio invests in a different mix of stocks, bonds and cash in 
        seeking different investment objectives. </p>
      <p><b>International and Global Funds</b> <br>
        These funds invest in securities issued in other countries. International 
        funds invest only in foreign securities, while global funds invest in 
        both foreign and U.K. securities. Many other mutual funds of all types 
        may invest a certain percentage of their portfolio in international investments. 
        Investors should see the risk factors section in the fund's prospectus 
        for details regarding international investing. </p>
      <p><b>Money Market Funds</b> <br>
        Unlike all other types of mutual funds whose share prices fluctuate, money 
        market funds attempt to maintain a stable share price of GBP £1. To try 
        to accomplish this, they invest in very short-term securities and commercial 
        paper, which have less price fluctuation. Money market funds are intended 
        as a parking place for money between investments and a place to maintain 
        cash reserves. Over the long term, they do not keep pace with inflation.	
      </p>

      <p class="orange-subhead"><a name="how"></a>How to invest in a mutual fund:</p>
      <p><b>Step 1</b> <br>
        Start investing now. The longer you wait, the longer it will take you 
        to get where you want to be. </p>
      <p><b>Step 2</b> <br>
        Have a Personal Investment Plan. You need to follow a long-term strategy 
        based on your goals, concerns, time frame and comfort level with risk. 
        Any mutual fund you buy should fit your personal profile. With thousands 
        of funds on the market, trying to choose the right ones can be confusing. 
        Your Personal Investment Plan can help you cut through the clutter by 
        recommending how you should allocate your assets among the many different 
        types of funds. It will also highlight specific funds that would be appropriate 
        for you, based on your personal profile, to help you meet your goals. 
      </p>
      <p><b>Step 3</b> <br>
        Follow a consistent investment strategy. Reap the potential benefits of 
        pound-cost averaging* when you invest the same amount each month. Enjoy 
        the benefits of compounding when you reinvest your dividends and other 
        distributions. And don't try to time the market; invest for the long term. 
      </p>
      <p><b>Step 4</b> <br>
        Review your investments once a year or when your situation changes. Why 
        not consult an independent financial advisor to periodically review your 
        portfolio? *Pound-cost averaging does not assure a profit and does not 
        protect against loss in declining markets. Pound-cost averaging involves 
        continuous investment in securities regardless of fluctuating price levels 
        of such securities. An investor should consider his or her financial ability 
        to continue making purchases through periods of low-level prices. </p>
