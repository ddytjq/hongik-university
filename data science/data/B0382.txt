<tr>
<td></td>
<td></td>
<td><br><span class="greyTableHeader">What is an ISA?</span><br><answer>ISA stands for Individual Savings Account.  It is the Government's tax-free savings scheme.</answer><br></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td></td>
<td></td>
<td><br><span class="greyTableHeader">What types of ISA are available?</span><br><answer>At C&G we offer a Mini Cash ISA and a TESSA-ISA.  You can invest &#0163;3,000 per tax-year in a Mini Cash ISA at present.  The Government will be reviewing ISAs in 2006.  A TESSA-ISA can be opened with original capital from a matured TESSA. You are allowed up to six months from the maturing of your TESSA to open a TESSA-ISA.</answer><br></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td></td>
<td></td>
<td><br><span class="greyTableHeader">Who can open an ISA?</span><br><answer>Anyone aged 16 and over can have an ISA.  There is no upper age limit and you do not need to be employed to have one, but you must be a UK resident for tax purposes.</answer><br></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td></td>
<td></td>
<td><br><span class="greyTableHeader">How long will the ISA scheme last?</span><br><answer>The Government has confirmed that the ISA scheme will be available for at least ten years from the launch of the scheme in April 1999, with a review of its future after seven years.</answer><br></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td></td>
<td></td>
<td><br><span class="greyTableHeader">What are the benefits of having an ISA?</span><br><answer>An ISA will grow tax-free and will be paid out free of both income tax and capital gains tax.</answer><br></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td></td>
<td></td>
<td><br><span class="greyTableHeader">How can I make a withdrawal from my C&G Cash ISA?</span><br><answer>By completing a pink withdrawal form for a postal ISA or visiting a C&G branch with your passbook if you have a branch ISA.  Remember though that, if you are making a withdrawal from a Cash ISA and have already invested the full &#0163;3,000 in the current tax-year you will not be able to replace the funds until the following tax-year.  Funds withdrawn from a TESSA-ISA can never be replaced. </answer><br></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td></td>
<td></td>
<td><br><span class="greyTableHeader">What types of ISA are available?</span><br><answer>
			Mini-ISAs comprise either a stocks and shares component, a cash component or a life insurance component.   The annual allowance limits for the 2002/2003 tax-year are:
			<br>
<table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" border="0">
<tr>
<td>Cash			- </td>
<td width="5"> </td>
<td>up to &#0163;3,000</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Life insurance	- </td>
<td> </td>
<td>  up to &#0163;1,000</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Stocks & Shares	- </td>
<td> </td>
<td>  up to &#0163;3,000</td>
</tr>
</table>
<br>
			Maxi-ISAs must contain a stocks and shares component and may also contain one or both of a cash and life insurance components.  The total annual allowance limit for 2002/2003 tax-year for all components is &#0163;7,000.  Within that limit you can invest the following:
			<br>
<table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" border="0">
<tr>
<td>Cash			- </td>
<td width="5"> </td>
<td>up to &#0163;3,000</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Life insurance	- </td>
<td> </td>
<td>  up to &#0163;1,000</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Stocks & Shares	- </td>
<td> </td>
<td>  up to &#0163;3,000</td>
</tr>
</table>
<br>
			Since you don't need to have either the cash or the life insurance components, you can put the whole &#0163;7,000 in stocks and shares if you want to.
			</answer><br></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td></td>
<td></td>
<td><br><span class="greyTableHeader">What happens if I've already got a TESSA?</span><br><answer>Any open TESSAs may continue to have funds added until maturity, subject to the annual TESSA allowance.  At maturity, the matured capital (but not the interest) can be transferred to a special ISA called a TESSA-ISA, without being counted against the annual ISA investment allowance.  Interest is paid tax-free on a TESSA-ISA.</answer><br></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td></td>
<td></td>
<td><br><span class="greyTableHeader">Can I make withdrawals from an ISA?</span><br><answer>The Government has designed ISAs so the investor can easily withdraw their money if they need to.  There is no statutory lock-in period, unlike the TESSA which has a five year lock-in.  Within the basic ISA framework, however, financial institutions may design their own products which may encourage customers to leave their money invested for a certain period, or require them to give notice of withdrawals in exchange for higher interest rates.</answer><br></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td></td>
<td></td>
<td><br><span class="greyTableHeader">Can you make withdrawals from an ISA and then make further additions?</span><br><answer>The maximum amount you are allowed to invest in an ISA is fixed.  Once you have paid an amount equivalent to the annual allowance, regardless of the account balance if withdrawals have been made, you are not allowed to pay in any more until the start of the next tax-year.</answer><br></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td></td>
<td></td>
<td><br><span class="greyTableHeader">How do I make a transfer from another ISA provider?</span><br><answer>ISAs can be transferred between financial institutions.  C&G will accept both previous and current year's Cash ISA subscriptions from investors transferring from another ISA manager. The minimum balance requirements must be met.
					<br><br>
				Contact your existing ISA manager to request a transfer of your Cash ISA to a C&G Cash ISA. You'll need to ask for a transfer form and cheque to be sent to C&G Invest Direct, PO Box 111, Fareham, Hants, PO15 5YS.
				<br><br>
				Some ISA providers operate combined TESSA/Cash ISAs. This is where all the funds are held in one account rather than in separate ISAs. Although C&G does
				not operate its own combined ISA, we can accept a transfer of combined funds provided they are all from previous tax-years.
				<br><br>
				If you have not already deposited the maximum amount in the Cash ISA for the current tax year, additional funds may be invested subject to ISA scheme limits.
			</answer><br></td>
</tr>
