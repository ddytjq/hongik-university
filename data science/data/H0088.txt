Lengths are typically greater than 50 repeats in holotrichous ciliates, less than 350 repeats in <i>Arabidopsis</i> and 300 to 500 bp in <i>Saccharomyces</i>.						<li>A <i>Drosophila</i> chromosome, an exception, has a <a href="../MGW3/MG32217.html">transposable element</a> at the end of one of its chromosomes.						<li>The G-T rich strand is longer than the C-A rich strand.					</ul>				</td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td width="170"><i>Oxytrichia, Euplotes </i></td>				<td>CCCCAAAA</td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td width="170"><i>Trypanosoma, Leishmania</i></td>				<td>CCCTA</td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td width="170"><i>Physarum</i></td>				<td>CCCTA</td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td width="170"><i>Saccharomyces</i></td>				<td>C<sub>1-3</sub>A</td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td width="170"><i>Arabidopsis</i></td>				<td>CCCTAAA</td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td width="170"><a href="http://hdklab.wustl.edu/~genlink/teldb/human.html"><i>Homo</i></a></td>				<td>CCCTAAA</td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td width="170"><i>Caenorhabditis</i></td>				<td>CCCTAAA</td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td width="170"><i>Drosophila</i></td>				<td>transposable element</td>			</tr>		</table>		</p>		<pre>....TTGGGGTTGGGGTTGGGGTTGGGGTTGGGGTTGGGGTTGGGGTT 3'<br>....AACCCCAACCCCAACCCC 5'													</pre>		<p>Telomere structure may explain functional features unique to telomeres.</p>		<ul>			<li><a href="MG135.html">Telomere ends</a> differ from ends at DNA breaks.			<li>Telomere ends associate with one another on the nuclear envelope. This results in a &quot;bouquet&quot; of chromosomes.		</ul>		<p>		<table border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="2">			<tr>				<td>One explanation may be an atypical structural feature of G-rich sequences:					<ul>						<li>The extra G-T rich sequences are not present in telomeres as single stranded DNA.						<li>G-T rich strands can, in the test tube, form an unusual structure in which 4 G's are paired with one another. A metal ion (M+) assists the formation of these G quartets. They stack on top of one another. The T-stretches connect the sides of the quartet.						<li>Intermolecular G quartets can form.					</ul>				</td>				<td><img src="i13521.gif" align="top" usemap="#"><map name=""></map></td>			</tr>		</table>		</p>		<p>Another explanation may be lariat-like structures (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=10338214&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>).</p>		<ul>			<li><img src="i13522.gif" width="96" height="126" align="right">Long DNA molecules, consisting of a stretch of double-stranded telomere repeat DNA flanked on one side by unique sequence DNA and on the other by single-stranded telomere repeat DNA (the G-rich strand), were <a href="MG1353.html">synthesized</a>. Electron microscopy revealed the molecules to be linear, as expected.			<li>After addition of purified TRF2 protein (but not several other proteins) to the DNA, electron microscopy revealed lariat-like figures with an electron dense blob at the lariat junction (see <u>Cell</u> cover at right)			<li>The electron dense blob and the lariat -like figures were not seen if TRF2 addition was followed by <a href="../MGW3/MG3543.html">phenol extraction</a> before electron microscopy.			<li>Psoralens are agents that cross-link DNA, but not protein, strands. Psoralen treatment of the TRF2-DNA complex produced molecules that retained lariat-like structure (but not the electron dense blob) after phenol extraction.		</ul>		<p>Lariat-like telomeres may exist in cells (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=10338214&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>).</p>		<ul>			<li>Because of their short repeated sequences, telomeres are not susceptible to digestion with several, otherwise frequently cutting <a href="../MGW4/MG421.html">restriction endonucleases</a>. As a result, the high molecular weight DNA fraction of cell DNA digested with these restriction enzymes is enriched in telomere DNA.			<li>When this fraction from DNA prepared from psoralen-treated nuclei is examined by electron microscopy, lariat-like structures were seen. Linear molecules were found if psoralen treatment was omitted.			<li>The lengths of telomere repeats from different cell lines varied, but correlated with the circumferences of the lariat circles in the DNA prepared as above.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Interp"></a>Interpretations</h4>		<ul type="circle">			<li>At their ends, telomeres can assume special structures, the G quartets.			<li>G quartets can account for the stability properties of chromosome ends.			<li>The formation of intermolecular G quartets can account for the apparent interaction of telomeres (bouqet formation).			<li>The studies using TRF2 suggest that the single strand telomeric end is not arranged as a doubled-back G-quartet, but rather invades duplex DNA near the junction of telomeric and non-telomeric DNA.			<li>The analysis of psoralen-treated nuclei suggests that the TRF2-facilitated structures probably exist in actual cells. There is no analogous evidence favoring a biological relevance of G-quartet structures.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a><br>			</p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Info"></a>Further information<img src="nav4.gif" width="69" height="71" align="right" usemap="#nav4B1F889C9"><map name=""></map></h4>		<ul type="square">			<li>Telomere <a href="http://resolution.colorado.edu/~nakamut/telomere/teltable.html">sequences</a> have been characterized from many organisms.			<li>Telomere <a href="MG1353.html">synthesis</a> requires a special enzyme.			<li>G-quartet RNA molecules specifically bind to <a href="MG11214.html">prion</a> proteins (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/htbin-post/Entrez/query?uid=9343239&form=6&db=m&Dopt=b">ref</a>).			<li><a href="MG1375.html">D-loops</a> (displacement loops) have been detected near the lariat junction.			<li>Certain TRF2 mutants have dominant negative effects (Bailey Cornforth 2001).  When the mutant protein is produced, <a href="MG135.html">end-to-end chromosome fusions</a>  and anaphase bridges occur.  DNA damage checkpoints are activated.  The fusions occur after <a href="../MGW3/MG3135.html">replication</a> but are not between sister chromatids because only those DNA ends formed during replication by leading strand synthesis appear to participate in the joining.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a name="bottom"></a><a href="MG1351.html">Last</a> | <a href="MG139.html">Vocabulary</a> | <a href="MG135.html">Overview</a> | <a href="#Facts">Top</a> | <a href="MG1353.html">Next</a><br>			<br>			</p>		</center>		<p>		<hr>		</p>		<p>This is page 1352 of <a href="../MG01.html">Molecular Genetics</a> by <a href="http://opbs.okstate.edu/~melcher/UM.html">Ulrich Melcher</a>, &copy; 1997-9, 2001<br>		<hr>		</p>		<address>E-mail inquiries to <a href="mailto:umelcher@biochem.okstate.edu">U.
