<TD WIDTH="70%" VALIGN="TOP"><P><B>There is a disappointing lack of real content on animal behavior on
the web. There are hundreds of the usual propaganda pages for societies,
university departments, and book sellers, but little information. Hopefuly
the situation will improve in the future. The two sites linked to below
are both excellent sites for information on animal behavior. In addition
you might wish to check out the general evolution sites on the <A HREF=
"genevol.htm">general evolution page</A> of <I>Nearctica</I>.</B></P>

<P>&nbsp;</P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://galaxy.ucr.edu/~chappell/Bio160/Bio160W98.html">Animal
Behavior, Biology 160</A>. Mark Chappell, University of California at Riverside.
An excellent set of lecture notes on animal behavior including sections
on the genetics of behavior, communication, habitat selection and dispersal,
territoriality, competition, foraging strategies, reproductive tactics,
social behavior, and more. Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.biology.ucsc.edu/people/barrylab/public_html/classes/old_animal_behavior/Animal_Behavior.html">Bio
170: Animal Behavior</A>. Barry Sinervo, University of California at Santa
Cruz. An excellent set of lectures on animal behavior heavily weighted to
evolutionary topics. The site can be used by those interested either in
behavior or evolutionary theory. There are a series of lectures and a group
of questions on the material contained in each lecture. Topics covered include
natural selection, speciation, reproductive strategies, dispersal, communication,
learning, macroevolution, and social evolution. Recommended.</B></TD></TR>
