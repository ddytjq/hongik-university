				<td rowspan="2" valign="top" width="196">

				<font class="newsdate"> Saturday, November 25, 2000 14:30 (GMT)</font><br>
				<img src="/1.gif" width="1" height="18"><br>
				<font class="news">There is a lot of talk about India lacking depth in the pace department and what should be done to produce some quality pace bowlers. But one important issue which is often overlooked is the need for established openers in the Indian Test line-up. India has been struggling in the last few years to come up with a solid opening pair in the longer version of the game, having tried a number of combinations without any great results. <br><br>For sometime Devang Gandhi and Sadagopan Ramesh opened. Before them others like VVS Laxman, Wasim Jaffer and Nayan Mongia were also tried but none of them could really establish themselves in the opening slot.<br><br>Ramesh has shown some promise but he too has not really been able to cement his place in the side till now. In fact one cannot remember any batsman apart from Navjot Sidhu who has managed to serve his nation as an opener for a length of time after great Sunil Gavaskar hung his bat in the late eighties.<br><br>Now it looks like that at least half the problem will get solved, and the man who has answered the selectors&#8217; prayers is the Orissa batsman Shiv Sunder Das who made his Test debut in Dhaka a few weeks back. The newcomer who is only the second Orissa player to earn a Test cap after medium pacer Debashish Mohanty, has shown a lot of promise right from the start. Though he did not get a big score in his first Test, getting out on 29 and 22 in Dhaka, but the way he batted in those short innings it was apparent that he had a lot of potential.<br><br>One got a better glimpse of his talent when Das hit a 58 in his second Test, against Zimbabwe. The 23-year-old finally realised that potential at the highest level in the second of the two Tests against Zimbabwe when he went on to score 110, reaching the three-figure mark in two sessions. He finally got out when a controversial decision went against him. His century was embellished with 17 hits to the fence and came off just 150 balls, displaying his wide repertoire of shots as well as his stroke-making ability. Ganguly was all praise for Das&#8217; innings but refused to comment on his future. &#8220;Das&#8217; innings was brilliant but it&#8217;s still early days and he will only be able to establish himself when he plays innings like this outside the sub-continent,&#8221; said Ganguly.<br><br>Das who has a very sound technique started playing cricket in Bhubaneshwar at the initiation of his elder brother Satya Shankar Das and the youngster was immediately fascinated by batting. Over the past few years he has worked very hard at the domestic level and having gone through the grind he knows what it means to play for his country. &#8220;I think it&#8217;s a great honour to play for your country and I am really happy to be doing so,&#8221; said Das.<br><br>Das, like his role model Sunil Gavaskar, is a very compact batsman and having played on matting wickets for the most part he is very strong off the back foot which will be good for the Indian team when they go outside the sub-continent where the tracks are fast and bouncy. There are other aspects of Das&#8217; batting which will stand him in good stead in future.<br><br>He is a very energetic runner between wickets and has excellent footwork which makes him a good player of spin bowling. His stints with the National Cricket Academy and it&#8217;s Australian counterpart the Australian Cricket Academy have really helped him. &#8220;When I was in the NCA I was really made to work hard on my fitness and in Australia I got an opportunity to refine my backfoot game,&#8221; believes the right-hander. <br><br>Like the Indian skipper said, Das has only won half the battle but from what we have seen of him till now it definitely looks like he will go on to do well outside the sub-continent as well.</font><br>
