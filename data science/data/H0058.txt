The DNA is <a href="MG112281.html">denatured</a>.			<li>Various concentrations of the DNA are incubated for varying times at a <a href="MG112281.html">temperature allowing reassociation</a>.			<li>The fraction of the DNA not reassociated in each sample is measured and plotted.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a name="Facts"></a><img src="i1122821.gif" width="440" height="280" usemap="#i1122821B0234476"><map name="i1122821B0234476"><area coords="296,38,411,236" shape="rect" href="#unique"><area coords="201,34,280,232" shape="rect" href="#mod"><area coords="81,31,170,228" shape="rect" href="#High"><area coords="42,244,427,278" shape="rect" href="#Cot"><area coords="-4,41,42,224" shape="rect" href="#HAP"><area coords="45,4,238,30" shape="rect" href="#FB"></map></p>		</center>		<ul>			<li><a name="HAP"></a>Hydroxylapatite (HAP) is used to distinguish single-stranded from double-stranded DNA. All DNA is bound at low salt concentrations. At intermediate salt concentrations, partially or completely double-stranded DNA remains bound to HAP, while single-stranded DNA elutes. <a href="#Facts">Top</a>			<li><a name="Cot"></a>C<sub>o</sub>t			<ul>				<li><strong>C<sub>o</sub></strong>is the concentration of single-stranded DNA at the beginning of the reassociation reaction. It is measured in moles of nucleotide units per liter.				<li><strong>t </strong>is the time of the reassociation reaction, in seconds.				<li>The amount of single-stranded DNA left (C/C<sub>o</sub>) is plotted against the product of the above two terms, C<sub>o</sub>t. C<sub>o</sub>t is used because it appears in the equation describing reassociation kinetics:				<ul>					<p>					<table border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" width="273">						<tr>							<td></td>							<td>								<pre>             1C/Co = -----------------          1 +k Cot </pre>							</td>						</tr>					</table>					</p>				</ul>				<li>When the DNA is 50% reassociated:<br>				0.5 = 1 / (1+ k C<sub>o</sub>t<sub>1/2</sub>)<br>				So, C<sub>o</sub>t<sub>1/2</sub> = 1 / k				<li>C<sub>o</sub>t<sub>1/2</sub> and k (reassociation rate constant) describe the kinetic behavior of DNA reassociation. <a href="#Facts">Top</a><br>				<br>			</ul>			<li><strong><a name="FB"></a>Fold-back</strong> (or snap-back) DNA renatures without incubation due to the presence of inverted repeats. The lengths of duplexes formed range from 200 to 1200 bp; some are as large as 6 kbp (see Table). Fold-back DNA can consist of double-stranded rods or stem-loop structures. Non-contiguous inverted repeats form loops:<br>			----------&gt; ..........&lt;-----------<br>			The fraction of the genome that forms foldback DNA ranges from 1 to 30% (see Table). Some fold back DNA is due to the chance proximity of stretches of repeated DNA. In <i>Drosophila</i>, much of the fold back DNA is due to the FB <a href="../MGW3/MG3221.html">transposable element</a>. Fold-back DNA has not been detected in bacteria. <a href="#Facts">Top</a>		</ul>		<center>			<p>			<table>				<tr>					<td></td>					<td colspan="4" align="center">Fold-back DNA<br>					</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td></td>					<td>length of duplex</td>					<td>looped molec/<br>						all FB molec</td>					<td>length<br>						of loop</td>					<td>FB-ntp/<br>						genome-ntp</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td><i>Drosophila</i></td>					<td align="center">&gt; 1300 bp</td>					<td align="center">0.80</td>					<td align="center">3.4 kbp</td>					<td align="center">0.03</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td><i>Homo</i></td>					<td align="center">250</td>					<td align="center">						<pre> - </pre>					</td>					<td align="center">-</td>					<td align="center">0.05</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td><i>Triticum</i></td>					<td align="center">300</td>					<td align="center">0.20</td>					<td align="center">1.0</td>					<td align="center">0.017</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td><i>Mus </i></td>					<td align="center">-</td>					<td align="center">-</td>					<td align="center">-</td>					<td align="center">0.03</td>				</tr>			</table>			</p>		</center>		<ul>			<li><a name="High"></a><a href="MG112284.html">Highly repeated DNA</a> generally has a C<sub>o</sub>t<sub>1/2</sub> &lt; 10<sup>-2</sup>. It accounts for 1% to &gt;15% of the genome, with most genomes in the 10-15% range. Highly repeated DNA is located near <a href="MG136.html">centromeres</a>, near <a href="MG135.html">telomeres</a>, and is associated with <a href="MG1131.html">heterochromatin</a>.  In some organisms, it may also be interspersed in gene-containing regions.  <a href="#Facts">Top</a>		</ul>		<center>			<p>			<table>				<tr>					<td></td>					<td colspan="3" align="center">Highly repeated DNA</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td></td>					<td>fraction</td>					<td>frequency</td>					<td>complexity</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td><i>Drosophila</i></td>					<td align="center">0.12</td>					<td>24,000</td>					<td>7 x 10<sup>2</sup></td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td><i>Homo</i></td>					<td align="center">0.13</td>					<td>50,000</td>					<td>5 x 10<sup>3</sup></td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td><i>Mus</i></td>					<td align="center">0.08</td>					<td>300,000</td>					<td>5 x 10<sup>2</sup></td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td><i>Nicotiana</i></td>					<td align="center">0.05</td>					<td>16,500</td>					<td></td>				</tr>			</table>			</p>		</center>		<ul>			<li><a name="mod"></a><a href="MG112285.html">Moderately repetitive DNA</a> consists of families of related sequences, not necessarily identical to one another. The stringency of reannealing allows some <a href="MG112281.html">mismatched</a> base pairs to form. Thus, renatured moderately repetitive DNA melts with a lower T<sub>m</sub> than renatured unique DNA or native DNA. Some moderately repetitive <a href="MG112285.html">DNA families</a> represent multi-copy structural genes. Note that in some organisms (see Table), the moderately repetitive class can be split into more than one group. Actually, there may be a continuum of frequencies of repetition. <a href="#Facts">Top</a>		</ul>		<center>			<p>			<table>				<tr>					<td></td>					<td colspan="3" align="center">Moderately repetitive DNA</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td></td>					<td>Fraction</td>					<td>Frequency</td>					<td>Complexity</td>				</tr>				<tr align="center">					<td align="left"><i>Drosophila</i></td>					<td>0.12</td>					<td>70</td>					<td>3 x 10<sup>5</sup></td>				</tr>				<tr align="center">					<td align="left"><i>Homo</i></td>					<td align="center">0.22</td>					<td align="center">500</td>					<td>1 x 10<sup>6</sup></td>				</tr>				<tr align="center">					<td align="left"><i>Mus</i></td>					<td>a. 0.11</td>					<td align="center">150</td>					<td>1.5 x 10<sup>6</sup></td>				</tr>				<tr align="center">					<td></td>					<td align="center">b. 0.14</td>					<td align="center">1500</td>					<td>2 x 10<sup>5</sup></td>				</tr>				<tr align="center">					<td align="left"><i>Nicotiana</i></td>					<td>0.60</td>					<td align="center">300</td>					<td></td>				</tr>				<tr align="center">					<td align="left"><i>Pisum</i></td>					<td>a. 0.32</td>					<td align="center">300</td>					<td></td>				</tr>				<tr align="center">					<td></td>					<td>b. 0.44</td>					<td align="center">10,000</td>					<td>(high?)</td>				</tr>			</table>			</p>		</center>		<ul>			<li><a name="unique"></a>The slowly annealing component is due to single copy (unique) sequences.
