The CEN consists of three regions, CDEI, CDEII, and CDEIII. Region I is not essential for CEN function, but plays an enhancing role. Region II is AT rich and binds <a href="MG1365.html">histone-like</a> proteins. Region III is essential. Morphological observation suggests a single microtubule binds at each centromere. Kinetochores are indistinct in this yeast.		</ul>		<center>			<p><img src="i13641.gif" width="258" height="49" naturalsizeflag="0"><map name=""></map></p>		</center>		<ul>			<li>In <i>S. pombe</i>, deletion and mutagenesis of CEN's revealed that they consist of several kbp of DNA (1 to 2% of the chromosome). Each of the three CEN's has a central core sequence flanked by inverted repeats. Flanking the repeats are members of <a href="MG112284.html">repeated sequence</a> element families. Some are tandemly repeated. Others are <a href="MG112287.html">interspersed</a> with other repeats. <i>S. pombe</i> chromosomes have clearly recognizable <a href="MG1365.html">kinetochores</a>.		</ul>		<center>			<p><img src="i13642.gif" width="512" height="43" naturalsizeflag="0"></p>		</center>		<ul>			<li>Primate <a href="MG112284.html">alpha satellite DNA,</a> <a href="MG1363.html">implicated</a> in chromosome segregation, is a tandemly repeated family of 171 bp sequences concentrated in the centromeric regions.  The genetic regions of <i>Arabidopsis thaliana</i> chromosomes identified as containing the <a href="MG1361.html">centromeres</a> consist of a tandemly repeated family of 180 bp sequences (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov:80/entrez/query.fcgi?cmd=Retrieve&db=PubMed&list_uids=10617454&dopt=Abstract">ref</a>).		</ul>		<center>			<p><img src="i13643.gif" width="304" height="26" naturalsizeflag="0"></p>		</center>		<ul>			<li><i>Caenorhabditis elegans</i> chromosomes are holocentric, microtubules attaching at various places along each chromosome.  Thus, despite the completion of the <i>C. elegans </i>genome sequence, no centromere-characteristic DNA sequences have been identified (Tyler-Smith &amp; Floridia).			<li>A <i>Drosophila</i> <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/htbin-post/Entrez/query?uid=9428523&form=6&db=m&Dopt=b">centromere region</a> consists of <a href="MG112284.html">highly repeated DNA </a>with islands of unique sequence DNA.			<ul>				<li>The Bora Bora region consists of an AATAT <a href="MG112284.html">satellite</a> with four embedded <a href="../MGW3/MG3221.html">transposable elements</a>.				<li>The Bora Bora island is required for maximal efficiency of segregation.				<li>Adjacent to Bora Bora is an AAGAG satellite with other embedded transposable elements in a region called Maupiti (not shown). Maupiti is essential for proper <a href="MG1361.html">segregation</a>.				<li>Neither the satellites nor the transposable elements are common to all <i>Drosophila</i> centromeres.			</ul>		</ul>		<center>			<p><img src="i13644.gif" width="448" height="34" naturalsizeflag="0"></p>			<p>&nbsp;</p>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Interp"></a>Interpretations</h4>		<ul type="circle">			<li>CEN structures vary widely from species to species. They may also vary from chromosome to chromosome within a species.			<li>The interchromosome variation suggests that a higher order structure rather than a primary sequence may be the feature that defines a centromere.			<li>In contrast to <a href="MG1351.html">telomeres</a>, CEN structures probably won't function across species barriers.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a><br>			</p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Info"></a>Further information</h4>		<ul type="square">			<li>Region III of the <i>S. cerevisiae</i> centromere is the site for binding of the <a href="MG1366.html">CBF3</a> protein.			<li>Rice centromeres consist of <a href="MG112287.html">dispersed</a> members of six moderately <a href="MG112285.html">repeated</a> DNA families and one <a href="MG112287.html">tandemly</a> repeated DNA (<a href="http://www.nsf.gov/cgi-bin/showaward?award=9872638">ref</a>).			<li>The structure of <a href="MG11226.html">nucleosomes</a> on centromeric DNA plays a role in centromere function.<img src="nav4.gif" width="69" height="71" align="right" usemap="#nav4B1F889C9"><map name=""></map> Hyperacetylation of histones by transient <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/htbin-post/Entrez/query?uid=9428524&form=6&db=m&Dopt=b">treatment</a> with an inhibitor of the enzyme that removes acetyl groups results in improperly functioning centromeres. The hyperacetylated state is <a href="MG11214.html">heritable</a>.		</ul>		<ul type="circle">			<li>The CEN structure determines characteristics of <a href="MG1365.html">spindle attachment</a>.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a name="bottom"></a><a href="MG1363.html">Last</a> | <a href="MG139.html">Vocabulary</a> | <a href="MG136.html">Overview</a> | <a href="#Facts">Top</a> | <a href="MG1365.html">Next</a><br>			<br>			</p>		</center>		<p>		<hr>		</p>		<p>This is page 1364 of <a href="/MG01.html">Molecular Genetics</a> by <a href="http://opbs.okstate.edu/~melcher/UM.html">Ulrich Melcher</a>, &copy; 1997-2001<br>		, 		<hr>		</p>		<address>E-mail inquiries to <a href="mailto:umelcher@biochem.okstate.edu">U.
