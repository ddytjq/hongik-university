    <p align="left"><font face="Arial" size="2"><img border="0" src="../../images/ClipArt/politc.gif" align="right" width="290" height="184">The CART paddock is split.&nbsp;
    Some argue CART should focus primarily on the USA market, while others say
    CART should go guns a-blazing after the wide open international
    market.&nbsp; The argument used by those who feel CART should focus on the
    USA is a valid one - sponsor dollars in the CART paddock come out of the USA
    advertising budget for a large majority of the teams, and the USA market is
    the biggest seller for many companies.</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font face="Arial" size="2">Their argument, therefore, is
    that CART should focus on the USA because that is where those sponsors want
    to sell product.&nbsp; They don't care about the rest of the world market
    some claim.&nbsp; While we respect their reasoning, we don't buy the
    argument.&nbsp; Are these companies part of a larger company that sell goods
    worldwide, and if so, doesn't the entire company benefit if CART exposes
    their products outside the USA too?&nbsp;&nbsp;</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">While the USA is ranked first in
    Gross national Product (GNP), and should always be the 'core' of CART, let's
    look at other countries with a high GNP rank, countries where sponsors might
    want to sell goods, and most probably already do.</font></p>

    <p align="left"><font face="Arial" size="2">Based on GNP alone, CART should
    already be in China and Italy.&nbsp; CART should not walk, it should run to Shanghai.&nbsp;

    becomes available.&nbsp; But it's not just based on GNP that I make this
    recommendation.&nbsp; It's also based on where CART's sponsors sell
    goods.&nbsp; Read on......</font></p>

    <p align="left">
    <font size="2" face="Arial">The conclusion I draw from the above analysis is
    simple - the vast majority of CART's sponsors are in some way, shape or form
    selling products globally.&nbsp; If CART were to declare itself a global
    company in a strong, vigorous way, it would not only attract more new
    sponsors, it's existing sponsors would be well served.</font>
    </p>
    <p align="left">
    <font size="2" face="Arial">Certainly some of the companies might want to
    change what 'brand' they have on the side of the car, but for the most part
    the brands they are already advertising through CART racing are recognized
    and sold worldwide.</font>
    </p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">There is, however, one small
    hurdle.&nbsp; Because existing budgets largely come out of US
    marketing/advertising budgets, the USA arm/subsidiary of a larger company is
    going to have to bring together other subsidiaries to chip in and contribute
    to the sponsorship deal.......because they are indeed benefiting from CART's
    global presence.&nbsp; It's up to CART's Rich Henley and Joe Heitzler to
    help the existing sponsors understand that CART's future is a global one,
    and they must start thinking 'global' when putting together their
    sponsorship package.&nbsp; Should CART change overnight?&nbsp; Perhaps, but
    it's best they take one strategic step at a time, for CART itself, in many
    respects, has to reinvent itself into a global-thinking, global-operating,
    company.</font>
    </p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">As Steven Levinson recently
    wrote on these pages, Sponsors like Motorola, Shell, Texaco (and most others
    above) who have global perspectives have also seen that CART can be a viable marketing and advertising vehicle to project their names on the "Global Market". I think that Honda (whomever they are HONDA USA or HONDA JAPAN) knows that the 115,000 people who come to Surfer's Paradise in Australia certainly get their HONDA name all over Australia and the rest of Asia during the 4 days of Honda's CART Indy Carnival. Who cares whether or not it's HONDA USA or HONDA JAPAN. The consumer and fan "down under" only see "HONDA," and this is certainly good for HONDA.  When HONDA wins in CART, do consumers in Japan, Europe, Australia or South America say, "that doesn't count, that's HONDA USA!"  Of course they don't, the consumer does not differentiate, to them it's all the same.<br>
    <br>
    A win by Honda endures to the benefit of the entire Honda "Brand name". The powers that be in Tokyo are pleased by any CART Honda win, as would be the Toyota Corporate heads in Tokyo.  If not, why is Honda Corporate furious that HONDA has not won the CART Motegi race in Japan. They are exasperated by not winning. And Honda certainly wants to beat Toyota at Motegi, and vice-versa. Its up to Corporate Honda to assess each of their worldwide subsidiaries to do the "bookkeeping" and assess each for the total costs of the program!&nbsp;</font>
    </p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">F1 sponsors pony up 10 times
    more for a F1 sponsorship than a CART sponsorship, yet CART has a far better
    on-track product.&nbsp; If CART wants to start to attract new sponsors, and
    the really big sponsorship dollars ala F1, it's going to have to define
    itself as a global racing powerhouse.&nbsp; Enough of this &quot;we are an
    American Series that sometimes races globally&quot;!&nbsp; The statement
    should be &quot;we are an international world series, based in North
    America&quot;!</font>
    </p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">Should CART be racing in
    China?&nbsp; Yesterday!&nbsp; And Italy?&nbsp; Tomorrow!&nbsp; The future is
    global my friends. There's a reason why the world below is green like money.&nbsp;
    It's even partially peeled off, there for CART's taking.</font>
    </p>
