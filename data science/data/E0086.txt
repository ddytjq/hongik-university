            <H1><!-- #BeginEditable "Heading" -->Comparison and Logical Operators<!-- #EndEditable --></H1>
            <!-- #BeginEditable "Content" --> 
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="4" CELLPADDING="5">
              <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFF0"> 
                <TD COLSPAN="2"> 
                  <P CLASS="SUBHEADING">More Than One Outcome</P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>Programs would be very boring if they only produced one outcome - we should be grateful for the conditional statements, which you'll 
                    encounter soon. These let us branch off at one point in the program, resulting in different outcomes. Notice how much branching goes 
                    on in our lives. </P>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="50%" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>For example: &quot;If it is raining I will stay indoors, else I will walk the dog!&quot; </P>
                  <P>But before I move on and talk about conditional branching, let me introduce two new families of operators. </P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
            </TABLE>
            <BR>
            <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="4" CELLPADDING="5">
              <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFF0"> 
                <TD COLSPAN="2"> 
                  <P CLASS="SUBHEADING">Comparison Operators</P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>For us to make decisions, we must act according to a condition. In the previous example, &quot;it is raining&quot; is a condition, 
                    where as &quot;I will stay indoors&quot; and &quot;I will walk the dog&quot; are results based on the condition. Programming uses a 
                    similar concept. We can say: </P>
                  <P>&quot;If x plus 1 is equal to 5, then x equals 4, else it is not equal to 4&quot;. </P>
                  <P> </P>
                  <P><SPAN CLASS="red">COMPARISON</SPAN> operators are used to compare numerical values. In the example, &quot;is equal to&quot; is the 
                    comparion operator. </P>
                  <P><SPAN CLASS="red">RELATIONAL</SPAN> operators is an alternative name for comparison operators. </P>
                  <P>

                  <P CLASS="justify">If you wanted to test for more conditions, you can use the logical operators. </P>
                  <P CLASS="justify">The first one is the NOT operator (some call it negation). It is denoted by the <TT>!</TT> symbol and takes one expression 
                    as its operand. </P>
                  <P CLASS="justify">Then we have the AND operator, represented by <TT>&amp;&amp;</TT>, and the OR operator, denoted by <TT>||</TT> (double 
                    broken vertical bar symbols - shift backslash on my home keyboard). Both of these operators requires two expressions as their operands. 
                  </P>
                  <P CLASS="justify">Now, their return values depend on the <SPAN CLASS="red">TRUTH VALUES</SPAN> of their operands - do they return 1 or 
                    0? Logical operators are commonly summarized using <SPAN CLASS="red">TRUTH TABLES</SPAN>, which lists all the different truth combinations

              <TR VALIGN="TOP" BGCOLOR="#EEFFEE"> 
                <TD WIDTH="50%" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>A summary of the operators so far: </P>
                  <TABLE WIDTH="70%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="0" CELLPADDING="0" ALIGN="CENTER" BGCOLOR="#CCCCCC">
                    <TR> 
                      <TD> 
                        <TABLE WIDTH="100%" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="1" CELLPADDING="3" ALIGN="CENTER">
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#CCEEFF"> 
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER"> 
                              <P ALIGN="CENTER">Highest Precedence</P>
                            </TD>
                          </TR>
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER" BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                              <P ALIGN="CENTER"><TT>( ) !</TT></P>
                            </TD>
                          </TR>
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFFF"> 
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER" BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> 
                              <P ALIGN="CENTER"><TT>* / %</TT></P>
                            </TD>
                          </TR>
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFFF"> 
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER" BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"><TT>+ -</TT></TD>
                          </TR>
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFFF"> 
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER" BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"> <TT>&lt; &lt;= &gt; &gt;=</TT></TD>
                          </TR>
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFFF"> 
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER" BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"><TT>== !=</TT></TD>
                          </TR>
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFFF"> 
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER" BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"><TT>&amp;&amp;</TT></TD>
                          </TR>
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#F8FFFF"> 
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER" BGCOLOR="#F0FFFF"><TT>||</TT></TD>
                          </TR>
                          <TR BGCOLOR="#CCEEFF"> 
                            <TD ALIGN="CENTER"> 
                              <P ALIGN="CENTER">Lowest Precedence</P>
                            </TD>
                          </TR>
                        </TABLE>
                      </TD>
                    </TR>
                  </TABLE>
                  <P CLASS="justify">This precedence stuff is a little tricky to explain, so let's have a look at an example. Suppose we had: </P>
                  <P CLASS="justify"><TT>x+5 == 6 </TT></P>
                  <P><SPAN CLASS="justify">Since <TT>+</TT> has higher precedence than <TT>==</TT>, <TT>x+5</TT> is evaluated first, then the result is 
                    compared with <TT>6</TT>. </SPAN> </P>
                </TD>
                <TD WIDTH="50%" CLASS="justify"> 
                  <P>But what about <TT>x&lt;3 &amp;&amp; y&lt;7 || y==5</TT> ? </P>
                  <P>Suppose <TT>x</TT> is <TT>4</TT> and <TT>y</TT> is <TT>5</TT>. Since the comparison operators have higher precedence than the logical 
                    ones, we can rewrite the line as:<BR>
                    <TT>0 &amp;&amp; 1 || 1 </TT>because <TT>4&lt;3</TT>, <TT>5&lt;7</TT> and <TT>5==5</TT> return <TT>0</TT>, <TT>1</TT> and <TT>1</TT> 
                    respectively. </P>
                  <P>Now, <TT>&amp;&amp;</TT> gets evaluated first, then <TT>||</TT>. </P>
                  <P><TT>0 &amp;&amp; 1 </TT>returns <TT>0</TT>. Then we have <TT>0 || 1</TT>, which returns <TT>1</TT>. </P>
                  <P>This tends to get confusing, which is why you should use brackets when you have many operators on a single line! The following is easier 
                    to understand than the original: </P>
                  <P><TT>(x&lt;3 &amp;&amp; y&lt;7) || y==5</TT></P>
                  <P>- Expressions in brackets are evaluated first. </P>
                </TD>
              </TR>
            </TABLE>
