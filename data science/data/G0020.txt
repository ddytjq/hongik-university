<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Anyone who is the least bit familiar with quasars will agree that these objects
and black holes are two of the most fascinating objects in the universe.
 And the two may be related because the current thinking is that a quasar
 <em>is</em> a black hole, with its brilliance resulting from the downward
spiral  of discrete matter into the black hole. 
  <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>At the moment (December 2001), well over 20,000 quasars have been discovered
and, based on deep surveys in a relatively small area of the heavens, the
best guess is that one to two million quasars would be detected if the current
more sensitive techniques could be extended to the entire sky. </p>
  <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>There are two primary ways to find new quasars<strong>:</strong> 
<blockquote>  
<ol>
 
    <p> </p>
    <li>Many quasars are active at radio wavelengths.   While there are numerous
radio sources in our own galaxy, if the subsequently measured red shift of
the radio source is quite high, then it's usually a quasar (less often a
Seyfert galaxy). 
      <p> </p>
    </li>
    <li>Most quasars are very active in the ultraviolet range and appear
very much like a bluish star in our own galaxy. &nbsp; But this is a characteristic
of very luminous stars near gaseous star forming regions along the Milky
Way. &nbsp; So if we have a blue star away from the plane of our galaxy with
a high red shift, it's typically a quasar. &nbsp; This method of detection
is called the "ultraviolet excess" technique. </li>
  </ol>
 </blockquote>
  <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Today most of the new quasars are discovered via the ultraviolet excess
technique by wide field telescopes which concentrate on selected strips in
the sky.   At the moment roughly 90% of the new quasars are found by this
method, although surveys at radio wavelengths continue to make a substantial
contribution to the growing number of quasars. </p>
  <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The primary source I'm using is the 10th edition of the Veron
quasar catalogue, and it has nearly doubled the number of quasars listed
since the publication of the 9th catalogue (23,760 vs the previous 13,214)
less than two years ago.   The 10th edition includes many quasars from
two ongoing surveys: <a href="http://www.2dfquasar.org">2dF QSO Redshift
Survey</a> and <a href="http://www.sdss.org/links.html">The Sloan Digital
Sky Survey (SDSS)</a>.  The 2dF survey includes quasars not much higher
than z = 3 (and it's also designated "2QZ" because this is the prefix in
the name of the quasars from this catalogue).   While the SDSS includes
quasars at <em>all</em> red shifts, at the moment the researchers seem to
be most interested in the quasars at the <em>higher</em> red shifts, and
those are the quasars which are included in the latest edition of the Veron
catalogue and thus appear in the quasar table which follows. </p>
  <p> </p>
  <blockquote> <font color="red">[The <a href="http://www.obs-hp.fr/www/catalogues.veron2_9/node2.html">
Introduction</a> of the Veron 9th Catalogue gives you a very good idea of
the growth in quasars counts over the last twenty years or so.   As you may
deduce, the dramatic increase in the number counts between the 9th and 10th
editions is mainly due to the 2QZ (aka 2dF) and SDSS quasars.   At the SDSS
site there is a catalogue with 3800 quasars, which includes 800 quasars already
discovered.   Those 3000 <em>new</em> quasars, <em>not</em> in the the Veron
10th edition, pushes the total count of quasars ever closer to 30,000. 
    <p>
Also I recommend you take the link on the wedge
in the upper lefthand corner of the 2dF link.   As you will soon see, I'm
going to suggest the paucity of quasars in the mid three range of red
shifts just might have <em>immense</em> cosmological significance.   What
follows shortly is the tabular version of these pictorial results.   And
the links on the SDSS file are as good as you're going to get in a single
page regarding research in modern cosmology.]
</font> </p>
    </blockquote>
 
    <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>There is also <A href="http://sundog.stsci.edu">a major survey</a> underway of radio sources using the VLA (Very Large Array) in Socorro, New Mexico, and many of these are quasars.  The survey is concentrating of <strong>F</strong>aint <strong>I</strong>mages of the <strong>R</strong>adio <strong>S</strong>ky at <strong>T</strong>wenty Centimeters (and so it's called - you guessed it! - FIRST).   After the 2QZ and SDSS quasars, the FIRST survey is probably the third most significant contributor of new quasars.  
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Several characteristics of the quasar population are unusual:  </p>
<blockquote>
<ol>
   
      <li>Quasars curiously seem to become brighter (rather than fainter)
with increasing distance, except at the very highest red shifts;    
        <p></p>
   </li>
      <li>Some of them appear to have high proper motion in deep space, moving
in several instances even <em>faster</em> than the speed of light (impossible
   according to the laws of physics);    
        <p></p>
   </li>
      <li>As mentioned in Essay #6, superluminal velocities are associated
with many quasars, in one instance as high as 30X the speed of light.
   
        <p></p>
   </li>
      <li>The distribution of quasars is unique in that the quasar counts
increase consistently out to around z = 2.2, with a decrease after that.
At the peak in distribution, quasars are typically referred to as being
1,000 times more numerous than they are in the current era. 
        <p>  </p>
      </li>
      <li>The are some reputable researchers who question whether or not
quasars are located at cosmological distances indicated.   Their primary
argument is that quasars seem to be located much nearer galaxies than should
occur randomly, and are therefore probably associated with the nearer galaxy,
even though the red shift of the quasar seems to indicate that it's much
farther than the galaxy.
</blockquote> 
        <blockquote> <font color="red"> [I believe that the quasars <em>are</em>
 at cosmological distances - you'll see why soon enough - but will concede
that the contrarians have formidable statistics on their side.] </font> 
          <blockquote>  </blockquote>
            </blockquote>
          </li>
        </ol>
 
        <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Remember from <a href="perceptions(2).html">Essay #2</a> that
the curvature of space in the vicinity of an antipode behaves like a gigantic
lens which causes objects to appear <em>larger</em>, <em>brighter</em>, and
to move across our field of view <em>faster</em>. Since two of these characteristics
- brighter and faster - are associated with the quasar  distribution, it's
a fair question to ask if these anomalies <em>might</em> be  affected by
an antipode.  </p>
        <p> </p>
        <blockquote><font color="red"> [The opinion of astronomers about
quasars' luminosity is somewhat divided.   Some researchers believe that
there is a reasonable explanation in astrophysics for the unusual luminosity
behavior, but confine this conclusion to quasars at the lower red shifts.
  Others say that there is no real good explanation for the global behavior
of luminosity if we include quasars at the higher red shifts, as <a href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=2001AJ....121...54F&amp;db_key=AST&amp;high=3bf93a82e113497">
 this abstract</a> by the SDSS team indicates.   (Please make a mental note
of the red shifts mentioned, especially the one at z = 3.5.)
<p>
<a href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=2001ApJ...555..673C&db_key=AST&high=3bf93a82e117566">This reference</a> emphasizes again that astronomers really can't explain the luminosity of quasars.   It is also interesting because it speculates about the lifetimes of quasars - a few hundred million years. 
  And here's still another <a href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=2001MNRAS.324..128C&amp;db_key=AST&amp;high=3bf93a82e113699">
 abstract</a> in which the author concedes that astronomers don't know the mechanism which causes the extremely bright quasars. 
          <p> Since no one seems to be that comfortable with the behavior of quasar luminosity at high red shift, I'm going to offer an explanation related to the morphology of Riemann's hypersphere.   If that explanation is correct, you'll soon know why the brightest quasars appear where they do.] </font> </p>
          </blockquote>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Since hardly anyone believes that an antipode is within our Hubble horizon
 and makes no adjustments to account for it, then, if there <em>is</em> an antipode
close enough to affect our perception of quasars, what we'll <em>notice</em>
 is that for some inexplicable reason the quasars seem to become brighter
as we look <em>toward</em> the antipode, fainter as we look <em>past</em>
 it. The very brightest quasars are likely to be very close to the antipode,
if there is one, and they will probably pass through a minimum in the number
counts at the same distance.  
          <p> </p>
          <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>So at what red shift do the <em>brightest</em> quasars  appear?   And where do they pass through a <em>minimum</em> in the number counts?
 </p>
          <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The table below shows the quasar counts from the 10th edition
of the Veron catalogue at the highest red shifts.   However, the Verons warn
that "the catalogue should not be used for any statistical analysis".   (This
caveat was <a href="http://www.obs-hp.fr/www/catalogues.veron2_9/node2.html">
in the 9th edition</a> of the catalogue, near the bottom of this link.  
I assume it's also in the 10th, but I haven't been able to access the Introduction
in the 10th edition.)   However, the global behavior of the quasars inferred
from the following table has been known for quite some time, and there are
no surprises. </p>
          <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Also, as indicated in <a href="antigal.html">an earlier file</a>
, there is no reason to assume there is an antipode at a red shift anywhere
          <em>below</em> z = 2. So we're looking for any unusual statistical
activity at a red shift that <em>exceeds</em> z = 2. <a name="table"> </a>
 </p>

 <A NAME="cutoff">  </A>
<font color="navy"> 
                <center>
                <font size=5>Brief Analysis of the Quasars Between z = 3 and z = 4</font>
                </center>
 </font> </p>
                <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>As things seem to get rather interesting in the quasar population between z = 3 and z = 4, let's concentrate our efforts here<strong>...</strong>
<pre>

</pre>
 <font color="navy" size=4> <strong>A. 2QZ Results: Croom's QSO Cutoff</strong>
 </font> </p>
                <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The fourth reference <a href="App8.html">in Appendix 8</a>
 mentioned Narlikar's/Sheshadri's idea that there might be a "cutoff" in
quasars if an antipode would be close enough to affect our perception of
them.   The "cutoff" idea at various red shifts has been suggested for years
as a valid reason for a decrease in the number counts of quasars at high
red shifts. </p>
                <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>I'm going to take the "cutoff" idea and modify it just
a tad.   Instead of just looking <em>toward</em> an antipode, I'm going to
assume that we can look <em>beyond</em> it.   If <em>that</em> happened,
the quasars would undergo an odd decrease in the number counts as we look
toward the antipode; then the number counts would increase once more when
we looked <em>beyond</em> the antipode. </p>
                <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Take a look at the way the 2QZ counts are decreasing
en route to the mid three range of red shifts, shown very dramatically in
                <a href="QSOTable7.html">Table 7</a>.   This is probably
the first study where the "law of large numbers" can be used in deep space
to verify statistically whether or not an antipode is close enough to affect
the distribution.  </p>
<blockquote>
<font color=red>
[Well<strong>...</strong>Maybe not<strong>...</strong>The "law of large numbers" was used about a half century ago to debunk the steady state theory, which predicted that the universe should look the same in every direction at all ages.   Radio astronomy was in its infancy in the middle of the twentieth century.   Even so, it was mature enough to show that radio sources did not obey the "radius-cube" rule; there were more fainter radio sources than would be expected if the radio sources were evenly distributed in Euclidean space.    By noting what happens to the <a href="App6.html#slope">traditional -3/2 slope</a> in deep space - that is, the counts of sources versus their luminosity - we might be able to gather a few hints suggesting an alternate model.]
</font>
</blockquote>
<p>    
                <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>If there's an antipode in our Hubble horizon, then it's
as if we are on the outside of a sphere looking <em>in</em> (rather than
our usual Euclidean position at the center of a sphere looking <em>out</em>
, as explained near the end of <a href="hierarchy(1).html#Myth2"> of Essay
#1</a>).   If there were a uniform distribution of radio sources (or quasars)
near an antipode, instead of their number <em>increasing</em> as the <em>
cube</em> of the radius from our position (like they would in normal space),
their number would <em>decease</em> as the <em>inverse cube</em> of the distance
(from the antipode). <a name="void">  </a> </p>
                <p> </p>
                <blockquote><font color="red">[As a matter of fact, many
researchers have remarked for years that there seems to be a curious "void"
in which we don't observe much of <em>anything</em>, except "absorbers".
  Radio sources, for example, show a similar decrease in the counts in the
same range.   And the way "radio loud quasars" are behaving is mentioned
                  <a href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=1999hrrl.conf..163S&amp;db_key=AST&amp;high=3bf93a82e127385">
in this abstract</a>. An obvious decrease in the number counts of many different
types of objects - such as quasars, radio sources, and galaxies - at the
same distance should be expected if an antipode is within our Hubble horizon. 
                 
                  <p>
If you're reading these essays in
sequence, you've seen that 2QZ decay before, <a href="antigal.html#lyman">in Essay #5</A> where I gave the link to Charles Steidel's <a href="http://www.astro.caltech.edu/~ccs/ugr.html">
Caltech link</a> for the Lyman-break galaxies.   The histogram at the end
of the file shows that the Lyman-break galaxies are behaving quite similarly
to the quasar cutoff in the the 2QZ survey.]  </font> </p>
                  </blockquote>
  
                  <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Consider the 2QZ quasars listed in the Veron 10th edition,
as shown in <a href="QSOTable7.html">Table 7</a>.   Let's work backwards
and assume (1) there's an antipode out in the mid three range of red shifts
(where Table 7 shows no quasars), and (2) there are two quasars in the first
"shell" at what we will consider a unit length from the antipode (the first
closer relative distance column).   Then if we assume there <em>is</em> antipode
and there are two quasars at a unit length away (corresponding to the number
we observe between z = 3.119 and z = 3.314), how many quasars can we <em>
predict</em> we ought to see as the radius increases from the antipode versus
how many we actually <em>do</em> see. </p>

                 <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>This is an especially good test to use with Croom's
catalogue because there was no particular bias in the way quasars are discovered
(since only a preselected range of visual magnitudes is considered).   At
first glance, it seems that the quasars in the 2QZ survey <em>are</em> decreasing
in reasonable conformity as the inverse cube of the distance, which is what
                  <em>should</em> happen if the decrease is due to the curvature
of space near an antipode. </p>
                  <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>One might be inclined to say "Hold on!   The quasars
in the 2QZ were in the visual magnitude range of 18.25 to 20.85, and so the
cutoff in this preselected range is because quasars are getting brighter
and fall outside the range we selected.   So this is not a <em>cosmological</em>
 cutoff; it's an <em>observational</em> one." </p>
                  <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>To a degree, you're right! </p>
                  <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>If you look at the <a href="#7A">7A Summary Table</a>
 above, you'll notice that between RD 0.78 and 0.80 the average quasar absolute
magnitude increases almost half a magnitude (but not quite so much in the
running-seven-average just below).   And the standard deviation is behaving
like it always does.   Then the increase in absolute magnitude is not quite
so pronounced at RD 0.81, at the same time the variance takes a noticeable
dip well below the usual 0.550 to 0.600 range.   Obviously we 're losing
the brighter quasars (which is why the standard deviation drops) <em>exactly</em>
 where I offer the simple table which points out the inverse cube behavior. 
                  </p>
                  <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>But if we're losing the brighter quasars for some inexplicable
reason at the top end of the pre-selected magnitude range, why don't fainter
quasars move into that range for the same reason?  </p>
                  <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>It's <em>still</em> a fair question<strong>:</strong> why does the quasar population between
visual magnitudes 18.25 and 20.85 decrease as the cube of the distance just
before the mid three range of red shifts? 
                  <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>I think the cutoff <em>might</em> be a cosmological
one<strong>...</strong> 
                  <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Caused by an antipode<strong>...</strong> 
                  <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>There is one other very intriguing clue suggesting
an antipode at the red shift of this initial estimate.   Essay #6 mentioned
the most interesting anomalies I could find in the red shift range from z
= 3 to z = 4.   One of the more fascinating ones was the <a href="antianom.html#GRB">
gamma ray burster</a> at - surprise! - z = 3.42, <em>exactly</em> the red
shift of the antipode crudely extrapolated by the inverse cube decay of the
2QZ quasars. </p>
                  <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>If this interpretation is correct, the "cutoff" in
the Croom survey places the antipode very near z = 3.4 and any discrete, luminous object near it, such as GRB971214 above, would appear unusually bright.   (Obviously I do
not believe that the single quasar at the higher red shift is particularly
significant.)   As you will see shortly, this initial estimate might be just
a tad low; an antipode nearer z = 3.5 seems more likely (if it's there at
all).  </p>
                  <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Assuming you've already looked at <a href="QSOTable7.html">
Table 7</a>, there's no denying that what seems to be the <em>high</em> end
of a "cutoff" in the Croom survey matches a "cutoff" at the <em>low</em>
end of the SDSS quasars (though the latter implied cutoff is not so dramatic
with much lower numbers in the SDSS survey at the moment and there is there
is probably observational bias in the first batch of SDSS quasars in Veron's
catalogue). </p>
                  <p><IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Perhaps it's time to ask those same two intriguing
questions<strong>:</strong> Are the numbers trying to tell us something?
  Or are we trying to read something into the numbers that isn't there?

<font color=navy size=4><strong>B. Other Surveys: SDSS, 2MASS, FIRST, etc.</strong></font> 
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left> Obviously, the 2QZ and SDSS quasars included in the Veron 10th catalogue are very intriguing.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left> But are these results typical?
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left> Is this unusual statistical behavior - particularly the noticeable minimum in quasar counts at z = 3.5 - confirmed or negated when we observe other distributions.   For decades the number of quasars at the higher red shifts have probably been inflated ("biased") because everybody wants Andy Warhol's "15 minutes in the sun" for discovering the recordholder of the moment.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left> But what happens in the surveys that aren't looking for anything in particular, the surveys that simply accept the number counts by red shifts as they are?
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left> There are a few other catalogues <a href="http://cdsweb.u-strasbg.fr/cats/Cats.htx">
at the CDS site</a> which have a sufficient number of quasars at the high red shifts of most  interest.   I'll provide a brief description of the catalogue and then summarize the results in a simple table below.   What I'm looking for primarily is a large enough "non-biased, non-Veron"  data base to see if it verifies what seems to be very unusual statistical behavior of the quasars.
<p>
<blockquote>
<font color=red>
[Looking for "non-Veron" results is probably an illusion since the Verons have done such a thorough job of surveying the literature.   What I'm looking for are the quasars which are <em>probably</em> in the Veron catalogue (or soon will be), but originally discovered with a <em>minimum</em> of bias, especially at the high red shifts.]
</font>
</blockquote>  
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left> If you insert <em>exactly</em> the red copy in the catalogue selection page at the CDS site, you'll get the referenced catalogue.  
<p>
<blockquote>
<OL>
<li><a href="http://www.sdss.org/links.html">The Sloan Digital
Sky Survey: SDSS1</a>. Cat No. <font color=red>J/AJ/121/2308</font>.   Although there is a quasar catalogue at the SDSS site with 3800 quasars (see 7. below), this catalogue of 898 quasars is also useful.   (The two catalogues have common quasars, particularly at the higher red shifts.  To avoid confusion, this catalogue has a "1" suffix, and the catalogue at their site is followed by "2".)
<p>
<li><a href="http://pegasus.phast.umass.edu">2MASS</A>: 2 <strong>M</strong>icron <strong>A</strong>ll <strong>S</strong>ky <strong>S</strong>urvey.   Cat. No. <font color=red> J/AJ/121/2843</font>.   Two 50" telescopes - one in each hemisphere - are surveying the sky at near infrared wavelengths.   Although an "ultraviolet excess" is a characteristic associated with many quasars, the fact that so many infrared sources have been matched with quasars leads many astronomers to believe that the infrared range may reveal many quasars overlooked by the usual techniques in the ultraviolet range.   There do not seem to be any <em>new</em> quasars in this catalogue with 2279 entries, simply a match with quasars already known.
<p>
<li>Gurvits: Cat. No. <font color=red>J/A+A/342/378</font>.   Although there are many catalogues of radio sources at the Strasbourg Center, not that many include the red shifts.   This is one of them, which surveyed part of the sky at 6 and 20 cm.   The total quasar count in this catalogue is 330, with Gurvits as the primary suthor.  
<p>
<LI><A href="http://sundog.stsci.edu">FIRST</a>: Cat. No. <font color=red>J/ApJS/126/133</font>.   As mentioned above, this is an ongoing survey of the sky at 20cm, using the very large array (VLA) near Socorro, New Mexico and overlaps the SDSS survey.   At the moment (February, 2002) only a few small catalogues are available; the largest is this one with 636 quasars.
<p>
<LI>
Parkes: Cat. No. <font color=red>VIII/15</font> Parkes is a radio telescope in Australia which can survey the sky at a variety of radio wavelengths.   Many quasars in the Veron catalogue have a prefix "PKS", meaning that they were first discovered by this telescope as a radio source.  The catalogue has 8264 radio sources, and the quasars at high red shifts from this catalogue are in the table which follows.            
<p>
<LI><a href="http://www.2dfquasar.org">2QZ</a>: Cat. No. <font color=red>VII/223</font>.   These quasars were discussed in the previous section.   However, only the 2QZ quasars listed in the 10th edition  of the Veron catalogue are included in Table 4.   The next table shows the actual counts by red shift interval, and are slightly higher counts (since a few of the quasars in the 2QZ catalogue were already known, and listed in the Veron catalogue under their original name).    
<p>
<li>
The last column, SDSS2, is presented at the SDSS site - more later in this file.   They call the quasars in this catalogue the "EDR" quasars which means (I think) "early data release".   Curiously, the red shifts of the same quasar disagree up to a few percent, especially at high red shifts, with common listings in SDSS1 and SDSS2.   Apparently, the initial red shifts were revised slightly.

<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left> The "subtotal" in the above table is simply the sum of the first five distributions directly above it.   I suspect there might some bias for high red shift quasars in the SDSS1 quasars, but on the whole I believe these are honest numbers, and are a good approximation of the relative number counts of quasars at high red shifts.   At least they agree reasonably well with the larger SDSS2 number counts at the critical range under consideration (two rows below).
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left> Still, skepticism is understandable because if you look at the way the first two distributions (SDSS1 and 2MASS) are behaving, you might think I'm "stacking the cards" since a sudden rise after z = 3.5 in the SDSS1 distribution is offset by a sudden drop just before z = 3.5 in 2MASS.   So combining the two of them makes it look like there is a dramatic minimum at z = 3.5.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left> Again, let's concede that the SDSS researchers are interested in the high red shift quasars, which may explain the reason why the counts are much larger at the high red shifts.   But then why the sudden drop in quasars in the 2MASS counts out there in the mid three range of red shifts?
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left> And why is there typically either (1) a noticeable <em>drop</em> in the mid three range of red shifts or (2) a <em>drop and then a rise</em> of a variety of quasar distributions which were compiled without any particular "hidden agenda" in mind? 
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left> The few catalogues with red shifts for radio sources (which are also quasars) in the Gurvits, FIRST, and Parkes surveys also indicate that quasars are passing through a minimum in the number counts out in the mid three range of red shifts.   Obviously, there's not that many of them, but enough to verify the global trend (which has been known for years).   The wavelengths used with radio telescopes are less likely to be affected by extinction - that is, the diminution of light caused by intervening galactic dust - than telescopes used at the normal optical wavelengths.   So what they tell us about the number counts vs red shift can be accepted with greater reliability. 
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left> How much salt you want to put on these complementary results is up to you.   For what it's worth, I believe these (intentional) "non-Veron" data suggest once more that something might interesting is going on out there in the mid three range of red shifts.

 <font color="navy" size=4> <strong>C. Additional Veron Intrigue</strong> </font> 
<pre>
</pre>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>One other thing is worth mentioning in the quasars
at the higher red shifts where the Croom counts are negligible<strong>:</strong> take a look at the <font face="symbol">
s</font> (standard deviation) on the bottom row of the <a href="#table">Table 4</a> above.   Note that it's usually between 0.900 and 1.000, with one exception<strong>
:</strong> for some unknown reason, there's "blip" in the distribution in the mid
three range of red shifts.   In fact, the standard deviation reaches a maximum value in the same relative distance column (RD = 0.86) where the quasars are passing through a minimum in the number counts. 
                    <p> </p>
                    <blockquote><font color="red"> [Obviously, this seems
to contradict the Croom results above.   But the 2QZ quasars were selected
within a certain range, and the innumerable hit and miss searches over the
years with a variety of visual and radio telescopes - there are over 2,000
references in the Veron catalogues - did not have a common set of limitations
(which is why the 2QZ survey is important).   And it should not come as a
surprise that the global results are not necessarily consistent with the
2QZ findings.] </font> </blockquote>
   
                      <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Also notice that the fainter quasars at the bottom
of the table are prominent in the lower lefthand corner, are scarce for awhile,
and then for some reason start appearing again in the red shifts from z =
3 to z = 4.   The reason for the increase in the standard deviation
is not because there are a higher number of <em>brighter</em> quasars in
the population; it increases because there are more <em>fainter</em> quasars
in the population. </p>
                      <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>So it's a fair question<strong>:</strong> why is there an increase in the
fainter quasars in the z = 3 to 4 range?    Even if you say "Well, there's
a lot of interest in high red shift quasars and so there is a tendency for
the number counts of the fainter quasars in this epoch to be artificially
or observationally inflated." 
                      <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Granted<strong>...</strong>Still, why do they appear <em>here</em> and not at higher red shifts? </p>
                      <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Although there may be a reasonable explanation
in astrophysics or observational bias, let's consider the phenomenon in terms
of the unusual morphology - antipode - in Riemannian space.  </p>
                      <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The 2QZ analysis above mentioned that if we had
a uniform distribution of objects near an antipode, they would decrease in
number as the inverse cube of the distance from the antipode.   As a matter
of fact, it looked like this theoretical consideration worked fairly well
in the Croom cutoff, as hinted at by Narlikar and Sheshadri back in 1985
(mentioned <a href="App7.html">in Appendix 7</a>).   Perhaps the reason it worked tolerably well was because only a preselected magnitude range (18.25 to 20.85, at a certain wavelength) in the 2QZ survey was considered.</p>
                      <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>But there's a second consideration<strong>:</strong> luminous objects become <em>brighter</em>, and how bright they appear <em>in theory</em> was explained in <a href="App6.html">Appendix 6</a>.   So if we have not a uniform distribution of luminous objects,
but a distribution of objects with a significant number of them in the less
luminous range, we might see the fainter objects in the population if, and
only if, they happen to be near an antipode. 
                      <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>And it does appear that the additional fainter
quasars in Table 4 in the mid three range of red shifts do two things<strong>:</strong> (1) they flatten out the dramatic rise in absolute magnitude as they become relatively more numerous and (2) they also cause a noticeable increase in the ordinarily well behaved standard
deviation.</p> 
                      <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Are we detecting fainter quasars that we would
not normally classify as absolute magnitude -23 or brighter were it not for
the fact that their luminosity is affected by the curvature of space near
an unsuspected antipode?   Are they really the nuclei of Seyfert galaxies
in disguise?   (Seyfert galaxies are the next less luminous objects below
quasars.)   </p>
                      <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Just exactly what is a <em>non-biased distribution</em>
 of quasars at any red shift?   Is the decline in fainter magnitudes an <em>
observational</em> one, limited by the light gathering power of modern telescopes.
  Or is it a <em>cosmological</em> one<strong>:</strong> there just aren't any below a certain
absolute magnitude?   Is the true distribution what we see in the mid-three
range of red shifts?   Or is the true distribution better reflected elsewhere?
  Or is a "true" distribution of quasars an oxymoron to begin with?  
                      <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>At the moment, no one really knows just exactly
how the smaller volume of space (r<sup>-3</sup>, with r measured from the
antipode) interfaces with a distribution of luminous objects like quasars
(which will become much brighter by r<sup>2</sup> near an antipode).   But
it would not surprise me if we're witnessing a fascinating interplay between
them in the mid three range of red shifts in the quasar population.

 <font color="navy" size=4> <strong> D. Maxima and Minima in the
Mid Three Range of Red Shifts</strong> </font>
<pre>
</pre>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>I'm not going to do anything real fancy here<strong>:</strong> simply list all the red shifts and the absolute magnitudes from z = 3.35 to just below z = 3.65 in the Veron
catalogue (10th Edition).   <strong>None</strong> of the quasars in Croom's
2QZ catalogue are in this range, although his results - that interesting
cutoff - seem to suggest there might be an antipode in the mid three range
of red shifts. 
                      <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>If the idea about the antipode is correct, at least
                      <em>two</em> things  - and maybe  <em>three</em> -
should happen if the quasar population is being affected by an antipode in
the mid three range of red shifts<strong>;</strong> 
                      <blockquote> <font color="red"> 
1. As the quasars pass
through a <em>minimum</em> in number counts at a certain red shift, then
 <br>
2. They should also pass through a <em>maximum</em> in absolute magnitude at the <em>
same</em> red shift. </font> </blockquote>
<p>
<a href="perceptions(2).html">Essay #2</a> explained that if we looked toward - and <em>beyond</em> - an unsuspected antipode, the curvature curvature of space in its vicinity would cause objects to pass through a <em>minimum</em> in number counts at the same time they would pass through a <em>maximum</em> in luminosity. 
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>All right.   Does it happen in the quasar population?
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The behavior of the standard deviation on the
quasar tables at high red shifts hint that we might try something else. 
 Suppose for the sake of argument that we usually don't detect the entire
range in magnitudes of quasars because of observational limits<strong>;</strong> that is, our telescopes - especially the ones used for the wide field surveys - detect quasars down to an observational threshold that is <em>higher</em> than their <em>natural</em> "floor". 
                        <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>In short, we might be missing the fainter quasars
in the population. </p>
                        <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>But if the entire quasar population seems to
get brighter because of the curvature of space near  an unsuspected antipode,
then perhaps we <em>might</em> detect the full range of quasars, including
the fainter ones we would normally miss, because they are near the antipode.
  The standard deviation of the population, artificially low because of our failure
to observe the fainter quasars due to the limits of our telescope, would
become larger near the antipode because the curvature would move the fainter
quasars up to and beyond the lower limit of our threshold of detection. </p>
                        <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Now if this sounds silly, take another look at
the behavior of the quasars in the Relative Distance columns of 0<strong>.</strong>85, 0<strong>.</strong>86, and 0<strong>.</strong>87 in the <a href="#table">quasar table</a> above.   How do <em>you</em> explain that obvious change in the standard deviation at
these red shifts? 
                        <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Obviously Table 4 above clearly indicates <em>
something</em> is happening statistically to the quasar population in the
mid three range of red shifts.   The standard deviation <em>is</em> getting
larger, then smaller. </p>
                        <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>This leads me to test a third statistical parameter
in the quasar population<strong>:</strong> 
                        <blockquote> <font color="red"> 3. Quasars will pass
though a <em>maximum</em> in standard deviation at the same red shift as
1. and 2. above. </font> </blockquote>
 
                          <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>So just exactly at what red shift does it change and how many trend lines change at or near the same red shift?    </p>
                          <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Here are the "raw data", that is, all the quasars with red shifts (z) and absolute magnitudes (Mabs) between z = 3.35 to just
below z = 3.65 in Veron's 10th edition.   When there is a "0" in the third decimal place of the red shifts, it usually means that the red shift has been measured to just <em>two</em>  places.</p>

<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Here's how we'll do it: we'll consider running totals of 11, 21, and 31
items above.   For example, the minimum in number counts is determined by
calculating the largest difference between the highest and lowest values
of the red shift in a running total of 11, 21, or 31 cells.   The difference
in red shifts between 10 cells on the above table is <em>highest</em> at
cell 50 - that is, the difference between the red shifts in cells 45 and
55 - because nowhere else does the difference in ten consecutive cells exceed
0.057 in red shift.  So in a running total involving 11 cells (#1 subtracted
from #11), this method indicates that the quasars pass through a minimum
in number count at cell 50 when z = 3.502. 
                          <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Similarly in a running total of 21 cells, the
absolute magnitude reaches a maximum at cell 53 (z = 3.52, average Mabs =
-28.581), which is the average of cells 43 through 63 divided by 21. Once
you have the average magnitude, it's easy to calculate the variance (<font face="symbol">
s</font><sup>2</sup>) and standard deviation (<font face="symbol">s</font>
, which is used on the quasar tables), provided you're using a "programmable"
spreadsheet.   For example, the standard deviation in the running total of
21 cells centered on cell 53 above is 1.630.   This isn't quite the high
point in the standard deviation, but it's close; the peak in the running
total of 21 occurs when <font face="symbol">s</font> = 1.674 at cell 59 (when
z = 3.550).     </p>

<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>I like numbers that behave nicely, and
at least the <em>results</em> are behaving fairly well...For instance, look
at the change in the size of the red shift change in the running totals of
11, 21, and 31.   If the red shift intervals from one cell to the next were
the same, the running total for 21 ought to two times higher than it is for
11.   But it's not; the change in red shift for the 21 running is 0.106,
slightly less than two times the running total for 11 running cells. </p>
                                <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>And ditto for what happens in the running
total of 31, which means the quasars are unquestionably passing through a
minimum in the number counts. </p>
                                <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Similarly, as we enlarge the magnitude
base from 11 to 21 to 31 running cells, the peak is the same for the running
totals of 11 and 21, and changes only slightly when we move to the running
total of 31.   But the average magnitude obviously increases as we enlarge
the base, which means there is likewise no doubt that we are passing through
a peak in luminosity in a rather narrowly defined range, and it's the same
range in which we're passing though a minimum in the number counts. </p>
                                <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The standard deviation is not quite so
well behaved, but that is not particularly disconcerting. &nbsp; We probably
have to be somewhat removed from the antipode (where the theoretical number
count is zero) before the numbers reach a sufficient critical mass that they
start behaving tolerably well statistically. &nbsp; Even so, the red shift
at the low point of standard deviation (cell 28 for the running total of
31) is z = 3.418, and the high point occurs at z = 3.55 (running total 21,
cell 59). &nbsp; In terms of relative distance, this difference is less than
1/100th of the distance to the singularity. </p>
                                <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>It just <em>seems</em> to be a large
range when we compare it to how well the data is behaving with respect to
the number counts and average magnitudes. </p>
                                <p>
 </p>
                                <blockquote><font color="red">[A word of
caution on the standard deviation<strong>...</strong>The data above eliminate
many of the brighter quasars just below the mid three range of red shifts.
  If they would have been included, the highest standard deviations would
have occurred there.   While the best guess for the red shift of the hypothetical
antipode is still in the mid three range of red shifts, the more frequent
very bright quasars in the lower z = 3 range suggests that using the standard
deviation is definitely a less reliable estimator than magnitudes and number
counts.   Because the standard deviation is an <em>exponential</em> function,
the significantly brighter or fainter quasars included in the sample will
have a greater impact on that number than they will on the (non-exponential)
average magnitude.] </font> </blockquote>
 
                                  <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>But I have to concede there's a chink in the armor<strong>:</strong> the behavior of luminous objects near the antipode should be  "asymptotic", which means we ought to get an unmistakable "peak" in the distribution instead of a modest "hill".    This is prediction of the <A href="App6.html#table">Table III</a> in Appendix 6.   Actually the quasar luminosity is in reasonable conformity to the table at high red shifts, until we reach the point of rapidly decaying numbers at around z = 2.5.   Apparently, the exclusion of the  brighter quasars ("luck of the draw") and the inclusion of the relatively more numerous fainter quasars are the main reasons for modifying what we would normally expect to be an asymptote.</p>          
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Nevertheless, I believe we can say with some confidence that at least the number counts and magnitudes both undergo a dramatic change
in trend lines at almost exactly the same red shift - in round numbers, z
= 3.5 - which is what should happen if the quasar population is affected
by an antipode.   The standard deviation seems to change also, but not quite
so dramatically, and not quite so well defined. </p>
                                  <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>If I had to make an estimate of the
red shift of the antipode to one more decimal place, I would pick z = 3.48,
because that's roughly in the middle of the largest gap in red shifts between
Cell 45 (z =3.471) and 46 (z = 3.488) in the range most likely to have an
antipode. </p>

The count of both RDs is rather low, since the antipode is near the boundary between them.   It's just the (bad) "luck of the draw" that this happened; otherwise the antipode's position would have been flagged even better if it happened to fall near the center of one of the RDs.

<strong>E. Counterimages?   Maybe!</strong>
</font>
<pre>

<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Finally, here's something else
you might find intriguing<strong>...</strong> 
                                    <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>If there's an antipode, then an object
quite near it should be seen as a counterimage in the opposite direction.
  (Well...Not quite...There are several considerations which indicate they
won't be seen in <em>exactly</em> opposite directions, which has been explained
in <a href="App7.html">Appendix 7</a>.) </p>
                                    <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>While quasars at Mabs equal to or
greater than -29.0 are not exactly rare - there are 187 of them at z = 3
or greater in the Veron 10th catalogue - what is unusual is to see several
of them bunched together between cells 46 and 51 <A HREF="#tableVI">in Table VI</a> above, while  the underlying quasar population is passing through a minimum in the number counts. &nbsp; (Don't forget: the largest gap in red shifts in this epoch in the 10th edition of
the Verons' catalogue is just below this cluster of bright quasars, between
Cells 45 and 46.) &nbsp; And the lone quasar in this group <em>greater than</em>
 (= <em>"fainter than"</em>) -29.0 is the quasar at -28.7 in cell 49. </p>
                                    <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>And that quasar at -29.9 in Cell
43 (z = 3.47) looks mighty interesting, too. </p>
                                    <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>If there's an antipode out there
in the mid three range of red shifts, these quasars are probably quite close to
it.   They are easily the best candidates to consider for counterimages.
&nbsp; The noticeably brighter quasars at cells 56 (z = 3.535, Mabs = -30.0)
and 58 (z = 3.550, Mabs = -31.0) might work too, but the farther we get from
the antipode, the longer the life of the quasars has to be if we are to see
them in two opposite directions. </p>
                                    <p> </p>
                                    <blockquote><font color="red">[Note that
the bright quasar in Cell 56 lies right on the borderline between RD 0.86
and 0.87.]  </font></blockquote>
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>The life of quasars has been estimated at several tens of millions of years and might be as long as a few hundred million years.  But  <a href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=2001ApJ...555..719C&db_key=AST&high=3bf93a82e117566">
this abstract</a> indicates they probably don't last longer than 300 million years.   Therefore we have to know almost exactly which red shift to consider, or we'll
miss the counterimages that may be there.   
                                      <p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>If quasars last long enough - a minimum of about 100 million years or more - we
should be able to detect the counterimages of those near z = 3.5 (or is it
z = 3.48<strong>?</strong>), and this will formally change the nature of space in the universe
from Friedmann space to Riemannian space.</p>
<blockquote>
<font color=red>[And here's another idea that <em>might</em> work.   The histogram of <a href="http://www.astro.caltech.edu/~ccs/ugr.html"> the Lyman-break galaxies</a> near the end of the file at Charles Steidel's site shows a large scale structure at z = 3.4.   <font color="#1F1FFF"> [But maybe not<strong>...</strong>Remember the curvature of space near the antipode is distorted in a way that it enlarges the space between two objects.   So this structure may not be as large as it seems.]</font>   If the technique can be used to detect galaxies at just a slightly higher red shift, that same large (medium? even small?) scale structure should appear in the opposite direction at z = 3.6, about 100 million years earlier in its evolution.]
</font>
</blockquote>
