src="../images/stadium/04a_arw.gif"></td>									<td width="443" align="left" valign="top"><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#3e2447">THE MOST FREQUENTLY MADE COMMENT ABOUT THE NEW STADIUM IS:<b> &quot;WHY DOESN'T IT HAVE A 100,000 CAPACITY.&quot;<br>										</b></font><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#231428"><br>										Capacities of 100,000 and over are products of days when fans were crowded closely together, usually standing on terraces.<br>										<br>										The new Stadium will have considerably greater legroom than at the old Wembley. The minimum standard of hip to knee room (a key way that architects measure spectator comfort) is currently 660mm. This will increase to be 800mm in the New Stadium. Seats will also be wider at a minimum 500mm in the new stadium, up from 440mm in the old ground. fig.1<br>										</font>										<center>											<p><a href="fig1.htm"><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#231428"><img height="140" width="186" src="../images/stadium/why/fig1_sml.gif" border="0" alt="click for enlarged view"></font></a><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#231428"><br>											</font><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#3e2447"><b>fig.1</b> (click image for enlarged view)</font><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#231428"><br>											</font></p>										</center>										<div align="left">											<p><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#231428">The bible for designing sightlines is an UK Government report called &quot;A Guide to safety in Sports Grounds&quot;, known to architects throughout the world as 'The Green Guide'. This sets out the maximum steeples of seating and generate 'C Standards' (recommended space between the sightline of one person sitting behind another). </font><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#3e2447">fig.2</font><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#231428"><br>											</font></p>										</div>										<center>											<p><a href="fig2.htm"><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#231428"><img height="102" width="150" src="../images/stadium/why/fig2_sml.gif" border="0" alt="click for enlarged view"></font></a><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#231428"><br>											</font><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#3e2447"><b>fig.2</b> (click image for enlarged view)</font><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#231428"><br>											</font></p>										</center>										<div align="left">											<p><font size="2" face="Verdana" color="#231428">The higher the 'C' number the greater the steepness of the seating, but the less obstructed the view. The challenge for the designers is to produce a high 'C' standard but without creating a stand so steep that spectators feel uneasy as they watch the game. The stands get steeper the further back the rows go as the architects try to keep fans as close to the action as possible. The high third tier in the new Wembley has a steepness of 34%, the maximum recommended in the 'Green Guide'. This geometry decides the shape of the bowl. The capacity is then decided by how many of these comfortably seated fans can be kept close to the action.<br>											<br>											<!-- The designers have used two measurements of how close fans should be to the action. The seats would probably have to be classed as restricted views. Because of this it is unlikely that any football ground will be built with a capacity larger than the new Wembley.<br>											<br>											The new Stadium manages to add 10,000 seats to the capacity of the existing Stadium, gives much better legroom for all fans, gives perfect views of the action, provide wider seats, but the last row of seats in the new Stadium is no further back than the last row in the current ground.<br>											</font></div>									</td>								</tr>								<tr>									<td width="180" align="left" valign="bottom">
