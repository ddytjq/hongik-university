	<td width="75%">
		<b>A beastly bad bill                                                                                  </b><br>
		<font size=2>Author: Robert D. Sopuck                                   <br>
		Source: Winnipeg Free Press                                                                                                                                   </font>
	</td>

	<td align=right VALIGN=TOP>		
				Tues.          June 25, 2002
	</td>
	</tr>
	<tr>
		<td colspan=2><br>
		<br>
				<P align=justify>The way I see it, Bill C-15B, the new federal animal cruelty law, is nothing but bad news for all Canadians. Let me explain. No one condones inhumane treatment of animals, but Bill C-15B moves the laws governing the treatment of animals from the "property" section of the law into a subsection of the Criminal Code. This moves animals closer to being granted rights, in line with the radical animal rights agenda. </P>
<P align=justify>Bill C-15B defines an animal as "any animal that has the capacity to feel pain." This will open biomedical research, farming and angling to frivolous legal challenges, especially since scientists cannot agree on which animals feel pain. </P>
<P align=justify>Bill C-15B removes the important legal defences for those who work with animals. In other words, a worm squirming on a fishhook is part of a legal angling activity but that activity could be challenged in court under C-15B. Animal activists have openly stated that legal test cases will be launched if the bill passes. </P>
<P align=justify>Bill C-15B allows authorized organizations such as humane societies to challenge legitimate animal uses and keep some of the proceeds from any fines. The charge would proceed to our overburdened court system only with the approval of an attorney general, but the person charged would incur the expense of defending themselves. In the meantime, the person against whom the frivolous charge was laid will no doubt see their name appear as having been charged with an offence under the sexual offences, public morals and disorderly conduct statute, where Bill C-15B resides. </P>
<P align=justify>Animal activists want the elimination of all animal use by people and the extension of human rights to animals. </P>
<P align=justify>A favoured tactic of animal activists goes like this: "If you can't win in the court of public opinion, get the laws changed so that you just might win in a court of law." A sympathetic judge, a test case, and voila, no more fishing or certain vital medical research or -- well, you fill in the blank. </P>
<P align=justify>The strategy of the animal activists was revealed by Clayton Ruby, a Toronto animal rights lawyer, who said in the March 18, 2000 edition of the Montreal Gazette "...that over the next 10 years, the changes will be subtle, masquerading as animal protection and continuing to develop as a moralistic adjunct to human rights until some of them at least get their own entrenched in law. We'll see the development of animal rights in a very hesitant way, through incremental changes in case law and judges' attitudes. </P>
<P align=justify>"We need legislative protections and legislative change, but you take what you can get. We're just at the beginning of the movement." </P>
<P align=justify>Animal rights groups actively campaign in closely contested constituencies to elect their candidates. In a fund-raising letter, the Animal Alliance of Canada is taking credit for the election of Anne McLellan, the minister who developed C-15B. </P>
<P align=justify>The letter says: "Because of a commitment made by the minister of justice, Anne McLellan, in the House of Commons to pass Bill C-15B, environment voters campaigned for her re-election. Ms McLellan was in a losing campaign. Environment voters stepped in and championed her election. We ran the better campaign." </P>
<P align=justify>McLellan won by 700 votes. True to her word, she introduced the animal protection legislation. </P>
<P align=justify>Now you might say: "So what? I don't fish, hunt, farm or have pets. C-15B doesn't make any difference to me." </P>
<P align=justify>Ahhh, but you'd be dead wrong (pun intended) on that one, since animal use in medical research is a major animal-activist target. </P>
<P align=justify>The group called Canadians for Medical Progress is alarmed about C-15B. Their stand is "Research saves lives." </P>
<P align=justify>C-15B is now in the Senate. Pierre Berton, a supporter of Canadians for Medical Progress, wrote to all senators to "...express our serious concern about the implications and lack of protection in the animal cruelty section of Bill C-15B for the research and scientific community... the animal rights movement regards this bill as a perfect match for their short- and long-term goals." </P>
<P align=justify>The Justice Department says C-15B is just some much-needed updating of an old act and will have little impact. If that is so, why do it? </P>
<P align=justify>Bill C-15B faced some heroic opposition, not the least of which came from the Liberal rural caucus, chaired by Ontario MP Murray Calder, and the Canadian Alliance, plus a coalition of farmers, hunters and medical researchers. But they could not prevail. </P>
<P align=justify>The only hope is that the Senate lives up to its mandate as the chamber of sober second thought and amends this bill. Otherwise, all Canadians will feel the pain. </P>
<P align=justify><EM>Robert D. Sopuck is a vice-president with the </EM><A href="http://www.deltawaterfowl.org/" target=_blank><EM>Delta Waterfowl Foundation</EM></A><EM>, a member of the Wildlife Information Network of Manitoba.</EM></P>
