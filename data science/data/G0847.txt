<hr> 
Planck is the third Medium-Sized Mission (M3) of 
<a HREF="http://www.esrin.esa.it/">ESA</a>'s
<a HREF="http://sci.esa.int/home/ourmissions/index.cfm" 
NAME="Horizon 2000">Horizon 2000 Scientific Programme</a>.
It is designed to image the anisotropies of the Cosmic Background
Radiation Field over the whole sky, with unprecedented sensitivity
and angular resolution. Planck will provide a major source of
information relevant to several cosmological and astrophysical issues,
such as testing theories of the early universe and the origin of
cosmic structure. The scientific development of the mission is 
directed by the <a href="Science_Team.html">Planck Science Team</a>.
</p>

Planck was formerly called <a href="cobras_name.html">COBRAS/SAMBA.</a> 
After the mission was selected and approved (in late 1996), it was renamed in honor 
of the German scientist <a HREF="mplanck/mplanck.html">Max Planck</a>
(1858-1947), Nobel Prize for Physics in 1918.</p>

Budgetary pressures within ESA's scientific programme have forced
a reconsideration of the original implementation plan for Planck,
which called for a launch in 2003.
It is now planned to launch Planck in the first quarter of 2007 
together with the
<A HREF="http://astro.estec.esa.nl/SA-general/Projects/First/first.html">
Herschel </A> satellite, in a configuration often referred to as the
"Carrier" (because Herschel is "carried" by Planck in the launcher - 
see picture at upper left). After launch, Planck and FIRST will separate 
and will be placed in different
orbits around the second Lagrangian point of the Earth-Sun System.
