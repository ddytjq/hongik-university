<td class="hdline1">How Old is the Universe?</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td class="bodytext">
<div class="linkstyle1">
              <p><br>
                Astronomers estimate that the Big Bang occurred between 12 and 
                14 billion years ago. To put this in perspective, the Solar System 
                is thought to be 4.5 billion years old and humans have existed 
                as a species for a few million years. Astronomers estimate the 
                age of the universe in two ways: 1) by looking for the oldest 
                stars; and 2) by measuring the rate of <a href="uni_101expand.html">expansion 
                </a>of the universe and extrapolating back to the Big Bang; just 
                as crime detectives can trace the origin of a bullet from the 
                holes in a wall. </p>
              <p><span class="hdline3"><b>Older Than the Oldest Stars?</b></span></p>

<p>Astronomers can place a lower limit to the age of the universe by studying globular clusters. Globular 
clusters are a dense collection of roughly a million stars. Stellar densities near the center of the 
globular cluster are enormous. If we lived near the center of one, there would be several hundred thousand
stars closer to us than Alpha Centauri, the star nearest to the Sun. </p>
<center>
<p><a href="../ContentMedia/M15GC.jpg" target="new"><img border="0" src="../ContentMedia/M15GC_s.jpg" alt="HST Image: Globular Cluster M15" align="MIDDLE" width="264" height="184"></a><br>
<a href="http://oposite.stsci.edu/pubinfo/gif/M15GC.txt" target="new"><span class="caption">Text Link</span></a><span class="caption"> to the HST press release describing this image</span></p>
</center>

              <p>The <a href="uni_101stars.html">life cycle of a star</a> depends upon 
                its mass. High mass stars are much brighter than low mass stars, 
                thus they rapidly burn through their supply of hydrogen fuel. 
                A star like the Sun has enough fuel in its core to burn at its 
                current brightness for approximately 9 billion years. A star that 
                is twice as massive as the Sun will burn through its fuel supply 
                in only 800 million years. A 10 solar mass star, a star that is 
                10 times more massive than the Sun, burns nearly a thousand times 
                brighter and has only a 20 million year fuel supply. Conversely, 
                a star that is half as massive as the Sun burns slowly enough 
                for its fuel to last more than 20 billion years.</p>

<p>All of the stars in a globular cluster formed at roughly the same time, thus they can serve as cosmic
clocks. If a globular cluster is more than 10 million years old, then all of its hydrogen burning stars will
be less massive than 10 solar masses. This implies that no individual hydrogen burning star will be more
than 1000 times brighter than the Sun. If a globular cluster is more than 2 billion years old, then there
will be no hydrogen-burning star more massive than 2 solar masses.</p>

              <p>The oldest globular clusters contain only stars less massive than 0.7 solar 
                masses. These low mass stars are much dimmer than the Sun. This 
                observation suggests that the oldest globular clusters are between 
                11 and 18 billion years old. The uncertainty in this estimate 
                is due to the difficulty in determining the exact distance to 
                a globular cluster (hence, an uncertainty in the brightness (and 
                mass) of the stars in the cluster). Another source of uncertainty 
                in this estimate lies in our ignorance of some of the finer details 
                of stellar evolution. Presumably, the universe itself is at least 
                as old as the oldest globular clusters that reside in it.</p>

<p><span class="hdline3"><b>Extrapolating Back to the Big Bang</b></span></p>

              <p>An alternative approach to estimating is the age of the universe is to measure 
                the &#147;Hubble constant&#148;. The Hubble constant is a measure 
                of the current <a href="uni_101expand.html">expansion rate</a> 
                of the universe. Cosmologists use this measurement to extrapolate 
                back to the <a href="uni_101bb1.html">Big Bang</a>. This extrapolation 
                depends on the history of the expansion rate which in turn depends 
                on the current density of the universe and on the <a href="uni_101matter.html">composition</a> 
                of the universe.</p>
              <p>If the universe is <a href="uni_101shape.html">flat</a> and composed mostly 
                of matter, then the age of the universe is</p>
<p>
									<center>2/(3 H<sub>o</sub>)</center>
									<p>where H<sub>o</sub> is the value of the Hubble constant.</p>
									<p>If the universe has a very low density of matter, then its extrapolated age is larger:</p>
<p>
									<center>1/H<sub>o</sub>.</center>
									<p>If the universe contains a form of matter similar to the 
<a href="uni_101accel.html">cosmological constant</a>, then the inferred age can be even larger.</p>
									<p>Many astronomers are working hard to measure the Hubble constant 
                using a variety of different techniques. The current best estimates 
                range from 65 km/sec/Megaparsec to 80 km/s/Megaparsec, with the 
                best value being about 72 km/sec/Megaparsec. In more familiar 
                units, astronomers believe that 1/H<sub>o</sub> is between 12 
                and 15 billion years.</p>

<p><span class="hdline3"><b>An Age Crisis?</b></span></p>

              <p>If we compare the two age determinations, there is a potential 
                crisis. If the universe is flat, and dominated by ordinary or 
                dark matter, the age of the universe as inferred from the Hubble 
                constant would be about 9 billion years. The age of the universe 
                would be shorter than the age of oldest stars. This contradiction 
                implies that either 1) our measurement of the Hubble constant 
                is incorrect, 2) the Big Bang theory is incorrect or 3) that we 
                need a form of matter like a cosmological constant that implies 
                an older age for a given observed expansion rate.</p>

<p>Some astronomers believe that this crisis will pass as soon as our measurements improve. If the
astronomers who have measured the smaller values of the Hubble constant are correct, and if the smaller 
estimates of globular cluster ages are also correct, then all may be well for the Big Bang theory, even
without a cosmological constant.</p>

<p><span class="hdline3"><b>MAP Can Measure the Age of the Universe</b></span></p>

              <p>Measurements by the <a href="../aboutmap.html">MAP</a> satellite 
                may help resolve this crisis. If current ideas about <a href="uni_101structures.html">the 
                origin of large-scale structure</a> are correct, then the detailed 
                structure of the <a href="uni_101Flucts.html">cosmic microwave 
                background fluctuations</a> will depend on the current density 
                of the universe, the <a href="uni_101matter.html">composition</a> 
                of the universe and its <a href="uni_101expand.html">expansion 
                rate</a>. MAP should be able to determine these <a href="../m_mm/sg_parameters.html">parameters</a> 
                with an accuracy of better than 5%. Thus, we should be able to 
                estimate the expansion age of the universe to better than 5%.</p>

<p>If the expansion age measured by MAP is larger than the oldest globular clusters, then the 
<a href="uni_101bb1.html">Big Bang</a> theory will have passed an important test. If the expansion age
measured by MAP is smaller than the oldest globular clusters, then there is something fundamentally wrong 
about either the Big Bang theory or the <a href="uni_101stars.html">theory of stellar evolution</a>.
Either way, astronomers will have to rethink many of their cherished ideas.</p>
