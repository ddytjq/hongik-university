				<font class="news"><HTML>You have been flooding us with your opinions about the English tactics during the Third Test in Bangalore. The tourists' skipper, Nasser Hussain, has come in for stick from some quarters, but has also been praised by others. Here's a selection of your comments:<br><br><br><i>Notoriously negative bowling? Shameful low and degrading tactics? These don't sound like the comments afforded a team who are now bowling at tailenders with the home side still 118 runs short of the visitors' score. These don't sound like comments of a team who should obtain wickets at all. If England are bowling defensively, then how are they able to be in such a position at the end of day three? If England continue to bowl a line outside the stumps, then surely they haven't got much chance of getting any wickets. By all accounts this should make it a whole lot easier for one of the greatest of all-time to see his way through not only a single but double-century!<br><br>It seems like there are quite a few Indian supporters not too happy with their side's position in this series. If the bowling is so appalling, then India should amass a very large total. Or could it be that the English actually have a plan? Maybe they've taken a leaf out of the world champion's book and are actually playing a Test for a win, not a draw. Maybe the Indian batsmen were expecting the English to make it a little easier for them. Maybe the Indian players should start thinking of how they are going to increase their total now that Tendulkar has gotten out. <br><br>Mark Schultz<br>Sunny Perth, Western Australia<br><br><hr><br>I think it was not negative practice. The Test Match is a test of players' patience and concentration. Giles was testing the concentration of Tendulkar and in which he succeeded.<br><br>Ritesh Ailawadi<br><br><hr><br>I am 69 and have watched all teams playing cricket with the India XI from the days of Lala Amarnath ji, way back in 1950 onwards.<br><br>The only comparison which comes to my mind is that of Jardine's Bodyline campaign against Don Bradman way back in 1932/33 in Australia on which a feature film is now in existence.<br><br>The only gentleman's game has been degraded beyond repair by the English team. The comments by all English commentators talk about strategy to take Tendulkar's wicket employed by Nasser Hussain in cohorts with his bowlers. Even the great Ravi Shastri, who won an Audi in Australia long back, cannot see the wrong done to the game and gets subdued by Ian Botham and David Gower. Similarly, the moderator and Sivaramakrishna in 20:20 show of Doordarshan does not have the guts to call a spade a spade. Here I appreciate the guts of Jagmohan Dalmia President of BCCI in standing up to the ICC in the matter of the match referee in respect of the Third Test in South Africa.<br><br>IT IS TIME TO TELL THE ENGLISH THAT OUR YOUNG MEN DO NOT FEAR THEIR TACTICS AND STRATEGY AND ARE UP TO IT. Win and defeat is not in one's hands but playing fairly is. <br><br>Sehwag, Tendulkar, Deep Dasgupta and SS Das deserve deep and profound appreciation for their batting and Kumble, Srinath, Harbhajan and Sarandeep for their bowling in this series.<br><br>Sincerely,<br>DN Loganey<br>New Delhi.<br><br><hr><br>What is wrong with the way Hussain's men have bowled? What will go down in the scoreboard and history is the fact that Hussain's England defeated/nearly defeated India in the Bangalore Test - with depleted resources at his command. I mean look at the English bowling lineup. And what are we complaining about? Here are the simple facts. It is a Test match. It calls for patience. That&#8217;s the job description. Can any Indian batsman, save for Tendulkar or Sehwag, say with a clear conscience that it was negative bowling that got them out?  For a team boasting of the 'Fab Four' (that's subliminal sullying of The Beatles), 218-7 sounds more like Altaf Raza than Abbey Road.<br><br>I don't mean to sound disparaging of the team. But let's face it, we are nowhere close to being an outfit of winners. When was the last time you recall an Indian team clinching a last-ball victory in a one-day match? You have to really think, don't you? After the entire Denness controversy, did we bat out the entire day of the last Test on a good pitch, if not for anything but for ego or pride? No. Because they bowled outside/at the off stump. <br><br>Let's really lobby with the ICC: I am an Indian cricketer. Please do not ball outside the leg stump to me. Also please do not bowl outside the off stump as I will give the slips catching practice.<br><br>Come on people. When did we stop loving cricket and start loving only Indian cricket? Look around. See Gary Kirsten. Appreciate Chris Cairns. Or for that matter Hoggard's bowling on the third day of this Test. And when the 'Wall' rebounds balls into gaps on the field, we can applaud that too.<br><br>Let's all grow up.<br><br>Regards<br>Arun Krishnan<br>New York, USA<br><br><hr><br>I think Mr Hussain used completely negative tactics to get rid of Tendulkar. Anyway, it is time for ICC to rectify outdated rules to make Test cricket acceptable and enjoyable for spectators. Almost the same techniques were used by the so-called bold captain Steve Waugh against the Kiwis. So, now the ball is in the ICC's court, are they are interested in making Test cricket acceptable or going to bury it out?<br><br>Sunil Parpiyani<br>Karachi, Pakistan<br><br><hr><br>I have just read your (rather biased) views on Nasser's captaincy and thought that I would offer a defense. Firstly, as regards Giles bowling into the rough, this has been a tactic used by many international sides in recent years. Yes, it may be construed as negative but with the rough acting as it is, it seems a reasonable plan in the current context. Anyway, I for one felt that the 'magic' of Tendulkar would be able to cope with this instead of running off to cry to mummy. Secondly, as for the Indians having played well, I question this. Apart from inspired knocks from Tendulkar (before he spat his dummy) and Sehwag, the rest of the Indian batting has proved turgid to say the least. For example, England have consistently outscored India in terms of run rate. As for Flintoff being dangerous and a cheat, what rubbish.<br><br>Let us not forget that it was Srinath who dismissed Craig White in the first innings with short stuff from around the wicket aimed at the body. Pot calling the kettle black me thinks.<br><br>Yours sincerely<br>Daniel Thatcher<br><br><hr><br>I was disgusted to see the tactics adopted by Nasser Hussain to contain Tendulkar. He might have thought that he won the battle after seeing the back of Tendulkar but it is the game that has lost out. When Ganguly took on the Australians, people said that he was not behaving as a gentleman would. When Steve Waugh did it, they called it gamesmanship. What rubbish!<br><br>Sehwag and the other cricketers were punished for bringing the game into disrepute. What Hussain has done is worse. He is drawing crowds away due to his negative and boring tactics. What he asked Flintoff to do was to bowl bodyline, which the English had used to Bradman. It is the same thing and the reason why people are not taking as much notice is because of the greater amount of equipment these days. With this tactic, Hussain has resigned to the fact that the Indians are far superior to them.<br><br>It was very disappointing to see some of the English commentators applauding his captaincy. The umpires should hand out punishments to negative and unsporting tactics and the captain should be the first to be punished. In this case, the ICC can win a lot of hearts by taking some serious action against Hussain and his team.<br><br>It was very unfortunate that due to this incident it was the little genius that missed out on a century. When I saw the dismissal, my reaction was to say some things which I cannot mention. I am sure that it would have been the case with million of others.<br><br>Thank you<br>Rahul</i></HTML><br><br><hr><br><b>COMMENT</b><br>Do you think England were wrong to adopt such tactics?
