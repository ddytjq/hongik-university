Cultured cells from two different			species can be induced to fuse to form hybrid cells whose nuclei			also fuse (heterokaryons). 			<LI>In interspecific fusions, selective loss of one parents' chromosomes			occurs during propagation of the fused cells. When mouse-human			hybrid cells are cultured they loose many of the human chromosomes.						<LI>Hybrid cell lines A, B, and C contained the human chromosomes			indicated in the table below. 			<LI>The cell lines were tested by <A HREF="MG11123.html">enzyme electrophoresis</A> for their ability to make the human hypoxanthine phosphoribosyl			transferase (HPRT) , glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD)			and thymidine kinase (TK). 			<LI>Since the extra chromosomes are not essential, they often undergo			deletions of parts of the chromosome, revealed by <A HREF="MG11322.html">chromosome banding</A>. 		</UL>		<CENTER>			<P><TABLE BORDER="0" CELLPADDING="0" CELLSPACING="2">				<TR>					<TD></TD>					<TD COLSPAN="8">					<CENTER>						<H5>Human NAME="Interp"></A>Interpretations</H4>		<UL TYPE="circle">			<LI>HPRT and G6PD genes are located on the X chromosome, while the			TK gene must be on chromosome 17. 			<LI>HPRT and G6PD are part of a <A HREF="MG111122.html">linkage group</A> containing other genes. These other genes must also be located			on the X chromosome. For example, phosphoglycerate kinase, known			to be linked to glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase is also on the			X chromosome. 			<LI>Identification of the chromosome location of one gene is sufficient			to assign the whole linkage group to that chromosome. 			<LI>Correlation of the <A HREF="MG1212.html">breakpoints</A> in a chromosome <A HREF="MG1214.html">deletion series</A> with the presence or absence of the enzyme can be used to assign			the gene for the enzyme to a specific chromosome band. 		</UL>		<CENTER>			<P><A HREF="#Facts">Facts</A> | <A HREF="#Interp">Interpretations</A> | <A HREF="#Info">Further Info.</A> | <A HREF="#bottom">Other Pages</A> <BR>			</P>		</CENTER>		<H4><A NAME="Info"></A>Further information<IMG SRC="nav4.gif" WIDTH="69" HEIGHT="71" ALIGN="right" USEMAP="#nav4B1F889C9">		<MAP NAME="nav4B1F889C9">			<AREA COORDS="3,55,25,68" SHAPE="rect" HREF="MG1212.html">			<AREA COORDS="4,29,26,50" SHAPE="rect" HREF="MG129.html">			<AREA COORDS="24,3,44,27" SHAPE="rect" HREF="MG12.html">			<AREA COORDS="43,29,58,56" SHAPE="rect" HREF="MG122.html">			<AREA COORDS="26,30,40,56" SHAPE="rect" HREF="MG121.html">			<AREA COORDS="42,59,66,69" SHAPE="rect" HREF="MG1214.html">		</MAP>		</H4>		<UL TYPE="square">			<LI>Large <A HREF="http://www.bis.med.jhmi.edu/Dan/coriell/dna/sumtable.html">collections</A> of cell lines containing different human chromosomes or parts			of chromosomes have been established. 		</UL>		<CENTER>			<P><A NAME="bottom"></A><A HREF="MG1212.html">Last</A> | <A HREF="MG129.html">Vocabulary</A> | <A HREF="MG121.html">Overview</A> | <A HREF="#Facts">Top</A> | <A HREF="MG1214.html">Next</A> <BR>			<BR>			</P>		</CENTER>		<P>		<HR>		</P>		<P>This is page 1213 of <A HREF="../MG01.html">Molecular Genetics</A> by <A HREF="http://opbs.okstate.edu/~melcher/UM.html">Ulrich Melcher</A>, &copy; 1997, 1998 <BR>		<HR>		</P>		<ADDRESS>E-mail inquiries to <A HREF="mailto:umelcher@biochem.okstate.edu">U.
