<P><CENTER><A HREF="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/external-search/?keyword=Human+Evolution&tag=nearctica"><B>Human
Evolution</B></A></CENTER></TD>
<TD WIDTH="70%" VALIGN="TOP"><P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.wsu.edu:8001/vwsu/gened/learn-modules/top_longfor/lfopen-index.html">The
Long Foreground: Human Prehistory</A>. Richard Law, Washington State University.
An absolutely great web site on human evolution. It covers all aspects of
our hominid ancestors in a non-technical, but thorough manner. In addition
to the general introduction there is specific information about each of
the hominid ancestors and places them in geological time. You'll also find
a glossary of terms. Highly recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.indiana.edu/~origins/">Human Origins and Evolution
in Africa</A>. Jeanne Sept, Indian University. A very extensive set of lecture
notes on human evolution from a number of different courses. This site is
also a good place to go to find links to other hominid sites on the web.
Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.wwnorton.com/college/anthro/bioanth/">How Humans
Evolved</A>. Robert Boyd and Joan B. Silk. A wonderful site on the evolution
of humans. There is a great, well illustrated summary of our current understanding
of human evolution. You'll find a general introduction to natural selection
and population genetics, human behavior and mating, and the fossil record.
Highly recommended.</B></P>

<P>&nbsp;<IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM"
NATURALSIZEFLAG="3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.leeds.ac.uk/chb/h1060.html">History
of Man: Human Evolution</A>. Terry McAndrews and Steve Paxton. A wonderful
introduction to human evolution beginning with primate taxonomy and progressing
to the fossil record for the primates and man. A series of 10 lectures are
contained in pdf files (Adobe Acrobat). Highly recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.talkorigins.org/faqs/homs/">Fossil Hominids</A>.
Jim Foley, Talk.Origins. A marvelous site with a wealth of information and
figures on human evolution and in particular the fossil record. All of the
major fossil hominds are discussed in the form of a critique of creationist
claims that none of them are ancestors to humans. Highly recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.sscf.ucsb.edu/~hagen/crania/">Human Evolution:
The Fossil Evidence in 3D</A>. Phillip L. Walker and Edward H. Hagen. This
sites features a number of three dimensional pictures of skulls from modern
primates and fossil homind ancestors. The pictures can be rotated 360 degrees.
You will need the Shockwave plugin, but you can download it from the site.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.geocities.com/CapeCanaveral/Lab/8853/index.html">The
Lucy Test</A>. Matthew Priestley. Australopicthecus afarensis is an extinct
primate sometimes cited as a direct ancestor of humans. The famous fossil
for this species is &quot;Lucy&quot;. At this site you can learn more about
afarensis and the debate between creationists and evolutionary biologists
about its role as an ancestor of humans. The most interesting feature of
the site is a test you can take comparing various parts of the &quot;Lucy&quot;
fossils with primate and human equivalents (you are not told which is which).
The results are scored to see if you think it is a hominid, a primate, or
some intermediate stage. Fun.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.asu.edu/clas/iho/science.htm">Institute of Human
Origins</A>. The web site for the Institute of Human Origins contains a
variety of information about fossil hominds, but is strongest on data for
&quot;Lucy&quot; the famous fossil of <I>Australopithecus afarensis</I>.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.cruzio.com/~cscp/index.htm">The Fossil Evidence
for Human Evolution in China</A>. Dennis A. Etler. An extensive and impressive
web site on the fossil evidence for hominds (mostly Homo erectus) in China.
There is an introduction to the topic and a number of arcticle.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.pro-am.com/origins/">Origins of Humankind</A>.
This web site has a number of features including a wide variety of articles,
both introductory and technical on hominid fossils and human ancestors.
The site is also important as a source of current news on new fossils and
current theories in human evolution. Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.emory.edu/LIVING_LINKS/">Living Links</A>. Emory
University. Living Links is an institute dedicated to research on primates.
If you get confused about how humans fit into the scheme of primate evolution
and just exactly what a hominid is, be sure to go here and choose taxonomy
from the box in the upper right hand corner.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.geocities.com/Athens/Acropolis/5579/TA.html">Anthropology
and Human Origins Website</A>. Kevin L. Callahan. The materials you will
find at this web site are for a course in human evoluiton taught at the
University of Minnesota. There is a wonderful jumble of material here including
original material and links to other web sites. Our favorite is a section
on primate skulls called &quot;Name that Skull&quot;. You are presented
with a series of skulls, and then have to take a test on them. The site
is sometimes confusing, but recommended anyway.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <A HREF="http://www.uiowa.edu/~bioanth/homosec2.html"><B>The African
Emergence and Early Asian Dispersals of the genus <I>Homo</I></B></A><B>.
Russell Ciochon. A great series of pages on the genus <I>Homo</I> during
the early years of the evolution of the genus. Well illustrated and recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.amnh.org/enews/iskulls.html">Fossil Skulls</A>.
American Museum of Natural History. A great site on hominid skulls. You
can view them in three dimensions and rotate them. You'll need a Shockwave
plug in to do it however.</B></P>

<P><A HREF="http://www.si.edu/resource/faq/nmnh/evolve.htm"><B>Selected
References</B></A><B>. Smithsonian Department of Anthropology. An extensive
bibliography of books and journal articles on human evolution.</B></TD></TR>
