    <H1><CENTER>The Biggest Explosions in the Solar System</CENTER></H1>

    <H4><CENTER>NASA's HESSI spacecraft aims to unravel an explosive
    mystery: the origin of solar flares.</CENTER></H4>
</TD>
    <TD WIDTH="16%">
    <P><CENTER><A HREF="http://www.nasa.gov/"><IMG SRC="../images/msfcmeatballsm.gif"

    audio</A>, a <A HREF="http://science.nasa.gov/headlines/y2002/images/hessi/audio/story.mp3">downloadable

    <P><B><IMG SRC="images/hessi/manmachine_sts103_med.jpg" WIDTH="250"
    HEIGHT="247" ALIGN="RIGHT" BORDER="1" NATURALSIZEFLAG="3" ALT="see caption">Feb.
    6, 2002</B> -- Astronauts love space walks. Floating weightless
    hundreds of kilometers above Earth, the terrain below racing
    by at 17,000 mph --no space traveler wants it to end. But it
    only takes two words to send one of those brave explorers racing
    back to their craft: &quot;Solar flare!&quot;</P>

    <P>&quot;Solar flares are the biggest explosions in the solar
    system,&quot; says Robert Lin of UC Berkeley's Space Science
    Lab. &quot;They erupt near sunspots with the force of a hundred
    million hydrogen bombs.&quot; Astronauts caught spacewalking
    during a solar flare or one of their cousins, a coronal mass
    ejection, can absorb a radiation dose equivalent to 100 chest
    x-rays -- reason enough to dash for shelter.</P>

    <P><B><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Above</FONT></B><FONT SIZE="-1"
     FACE="Arial">: Astronaut Steven Smith floats above Earth during
    shuttle mission STS-103. [<A HREF="http://antwrp.gsfc.nasa.gov/apod/ap010529.html">more</A>]</FONT></P>

    <P>Flares pose little direct danger to Earth dwellers because
    our planet's atmosphere protects us from their deadly radiation.
    But unpredictable solar explosions do affect our lives. They
    can disable satellites, scramble aircraft navigation, and interrupt
    high-frequency radio communications for hours.</P>

    <P><TABLE WIDTH="115" ALIGN="RIGHT" BORDER="1" CELLPADDING="5"
    CELLSPACING="0">
      <TR>
        <TD BGCOLOR="#ffffff">
        <P><CENTER><A HREF="../../news/subscribe.htm"><IMG SRC="../images/brightflare/newshat.gif"
        ALIGN="BOTTOM" BORDER="0" ALT="subscription image" WIDTH="89"
        HEIGHT="86" NATURALSIZEFLAG="3"><BR>
        <FONT SIZE="-2" FACE="Arial,Helvetica,san-serif">Sign up for
        EXPRESS SCIENCE NEWS delivery</FONT></A></CENTER></TD>
      </TR>
    </TABLE>&quot;One of the most amazing things about solar flares,&quot;
    says Brian Dennis of NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, &quot;is
    the efficient way they accelerate subatomic particles to energies
    exceeding 10<sup>9</sup> eV.&quot; As much as 50% of the total
    explosion energy emerges as electrons and atomic nuclei traveling
    at nearly the speed of light. &quot;Flares operate much more
    efficiently than any particle accelerator we've been able to
    build here on Earth.&quot;</P>

    <P>&quot;How do flares do that?&quot; he asks. We don't know,
    but terrestrial particle physicists would love to find out.</P>

    <P>What ignites solar flares? How do they unleash so much energy
    so quickly? And is it possible to predict when they will happen?</P>

    <P>Such questions have vexed astronomers since 1859 when Lord
    Carrington spotted a solar flare for the first time. &quot;I
    was [counting sunspots on a projected image of the Sun],&quot;
    he recalled, when suddenly &quot;two patches of intensely bright
    and white light broke out&quot; near a remarkably large sunspot
    group. &quot;Flurried by the surprise,&quot; Carrington rushed
    from his telescope to call a second witness, but by the time
    he returned minutes later the outburst had vanished.</P>

    <P><A HREF="http://science.msfc.nasa.gov/ssl/pad/solar/images/limb_flare.mpg"><IMG 
    SRC="images/hessi/flare_med.jpg" WIDTH="225" HEIGHT="194" ALIGN="LEFT"
    BORDER="1" NATURALSIZEFLAG="3" ALT="see caption"></A><B><FONT
     SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Left</FONT></B><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">:
    A <A HREF="http://science.msfc.nasa.gov/ssl/pad/solar/images/limb_flare.mpg">4.2
    MB mpeg movie</A> shows a solar flare in action, blasting hot
    gas away from the limb of the Sun. [<A HREF="http://science.msfc.nasa.gov/ssl/pad/solar/flares.htm">more</A>]</FONT></P>

    <P>Carrington knew he had glimpsed something enormously powerful,
    but what he saw was just the tip of the iceberg. Fast-moving
    particles that emerge from flares radiate mostly high-energy
    x-rays and gamma-rays. Lower-energy visible light isn't as important.</P>

    <P>And therein lies the reason that flares have been able to
    guard their secrets for so long. The explosions are brightest
    at wavelengths that Earthbound observers can't see with their
    eyes. Telescopes are hobbled, too, because our atmosphere is
    opaque to x-rays and gamma-rays.</P>

    <P>Now a new NASA satellite aims to change all that. The High
    Energy Solar Spectroscopic Imager (HESSI for short), launched
    on Feb. 5, 2002, is <FONT COLOR="#000000">orbiting Earth nearly
    600 km above our planet's obscuring atmosphere</FONT>, where
    it can record x-ray and gamma-ray emissions from flares. HESSI
    isn't the first spacecraft capable of detecting such radiation.
    But it will be the first to capture crisp hard x-ray and gamma-ray
    images of the violent explosions.</P>

    <P>&quot;The angular resolution of HESSI's hard x-ray images
    will be about 2 arcseconds or about as good as you could get
    from an optical telescope on the ground.&quot; says Lin, the
    mission's principal investigator. The gamma-ray images will be
    a little less detailed, with resolutions between 7 and 36 arcseconds.
    But, Lin notes enthusiastically, &quot;we've never seen <I>any</I>
    gamma-ray image of a solar flare before.&quot; HESSI's will be
    the first.</P>

    <P><B><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Right</FONT></B><FONT SIZE="-1"
     FACE="Arial">: An artist's rendering of HESSI in orbit 600 kilometers
    above Earth. [<A HREF="http://hesperia.gsfc.nasa.gov/hessi/frontgridframe.htm">more
    information</A>]</FONT></P>

    <P><IMG SRC="images/hessi/hessi_earth_med.jpg" WIDTH="250" HEIGHT="195"
    ALIGN="RIGHT" BORDER="1" NATURALSIZEFLAG="3" ALT="see caption">To
    put these numbers into perspective, consider the following: When
    a solar flare erupts, it heats a region of the Sun's atmosphere
    many Earth-diameters across. (What Carrington saw in 1859 was
    the white light &quot;bloom&quot; from such a flare.) HESSI's
    hard x-ray images will reveal details only 1700 km wide -- about
    the distance between Los Angeles and Seattle.</P>

    <P>That's amazing because such high-energy x-rays and gamma-rays
    can't be focused; they fly right through conventional lenses.
    Instead, HESSI forms images by looking at the Sun through finely-spaced
    parallel slats --like microscopic Venetian blinds-- that cast
    shadows across onboard radiation detectors. &quot;We'll rotate
    the spacecraft every 4 seconds to create a modulation pattern
    from the shadows that we can analyze to form an image of the
    Sun,&quot; explains Lin. The <A HREF="http://hesperia.gsfc.nasa.gov/hessi/hessi_show_image.htm">process</A>
    is similar to a medical x-ray, except scientists are interested
    in the source of the rays (the Sun), not the material that blocks
    them (the slats).</P>

    <P>HESSI's cameras can make pictures of the entire Sun, but researchers
    will be especially interested in sunspots. &quot;That's where
    flares erupt -- in the vicinity of sunspots with intense, twisted
    magnetic fields,&quot; says George Fisher, a colleague of Lin's
    at Berkeley. &quot;Twisted magnetic fields are like rubber bands
    stretched taut,&quot; he explained. &quot;They want to snap back
    -- violently. Reconnecting fields are probably the power source
    for flares.&quot;</P>

    <P>At least that's what most solar physicists believe. The problem
    is, no one has ever seen it happen. &quot;Before HESSI we couldn't
    locate the onset of an eruption with sufficient precision to
    make the connection between flares and kinks in the magnetic
    field,&quot; says Fisher. &quot;I'm dying to know where flare
    particles are accelerated, and I think HESSI is finally going
    to show us.&quot;</P>

    <P><IMG SRC="images/hessi/0106009_box.jpg" WIDTH="250" HEIGHT="227"
    ALIGN="LEFT" BORDER="1" NATURALSIZEFLAG="3">HESSI's job is important
    fundamental physics, adds NASA's mission scientist Brian Dennis.
    Understanding how flares work could teach us how to build better
    particle accelerators on Earth and maybe even advance the cause
    of fusion power, which also involves superheated gases threaded
    by magnetic fields.</P>

    <P><B><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Left</FONT></B><FONT SIZE="-1"
     FACE="Arial">: Inside the CERN high-energy particle accelerator
    in Geneva. Basic research on solar flares might one day improve
    such devices on Earth. &COPY; CERN Geneva</FONT></P>

    <P>HESSI's findings will also shed light on mysterious happenings
    far outside our solar system. &quot;Whatever triggers solar flares
    could be the same mechanism that blasts jets of particles from
    the magnetized accretion disks of black holes and neutron stars,&quot;
    says Dennis. &quot;The Sun is comparatively nearby, so it's a
    natural laboratory for studying such exotic processes.&quot;</P>

    <P>Meanwhile most astronauts would be satisfied with simple timely
    predictions of garden-variety solar flares, a potential spinoff
    of the HESSI mission. If the spacecraft can accomplish that one
    thing, space will become a safer place ... for everyone.</P>

    <P><U><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Editor's note</FONT></U><FONT
     SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">: Solar flares are closely related to
    <A HREF="http://hesperia.gsfc.nasa.gov/sftheory/cme.htm">coronal
    mass ejections</A> (CMEs) -- billion-ton clouds of gas that billow
    away from the Sun and trigger geomagnetic storms when they strike
    Earth's magnetosphere. Scientists once thought flares propelled
    CMEs into space, but we've since learned that flares and CMEs
    can happen together or separately. Perhaps the two are different
    aspects of the same kind of explosion triggered by changing magnetic
    fields on the Sun. No one is sure. &quot;One of HESSI's goals
    is to understand the relationship between solar flares and CMEs,&quot;
    says Dennis. [<B><A HREF="http://www.exploratorium.edu/solarmax/ram_files/bl-cmes-final2.ram">Listen</A></B>
    to Bob Lin discuss CMEs].</FONT></TD>
