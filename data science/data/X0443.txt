				<font class="news">It was yet another home series win but there wasn&#8217;t anything for the Indian team to crow about. Certainly it wasn&#8217;t a victory that will be remembered for long.<br><br>A team that was dismissed as the weakest from England to tour this India, and one that seemed doomed to a 3-0 rout in the Test series, ultimately lost it only by a 1-0 margin, besides having the better share of the exchanges in the two remaining matches.<br><br>It was a hollow victory at best and one that raised more questions regarding many aspects concerning the Indian team. <br><br>Only one visiting side in the last 14 years has won a Test series in India, giving rise to the phrase `tigers at home and lambs abroad&#8217;. But if the just concluded contest proved anything, it is by no means certain that the Indians are tigers at home.<br><br>An impoverished England side, bereft of some of their leading players when they arrived and without their most accomplished batsman Graham Thorpe after the First Test, exposed several chinks in the Indian armor. Indeed, the home team finished second best in every department of the game &#8211; batting, bowling, fielding, wicketkeeping, captaincy and team selection. <br><br>A line-up of seven batsmen and four bowlers may be the best bet when India are playing Australia or South Africa or away from home when playing to avoid defeat is the first priority, given the country&#8217;s abysmal record abroad.<br><br>But at home, where the Indians do have a very impressive record, and against this England side, the selectors should have played five bowlers. If six batsmen, on paper arguably the strongest batting line up in the world and headed by the leading player in the game today, are not able to get the required runs to win a match, the team deserves to lose. Given the state of the bowling &#8211; which is easily the weaker of the two main departments &#8211; five bowlers are a must if India are to win Test matches. <br><br>And yet in all the three Tests, India played only four bowlers. The non-inclusion of Sarandeep Singh at Ahmedabad and the omission of Tinu Yohannan at Bangalore were selection blunders which led to the team struggling in both matches.<br><br>The Indians won the First Test because the Englishmen could not tackle Harbhajan Singh and Anil Kumble. But by the Second Test, they had gained in confidence and avoided the mistakes they committed at Mohali.<br><br>If any proof is needed, one has only to look at the figures of the two bowlers in the First Test and compare them to their figures in the other two Tests. The sub-standard fielding and shoddy work by Deep Dasgupta behind the stumps added to the woes. <br><br>The batting too, apart from Sachin Tendulkar, failed to perform up to potential. Rahul Dravid batting 74 minutes and 61 balls for three runs at Bangalore perhaps best symbolized the over cautious approach.<br><br>The Indian middle order batting has a formidable look but rarely have their lived up to their reputation. Consistency was again lacking and Sourav Ganguly&#8217;s poor run continued, putting more pressure on the other batsmen, who more often than not, cracked under the mental onslaught.<br><br>Even Tendulkar found the pressure too much, as proved by his dismissal at Bangalore. <br><br>The inability of Shiv Sundar Das and Dasgupta to give a really good start &#8211; except in the second innings at Ahmedabad &#8211; added to the batting problems. And the lackadaisical approach of the Indians was perhaps symbolized by events in this game, when they did not go for the runs on the final day.<br><br>Granted, it was a challenging target but at least some sort of attempt should have been made, more so after the openers had given a sound start. It was obvious that the Englishmen just did not have the bowling to bowl the Indians twice in a Test and yet the Indians adopted safety-first tactics quite needlessly.<br><br>In matters of captaincy, Nasser Hussain easily scored over Ganguly. There is added pressure leading a side at home, whoever the opponents, because of the high expectations. Ganguly, already under a cloud because of his batting failures, allowed things to drift quite often.<br><br>This was evident in the unimaginative handling of his bowlers and by the fact that the England lower order piled up the runs at both Ahmedabad and Bangalore. <br><br>Many cricketers have made a name for themselves in India after arriving here unheralded, thanks chiefly to the over confidence of the home team. This time, players like Hoggard, Flintoff, Vaughn, Giles, White, Foster and Dawson came to India either as rookies or with a rather dubious international record.<br><br>By the end of the tour, they had undoubtedly gained in confidence and some of them could well earn permanent slots in the England team. As the England team left home for a well-deserved Christmas break, the future looked brighter for them than the home side and when they come back here for the series of one-day internationals next month, they could well be taking on India on level terms.<br><br>- <i>Partab Ramchand</i><br><br><b>COMMENT</b><br>Do you agree with Partab's comments?
