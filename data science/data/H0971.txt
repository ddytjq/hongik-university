Molecular cloning for <a href="MG43.html">modification of cells</a> and organisms is covered elsewhere. This section concentrates on techniques for production of molecules (DNA, protein) for further molecular study.			<li>Efficient <a href="http://www.blc.arizona.edu/INTERACTIVE/recombinant3.dna/clones.html">molecular cloning</a> has five requirements.			<ul>				<li>A host organism is needed.				<ul>					<li><a href="http://research.nwfsc.noaa.gov/protocols/inoue.html"><i>E. coli</i></a> is a frequently used bacterial host because decades of research have made it a well understood and conveniently manipulable organism.					<li><i>S. cerevisiae</i> is a frequently used eucaryotic host that retains many of the convenient features of <i>E. coli</i>.					<li>Other hosts include <a href="http://www.invitrogen.com/content.cfm?pageid=3439&cfid=10389577&cftoken=61708128#oclsPichiaPastorisExpressionServices"><i>Picchia pastoris</i></a><i><a href="http://www.invitrogen.com/cat_pichia.html"> </a></i>(another yeast) and cultured cells of <i>Spodoptera frugiperda</i> (an insect; for use with <a href="MG4374.html">baculoviruses</a>).				</ul>				<li>A <a href="MG4281.html">vector</a> DNA carries the payload DNA into the host cell and facilitates its replication in that cell.				<li>A means of placing DNA within the vector links the payload DNA and vector DNA covalently. The method used depends on the application:				<ul>					<li>ligation of DNA fragments into <a href="MG421.html">restricted</a> vector DNA;					<li>exchange of engineered DNA into vector DNA by homologous <a href="MG4374.html">recombination</a>.				</ul>				<li>A method of placing the in vitro modified (engineered) DNA into the host cell is needed. Most commonly used are:				<ul>					<li><a href="../MGW1/MG11211.html">transformation</a>;					<li><a href="../MGW1/MG11213.html">transfection</a>, the in vitro packaging of the DNA into phage particles then used to infect the cells;					<li><a href="MG431.html">electroporation</a>.				</ul>				<li>Methods for selection and/or screening allow recovery of cells with the desired constructs.				<ul>					<li><strong>Selection</strong> refers to applying conditions that allow the desired cells or phages (containing vector or vector and insert) to replicate while preventing others from replicating. Typical selections include:					<ul>						<li>antibiotic sensitivity and resistance;						<li>nutrient requirements;						<li>plaque formation.					</ul>					<li><strong>Screening</strong> allows all cells to grow, but tests the resulting clones for particular properties. Often these are indicative of the presence of an insert in the vector. Screening properties used include:					<ul>						<li>antibiotic sensitivity and resistance;						<li>nutrient requirements ;						<li>plaque type;						<li>blue-white selection (<font face="Symbol">b</font>-galactosidase activity);						<li>specific (by nucleic acid <a href="MG411.html">hybridization</a> or <a href="MG415.html">antibodies</a>).					</ul>				</ul>			</ul>			<li>Purposes of molecular cloning for molecule production include:			<ul>				<li>isolation of specific DNA for use as <a href="MG425.html">probes</a>, for <a href="../MGW1/MG11231.html">restriction mapping</a>, for nucleotide <a href="MG427.html">sequencing</a> and for <a href="MG43.html">reintroduction</a> into organisms.				<li>establishment of collections of cells or viruses each containing a different segment of a large DNA population and collectively containing a representative of almost every sequence in the population (<a href="MG416.html">libraries</a>). <img src="nav4.gif" width="69" height="71" align="right" usemap="#nav4B1F889C9"><map name=""></map> 				<li>production of quantities of specific proteins for further study (expression of proteins).			</ul>		</ul>		<h4><a name="Info"></a>Further information<br>		</h4>		<ul>			<li>Transformation and transfection have different <a href="MG431.html">meanings</a> when applied to mammalian cells in culture.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a name="bottom"></a><a href="MG427.html">Last</a> | <a href="MG42.html">Overview</a> | <a href="#Facts">Top</a> | <a href="MG4281.html">Next</a></p>		</center>		<p><br>		<hr>		This is page 428 of <a href="../MG01.html">Molecular Genetics</a> by <a href="http://opbs.okstate.edu/~melcher/UM.html">Ulrich Melcher</a>, &copy;1998<br>		<br>		<hr>		</p>		<address>E-mail inquiries to <a href="mailto:umelcher@biochem.okstate.edu">U.
