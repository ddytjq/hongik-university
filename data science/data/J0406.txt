<h1>Cola 600 win puts Jarrett in $1 million hunt</h1>

CONCORD, N.C. -- Dale Jarrett dominated the second half of the Coca-Cola 600 Sunday and set himself up for a shot at a $1 million bonus in the process.
<p> 
           Jarrett finished 11.98 seconds ahead of Dale Earnhardt, giving him a cushion of more
than one-third of a lap on the 1.5-mile Charlotte Motor Speedway trioval in NASCAR's longest race.<p>

           The victory put Jarrett in position to join Bill Elliott as the
only drivers to win the Winston Select Million. The bonus goes to
any driver who wins three of the sport's four jewels in a year.<p>

           Jarrett, who won the season-opening Daytona 500 and finished
second in the Winston Select 500, can claim the bonus with a
victory in the Southern 500 on Sept. 1 in Darlington, S.C.<p>

           Since Elliott collected the $1 million in 1985, only two other
drivers have had a shot at the bonus -- Darrell Waltrip in 1989 and
the late Davey Allison in 1992. Both failed to get needed victories
at Darlington.<p>

           
&quot;We're going to see if we can win us a million bucks come Labor
Day,&quot; Jarrett said. &quot;If you're going to win just a few, it's good
to win the big ones.&quot;<p>

           Jarrett's sixth Winston Cup career victory came in a race that
was delayed 90 minutes at the start by rain and was then delayed by
six crashes.<p>

           The last 184 laps of the 400-lap, 600-mile race were
caution-free, allowing Jarrett to take control. Except for
green-flag pit stops, he kept his Ford Thunderbird up front almost
every lap over the last half of the event.<p>

           Before coming in for his final service stop on lap 336, Jarrett
had built his advantage over Earnhardt to 16 seconds -- more than
one-half lap.<p>

           Some quick work by Earnhardt's pit crew allowed him to reduce Jarrett's
lead to 9.5 seconds, but once both drivers were back on the track
again, it didn't matter.<p>

           Jarrett gradually built his lead again to the 16-second mark,
then cruised through traffic over the last 10 laps.<p>

           Terry Labonte and Jeff Gordon were the only other drivers on the
lead lap at the finish.<p>

           Four of the top five finishers were in Chevrolets, but five of
the top 10 were in Fords. It was the first race in which Fords and
Pontiacs were using body changes approved by NASCAR to help the
Thunderbirds and Grand Prixs compete with the redesigned Monte
Carlo, which has won 28 of 42 races since it was introduced last
year.<p>
