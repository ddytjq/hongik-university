<td class="ClubCustom3Body"><DIV class=Section1><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=red>THE HISTORY OF THE FOOTBALL LEAGUE<?xml:namespace prefix = o ns = "urn:schemas-microsoft-com:office:office" /><o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><o:p><FONT color=red></FONT></o:p></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>Few would have imagined that the actions of a Perthshire-born draper would lead to the creation of the greatest football competition in the world and set the standards for everyone else to follow.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><FONT color=navy><B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal"><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'">The Football League</SPAN></B><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"> was the brainchild of <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">William McGregor</B>, a portly and genial Scotsman who became a director at Aston Villa after being attracted to the club by the fact they had three of his countrymen in their side.<o:p></o:p></SPAN></FONT></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>McGregor had moved to Birmingham to seek his fortune and opened a linen draper’s shop near Villa Park. His love of football then took over and allowed him to change the course of the national game.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><FONT color=navy><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'">With the onset of the age of professionalism in 1885, football had become a disorganised shambles with games constantly being called off because clubs had arranged more lucrative matches elsewhere.<BR><BR></SPAN><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'">McGregor decided that football needed order and he wrote to the leading clubs of the time putting forward a proposal that they should combine to arrange home and away fixtures each season – and thus the seeds of <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">The Football League</B> were sown.<o:p></o:p></SPAN></FONT></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>The plans were announced at a meeting at Anderton’s Hotel in Fleet Street on 22 March 1888, and the first league kicked off on 8 September 1888 with12 member clubs: <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Preston North End, Bolton Wanderers, Everton, Burnley, Accrington, Blackburn Rovers, Aston Villa, West Bromwich Albion, Wolverhampton Wanderers, Notts County, Derby County and Stoke</B>.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><FONT color=navy><B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal"><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'">Jack Gordon</SPAN></B><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"> of <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Preston</B> is widely regarded as scoring the first goal of the new competition - although this is disputed in some quarters due to claims that North End's game kicked off 45 minutes later than others that day. But what is not disputed is the fact that North End went on to claim the first <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Football League Championship</B> without losing a game, and win the FA Cup without conceding a goal.<o:p></o:p></SPAN></FONT></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>After Preston had repeated their success the following season the remainder of the century was dominated by McGregor’s beloved <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Aston Villa</B> and <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Sunderland</B> (a replacement for Stoke in The League), who won eight titles between them before the end of the century.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>Minor changes to the rules drawn up at the first meeting of the clubs were made along the way, including the introduction of goal nets and penalty kicks in 1891, and promotion and relegation introduced after the league expanded to two divisions of 18 teams in 1898.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>The powerbase shifted to <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Liverpool</B>, <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Newcastle</B> and <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Manchester United</B> at the start of the new century, but southern sides were gradually finding a foothold in the previously northern dominated league. <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Woolwich Arsenal</B>, <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Tottenham</B>, <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Clapton Orient</B> and <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Chelsea, </B>with the legendary 6ft 2in, 22 stone Bill ‘Fatty’ Foulke in goal, were all elected to the league during the first decade of the 20<SUP>th</SUP> century.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>The Great War interrupted the progress of the League for four years, but not before the north had continued its dominance with title wins for <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Blackburn</B> (twice), <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Sunderland</B> and <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Everton</B> in the four years preceding the onset of hostilities.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>The post war period saw major changes in the rules and composition of the league. The first and second divisions were expanded to 22 teams immediately after the war and within two years the <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Third Divisions North and South</B> were introduced.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>Perhaps more notable was the change to the offside rule which came into effect in 1925 with the number of opponents needed to keep a player onside reduced from three to two. This resulted in a huge increase in the number of goals scored, from 4,700 in 1924 to 6,373 in the first season under the new law.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>One of the main beneficiaries of the new law was the incomparable <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Everton</B> forward <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Dixie Dean</B> who set a league record of 60 goals in the 1927-28 season.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>The 1920’s also saw the emergence of one of the greatest managers in the history of the game – <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Herbert Chapman</B>.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>The Yorkshireman led <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Huddersfield</B> to two consecutive titles before he moved south to take charge of <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Arsenal</B>, who had just avoided relegation the previous season, and led his new side to second place – five points behind his old club who claimed an unprecedented third title in a row.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>But Chapman’s ability to create a winning team was to reinforced in the 1930’s as, despite his death from pneumonia in 1934, the Gunners dominated the League claiming five titles during the decade, including the matching of Huddersfield’s record of a hat-trick of titles.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>The Highbury giants’ success ended the north’s dominance after it had claimed the first 38 titles on offer.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>Chapman’s success allowed him to exert a huge influence on the game and he was the first to put forward the idea of floodlit games, numbered shirts (which were officially introduced in 1939), white balls and all-weather pitches.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>The League was suspended at the end of the 1939 season at the onset of the Second World War and returned to huge crowds on Saturday 31 August 1946.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>Aggregate crowds in that first season were 35,604,606 and they were to keep on rising.<o:p></o:p></FONT></SPAN></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY: 'Arial Narrow'"><FONT color=navy>The first two seasons saw normal order resume with Championship successes for <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Liverpool </B>and <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Arsenal</B> before a new face emerged in the unlikely guise of <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Portsmouth</B>. Cup</B> with a 4-1 win against <B style="mso-bidi-font-weight: normal">Benfica</B> on an emotional night at Wembley.<o:p></o:p></SPAN></FONT></P><P class=MsoNormal><SPAN lang=EN-GB style="FONT-FAMILY:
