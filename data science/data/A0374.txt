<p><font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2"><b>We've made it easy to 
understand the terms used in this site. Look below for simple explanations.</p>
<p><font size="3">Introduction</font><br>
Savings</b><br>
These accounts are a safe and secure place to keep your cash. The Royal Bank 
pays you interest at various rates depending on the type of account and the 
amount of money you keep in it. So by keeping your money in a savings account, 
you can watch it grow. Some savings accounts allow you to take your money out 
immediately at any branch. Other types of account mean you have to let us know 
in advance if you want to withdraw money. The accounts that ask you for notice 
usually pay a higher rate of interest. You can open a savings account at any 
branch. We ask for some identification and there&#146;s often a minimum amount you 
are allowed to deposit.<br>
<br>
<b>Investments</b><br>
These can be made in many ways, for example:<br>
<a href="#ISAs">ISAs</a><br>
<a href="#Bonds">Bonds</a><br>
<a href="#Unit Trusts">Unit Trusts</a><br>
<a href="#Endowment">Endowments</a><br>
<br>
<font size="3"><b>Interest</b><br>
</font>This is money that we pay into your account as a reward for keeping your 
savings with us. Simple Interest is the percentage applied each day on the 
amount of money in your account. Because interest is added to your account 
(either monthly, quarterly or annually) your total savings are always 
increasing, so you're earning interest on the original amount invested plus 
interest on the interest. This feature is known as Compound Interest.<br>
<br>
<b>Tiered Interest</b><br>
This means each time your total savings reach the next band or tier, you get a 
higher interest rate. An example is listed in the table below:<br>

<p><br>
<b>Gross and Net Interest</b><br>
Gross interest means interest paid without tax being taken off. The bank can pay 
interest gross if you are a non tax payer - all you need to do is fill out a 
simple R85 form (these forms are available from any branch). Net interest is 
interest paid after tax has been taken off.<br>
<br>
<font size="3"><b>Types of accounts</b><br>
</font>There are two main types of savings account. Instant access and Notice 
accounts:<br>
<br>
<b>Instant Access</b><br>
These accounts allow you to take money out at any time. And because these 
accounts usually come with a Cashline card, you can withdraw your money from 
cash machines across the country. An example of this type of account is the 
Royal Bank's <a href="accounts/instant_access.htm">Instant Access Savings Account</a>.<br>
<br>
<b>Notice accounts</b><br>
These accounts ask you to give us warning in writing if you&#146;re going to take any 
money from the account. Different notice accounts ask for different periods of 
notice and it&#146;s easy to select the period that suits you best. Generally 
speaking, the longer the period of notice, the higher the rate of interest we 
pay. It is still possible to take money out of notice accounts without giving 
notice, but there is a penalty to pay depending on which type of account. 
Examples of this type of account include the Royal Bank's
<a href="accounts/30day.htm">30 Day Savings Account</a> and
<a href="accounts/60day.htm">60 Day Savings Account</a>.<br>

<font size="3"><b>Glossary</b></font><br>
The following is a glossary of the Savings and Investment Terms:<br>
&nbsp;</p>
<table border="0" cellPadding="2" cellSpacing="1" width="100%">
  <tr>
    <td bgColor="#66ccff" colSpan="2">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1"><b>Glossary</b></font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="33%"><a name="Bonds">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1"><b>Bonds</b></font></a></td>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="67%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1">Life assurance 
    lump sum savings plans. </font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="33%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1"><b>Bonus 
    payment</b></font></td>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="67%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1">Some savings 
    accounts reward you with an extra payment if you meet certain conditions. 
    For example an annual bonus may be paid if no withdrawals are made in the 
    year and the balance doesn&#146;t fall below an agreed amount.</font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="33%"><a name="Cirrus">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1"><b>Cirrus</b></font></a></td>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="67%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1">Cirrus is an 
    international network of cash machines which allow you to withdraw money in 
    local currency in a number of different countries.</font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="33%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1"><b>Annual 
    Equivalent Rate</b></font></td>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="67%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1">AER stands for 
    Annual Equivalent Rate and illustrates what the gross interest rate would be 
    if interest was paid and compounded each year.&nbsp; As every advert for a 
    savings product which quotes a rate of interest will contain an AER, you 
    will be able to compare more easily what return you can expect from your 
    savings over time.</font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="33%"><a name="Endowment">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1"><b>Endowment</b></font></a></td>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="67%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1">A savings plan 
    that involves making monthly payments for a set period. At the end of the 
    period the policy &quot;matures&quot; and pays out a tax-free lump sum. The savings 
    plan comes in the form of a life assurance policy and includes cover in the 
    event of death.</font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="33%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1"><b>Interest</b></font></td>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="67%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1">This is the 
    bonus that banks promise to pay if you keep your money with them. Rates are 
    usually a percentage of the amount in the account and are calculated on a 
    daily basis.</font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="33%"><a name="Maestro">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1"><b>Maestro</b></font></a></td>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="67%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1">Maestro is an 
    international debit card system which allow you to pay for goods and 
    services overseas at retailers who display the sign. It works in a similar 
    way to Switch in the UK.</font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="33%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1"><b>Penalty</b></font></td>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="67%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1">Penalties are 
    occasionally incurred if you break the conditions of the account. For 
    example if the account required 60 days notice and you make an instant 
    withdrawal, you would be penalised the equivalent of 60 days' interest.</font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="33%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1"><b>
    <a name="ISAs" class="bookmark">ISAs</a></b></font></td>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="67%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1">ISAs are the 
    new tax-efficient way to save and can be made up of three components: cash, 
    stocks and shares and life insurance. There are two types of ISA &#150; mini and 
    maxi. With a mini ISA, you can opt for just one of these components, though 
    you can take out up to three mini ISAs in any tax year &#150; one for each 
    component. All of these could be held with different ISA providers. A maxi 
    ISA lets you combine the stocks and shares component with cash and/or 
    insurance. You can only take out one maxi ISA in any tax year and this must 
    be with a single provider. The Royal Bank of Scotland offer two
    <a class="table" href="accounts/isa/mini.htm">mini cash ISAs</a>.</font></td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="33%"><a name="Unit Trusts">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1"><b>Unit Trusts</b></font></a></td>
    <td bgColor="#ccffff" vAlign="top" width="67%">
    <font class="solchart" color="#000000" face="Arial" size="1">When you invest 
    in a Unit Trusts, your money is grouped together with that of many other 
    investors so that costs are kept to a minimum. Your investment is spread 
    over many different types of stocks and shares by an investment management 
    team and so the risks are also lower. **</font></td>
  </tr>
