<HR WIDTH="100%" SIZE=7 ALIGN=left><B>Biotechnology and Genetics </B><BR>
<BR>
The great discoveries of molecular biology are having profound effects on our society in areas from human health to the environment.  They have led to creation of a new industry, biotechnology, and are affecting the nature of the life sciences themselves.  These changes have created major social issues that must be addressed.  For this reason, the Board on Life Sciences has made molecular biology and biotechnology one of its primary focuses and has established a standing <A HREF="http://www4.nationalacademies.org/cls/agbiotec.nsf">Committee on Agricultural Biotechnology, Health, and the Environment</A>. <BR>
<BR>
<A HREF="http://www.nap.edu/catalog/10195.html"><B><U>Stem Cells and the Future of Regenerative Medicine</U></B></A> (2001) examines the state of stem cell science and its potential biological and biomedical applications.  Based on information gathered at a workshop from scientific and public-policy experts, this report puts forth reccomendations to maximize the potential of research in this field.<BR>
<BR>
<A HREF="http://www.nap.edu/catalog/10068.html"><B><U>Ecological Monitoring of Genetically Modified Crops</U></B></A> (2001) is the summary of a workshop that focused on monitoring for effects that genetically modified crops might have on the surroundingecosystems, including plants, animals, and microorganisms.  The purpose was to lay out the issues surrounding such monitoring, to describe what was known, and to identify what needed further attention.<BR>
<BR>
<A HREF="http://books.nap.edu/catalog/9988.html"><U>Opportunities for  Environmental Applications of Marine Biotechnology</U></A> (2000)  is the proceedings of a workshop that was held to review research funding priorities for the environmental aspects of marine biotechnology.  The workshop addressed such topics as bioremediation, restoration, phytoengineering and biocontrol, biomaterials, and socioeconomics aspects.<BR>
<BR>
<A HREF="http://books.nap.edu/catalog/9795.html"><U>Genetically Modified Pest-Protected Plants:  Science and Regulation</U></A> (2000)  explores the risks and benefits of crops that are genetically modified for pest resistance, the urgency of establishing an appropriate regulatory framework for these products, and the importance of public understanding of the issues.  The committee critically reviews federal policies toward transgenic products, the 1986 coordinated framework among the key federal agencies in the field, and rules proposed by the Environmental Protection Agency for regulation of plant pesticides.<BR>
<BR>
<A HREF="http://books.nap.edu/catalog/5295.html"><U>Biobased Industrial Products: Priorities for  Research and Commercialization</U></A> (1999) states that biological sciences are likely to make the same impact on the formation of new industries in the next century as the physical and chemical sciences have had on industrial development throughout the century now coming to a close. The biological sciences, when combined with recent and future advances in process engineering, can become the foundation for producing a wide variety of industrial products from renewable plant resources.<BR>
<BR>
<A HREF="http://books.nap.edu/catalog/5758.html"><U>Intellectual Property Rights and the Dissemination of Research Tools in Molecular Biology</U></A> (1997) examines how intellectual property protection has affected the development, dissemination, and utilization of research tools in molecular biology.<BR>
<BR>
<A HREF="http://books.nap.edu/catalog/5955.html"><U>Evaluating Human Genetic Diversity</U></A>  (1997) evaluates the proposed Human Genome Diversity project, which would perform a worldwide survey of human DNA.<BR>
<BR>
<A HREF="http://books.nap.edu/catalog/5141.html"><U>The Evaluation of Forensic DNA Evidence</U></A> (1996) updates the 1992 report.  The study emphasizes statistical and population genetics issues in the use of DNA evidence.<BR>
<BR>
<A HREF="http://books.nap.edu/catalog/9156.html"><U>Sharing Laboratory Resources:  Genetically Altered Mice</U></A> (1994) explores the factors that influence the sharing of valuable and unique laboratory resources in biological research, with a focus on the distribution of genetically altered mice as a case study.<BR>
<BR>
<A HREF="http://books.nap.edu/catalog/2052.html"><U>Putting Biotechnology to Work:  Bioprocess Engineering </U></A>(1992) examines the status, technologies, products, and opportunities in bioprocess engineering; how to address global challenges of competition in industrialization of biotechnology; and how to address national needs.<BR>
<BR>
<A HREF="http://books.nap.edu/catalog/1866.html"><U>DNA Technology in Forensic Science</U></A> (1992) assesses applicability and appropriateness of forensic uses of DNA technology; standards for data collection and analysis; management of DNA typing data; and ethical, legal, and social issues related to DNA typing. (see updated 1996 report above)<BR>
<BR>
<A HREF="http://books.nap.edu/catalog/1431.html"><U>Field Testing Genetically Modified Organisms:  Framework for Decisions</U></A> (1989) recommends guidelines on how to determine environmental safety and risks for tests of microorganisms and plants.<BR>
<BR>
<A HREF="http://books.nap.edu/catalog/1097.html"><U>Mapping and Sequencing the Human Genome</U></A> (1988) provides recommendations on goals, technology development, programs and research for mapping and sequencing human and other genomes.<br>

<BR>
The Board also has established an ongoing <B>Forum on Biotechnology</B> to foster open communication among scientists, administrators, policymakers, and executives engaged in biotechnology research, development, and commercialization.  <BR>

February 16, 2000<BR>

<BR>

<A HREF="http://books.nap.edu/catalog/6326.html"><U>Privacy Issues in Biomedical and Clinical Research </U></A> (1998) is a summary of a forum which looked at pending legislation in Congress (Fall 1997) and considered, if enacted, how this would affect genetic research.<BR>
<BR>
April 26, 1997<BR>
<A HREF="http://books.nap.edu/catalog/6081.html"><U>Designing an Agricultural Genome Program</U></A>  (1998)   is a summary of a symposium that brough together researchers from industry, academia, and federal agencies to share their experiences in mapping genes from humans, mice, livestock, and plants in the context of the federal governments consideration of starting an agriculture genome research program to map and sequence genes in plants and animals.  <BR>
