      <td width="620" height="14">
        <p align="center"><font size="5">Carbamylation of Proteins and Peptides</font>
        <p align="center"><font size="2">(the dangers of using urea in protein
        characterization)</font></td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td width="620" height="149">&nbsp;<p>Urea is a common chaotrope used in the solublization and denaturation
        of proteins.&nbsp;Urea is sometimes used as a
        protein denaturant in <a href="../cmc/cmc.htm" target="_blank">S-carboxymethylation reactions</a> 
        .&nbsp; In this reaction a reducing agent is used to break disulfide
        bonds as a prelude to cysteine modification.&nbsp; Urea helps to make
        the buried disulfides accessible to reduction and modification.&nbsp;
        This process goes by the common abbreviation
        &quot;RCM.&quot;&nbsp;&nbsp; After the reduction and
        S-carboxymethylation (RCM) process the next step is often enzymatic digestion of
        the modified protein in the course of protein characterization.</p>
        <p>Urea in solution is in equilibrium with ammonium cyanate.&nbsp; The form
        that reacts with protein amino groups is isocyanic acid.&nbsp;
        Carbamylatoin by isocyanic acid is detrimental
        to further protein characterization because isocyanic acid notably reacts with the amino terminus
        of proteins blocking the peptide or protein to N-terminal sequencing.&nbsp;
        Isocyanic acid also attacks the side chains of lysine and arginine residues rendering a
        protein unsuitable for many enzymatic digests.&nbsp; In addition even if
        the carbamylation does not prevent the enzymatic digest it will often confound
        the results with peptides that have unexpected retention times and
        masses.&nbsp; It does not seem to make a difference what grade of
        urea is used because, urea + heat + protein = carbamylation.&nbsp; The
        bottom line is that a urea solution will always degrade to isocyanic
        acid no matter what
        the grade.&nbsp; Urea
        solutions should always be used fresh and should be treated to remove
        isocyanic acid.&nbsp; Urea solutions can be acidified (100mM HCl) to
        drive the equilibrium to favor urea over the isocyanic acid, or a urea solution
        can be bulk deionized by using an ion exchange resin such as AG 501-X8
        Resin<font size="1"><sup> TM</sup></font>.&nbsp;
        It is not always necessary to use urea in an RCM procedure and the use of
        urea should be investigated on a case by case basis.&nbsp; If a chaotrope is required one should investigate whether
        guanidine hydrochloride will work just as well.&nbsp;</p>
        <p><b><font size="2">Remove Urea Before a Proteolytic Digest</font></b></p>
        <p>Even though some enzymes will tolerate small amounts of urea it is
        important to remove urea from the digest mixture because normally enzyme
        digests are preformed at elevated temperature over a relatively long period of
        time.&nbsp; Remember, urea + heat + protein = carbamylation.&nbsp; Urea can be removed after the reduction and
        S-carboxymethylation reaction by fast reversed phase chromatography or
        by using spin columns (small SEC columns), or dialysis prior to the enzyme digest.</p>
        <p>Interestingly, there is ample evidence of the carbamylation of proteins in
        vivo which have been observed in
        several disease states, references are provided below.&nbsp; In
        addition researchers have found evidence for cysteine carbamylation. As
        always it is important to remember that careful sample preparation is needed to insure that the
        modification observed in an analysis is natural and not an artifact of
        the sample work-up.</p>
