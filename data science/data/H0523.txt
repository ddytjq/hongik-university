<td valign="top"><p class="articleheadline">Plant Biotechnology: Creating Solutions for the Developing World</p></td>
<td rowspan="2"><img src="html/images/clip0001ll.gif" width=190 height=152 align=right></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td valign="bottom">
<p class="articlesubheadline">A new biotech papaya helped save the Hawaiian 
papaya industry and could also aid subsistence farmers in Asia.</p>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<br>

<p>By 1997, a deadly papaya ringspot
virus had decimated Hawaii’s fifth largest crop. Production had fallen by
nearly 40 percent, farmers were going out of business and Hawaii’s $17 million
papaya industry was in danger of collapsing.<sup>1</sup>
</p>

<p>But in that same year, the U.S.
government gave its approval for about 200<sup>2</sup>
small papaya farmers to begin commercially producing genetically improved
varieties that culminated more than 20 years of work led by researchers from
Cornell and the University of Hawaii.<sup>3</sup>  </p>

<p>The researchers inserted a gene that
made the plants resistant to the ringspot virus &mdash; similar to the way a vaccine
makes people immune to disease. The improvement produced immediate results.<sup>4</sup>
</p>

<p>Within four years, the genetic
improvement had not only stopped the rapid decline of the Hawaiian papaya
industry, but production had actually <i>rebounded
</i>to levels near where they were before the ringspot virus invasion.<sup>5</sup>
</p>

<p>“These seeds give the papaya
industry in Hawaii a second chance,” said Emerson Llantero, manager of the
state’s Papaya Administrative Committee, whose grower members financed much of
the research.<sup>6</sup>   </p>

<p>This turnaround in the fortunes of
Hawaii’s small papaya farmers is one example of how plant biotechnology is
making a difference today. In fact, Hawaii Gov. Ben Cayetano credits biotechnology
with rescuing Hawaii’s entire papaya industry<sup>7</sup>
and restoring the state’s position as the No. 1 provider of papaya to the U.S.
mainland.<sup>8</sup>  </p>

<p>But plant biotechnology in general
&mdash; and improved papayas in particular &mdash; will do a lot more in the future to
provide more and better food and to protect the environment.</p>

<p>A project is already underway in
Southeast Asia to get the ringspot-resistant papaya technology from Hawaii into
the hands of subsistence farmers.<sup>9</sup>
</p>

<p>“It is a science that has more to
offer the developing world than the developed world,” says C.S. Prakash, a  native of India who directs the Center for
Plant Biotechnology Research at Tuskegee University. </p>

<p>The reason is very simple: The
developing world has the most to lose and potentially the most to gain.</p>

<p class="articlepullquote">More food for more people</p>

<p>With the world population projected
to top 8 billion by 2030, there will be another 2 million mouths to feed<sup>10</sup>  &mdash; about 73 million more people per year,
most of them in developing regions. With income growth also fueling demand for
better diets, farmers will need to more than double their production over the
next 25 years to feed these additional mouths.<sup>11</sup></p>

<p>But increases in agricultural
yields in recent years are holding at just 1.3 percent a year<sup>12</sup>
&mdash; less than half of what they were 30 years ago. </p>

<p>“Far from being a luxury add-on to
the conventional research scene, biotechnology provides us with a vital new set
of tools that offers us real hope for a better future,” says Florence Wambugu,
founder of A Harvest Biotech Foundation International and author of the recent
book, <i>Modifying Africa: How Biotechnology
Can Benefit the Poor and Hungry, A Case Study from Kenya</i>.  “Agri-biotechnology matters to Kenya, as to
most other African countries, for the most basic of reasons: Our people do not
have enough to eat.”</p>

<p>Other African researchers share
that opinion. Recently, the International Society of African Scientists issued
a statement calling agricultural biotechnology a “major opportunity” to enhance
food production in Africa, the Caribbean and other developing areas.</p>

<p>“Africa and the Caribbean cannot
afford to be left further behind in acquiring the uses and benefits of this new
agricultural revolution,” wrote the scientists.
<sup>13</sup>  </p>

<p>The research into improving crops
important to the developing world is promising.</p>

<p>New varieties of rice are being
developed that are expected to have 50 percent higher yields and higher protein
content, mature 30 to 50 days earlier, be more disease and drought tolerant and
resistant to certain insect pests, and be grown with less fertilizer or
pesticides. <sup>14</sup> </p>

<p>“This initiative shows the enormous
potential of biotech to improve food security in Africa, Asia and Latin
America,” states the United Nations Development Program in its 2001 Human
Development Report.<sup>15</sup>
</p>

<p>Biotech banana tissue cultures have
been developed to cultivate clean, disease-free banana plantlets<sup>16</sup>
so they can be distributed to small farmers in Kenya, where pests and diseases
have reduced average yields to less than a third of the crop’s potential.<sup>17</sup>
The result will be higher yields. </p>

<p>Also in Kenya, researchers, led by
Wambugu, are continuing the development of a biotech sweet potato that is
resistant to feathery mottle virus, a pest which consumes (along with several
related virus strains) an estimated three-fourths of the annual harvest.  The improved sweet potato, a staple in many
countries, is expected to deliver much higher yields. Chinese researchers have
also developed a virus-resistant sweet potato that has improved yields by as
much as 160 percent.<sup>18</sup>
</p>

<p>Worldwide, an estimated 35 percent
of agricultural production is lost to insects and disease at a cost of more
than $200 billion per year. Plant biotechnology is emerging as another tool to
help farmers prevent those losses.</p>

<p class="articlepullquote">Better food for better nutrition</p>

<p>In addition to providing more food,
research is underway to improve the nutritional and medicinal value of food in
the developing world. </p>

<p>Researchers are currently working
to build a vaccine into bananas for hepatitis B, a deadly disease that afflicts
some 300 million people worldwide. <sup>19</sup>  Traditional vaccines require refrigeration
and sterilization equipment that is expensive and scarce in the developing
world. The edible vaccine could be delivered for as little as 2 cents a dose
vs. $125 for conventional treatment.<sup>20</sup>
</p>

<p>Researchers are also working to
build a gene into rice that produces more beta carotene, a precursor to vitamin
A. Worldwide, an estimated 100 million to 140 million children suffer from
vitamin A deficiency, which can lead to illness and death. <sup>21</sup>
Up to half a million children worldwide go blind each year due to vitamin A
deficiency, according to the World Health Organization.</p>

<p>New varieties of high-protein corn
are being developed that could help alleviate chronic deficiencies that afflict
still more children in parts of Africa, Asia and Latin America. </p>

<p>These are just a few of the
improved products that may enhance the quality of life for people in the
developing world. Although the technology is new, the concept behind the
improvements is centuries old. As Hippocrates, the Greek physician who’s known
as the father of modern medicine, said in 400 B.C., “Let food be your medicine
and medicine be your food.”</p>

<p class="articlepullquote">Protecting the environment</p>

<p>Plant biotechnology is also helping
protect the environment in many regions of the developing world by improving
the productivity of existing agricultural land and by allowing the use of more
conservation tillage and less spraying.</p>

<p>“Unless we are ready to accept
starvation, or place parks and the Amazon basin under the plow, there really is
only one good alternative: Discover ways to increase food production from
existing resources,” says Martina McGloughlin, director of the biotechnology
program at the University of California-Davis and head of the life sciences
program for the California university system. </p>

<p>Environmentalists fear that up to
half of the world’s remaining 6 billion acres of tropical forests will be lost
to agricultural expansion. And some biologists are warning that as many as 20
percent of all tropical forest species could be extinct within 30 years if
forests continue to disappear at the current rate. <sup>22</sup>
</p>

<p>Researchers are using plant
biotechnology to boost productivity from existing land in a number of ways. </p>

<ul>

<li><p>In Mexico, researchers are working to make rice and
corn more tolerant of aluminum &mdash; a common soil toxin. <sup>23</sup>
</p></li>

<li><p>In California, researchers have developed a genetically
enhanced tomato that can thrive in salty soils<sup>24</sup>
&mdash; a problem in arid regions where irrigation is used. The hope is to make other
plants salt-tolerant as well. </p></li>

<li><p>In India, scientists are adding two genes to rice
plants so they can survive long periods under water. <sup>25</sup>  </p></li>

</ul>

<p>That’s important because more than
a fourth of the world’s 19.7 billion acres of agricultural land, pastures,
forest and woodlands have already degraded from overuse or misuse, such as
over-irrigation or erosion. And another 12.3 million to 24.6 million acres are
afflicted every year, according to the CGIAR.    </p>

<p class="articlepullquote">Enhancing lives through technology sharing</p>

<p>Research on plant biotechnology is
being conducted on many different continents by many different people &mdash; from
university and government scientists to researchers at public institutions and
private companies. </p>

<p>In 1998, the “Papaya Biotechnology
Network” was formed to share this technology with small subsistence farmers in
Southeast Asia. The network is composed of experts from both the public and
private sector to develop enhanced papayas in Indonesia, Malaysia, the
Philippines, Thailand and Vietnam, which have all experienced ringspot virus
problems similar to the one that nearly destroyed Hawaii’s crop.   </p>

<p>Field trials are set to begin soon
in these countries and farmers &mdash; who rely on the crop to help feed their
families and to earn cash &mdash; are hopeful.<sup>26</sup>
They’ve heard what these new seeds have done for Hawaii’s papaya farmers. </p>

<p>The fact is, plant biotechnology is
enhancing lives right now, and can improve <i>more
</i>lives in even <i>more </i>ways in the
future. </p>
