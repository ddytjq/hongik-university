<P><A NAME="anchor172906"></A><B><FONT SIZE=+2>GENERAL SYSTEMATICS</FONT></B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://entmuseum9.ucr.edu/ent112/lec_outl.html">Systematics</A>.
John Heraty and Richard Whitkus, University of California at Riverside.
An excellent series of lecture notes on all aspects of systematics including
a brief history of systematics, speciation, classification, cladistic methods,
phenetic methods, character evidence, nomenclature, and applications of
systematics to evolution and biodiversity. Highly recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.princeton.edu/~rdknight/211_systematics.html">Systematics
Lab</A>. R.D. Knight, Princeton University. An introduction to the basic
principles of constructing classifications and phylogenies. Some of the
topics include the classifical methods of evolutionary taxonomy, phenetics,
cladistics, and molecular systematics. Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://weber.u.washington.edu/~anthro/BOOK/book.html">Systematics
in Prehistory</A>. Robert C. Dunnell. Although this online book was written
for anthropologists and deals with the concepts of classification from the
anthropologist's point of view, many of the basic philosophical principles
apply to biological classifications as well.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.kent.edu/biology/freuden/systemat.htm">What
is Systematics and Why is It Important</A>. John V. Freudenstein, Kent State
University. A introduction to the basic principles of systematics and the
role systematics serves in modern biology. </B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://jrscience.wcp.muohio.edu/lab/TaxonomyLab.html">The
&quot;Nuts and Bolts&quot; of Taxonomy and Classification</A>. R. Hays Cummins.
A simple, basic introduction to the principles of taxonomy and a classroom
exercise to give students practice in applying the principles.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.csdl.tamu.edu/FLORA/tfp/tfphome1.html">Botany
301: Taxonomy of Flowering Plants</A>. This excellent introduction to plant
systematics at Texas A and M University has a short introduction to plant
nomenclature. Choose &quot;Lecture Notes&quot; and then &quot;Nomenclature&quot;.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.sel.barc.usda.gov/scalenet/glossary.htm">Glossary,
Scale Net</A>. Systematic Entomology Laboratory, USDA. A glossary of terms
commonly used in taxonomy.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.best.com/~atta/taxonomy.html">Curiosities of
Biological Nomenclature</A>. Mark Isaak. Not all scientists are humorless.
There have been some rather peculiar names proposed for various plants and
animals. Come here is see some of the more unusual and hilarious. Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.bishop.hawaii.org/bishop/HBS/BOGUS/menke.html">Funny
of Curious Zoological Names</A>. Arnold S. Menke. Not to belabor the point,
but here is the classic list of peculiar or funny scientific names in Zoology.
Also recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://ipmworld.umn.edu/chapters/pinto.htm">Systematics
and Biological Characteristics</A>. John D. Pinto, University of California
at Riverside. Systematists most commonly use morphological characters in
deriving classifications and phylogenies for their groups. However biological
characters such as behavior can also be used. This page is an article discussing
biological characters in systematics with examples taken from the insect
world.</B></P>

<P>&nbsp;</P>

<P><A NAME="anchor173585"></A><B><FONT SIZE=+2>NOMENCLATURE</FONT></B></P>

<P><B>Nomenclature rules come in codes very muchlike the U.S. Code. There
are separate codes for animals, plants, cultivated plants, bacteria, and
viruses. So far only the botanical code is available on the web.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.bgbm.fu-berlin.de/iapt/nomenclature/code/tokyo-e/Contents.htm">International
Code of Botanical Nomenclature</A>. International Association for Plant
Taxonomy. The complete text for the most recent (Tokyo) version of the official
Code of Botanical Nomenclature.</B></P>

<P>&nbsp;</P>

<P><A NAME="anchor177886"></A><B><FONT SIZE=+2>PHYLOGENETIC SYSTEMATICS
(CLADISTICS)</FONT></B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.ucmp.berkeley.edu/clad/clad4.html">Journey into
Phylogenetic Systematics</A>. University of California at Berkeley Museum
of Paleontology. An introduction to the principles of cladistics (phylogenetic
systematics). The site includes an introduction to the subject, some discussion
of the methodology involved, the implications, and the importance of cladistics.
The strongest point of this site is a glossary of terms commonly bantered
about by cladists. Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.gwu.edu/~clade/faculty/lipscomb/">A Basic guide
to Phylogenetic Analysis</A>. Diana Lipscomb, George Washington University.
If you really want to get into the nitty-gritty of cladistics, then you'll
want to download this manual to phylogenetic systematics. The manual is
in the form of an Adobe Acrobat document (pdf) and covers most of the major
topics in cladistics including both philosophy and methodology. Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.nhm.ukans.edu/cc.html">The Compleat Cladist</A>:
A Primer of Phylogenetic Procedures. E.O. Wiley et al. It is difficult to
be too enthusiastic about this online book. This web page is based on a
manual of cladistic procedures. You have to like systematics and cladistics
to really get into it, but if you are, go here. The book contains an introduction
to the philosophy of cladistic methodologies, specific information about
different techniques and methods, and applications of the methods to real
data. The manual is online now, but may be taken off when a new printed
version of the manual is available. So hurry. Highly Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://members.tripod.de/GBechly/glossary.htm">Glossary
of Phylogenetic Systematics with a Critic of Mainstream Cladism</A>. G&uuml;nter
Bechly. A very extensive glossary of the terms and jargon encountered in
phylogenetic systematics (cladistics) with the comments of the author.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.utexas.edu/ftp/depts/systbiol/info/cladliterature.html">Cladistic
Literature</A>. Vicki Funk and Michael Donoghue. A very extensive bibliography
of papers and books related to cladistic classification techniques.</B></P>

<P>&nbsp;</P>

<P><A NAME="anchor179178"></A><B><FONT SIZE=+2>COMPUTER PROGRAMS FOR SYSTEMATICS</FONT></B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://phylogeny.arizona.edu/tree/programs/programs.html">Phylogenetic
Analysis Computer Programs</A>. A very complete listing of programs involved,
one way or another, in phylogenetic analysis. An excellent way to find out
about the computer programs out there.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://www.vims.edu/~mes/hennig/software.html">Software</A>.
Willie Hennig Society. A very complete listing of software available for
phylogenetic analysis with information on the purpose of the program, its
requirements, and their availability. Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://evolution.genetics.washington.edu/phylip/software.html">Phylogeny
Programs</A>. Joe Felsenstein. An extremely complete list of 136 phylogeny
packages for phylogenetic analysis. Highly recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://evolution.genetics.washington.edu/phylip.html">PHYLIP</A>.
Joe Felsenstein. PHYLIP is a free package of programs for phylogenetic analysis.
There are other computer programs for inferring phylogenies, but this one
is free. Recommended.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://evolve.zps.ox.ac.uk/CAIC/CAIC.html">CAIC - A Comparative
Analysis Package for the Apple Macintosh</A>. Andy Purvis and Andrew Rambaut.
A program for analyzing continuous variables in phylogenetic estimation.
Free.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://work.uoregon.edu/~COMPARE/">Compare 2.0</A>. Emilia
P. Martins A package of programs for character analysis, randomization,
and tree conversion in phylogenetic estimation studies. Free.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://evolve.zps.ox.ac.uk/">Evolutionary Biology, Molecular
Phylogenetics, and Epidemiology, University of Oxford</A>. A suite of computer
programs primarily intended for molecular phylogenetics. Free.</B></P>

<P><IMG SRC="../pics/globe.gif" WIDTH="20" HEIGHT="16" ALIGN="BOTTOM" NATURALSIZEFLAG=
"3"> <B><A HREF="http://phylogeny.arizona.edu/macclade/macclade.html">MacClade</A>.
Wayne P. Maddison and David R. Maddison. A computer program for character
analysis as a complement to other programs such as PAUP for phylogenetic
estimation. For Sale.</B></TD></TR>
