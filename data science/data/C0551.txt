<TABLE CELLSPACING="3" CELLPADDING="0" BORDER="0">

<TR VALIGN=TOP>
<TD><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1"><B>Pension Planning</B></FONT></TD>
<TD ALIGN=RIGHT><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1">7/11/2000</FONT></TD></TR>
<TR><TD COLSPAN=2><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1">Thinking of Early Retirement?<BR>&nbsp;</FONT></TD></TR>
<TR><TD COLSPAN=2><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1">      <p>Nearly four in ten members of final salary pension schemes left their 
        employer before reaching age 55 in the year to 31 March 1999, according 
        to a survey by Incomes Data Services (IDS).<sup><font size="1">1</font></sup> 
        Two-thirds departed before reaching 60. These statistics will not please 
        the government. As the name suggests these are schemes where the pension 
        is directly linked to the employee's final earnings before they stop work. 
        Typically they offer 1/60th of salary for each year of employment with 
        a normal maximum of 40/60ths or two-thirds of final salary. They are mostly 
        offered by large companies and public sector employers.</p>
      <p> In April 2000, the Cabinet Office published a paper entitled Winning 
        the Generation Game which looked at the issue of early retirement. One 
        of the paper's main proposals was that the normal minimum age at which 
        pensions could be drawn should be raised from 50 to 55.</p>
      <p> Not surprisingly, the idea of increasing the early retirement age generally 
        received a rather lukewarm reception. What happens next remains to be 
        seen. The government is already busy on two pension fronts with the launch 
        of stakeholder pensions and the replacement of SERPS. The chances are 
        that the Cabinet Office paper will be left on the shelf, at least until 
        after the next general election.</p>
      <p> Early retirement is not an issue the government can avoid &#151;whatever 
        its hue &#151; at least in the longer term. According to the Cabinet Office 
        paper, nearly half of the 2.8 million people between age 50 and state 
        pension age who are not currently employed rely on state benefits for 
        their income. Almost three-quarters receive some form of benefit.</p>
      <p> If you are thinking of early retirement, there are a number of points 
        to be drawn from these statistics and the Cabinet Office paper:</p>
      <ul>
        <li> 
          <p> Early retirement has usually been easier for members of final salary 
            pension schemes. Employers have used early retirement as a relatively 
            painless way to cut staff numbers. This approach has been helped by 
            the financial surpluses that many schemes built up over the years. 
            But these surpluses are running out. According to the IDS survey, 
            in 1999 employer contributions to final salary schemes rose for the 
            first time in many years.<br>
            <br>
        </li>
        <li> 
          <p> Early retirement often means having to live on a much reduced income&#151;hence 
            the large proportion of people claiming benefit. If you retire early, 
            your pension fund suffers a triple blow: fewer contributions are paid 
            in, the fund has less time to grow and the scheme has to pay your 
            pension for a longer period.<br>
            For example, if you retire five years early, after 20 years' service 
            in a typical final salary scheme, your pension could be about two-thirds 
            of what you would have received for continuing to work until your 
            official retirement date. Leave ten years early &#151; but still with 
            20 years' service &#151; and you could be looking at well under half 
            the full amount you would expect to receive at the normal retirement 
            date.<br>
            If you have personal pensions or are a member of a money-purchase 
            pension scheme, you do not avoid this pension cut-back problem. With 
            these schemes, you get a pension based on the amount you have built 
            up in your fund. For instance, to provide a £20,000 a year inflation-linked 
            pension for a 55-year-old man currently costs about £400,000, and 
            for a woman of the same age, £435,000. By the age of 65 the costs 
            fall to £285,000 for a man and £335,000 for a woman.<sup><font size="1">2</font></sup></p>
        </li>
        <li> 
          <p> Retirement planning needs to build in flexibility. Investing in 
            pensions still offers major tax benefits, but these must be set against 
            the government-imposed restrictions on when benefits are drawn. No 
            such constraints apply to many other means of saving for retirement, 
            such as Individual Savings Accounts (ISAs).</p>
        </li>
      </ul>
      <p><i><font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="1">1 Source: IDS, 
        September 1999</font></i><br>
     <i><font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="1"> 2 Source: Financial 
        Times, 8 July 2000 </font></i></p>
