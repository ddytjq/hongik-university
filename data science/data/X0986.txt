				<font class="newsdate"> Thursday, January 31, 2002 10:50 (GMT)</font><br>
				<img src="/1.gif" width="1" height="18"><br>
				<font class="news">A 5-0 whitewash in the one-day internationals, a heartening performance in the drawn one-off Test match and a couple of victories in the tour matches does not give an overall picture of a successful tour and the England women&#8217;s team could not have been very happy at the end of their four week tour of India which concluded last week.<br><br>But from the visitors&#8217; angle, the one real highlight of the tour culminated in a world record opening partnership, and that is something to be proud of. <br><br>This came about in the Test match at Lucknow when England's opening batters Caroline Atkins of Sussex and Arran Thompson of Lancashire put on a round 200 to break the previous mark of 178, held by Australia openers Haggett and Clark, also against India, at Sydney in 1990.<br><br>Atkins and Thompson were opening for just the seventh time together, and in only their third Test match. But they showed remarkable understanding besides monumental patience and intense concentration in putting together a stand that lasted 450 minutes and 123.2 overs, before the 21-year-old Atkins was run out for 90.<br><br>Thompson, 19, went on to make 85 before being bowled. At the end of it all, Atkins summed it up well by saying that "we were just really pleased to have that much time at the crease, and for us starting out as openers together it's great for our development and learning. It's given the team a great basis for this game and hopefully the future." <br><br>The partnership did lay the basis for a good score but the batting approach was funereal. England for example took 165.3 overs to compile a total of 314. India in reply were only slightly better, scoring 312-9 off 146 overs with Hemlatha Kala getting 110. Predictably enough, the match, slightly affected by rain and bad light, meandered to a draw.  <br><br>But as Atkins rightly put it, development and learning were the twin factors that governed the tour from England&#8217;s viewpoint.<br><br>They had come with a largely young and inexperienced side with many experienced players having just retired or unavailable. And in the one-day internationals, they fared badly against a home team that had been good enough to make the semifinals of the World Cup in New Zealand a year ago.<br><br>The batting was the main problem. Time and again they floundered against the spinners which, given their inexperience against this mode of bowling, was not exactly a surprise. But they also conceded many wickets to the medium pacers and this did come as a surprise.<br><br>There was no justification for scores of 71-7 in 27 overs and 78 all out in 42.3 overs in the second and third games of the series, both played at Hyderabad and just eight days into the tour, the visitors had already lost the limited overs series.<br><br>Then came the vastly improved performance in the Test match and the batting did show signs of substance in the final two one-dayers but all this was in the nature of too little too late.<br><br>Two victories in the tour matches, one at the start of the trip in Chennai and the other towards the end at Pune, served only as a few crumbs of comfort.  However from the development and learning point of view, there is no doubt that the tour would have done wonders for many of the youngsters. <br><br>The inexperience was showed quite plainly, for the visitors did have opportunities in the one-day games but failed to cash in on them. Skipper Claire Connor did the best she could under difficult circumstances. On her third tour of India, the 25-year-old left arm spinner made the most of the limited resources at her command.<br><br>Connor, a veteran of seven Tests and 50 one-dayers when the tour commenced, admitted at the start that hers was a young squad that had a long way to go.<br><br>&#8220;With the help of our new coach, John Harmer from Australia, I hope I am leading them in the right direction,&#8221; she said. &#8220;I just hope that some of the girls get confidence from this tour and that we have some good results."<br><br>Well, if the results were not exactly successful, the girls would have gained much in confidence and the credit for this must go to Connor and Harmer, a man who has coached Australia to three World Cup finals.  <br><br>The matches were fairly well supported by spectators, for whom the rare sight of women playing cricket is still a curiosity. The visitors found the experience of touring India fabulous and players like wicketkeeper Jane Cassar, Dawn Holden, Sarah Clarke, Caroline Atkins, Arran Thompson, Laura Newton and Clare Taylor made the most of the opportunities that came their way.<br><br>From the Indian viewpoint, the results were not the only encouraging aspect. The form of established players like Mithali Raj, Anju Jain, Hemlatha Kala, Neetu David and skipper Anjum Chopra augured well for the home team who are already in the process of preparing for the next World Cup.<br><br>- <i>Partab Ramchand</i></font><br>
