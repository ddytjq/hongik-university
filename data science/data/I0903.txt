<TD height=14><SPAN class=moduletitles>The National Game</SPAN></TD>
<TD width=12><IMG height=1 src='http://www.thefa.com/static/framework/skins/grassroots_skin/images/spacer.gif' width=1 border=0></TD>

<TD width=12><IMG height=1 src='http://www.thefa.com/static/framework/skins/grassroots_skin/images/spacer.gif' width=1 border=0></TD>
<TD vAlign=top align=left><P><SPAN class=body12>20 May 2002</SPAN><BR><SPAN class=editorialtitle>The National Game</SPAN></P></TD>
<TD width=12><IMG height=1 src='http://www.thefa.com/static/framework/skins/grassroots_skin/images/spacer.gif' width=1 border=0></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD colspan=3>&nbsp;</TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD width=12><IMG height=1 src='http://www.thefa.com/static/framework/skins/grassroots_skin/images/spacer.gif' width=1 border=0></TD>
<TD>
<TABLE height=108 cellSpacing=0 cellPadding=0 align=left border=0>
<TBODY>
<TR>
<TD><IMG src="http://www.thefa.com/static/images/Grassroots/Default_Images/default_large.jpg" border=0 align="left"><P class=body08>The F.A.'s National Game Division has been created to work with County FAs, Local Football Partnerships, the Football Foundation and The F.A.'s commercial partners to develop football in England at every level below the Football League, from the Football Conference to parks and schools. </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>Its responsibilities range from creating the strategy for grass-roots football to developing non-League football and overseeing the distribution of the major new sums of money that have already started to restore the lower reaches of the game.  </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>"There are, however, really six things that we are about", explains Steve Parkin, Director of the National Game Division. They are as follows: </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08><strong>1. Raising Standards</strong>

</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>This means raising the standards of both schools and grass-roots clubs, through The F.A.'s Charter Standard programme. Thus, getting on for 500 clubs have already achieved F.A. Charter Standard status. The target is for 30 per cent of all schools, and a total of 5,000 clubs, to achieve it within five years. </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>This Charter Standard acts as a kite-mark endorsement, designed to raise and reward quality standards in football provision, in both schools and clubs.  </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08><strong>2. Improving Facilities</strong>

</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>The results of work done in compiling a Register of English Football Facilities (REFF) are soon to be published. REFF comprises a national survey of all grass-roots facilities in England, undertaken by Price Waterhouse Cooper and designed to identify where investment is most needed. This has been funded by the Football Foundation. </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>"We've found that one-third of facilities don't even have changing rooms", says Parkin, by way of a sneak preview. </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>The objective is to change that. Hence, The F.A. is investing at least £45m via the Football Foundation over the next three years into improving grass-roots pitches, club-houses and other facilities. "We want both to improve current facilities and create new ones", explains Parkin. </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>"Decades of neglect can't be put right in 12 months", he stresses. "This is a long-term programme, that will take 10 year s or more. But the impact, I hope, will be seen year on year. </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08><strong>3. Developing Football Administration</strong>

</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>The work in this area includes getting more people involved in football as volunteers, whether in coaching teams, running clubs or refereeing games. Thus the National Game Division is working with County FAs on delivering a programme of Effective Football Club Administration (EFCA), consisting of courses to support volunteers and building on the key role that they play at grass-roots level. </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>"We also want to train and support county, league and club officials in their administration of the game", says Parkin. </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>Meanwhile, The F.A. is investing £12 million over four years in a custom-designed IT system that will link the football community and ease the administration burden on clubs, leagues and County FAs. As part of this, a League Administration is already live on the the Internet, while in the near future, all County FAs will be operating under a new County Administration System. </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08><strong>4. Developing Women's Football</strong>

</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>Women's football is already the biggest sport for women in this country and The FA.. is putting the structures in place to help it expand further, with the hope of a doubling of the number playing within the next decade, if not sooner. The F.A. also has plans to create a professional league for women. "This is so that we can further raise the standards and create a new platform for women's football", says Parkin. </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08><strong>5. Developing Leagues, Competitions and Clubs</strong>

</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>This means developing everything from The F.A. Trophy and F.A. Vase to ambitious plans for developing hundreds of what are being called "community clubs". </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>These are sort of "super-clubs", each with at least ten teams, ranging from junior to women's and senior teams. "It is the sort of club where you can start as a youngster, playing in a junior team, and go all the way through to playing as a veteran", explains Parkin. Such clubs will also have a social area or bar, where the life of the club can continue before and after the game. </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>This approach is based on the model of grass-roots clubs in Holland, where clubs get Government investment and boast first-rate facilities.</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>"We want community clubs to be at the heart of our plans", says Parkin. "Our objective in the next five years - or, it could take ten - is to create 500 community clubs. We could probably today already identify 100 clubs which are candidates for community club status."</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08><strong>6. Raising Awareness</strong>

</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>The final priority is simply to raise awareness among everyone from the media and the general public to players and parents of the work being done by The F.A. and County FAs to develop our national game. </P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
