        <h2><a name="_Toc494115396"></a><a name="_Toc496592766"></a><a name="_Toc496593225"></a><a
        name="_Toc496593325"></a><a name="_Toc496593384"></a><a
        name="1.&nbsp; Objectives and general structure of the study"><span
        style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496593384"><span style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496593325"><span
        style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496593225"><span style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496592766"><span
        style="mso-bookmark:_Toc494115396"><span lang="EN-US">1.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>Objectives and general structure of the study</span></span></span></span></span></span></a></h2>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">This is a multicentre
        prospective epidemiological study of the birth prevalence of congenital and genetic
        disorders. The study will provide an evidence base for interventions aiming to reduce the
        burden of these disorders.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">The study will involve
        training health workers to collect data and examine new-borns in a standardised way, and
        will establish a basis for national surveillance of congenital and genetic disorders.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">There are six basic
        objectives. Objectives 1 and 2 are <i style="mso-bidi-font-style:normal">core objectives</i>
        to be carried out by all participants. The other, optional objectives can be met at
        varying levels, according to the resources available to each study team<b
        style="mso-bidi-font-weight:normal"><i style="mso-bidi-font-style:
normal">.</i></b><u><o:p></o:p></u></span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US"><b
        style="mso-bidi-font-weight:
normal">Objective 1:<span style="mso-tab-count:1">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
        </span></b>To gather basic demographic data on parents, including data on parental
        consanguinity.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US"><b
        style="mso-bidi-font-weight:
normal">Objective 2:<span style="mso-tab-count:1">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
        </span></b>To establish the birth prevalence (on a community, geographical base wherever
        possible) of structural congenital anomalies detectable by clinical examination of
        new-borns, including stillbirths.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US"><b
        style="mso-bidi-font-weight:
normal">Objective 3:<span style="mso-tab-count:1">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
        </span></b>To establish the prevalence of risk factors for congenital disorders in mothers
        through blood testing (for example, haemoglobin disorders, rubella, syphilis, HIV and
        other maternal infections, folate and iodine levels, and G6PD deficiency).</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US"><b
        style="mso-bidi-font-weight:
normal">Objective 4:<span style="mso-tab-count:1">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
        </span></b>To establish the birth prevalence (on a community,<span
        style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>geographical<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp;
        </span>base wherever possible) of haemoglobin disorders, G6PD deficiency and a range of
        metabolic disorders, by analysis of Guthrie blood spot samples in new-borns, including
        stillbirths.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US"><b
        style="mso-bidi-font-weight:
normal">Objective 5:</b><span style="mso-tab-count:1">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
        </span>To establish the frequency of antenatally diagnosed congenital disorders in the
        study population.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US"><b
        style="mso-bidi-font-weight:
normal">Objective 6:<span style="mso-tab-count:1">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
        </span></b>To link the later outcome in the babies with observations at birth, and to
        establish the prevalence of congenital disorders presenting after the neonatal period.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">Standardised methods will
        allow international pooling of "core" and optional data.<b
        style="mso-bidi-font-weight:normal"><o:p></o:p></b></span></p>
        <h2><a name="_Toc494115397"></a><a name="_Toc496592767"></a><a name="_Toc496593226"></a><a
        name="_Toc496593326"></a><a name="_Toc496593385"></a><a name="2.&nbsp; Study design"><span
        style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496593385"><span style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496593326"><span
        style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496593226"><span style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496592767"><span
        style="mso-bookmark:_Toc494115397"><span lang="EN-US">2.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>Study design</span></span></span></span></span></span></a></h2>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">The core study of the
        target population can be set up either (a) using anonymous data only, for purely
        epidemiological purposes, or (b) using named data, in order to allow the possibility of
        follow-up of target babies. Research teams need to look carefully at the advantages and
        disadvantages of each approach.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">An anonymous study is the
        simplest to execute. Clearly, mothers and babies with abnormalities detected at the time
        of<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>ata collection must be referred for
        appropriate clinical management, when possible. However, in an anonymous study it is not
        possible to arrange for follow-up of babies when abnormal findings emerge later, e.g.
        through tests carried out on the mother's blood, or on Guthrie cards. The working
        group did not favour an anonymous study, for these reasons.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">When the investment has
        been made to carry out the initial epidemiological study, it may prove desirable and
        feasible in some areas to develop it into a prospective follow-up study of the cohort of
        registered babies, in order to identify congenital and genetic disorders that cannot be
        readily diagnosed at birth (such as many congenital malformations, thalassaemia,
        haemophilia, mental retardation,<a style="mso-footnote-id:ftn2" href="#_ftn2"
        name="_ftnref2" title><span class="MsoFootnoteReference"><span
        style="mso-special-character:
footnote">[1]</span></span></a>). This requires recording
        of confidential details of the mother and baby, including name and address.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">With appropriate support,
        birth cohorts can be followed at regular intervals for a long period (e.g. the 3 UK birth
        cohorts (1947, 1958, 1970), still being followed, include a random sample of all babies
        born in one week of that year). Such studies give an increasingly valuable yield of health
        information with the passage of time.<b style="mso-bidi-font-weight:normal"><o:p></o:p></b></span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">It is essential for each
        study team to decide at an early stage whether they will conduct an anonymous core study
        or a follow-up study, since the decision will affect all aspects of data collection. For
        example, if follow-up is contemplated, a parent's consent to all parts of the study ,
        including later follow up, should be obtained at the outset. If a team is undecided, a
        named study may be selected, since it allows room for a later decision to follow up the
        cohort.</span></p>
        <h3><span lang="EN-US">2.1<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>Concerns about
        ethics and confidentiality</span></h3>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">There are ethical issues
        and concerns about confidentiality for any study with named data, or studies where
        subjects can be identified through case numbers. In a named study, a parent will need an
        explanation of the study objectives before they can give informed consent. They also need
        to know if samples of either maternal or infant blood will be stored for future use. The
        consent of a parent may be refused for all or parts of the study and<span
        style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>this will remove data on that mother and baby from
        results.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">Measures to ensure
        confidentiality of study data are extremely important if named data are collected in order
        to facilitate follow up. These may include using stand-alone computer systems and
        passwords, and avoiding accidental access to study data by setting up appropriate security
        systems.</span></p>

        <h2><a name="_Toc494115400"></a><a name="_Toc496592768"></a><a name="_Toc496593227"></a><a
        name="_Toc496593327"></a><a name="_Toc496593386"></a><a name="3.&nbsp; Study size"><span
        style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496593386"><span style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496593327"><span
        style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496593227"><span style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496592768"><span
        style="mso-bookmark:_Toc494115400"><span lang="EN-US">3.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>Study size</span></span></span></span></span></span></a></h2>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">The<span
        style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>target population of<span
        style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>mothers and babies studied must be large enough to
        link social and health data on the parents with the outcome of the present pregnancy.
        Ideally, the study should focus on all women living within a defined geographical area,
        whether they have their baby at home or in an institution.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">The minimum<span
        style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>target population size is 15,000, but a population
        of up to 50,000 would provide more valuable figures. The size of the study population
        selected, and the duration of the study, will inevitably differ from one place to another,
        depending on local circumstances. Some small countries may aim for complete national data
        collection. Others may select a<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>geographical
        area, such as a health region or district for study. As the study is population- rather
        than institution- based, the study team must try to cover as many pregnant women resident
        in the area as possible, including births at home and those in<span
        style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>private and military hospitals which serve the
        population under study.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Failure to include
        births outside large institutions will cause bias.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>In
        countries where almost all births occur in hospitals, a population-based study will also
        be institution-based. Countries where home births are common may have to use birth
        registration systems, local health offices, centres or clinics and traditional birth
        attendants to ascertain which babies are born at home.</span></p>
        <h2><a name="_Toc494115401"></a><a name="_Toc496592769"></a><a name="_Toc496593228"></a><a
        name="_Toc496593328"></a><a name="_Toc496593387"></a><a name="4.&nbsp; Data collection"><span
        style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496593387"><span style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496593328"><span
        style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496593228"><span style="mso-bookmark:_Toc496592769"><span
        style="mso-bookmark:_Toc494115401"><span lang="EN-US">4.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>Data collection</span></span></span></span></span></span></a></h2>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">Core data will be
        captured by existing health workers involved in maternal and child health as soon after
        delivery as possible, as mothers pass through health care and birth registration systems.
        Data will be collected through a standardized interview with the mother and by
        standardized examination of each baby. </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">The health workers
        involved in the study will require careful training, and in view of staff turnover,
        regular training and education sessions will be needed. The content of the training should
        be defined. It is hoped that by using health professionals to record data, awareness of
        the effect of congenital disorders on morbidity and mortality will be strengthened. Health
        professionals will also gain clinical skills by learning how to examine new-borns
        systematically.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">When one of the primary
        workers suspects a congenital abnormality, the baby should also be examined and the
        findings recorded by an expert member of the research team.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">Local study teams should
        decide whether the staff who collect the basic data should code information, or whether
        the data they collect will later be coded by the expert group.<i
        style="mso-bidi-font-style:normal"><o:p></o:p></i></span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">A<span
        style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>standardized user-friendly<span
        style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>and flexible computer programme<span
        style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>will be devised for protocol generation and
        data-entry in each country.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Data-collection
        may be computer generated, and &#147;personalised&#148; for the institutions and countries
        participating.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">Data collection is not
        necessarily limited to that on the standardized forms.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>Research teams may choose to modify the forms to include additional items of
        interest to themselves.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>They will be
        responsible for recording and analysing any additional data: only the standardized items
        will be included in the EMRO Mother and Baby Study.</span></p>
        <h3><span lang="EN-US">4.1<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>Collecting data
        on mothers</span></h3>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">The<span
        style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>consent<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>of
        a parent<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>for all<span
        style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>objectives of the study which are to be included<span
        style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>should be obtained at the time of completing the
        first data collection form. Each participating mother will be given a unique identifying
        number.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">A target population of<span
        style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>at least 15,000<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>recently delivered women will be interviewed (where possible while in hospital or

        style="mso-bidi-font-weight:normal">Mother's data collection sheets</b>. </span></p>

        <h3><span lang="EN-US">4.2<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>Collecting blood

normal">Baby data collection

yes">&nbsp; </span>The findings and

        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:6.0pt"><span lang="EN-US">Special attention should
        be directed to ensuring that all babies in intensive care are included in the study.</span></p>
