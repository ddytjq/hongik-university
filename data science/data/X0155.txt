<h1>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">A short story of materials science</font></h1>

<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Introduction</font></h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">On this page you can get some information
about what materials you can use for LDbooms. We try to refer every material
that is used or we know of. The density declared in the chapters is calculated
by Tibor measuring volume and weight of samples.</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">If you have additional knowledge or disagree,
please contact us.</font>
<p>
<hr>
<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Wood (Birch Cross Ply 10 layers on 5mm of
Finland)</font></h2>

<table BORDER=0 >
<tr>
<td><img SRC="wood.jpg" height=100 width=100></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: (0.801 +- 0.026) g/cm&sup3;</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">You know wood, the thing trees are made
of. It&acute;s a great material to begin LDthrowing. <a href="smith/index.htm">Herb
Smith</a> made 100m throws with wood. And Michel Dufayard, too and ...&nbsp;</font></td>
</tr>

<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Advantage</font></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Easy to work with.</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">The glue layers show how accurate you
are working.</font></td>
</tr>

<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Disadvantage</font></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Breaks at stronger throws/if there&acute;s
too much lead on it.</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Sensitive to humidity</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Low Density</font></td>
</tr>
</table>

<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Paxolin</font></h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: (1.359 +- 0.043) g/cm&sup3;</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Other names: Pertinax, Bakelite, Hartpapier,
...</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Commonly used for LDBooms. It&acute;s
made out of paper impregnated with phenolic-formaldehyd resin. If you&acute;re
interested in chemical formulas, watch this:</font>
<br>&nbsp;
<center><table BORDER=0 CELLSPACING=0 CELLPADDING=0 WIDTH="100%" BGCOLOR="#FFFFFF" >
<tr>
<td><img SRC="paxolin.gif" ALT="chemical formula" height=140 width=181></td>
</tr>
</table></center>

<p><font face="Arial,Helvetica">There are different types of Paxolin. Be
sure to use to paper impregnated type as the cloth-type [Density: (1.314
+- 0.044) g/cm&sup3;] is not stiff enough (although it is able to resist
a higher impact than the paper-type).</font>
<br>&nbsp;
<table>
<tr>
<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Paper impregnated</font></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Cloth impregnated</font></td>
</tr>

<tr>
<td><img SRC="hpapier.jpg" height=100 width=100></td>

<td><img SRC="hgewebe.jpg" height=100 width=100></td>
</tr>
</table>

<table BORDER=0 >
<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Advantage</font></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Very stiff</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Interesting density</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Cheap</font></td>
</tr>

<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Disadvantage</font></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Very dangerous dust producing; Health
(Cancer) risk</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Can break if there is too much lead (20g+)
or the boom is not thick enough</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Attracts water and looses in the course
the stiffness (always seal the boom with a coat of water resistant color)</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Needs powertools at least for cutting</font></td>
</tr>
</table>

<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Glassfiber/Epoxy Composite</font></h2>

<table BORDER=0 >
<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP BGCOLOR="#FFFFFF"><img SRC="ep.gif" ALT="chemical formula" height=348 width=368>
<table>
<tr>
<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica"><font color="#000000">yellow</font></font></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica"><font color="#000000">light green</font></font></td>
</tr>

<tr>
<td><img SRC="gfecgood.jpg" height=100 width=100></td>

<td><img SRC="gfecbad.jpg" height=100 width=100></td>
</tr>
</table>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">&nbsp;</font></td>

<td VALIGN=TOP><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: (1.794 +- 0.060) g/cm&sup3;</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Other names: GFEC, G10&nbsp;</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Commonly used for LDBooms. It&acute;s
made out of epoxy resin and glassfibers.&nbsp;</font>
<p><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Here are also different types of G10. From
the outside you can not tell which quality you have. But after your first
boom you know it. If your boom gets habitual tuning after a few (say five)
throws, then you have the bad thing. For us the yellow type is stiff enough,
while the lightgreen type is trash (The color can vary from other manufacturer).
There seems to be a G11 type which is even more expensive. Maybe we find
out sometime.&nbsp;</font></td>
</tr>
</table>

<table BORDER=0 >
<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Advantage</font></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Stiffness</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Strength (we had no fractures up to now)</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Toughness (resistant agains severe impacts)</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Good density</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Easy to shape</font></td>
</tr>

<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Disadvantage</font></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Glassdust is also dangerous; Beware</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Needs powertools</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Special cutting-equippment needed</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Can get <i>habitual tuning</i></font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">UV-sensitive ?</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">expensive</font></td>
</tr>
</table>

<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Carbonfiber/Epoxy Composite</font></h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: 1.5 g/cm&sup3; (not measured by myself)</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Be carefull: the fibers are so thin, they
can stick in your body and you won&acute;t even feel it, at first. Use
gloves and cover all your body. Don&acute;t work without dust-(and gas)mask!</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica"></font>&nbsp;
<table BORDER=0 >
<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Advantage</font></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">verrry stiff</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">incredible strength</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">toughness, almost unbreakable</font></td>
</tr>

<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Disadvantage</font></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">verrry dangerous dust</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Special cutting-equippment needed</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">needs powertools</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">verrry expensive&nbsp;</font></td>
</tr>
</table>

<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Aluminium (alloys)</font></h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: (2.550 +- 0.092) g/cm&sup3;</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Beware! Very dangerous! Metal booms are
not allowed in competitions.</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica"></font>&nbsp;
<table BORDER=0 >
<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Advantage</font></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Heavy</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Stiff</font></td>
</tr>

<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Disadvantage</font></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Did you see Mad Max II?</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Needs powertools</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Gets easily warped</font></td>
</tr>
</table>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica"></font>
<p><font face="Arial,Helvetica">We only used pure-Al boards, which is very
soft and therefore not fit for boomerangs. High-strength Al-alloys, however,
are better. Christian Jabet, a frechman, used 'Zicral', a chromium and
zirconium alloyed aluminium, for his V-shaped boomerang and made an inofficial
world record of 149m. It is said that he had an anvil and a hammer to flatten
his boom after crashes.&nbsp;</font>
<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Circuitboard</font></h2>

<table BORDER=0 >
<tr>
<td><img SRC="cboard.jpg" height=100 width=100></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: (2.041 +- 0.088) g/cm&sup3;&nbsp;</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">What I mean with circuitboard (it's used
for Computermainboards and stuff like that.)is a lamination of G10 and
coppersheets. This material is quite new to the boomsport. For LD it would
be interesting in thickness of 3 to 3.5 mm. Unfortunatly we were unable
up to now to acquire such sheets.</font></td>
</tr>
</table>

<hr width=300 size=20>
<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Unsaturated Polyester (UP) Resin</font></h2>

<table BORDER=0 >
<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP BGCOLOR="#FFFFFF"><img SRC="up.gif" ALT="chemical formula" height=217 width=265></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: 1.25 g/cm&sup3; , reiforced with
glass fibers: 1.5-1.8 g/cm&sup3;&nbsp;</font>
<br>&nbsp;
<p><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Usually, the material is reinforced with
glassfibers. We saw it being used for medium-range boomerangs. Density
and stiffness is comparable to GFEC. Unfortunaltely, it breaks more easily
and is difficult to tune. We have not built LD models with UP-resin, it
seems to be an interesting material for this event though.</font></td>
</tr>
</table>

<hr>
<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Polypropylene (PP)</font></h2>

<table BORDER=0 >
<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP BGCOLOR="#FFFFFF"><img SRC="polyprop.gif" ALT="chemical formula" height=64 width=84></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: 0.9 g/cm&sup3; (not measured
by myself)</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">It&acute;s not stiff. It&acute;s a thermoplast:&nbsp;</font>
<p><font face="Arial,Helvetica">PP is commonly used for short range tribladers,
for instance Eric Darnells Tri-Fly. In warm weather it is soft and in cold
conditions it gets brittle. There is a Polypropylene material with additional
glassfibers.&nbsp;</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: (1.174 +- 0.038) g/cm&sup3;</font></td>
</tr>
</table>

<hr>
<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Acrylonitrile-Butadiene-Styrene-Copolymer
(ABS)</font></h2>

<table BORDER=0 >
<tr>
<td BGCOLOR="#FFFFFF"><img SRC="abs.gif" ALT="chemical formula" height=200 width=202></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: 1.0 g/cm&sup3;&nbsp;</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">It is a impact resistant Polystyrene.&nbsp;</font>
<br>&nbsp;
<br>&nbsp;
<p><font face="Arial,Helvetica">The new Tri-Fly from Eric Darnell is ABS.
It is an excellent material for short range boomerangs. It's stiff, machined
easily, resistant against humidity and does not loose its shape in warm
weather.</font></td>
</tr>
</table>

<hr>
<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Polyvinylchloride (PVC)</font></h2>

<table BORDER=0 >
<tr>
<td VALIGN=TOP BGCOLOR="#FFFFFF"><img SRC="pvc.gif" ALT="chemical formula" height=54 width=79></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: ~1.4 g/cm&sup3;</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">It is rather heavy for a thermoplast.&nbsp;</font>
<p><font face="Arial,Helvetica">We did not often see PVC used for booms.
But why not ? Try it! It is probably especially fit for wind boomerangs,
considering the high density.&nbsp;</font></td>
</tr>
</table>

<hr>
<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Forex</font></h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: (0.669 +- 0.021) g/cm&sup3;</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">It&acute;s a foamed PVC.</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Forex is very light and flabby. It breaks
rather easily. Forex is excellent, however, for small fun or beginners
boomerangs - easy to throw and catch.</font>
<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Acrylic glass</font></h2>

<table BORDER=0 >
<tr>
<td BGCOLOR="#FFFFFF"><img SRC="plexigla.gif" ALT="chemical formula" height=134 width=103></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: (1.161 +- 0.040)g/cm&sup3;&nbsp;</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Other names: Polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA),
Plexiglass&nbsp;</font>
<p><font face="Arial,Helvetica">&nbsp;It&acute;s a substitute for glass
but less brittle, but still brittle. It is nice to have a translucent boomerang,
but otherwise: forget it.</font></td>
</tr>
</table>

<h2>
<font face="Arial,Helvetica">Polyamide (PA)</font></h2>

<table BORDER=0 >
<tr>
<td BGCOLOR="#FFFFFF"><img SRC="polyamid.gif" ALT="chemical formula" ></td>

<td><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Density: (1.148 +- 0.032) g/cm&sup3;&nbsp;</font>
<br><font face="Arial,Helvetica">Other names: Nylon&nbsp;</font>
<p><font face="Arial,Helvetica">&nbsp;Similar to Polypropylene, but even
more flabby. Besides it takes up humidity.</font></td>
</tr>
</table>
