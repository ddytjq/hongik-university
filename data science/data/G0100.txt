<center><strong><font color=navy size=4>Comments on Table 7, 8A, and 4A</font></strong></center>
<p>
<blockquote>
<font color=red>
[<strong>Technical Note:</strong>The formal dividing line on this table between the 2QZ and SDSS quasars is at RD = 0.86, when z = 3.5   The few SDSS quasars below z = 3.5 are not considered in the calculations for the average magnitudes and standard deviation for the 2QZ quasars.   It's the other way around for the single 2QZ quasar in the 0.88 RD increment.]
</font>
</blockquote> 
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>In my opinion, of all eight quasar tables, this is without question the most interesting one.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>It shows the curious void out there in the mid three range of red shifts.   As the void also happens with radio sources and the Lyman-break galaxies - links given in the main text <A  HREF="qta.html#void">of Essay #7</A> - it's fair question to ask whether or not the root cause of the common void is a cosmological one.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>There is a SDSS quasar catalogue - the link is given at the end <A HREF="qta.html#SDSS">of Essay #7</A> - which also shows that quasars are passing through a <em>minimum</em> in the number counts out in the mid three range of red shifts.   While the minimum in number counts in the SDSS catalogue is not quite so dramatic as the above table, it still provides independent statistical confirmation of the same intriguing effect. 
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Once the quasars become more numerous after the void, they're obviously decreasing in absolute magnitude.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>At the moment, Table 7 probably gives us as good a <em>visual</em> clue as we're going to get which indicates an antipode out there in the mid three range of red shifts.
