<td class="bodytext">

								<div class="linkstyle1"> <p>&nbsp;</p>



              <p>Galactic foreground signals are distinguishable from CMB anisotropy by their differing spectra and spatial distributions. Multiple   frequency coverage is needed to reliably separate Galactic foreground signals from CMB anisotropy. MAP will observe with five frequency   bands between 22 and 90 GHz. </p>



              <p><a href="../ContentMedia/990060b.jpg" target="new" onclick="CSAction(new Array(/*CMP*/'B68CA6D90'));return CSClickReturn();" csclick="B68CA6D90"><img border="0" src="../ContentMedia/990060s.jpg" alt="Galactic signal spectra" align="LEFT" width="210" height="283"></a>The 

                figure at left shows the frequency dependence of the expected 

                CMB anisotropy (gray band) and of three known sources of foreground 

                emission from our Galaxy. The five MAP frequency bands are indicated 

                in red near the top of the plot. There are at least three physical 

                mechanisms that contribute to the Galactic microwave emission: 

                synchrotron radiation, free-free radiation, and thermal radiation 

                from interstellar dust. There are hints of a possible fourth mechanism 

                that might be due to rapidly spinning dust grains emitting microwave 

                radiation, though the evidence for this is still quite tentative 

                and so has been omitted from this plot. Results from existing 

                CMB and other measurements show that at high Galactic latitudes 

                CMB anisotropy dominates the Galactic signals in the range ~30-150 

                GHz. However, the Galactic foreground will need to be measured 

                and removed from some of the MAP data. The two lowest frequency 

                MAP bands will be especially valuable for characterizing a possible 

                fourth galactic emission component, as well as constraining the 

                synchrotron and free-free emission.</p>

              <p><a href="../ContentMedia/990400b.jpg" target="new" onclick="CSAction(new Array(/*CMP*/'B68CA7041'));return CSClickReturn();" csclick="B68CA7041"><img src="../ContentMedia/990400s.jpg" border="0" align="left" width="210" height="208" alt="Graph showing the expected observing window through the galactic foreground sources."></a>The figure at left, adapted from <a href="http://www.hep.upenn.edu/%7Emax/wiener.html">Tegmark 

                &amp; Efstathiou</a> extends the above figure to an extra dimension 

                by showing the expected observing window through the galactic 

                foregrounds as a function of both frequency (vertically) and angular 

                scale (multipole moment, horizontally). The gray region indicates 

                the window in this parameter space where the CMB anisotropy signal 

                is expected to dominate the foreground signals. Again the MAP 

                bands are indicated in red with the horizontal length of the band 

                representing the <a href="ob_techres.html">angular resolution</a> 

                at each frequency.</p>



              <p>There are two techniques that MAP will use to evaluate and remove the Galactic foreground. The first uses existing Galactic maps   at lower (radio) and higher (far-infrared) frequencies as foreground emission templates. Uncertainties in the original data and position-dependent spectral index variations introduce errors with this technique. There is no good free-free emission template because there is no frequency where it dominates the microwave emission. However, high resolution, large scale maps of H-alpha emission will serve as a template for the free-free emission in regions where it has been mapped.</p>



              <p>The second technique is to form linear combinations of multi-frequency MAP observations such that signals with specified spectra are canceled. The linear combination of multi-frequency data makes no assumptions about the foreground signal strength or spatial distribution, but requires knowledge of the spectra of the foregrounds. Both techniques were successfully employed by COBE (e.g., <a href="http://www.journals.uchicago.edu/ApJ/journal/issues/ApJL/v464n1/5014/5014.html">Kogut et al.</a>).</p>



              <p>If one is willing to assume something about the angular power 

                spectra of the foreground emissions (based on observations at 

                lower and higher frequencies), then the multi-frequency combination 

                method can be generalized to the two-dimensional space of frequency 

                spectra and angular power spectra with a substantial increase 

                in sensitivity. However, the technique that has been used most 

                frequently on actual CMB data is the template fitting method.</p>



              <p>Five frequency bands with comparable sensitivity are necessary 

                to solve for the five possible signals: synchrotron, free-free, 

                dust, CMB anisotropy, and perhaps spinning dust. The range of 

                frequency coverage is more important than the specific choice 

                of frequencies within the range. The lowest frequency to survey 

                from space should be at the 22 GHz atmospheric water line since 

                frequencies below this can (with some difficulty) be accurately 

                measured from the ground. The highest frequency to survey should 

                be ~100 GHz to reduce the dust contribution and minimize the number 

                of competing foreground signals. The choice of frequencies between 

                22 and 100 GHz can be dictated by the practical consideration 

                of standard waveguide bands. Based on these considerations, MAP 

                will observe in the following five frequency bands, which are 

                indicated on the above plot: </p>
