    <td width="100%" valign="top" align="left" class="normal"
    background="../../../../images/Backgrounds/paper_back.gif"><h2>Talking X's and O's - <font
    color="#8080C0">Interactive Coach</font></h2>
    <h2 align="center"><font color="#FF0000">Coaches Communication Corner</font></h2>
    <div align="center"><center><table border="0" width="95%">
      <tr>
        <td><font FACE="Helvetica,Arial"><b><h2 ALIGN="CENTER"></b></font><font size="1">Jousting
          With the Press</font></h2>
          <p ALIGN="CENTER"><font size="1"><i>(A reporter’s job is to ask
          tough questions.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Learning
          how to anticipate&nbsp;<br>
          and respond in invaluable to maintaining control of your message)</i></font></p>
        <h3 ALIGN="CENTER"><font size="1">By Kathleen Hessert<br>
        President, Sports Media Challenge<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></h3>
          <p class="MsoBodyText"><font size="1">For every athletic director and
          coach, dealing with the media is a necessary part of a public
          relations strategy.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Whether
          you’re trying to garner support for a facility upgrade or defending
          a recent coaching hire, It’s important that you understand how your
          message will play in the local papers and on your constituents’
          television sets.<o:p>
          </o:p>
          </font></p>
          <p class="MsoNormal"><font size="1">Working with today’s mainstream
          and new electronic media also means you need to be prepared to answer
          some tough questions.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Media
          outlets are trying to beat their competitors for the most compelling
          story, thus reporters often ask difficult questions in attempt to
          uncover some drama.<o:p>
          </o:p>
          <o:p>
          </o:p>
          </font></p>
          <h2><font size="1" color="#FF8000">Preparation</font></h2>
          <p class="MsoBodyText"><font size="1">The best way to avoid being
          blind-sided by the media is to have a strategy in place before an
          interview.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Decide what
          your specific goals are and what message you want to communicate.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
          </span>Then, also anticipate some of the more difficult questions that
          could be thrown at you.</font></p>
          <p class="MsoNormal"><font size="1">The average audience walks away
          remembering one major point you’ve made.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
          </span>By deciding what you want that point to be and being able to
          convey it in a single concise sentence, you’ve positioned yourself
          to influence the direction of the interview.<o:p>
          </o:p>
          </font></p>
          <p class="MsoNormal"><font size="1">When a reporter (or anyone else)
          asks a difficult question, the idea is to respect the question, yet
          still steer the topic back toward your main point.<o:p>
          </o:p>
          </font></p>
          <p class="MsoNormal"><font size="1">The Key is to “bridge’ to your
          primary message and give a concise response.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>You can easily remember these steps as ABC.<o:p>
          </o:p>
          </font></p>
          <ol style="margin-top:0in" start="1" type="A">
            <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l0 level1 lfo1;tab-stops:list .5in"><font size="1"><b>Acknowledge</b>
              their emotional position and/or opinion.<o:p>
              </o:p>
              </font></li>
            <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l0 level1 lfo1;tab-stops:list .5in"><font size="1"><b>Bridge</b>
              to your point of view.<o:p>
              </o:p>
              </font></li>
            <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l0 level1 lfo1;tab-stops:list .5in"><font size="1"><b>Clearly</b>
              and concisely state your point of view<o:p>
              </o:p>
              .</font></li>
          </ol>
          <h3><font size="1" color="#FF8000">In The Trenches</font></h3>
          <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent"><font size="1">When in front of the
          media, inevitably you’ll be faced with a question that makes you
          want to head for the nearest door. The best thing to do is try to
          analyze the question. Quickly, think about why it’s a difficult
          question and how to make it a less difficult question.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
          </span>In most cases, questions are made difficult in two different
          ways: when the reporter puts a strict format on the question; and when
          the query triggers an emotional response.<o:p>
          </o:p>
          A question like, “Name two reasons why you choke in big games,”
          covers both cases.<o:p>
          </o:p>
          <o:p>
          </o:p>
          </font></p>
          <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent"><font size="1">When a reporter asks for
          “two reasons,” most people will search for and typically give two
          reasons because they were asked for two.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>In many cases, you feel compelled to find those “two
          reasons” and that becomes the focus of your answer.<o:p>
          </o:p>
          <o:p>
          </o:p>
          Being sidetracked by emotional words and phrases like “choke” can
          also throw off your game plan.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>It’s
          easy for your blood to boil at the idea of choking and to let yourself
          get caught up in that emotion.<o:p>
          </o:p>
          Maybe you don’t agree at all that you “choke” in big games, or
          maybe there are six or seven reasons you fall short of expectations!<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
          </span>If you take a moment to recite your ABC’s you can get back on
          track and provide the answer you really want the audience to hear.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
          </span>For example, you may chose to say:<o:p>
          </o:p>
          </font></p>
          <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent"></center><font size="1">A: “I realize
          the last minute losses we’ve had this season are disappointing for
          all our great fans, as they’ve been for the players and coaches, but
          what happened is very different than it looks.”<o:p>
          </o:p>
          </font></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent"><font size="1">B: “We’ve got a great
        group of young athletes.”<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent"><font size="1">C: “As this team matures
        you’ll see better decision making and better end results.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>This is a team worth watching.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>It’s only going to get better.”<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <h3 align="left"><font color="#FF8000" size="1">Other Hazards</font></h3>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent" align="left"><font size="1">Along with the
        formatted question and emotional triggers, there are some other classic
        types of questions that are really tough to answer.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>Here’s a look at how to handle them:</font></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left:.25in" align="left"><font size="1"><i>Hypothetical
        questions:</i> “If this were to happen, then what would you do?”<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent" align="left"><font size="1">Unless you know
        exactly what you’d do in that situation, don’t answer hypothetical
        questions.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>There are at least three alternatives:</font></p>
        <ol style="margin-top:0in" start="1" type="1">
          <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l1 level1 lfo2;tab-stops:list .5in">
            <p align="left"><font size="1">If you think you know what the
            reporter really wants and you can speak from experience, do.<span style="mso-spacerun:
     yes">&nbsp; </span>For instance, if the general question is too broad, you
            can respond with a narrow, more specific answer.<span style="mso-spacerun:
     yes">&nbsp; </span>If the specificity is the problem, you can respond in a
            more general manner, whichever is most to your benefit.<o:p>
            </o:p>
            </font></li>
          <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l1 level1 lfo2;tab-stops:list .5in">
            <p align="left"><font size="1">You can refute the assumption made,
            e.g., “We’re committed to making certain that doesn’t
            happen.”<o:p>
            </o:p>
            </font></li>
          <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l1 level1 lfo2;tab-stops:list .5in">
            <p align="left"><font size="1">You can simply say, “It’s
            counterproductive to speculate here.”<o:p>
            </o:p>
            </font></li>
        </ol>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left:.25in" align="left"><font size="1">&nbsp;<o:p>
        </o:p>
        <i>Yes or no questions:</i> “Do you think this was fair: yes or no/”<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent" align="left"><font size="1">Yes or no
        questions aren’t easy either, but don’t let them corner you.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>There’s typically wiggle room in every issue.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>Point out the gray areas and explain your answer with something
        like, “It was a tough decision to make and much care was given to
        their concerns.’<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left:.25in" align="left"><i><font size="1">A
        or B questions: </font></i><font size="1">“What’s more important to
        you: A or B?”<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent" align="left"><font size="1">Don’t be
        forced into choosing an A or B answer.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>You can usually say, “It’s not a matter of either-or.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>We’ve institutionalized those standards so we’re able to
        refocus our efforts to include…”</font></p>
        <h3 align="left"><font color="#FF8000" size="1">More Tips</font></h3>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent" align="left"><font size="1">Answering any
        question from the media entails being comfortable with talking to
        reporters.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Consider the
        following tips when faced with challenging questions.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent" align="left"><font size="1"><u>Be yourself</u>:<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>All too often, people try to be something they’re not when
        a microphone is put in front of them.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>This strategy might be successful initially, but it generally
        catches up to them.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The
        most important thing to remember is to be yourself.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent" align="left"><font size="1">When you use
        one of your formulated answers, do it in a way that doesn’t sound
        canned.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Having a good
        answer to a difficult question and coming across as genuine builds
        confidence and helps you relax.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent" align="left"><font size="1"><u>Twenty-second
        rule: </u>Watch one newscast or sports report and you’ll realize how
        important sound bites are.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Those
        short clips shape public perception.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>To avoid a damaging sound bit, you’ve got to make your point
        clearly and concisely-in 20 seconds or less.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>Only elaborate if the questioner has time, interest, or need.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent" align="left"><font size="1"><u>Be precise: </u>When
        you’re dealing with the media, leave nothing to their imagination.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>If you’re not specific, they may misunderstand and use your
        comments to prove something you didn’t mean.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>Avoid generalities and use specific examples that clarify and
        make people care about your point of view.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent" align="left"><font size="1"><u>Don’t be
        baited: </u>If you’ve ever seen Mike Tyson interviewed; you understand
        what it looks like when someone is baited by a reporter.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>No matter what the question is, remain calm.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>If you allow the questioner to get to you, you’ll only make
        things worse by becoming confrontational.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>When you’re defensive, you’ll typically abandon your strategy
        and say things you may regret later.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent" align="left"><font size="1">Breathe deeply,
        stay clam, and return to your strategy.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent" align="left"><font size="1"><u>Don’t use
        jargon: </u>One mistake commonly made by interviewees is unnecessarily
        using jargon.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The broader
        audience, as opposed to hardcore fans, probably won’t understand your
        buzzwords, acronyms, and language relating to your sports, and it’s
        certainly not as clear and colorful.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>The media writes on a sixth-grade level to ensure that the
        general public understands.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Use
        words that allow you to show you know what you’re talking about, while
        appealing to the masses.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </font></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent" align="left"><font size="1">Walter Cronkite
        once said, “There are no bad questions, only bad answers!”<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>However, some tough questions aren’t always as easy to spot.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>Many times questions may seem harmless.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
        </span>It’s the answers to those questions that could potentially do
        damage.<o:p>
