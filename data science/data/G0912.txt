<p><a href="/docs/science/nature.html"><span class="subheader3">What's the nature of space-time?</span></a>  These missions aim to find out by probing the extremes of gravity and searching for new physics:</p>

<blockquote>
<p><a href="http://heasarc.gsfc.nasa.gov/docs/xte/xte_1st.html">RXTE</a> and <a href="http://xmm.vilspa.esa.es/">XMM</a> probe the region around black holes and neutron stars, finding that the fabric of  space twists and moves around these objects. In 2006 <a href="http://glast.gsfc.nasa.gov/">GLAST</a> will determine how black holes, notorious for pulling matter in, can accelerate matter away in narrow jets at near light speed. The <a href="http://swift.gsfc.nasa.gov/">Swift</a> mission in 2003 will observe in detail Gamma ray bursts, which are thought to mark the creation of black holes. At the end of this decade the <a href="http://constellation.gsfc.nasa.gov/">Constellation-X</a> and <a href="http://lisa.jpl.nasa.gov/">LISA</a> mission will take the next steps forward. Constellation-X will examine the region close to black hole event horizon with much greater detail to observe the effects of Einstein's General Relativity and also observe the evolution of black holes with cosmic time. An important precursor mission to Constellation-X will be <a href="http://heasarc.gsfc.nasa.gov/docs/astroe/astroegof.html">Astro-E2</a>, which will provide detailed X-ray spectroscopy of the universe. A major breakthrough mission will be LISA which will search for gravity waves expected from the collision of black holes in the center of galaxies, the signature of which might be used to directly test Einstein's theory of general relativity.</p>

<p>In the longer term, <a href="http://exist.gsfc.nasa.gov/">EXIST</a> is a mission being discussed to survey the sky for heavily absorbed black holes. Another mission being considered is <a href="http://generation.gsfc.nasa.gov/">Generation-X</a>, which will be a large X-ray telescope (20 times larger than Constellation-X with the imaging power of <a href="http://chandra.harvard.edu/">Chandra</a>) designed to image the first black holes and galaxies as they appear in the early Universe. The <a href="http://arise.jpl.nasa.gov/">ARISE</a> radio mission is being considered as a radio telescope in space to zoom in on black holes. A far infra-red mission SPECS will measure the total energy output of back holes. Ultimately, <a href="http://maxim.gsfc.nasa.gov/">MAXIM</a> will image a black hole event horizon, the ultimate proof of objects of infinite density that warp space-time and directly measure the effects of general relativity predicted by Einstein.</p>
</blockquote>

<p><a href="/docs/science/bigbang.html"><span class="subheader3">What powered the big bang?</span></a>  These missions aim to find out by journeying back in time to the moment the Universe was set in motion:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>The recently launched <a href="http://map.gsfc.nasa.gov/">MAP</a> mission has begun to collect the first light in the Universe, a reflection of the physics and geometry of the Universe. This will be the premier measurements of the parameters of the universe. The <a href="http://astro.estec.esa.nl/SA-general/Projects/Planck/">Planck</a> mission, a joint mission with ESA, is a detailed follow-up to MAP planned for later this decade.</p>

<p>In the longer term a possible future mission currently being discussed will search for polarization of the light that MAP and Planck see, which reflects the gravitational waves emitted by inflation at 10<sup>-32</sup> second after the big bang. Another possible future mission is <a href="http://lheawww.gsfc.nasa.gov/docs/gamcosray/hecr/owl_new.html">OWL</a>, which will search for the highest-energy cosmic rays, which may  reflect the physics of the Universe at 10<sup>-35</sup> second. These missions will be eyewitnesses to the initial expansion of the Universe.</p>
</blockquote>

<p><a href="/docs/science/universe.html"><span class="subheader3">What's the Universe made of?</span></a> These missions aim to find out by taking inventory of all the Universe's exotic contents, seen and unseen:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>MAP and Planck will measure the ratio of ordinary matter to dark matter and dark energy in an era before stars formed. Chandra is now mapping regions of dark matter in the "modern" universe. Constellation-X will continue this quest by searching for the missing 90% of  ordinary matter (from which we are made) which is thought to be trapped as hot plasma in a cosmic web of dark matter, study the formation of the elements in supernovae and star burst galaxies, and observe the formation of the first clusters of galaxies (the largest bound objects in the Universe). GLAST will search for WIMPs and probe the content and density of various regions of the Universe through the epochs. HSI and ultimately ACT will search for the radioactive decay of supernovae in distant galaxies.</p>
</blockquote>
