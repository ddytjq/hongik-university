<TD><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1"><B>Ethical Investment</B></FONT></TD>
<TD ALIGN=RIGHT><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1">7/11/2000</FONT></TD></TR>
<TR><TD COLSPAN=2><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1">Investment With a Conscience<BR>&nbsp;</FONT></TD></TR>
<TR><TD COLSPAN=2><FONT FACE="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" SIZE="-1">      <p> New regulations came into force on 3 July 2000, governing how most occupational 
        pension schemes offered by employers deal with the ethical, social and 
        environmental issues associated with investment. This is known as socially 
        responsible investment (SRI).</p>
      <p> Pension scheme trustees are now obliged to make a statement on their 
        approach to SRI and how they put their SRI policy into force. All this 
        falls a very long way short of being a requirement that pension funds 
        adopt an ethical investment strategy, but it does indicate a change in 
        attitudes to investment.</p>
      <p> Ethical and environmental funds have become increasingly popular among 
        private investors. According to the Ethical Investment Research Service 
        (EIRIS) (April 2000), £2.6 billion was invested in ethical unit trusts 
        and life company funds by January 2000 - an increase of nearly 50% 
        from the level of two years earlier and seven times the level in 1992.</p>
      <p> In the early days, ethical funds were often structured on purely negative 
        criteria, such as not investing in companies connected with weapons, alcohol, 
        tobacco or gambling.</p>
      <p> Nowadays a more sophisticated approach is generally used, with negative 
        de-selection counter-balanced by a positive selection of companies. For 
        instance, managers may seek out companies refusing to use animal testing 
        for their products or involved in environmental improvements.</p>
      <p> Some funds also apply a best-in-class process, supporting, for example, 
        the oil company with the most environmentally-friendly policies rather 
        than automatically excluding all oil companies.</p>
      <p> While your choice of funds may be on ethical grounds, there are also 
        sound financial reasons for selecting funds that include social responsibility 
        in their selection criteria:</p>
      <ul>
        <li> 
          <p> The greater the role for SRI, the more likely it is that companies 
            which fail the criteria could under-perform, if only because there 
            is less demand for their shares.</p>
        </li>
        <li> 
          <p> The avoidance of some areas that have long been on the ethical blacklist 
            would have benefited investors. In recent years the most obvious example 
            has been the abysmal performance of the major tobacco companies. </p>
        </li>
        <li>
          <p> Companies whose managements ignore their social responsibilities 
            are potentially more likely to suffer negligence claims and fines 
            for regulatory breaches.</p>
        </li>
        <li> 
          <p> Companies perceived to have unacceptable policies face an increased 
            threat of consumer boycotts. According to research undertaken by PriceWaterhouseCoopers,* 
            in 1999 four out of ten global consumers boycotted a company they 
            thought was unethical. The power of the boycott has already forced 
            some companies into embarrassing changes of tack. A high profile example 
            of this was Shell's about-turn on the disposal of the Brent Spar oil 
            platform.</p>
        </li>
      </ul>
      <p> <i><font size="1">* Source: FT Money 8/9 July 2000</font></i> </p><BR>&nbsp;</FONT></TD></TR>
