		  <p class="contentsubhead">Other Costs</p>
		  <p>There are other costs involved in buying a home. Some are fixed and beyond your control eg. Stamp Duty, others you cannot avoid however you can negotiate eg. Solicitor's Fees. The main expenses you should bear in mind are outlined below.</p>
		  <p><strong>Arrangement Fee</strong><br>
		  The Arrangement Fee covers the administrative expenses involved in setting up your Home Loan.</p>
		  <p><strong>Mortgage Guarantee Insurance</strong><br>
		  If the amount you wish to borrow is greater than 90% of the cost or valuation of the property, whichever is lower, we will need extra security in the form of a separate guarantee from an insurance company, that the Home Loan will be repaid in event of a shortfall on sale. This insurance is effected on payment of a "one-off" premium and a separate loan can be arranged to assist you in paying this premium, if required.</p>
		  <p><strong>Valuation Fee</strong><br>
		  We strongly recommend that you have a thorough survey carried out on the property you are going to buy, as this will tell you about its condition and highlight any specific problems. Valuations are required by the Bank to make sure that the property is worth at least as much as you are borrowing. They are also essential for you, as you want to make sure that the property you are going to buy is structurally sound and in a good state of repair.</p>
		  <p>Three types of Valuations are possible:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Valuation for Mortgage Purposes</strong><br>
This is based on a limited visual inspection of as much of the exterior and interior of the property as is accessible without undue difficulty. This type of report is prepared so that the Bank can be certain that you have chosen a suitable property for a Home Loan. It will contain information about the property which you may find useful, so a copy will be provided</li>
<li><strong>Report on Condition and Valuation</strong><br>
This report gives you general information on the state of repair and condition of the property you want to buy and gives you an estimate of its value in the property market. It covers all parts of the property which are easily accessible and provides a concise report concentrating on the major defects, along with any further investigations that the Surveyor thinks are appropriate. It will not include areas which are not easily accessible eg. floors covered by fitted carpets or parts of the roof which cannot be seen.</li>
<li><strong>Structural Survey</strong><br>
This is the most thorough type of survey and is a must if you are concerned about the condition of an older property. You will receive a complete description of the property's structure, a list of all major and minor defects and a guide to how much essential repairs will cost. This survey may take a few days longer to complete and costs will depend on the size and age of the property.</li>
</ol>
<p>The more thorough the survey the higher the surveyors fee will be. The cost of a Valuation for Mortgage Purposes and a Report on Condition and Valuation, usually depend on the value of the property. The cost of structural survey depends on the work involved. Although a thorough survey may be more expensive, it could be worthwhile if you are buying an older property - if the surveyor recommends costly repairs you could negotiate to bring the price of the property down according to the work required</p>
<p><strong>Legal Fees</strong><br>
You will need a Solicitor to help you through the legal aspects of buying a house. If you were happy with the way your firm of Solicitors handled the Conveyancing when you bought your present home, you will probably want to use them for the sale of this property and the purchase of your new one. Fees can vary, so it may be worth while to get two or three quotations from different firms.</p>
<p>The Solicitor will also add to their bill, obligatory expenses known as "disbursements" incurred on your behalf, such as fees for Land Registry and Local Authority searches. Solicitor's fees are generally fixed in advance and they vary depending on the value of your property and the Home Loan you require. It is usual for the Solicitor to submit their bill for payment, prior to you moving into your home.</p>
<p>Your legal representative is responsible for checking all the legal documents such as the deeds or the lease of your new home, to ensure that there are no irregularities. They will also be responsible for checking with the relevant local authority and the seller's Solicitor to make sure that there are no development plans that would adversely affect the value of your property in the future. If there are, you will have an opportunity to ask for a reduction in the price or to withdraw from the deal.</p>
<p>It is also the job of your legal representative to ensure that any conditions attached to the mortgage offer are satisfied.</p>
<p><strong>Registering your Deeds</strong><br>
The deeds to your property need to be registered, to show that you are the new owner of the property. Two types of registration are possible, depending on the area your new property is in:</p>
<ol>
<li>Registry of Deeds - the "deed" confirming you as the new owner of the property is registered with the "Registry of Deeds"</li>
<li>Land Registry - your name is added to the "folio" which traces the history of the parcel of land your property is built on, with the "Land Registry"</li>
</ol>
<p>The cost of registering your deeds will vary depending on which Register is in force. Your Solicitor will be able to advise you which Register applies to your property and the cost involved with registering your deeds.</p>
<p><strong>Stamp Duty</strong><br>
Stamp Duty is a Government Tax which has to be paid whenever you buy a property for more than £60,000. The amount payable is a percentage of the total purchase price which varies according to the amount of the purchase price.</p>
<ul>
<li>&#163;60,000-&#163;250,000&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;1%</li>
<li>&#163;250,000-&#163;500,000&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;3%</li>
<li>Over &#163;500,000&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;4%</li>
</ul>
<p>For example on a £70,000 property, the Stamp Duty payable would be £700. Stamp Duty is usually itemised with your Solicitor's bill.</p>
<p><strong>Buildings & Contents Insurance</strong><br>
When you buy your home with a Northern Home Loan, you will need to take out a Buildings Insurance Policy. At the same time we recommend that you take out a policy which insures your contents. Choosing the right policy for household protection can often be a confusing and difficult decision. Causeway Home Care is a simple innovative way to arrange your home insurance, giving peace of mind at a competitive premium. If an insured risk causes damage to your property, the policy will cover the cost of repair at current market rates. Causeway Home Care offers you a simple way to arrange your Home and Contents Insurance and protect one of the most valuable assets you will ever purchase.</p>
<p><strong>Payment Protection</strong><br>
Once you have purchased your new home, it is important that you consider protecting your repayments.</p>
<p>More and more borrowers are coming to realise that they need some form of security in what, for many, has become an uncertain world. Sudden unemployment is, unfortunately, an occupational hazard. Accidents can and do happen, illness can strike at any time, and without warning. So how would your repayment be made?</p>
<p>By taking out Payment Protection, you can have the peace of mind which comes from the knowledge that if you lose your income, your Home Loan repayments will still be met (after a deferred period and for a maximum of 12 months).</p>
<p><strong>Other Expenses</strong><br>
In addition to all the above expenses, you will also need to take into account the costs of moving, which can include the following :</p>
<ol>
<li>Moving Costs - it may be worth obtaining quotes from specialist removal companies or you may prefer to hire a self drive van</li>
<li>Connection Costs - if applicable for telephone, electricity and water supplies and gas,</li>
<li>Furniture, fixtures and fittings - eg. did the house come complete with light fittings, curtains and carpets.</li>
<li>Decorating Costs</li>
</ol>
