<H1>Bt Corn and the Monarch Butterfly</H1>
<P><B>Council for Biotechnology Information,  March 4, 2001</B></P>

<P>The monarch butterfly has become the subject of significant scientific and 
media interest regarding the impacts of biotech crops on non-target organisms. 
While some biotech crops are capable of combating certain insects, concern has 
been raised that non-target insects such as monarch butterfly larvae might also 
be affected. </P>
<P>While one early laboratory study showed that monarch larvae could be harmed 
if they ate large amounts of Bt corn pollen, subsequent lab and field studies 
have concluded that the larvae are not likely to encounter harmful 
concentrations of the pollen in their natural environment.</P><B>
<P>Bt corn: Natural protection against pests</P></B>
<P>"Bt corn" refers to corn that has been enhanced through biotechnology to 
produce its own insecticidal protein from <I>Bacillus thuringiensis</I> (Bt), a 
common and naturally occurring soil bacterium that works as a pesticide. Bt 
protects corn from the European corn borer, which can have devastating and 
irreversible effects on corn crops. The Bt protein targets only specific insect 
pests. As a result, it is not harmful to agriculturally beneficial insects 
(MacIntosh et al., 1990; Orr and Landis, 1997; Pilcher et al., 1997), animals or 
humans (McClintock et al., 1995; EPA 1998), and does not persist in the 
environment (Palm et al., 1994; Sims and Holden, 1996). </P><B>
<P>Bt corn pollen and monarch butterfly larvae</P></B>
<P>In May 1999, a communication in the journal <I>Nature</I> (Losey et al., 
1999) highlighted what scientists and regulators working with Bt corn already 
understood: namely, that a Bt protein aimed at specific crop pests at certain 
doses is toxic to some caterpillars. In laboratory 
feeding experiments, Cornell University researchers found that monarch butterfly 
larvae that were fed milkweed leaves coated with high levels of pollen from Bt 
corn ate less, grew slower and suffered a higher death rate than larvae that 
consumed milkweed leaves with pollen from non-Bt corn or with no pollen at all. 
The researchers suggested that this hazard, if demonstrated in the natural 
environment, could pose a risk to monarch butterflies that migrate through the 
central U.S., where the majority of corn is grown.</P><B>
<P>EPA - "No unreasonable adverse effects"</P></B>
<P>In the U.S., Bt corn and other insect protected crops derived through 
biotechnology are regulated by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) to 
make sure their use causes no unreasonable adverse effects on the environment, 
non-target insects and other life. Before approving Bt corn in 1995, 1996, and 
1998, the EPA concluded that the corn does not cause any "unreasonable adverse 
effects" on non-target organisms, including butterflies (EPA, 1995; EPA, 1996; 
EPA 1998). To reach this conclusion, the EPA evaluated both toxicity (or 
"hazard" with potential to cause harm) and exposure (the condition of being 
subjected to the hazard). Toxicity and exposure must be combined to determine 
the risk of potential adverse effect. </P>
<P></P>
<P>The EPA determined that exposure of non-target organisms, such as monarch 
larvae, to Bt pollen would be minimal. This conclusion was based on the fact 
that:</P>
<UL>
<LI>The majority of pollen moves only a short distance away from cornfields; 
<LI>Corn fields typically contain a low concentration of weeds, including 
milkweeds; and, 
<LI>The exposure of monarchs would be limited only to larvae developing on 
milkweeds within the cornfield or very near to cornfields during pollen shed. 
</LI></UL>
<P>Since only a portion of the milkweed population is likely to be exposed to Bt 
pollen, and only a portion of those plants would be expected to harbor monarch 
larvae during pollen shed, the EPA scientists concluded that Bt corn does not 
present any risk of "unreasonable adverse effects" to butterflies. </P><B>
<P>Field studies confirm EPA assessment</P></B>
<P>In 1999, researchers in the U.S. and Canada conducted numerous field tests to 
further investigate the potential effects of Bt corn on monarch butterflies. 
Scientists from nine universities, Agricultural Extension Office and the U.S. 
Department of Agriculture presented their findings at two Monarch Butterfly 
Research Symposia in 1999. Additional results also were reported at a Monarch 
Butterfly Workshop sponsored by the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA), 
Agricultural Research Service in 2000.</P><I></I>
<P>The studies show that monarch exposure to pollen under field conditions is 
very low (Sears et al., 2000a; Wraight et al., 2000). Because corn pollen grains 
are relatively heavy, most of the pollen grains produced by a corn plant settle 
in the immediate vicinity of the field. Although wind can move pollen away from 
the field, the pollen carried away is deposited over a large area so that the 
concentration of pollen on surfaces is low.<I> </I>Dr. Galen Dively, University 
of Maryland, said: "The pollen drops off very rapidly beyond the edge of the 
corn field…within the first three meters it drops 90 percent…beyond that it 
drops 99.7 percent." Because pollen movement is limited, the potential for 
monarch exposure to Bt pollen also is limited to larvae that develop within or 
very near to Bt corn. </P>
<P>Field studies also demonstrate that pollen does not adhere easily to leaf 
surfaces and that wind and rainfall dislodge pollen grains, causing them to fall 
to the ground. Further, the Bt protein in pollen grains adhering to milkweed 
leaves is degraded by sunlight. Dr. Richard Hellmich and Dr. John Pleasants, 
Iowa State University, found that milkweed leaves captured only 30 percent of 
the pollen available and that rain can reduce this amount by an additional 90 
percent (Dively et al., 2000). Dr. John Foster, University of Nebraska, also 
reported that rain and morning dew washed pollen off of milkweed leaves in his 
studies. Dr. Graham Head, Monsanto Company, showed that pollen exposed to 
sunlight for 8 days had very little activity on monarch larvae, presumably due 
to degradation of Bt by the sun’s ultraviolet rays (Head and Brown, 1999).</P>
<P>Monarch larvae developing within or very close to Bt cornfields during pollen 
shed may encounter small quantities of Bt pollen grains on the surface of 
milkweed leaves. However, field studies demonstrate that the amount of Bt pollen 
deposited on milkweed leaves within cornfields or a short distance away 
typically is below the threshold amount that, if eaten, could harm monarch 
larvae. Researchers have found that small monarch larvae can safely consume 
milkweed leaves with up to 1100 Bt pollen grains per square centimeter deposited 
on the leaf surface (Hellmich et al., 2000). This level of pollen deposition was 
rarely encountered in field situations. A survey of 81 fields in Maryland showed 
the mean pollen level inside of corn fields or at the edge was 56 pollen grains 
and only 10 leaves out of 127 leaves examined had higher levels (Dively et al., 
2000). Furthermore, several researchers found that monarch larvae avoided 
feeding on Bt or non-Bt pollen if milkweeds free from pollen were readily 
available (Hellmich et al., 2000).</P><B>
<P>Recent studies show monarch larvae survive during pollen shed</P></B>
<P>Results from studies conducted in 2000 found that monarch larvae survived and 
developed normally on milkweed plants within Bt cornfields during the period of 
pollen shed. In a study conducted in Canada, Dr. Mark Sears and co-workers at 
the University of Guelph found monarch survival, weight gain and milkweed 
consumption were similar for larvae feeding for five days on milkweed plants in 
Bt corn and non-Bt cornfields during pollen shed (Sears et al., 2000b). A field 
study in Iowa produced the same result: survival and development of monarch 
larvae on milkweed plants inside a Bt cornfield were not impacted by Bt pollen 
during the peak period of pollen shed (Hellmich et al., 2000b). These field 
studies confirm that the level of Bt pollen adhering to milkweed leaves within 
or close to Bt corn is typically too low to impact the normal development of 
monarch larvae. The research studies conducted in 2000 were sponsored by the 
USDA and the Agricultural Biotechnology Stewardship Technical Committee.</P>
<P>The results reported at four scientific gatherings confirm the EPA’s 
determination that pollen from Bt corn plants poses little or no adverse effects 
for monarch butterflies. Exposure levels of monarch butterflies to Bt corn 
pollen under field conditions is usually well below the level which would cause 
harm, and the Bt pollen hazard to monarch butterflies in the laboratory does not 
translate into risk in nature. </P>
