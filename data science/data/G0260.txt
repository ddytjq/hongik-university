<strong><center><font color=navy size=4>Comments on Tables 2 and 5A</font></center></strong>
</strong>
<pre>
</pre>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>In Table 2 the quasars have settled down to a standard deviation that's usually pretty close to one.   Notice that when there are several quasars in the upper blue area, then <font face=symbol>s</font> is somewhat greater than one.   This is also the first table where I worked up the 2QZ quasars (Table 5).   Notice that the magnitudes of the 2QZ quasars are, on average, about 0.5 to 0.7 magnitudes less than those shown in the Veron 10th (whose average includes the 2QZ quasars).   Without the 2QZ quasars, the absolute magnitudes would be about an absolute  magnitude higher.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Something else worth noting<strong>:</strong> there seems to be a dramatic falloff in the number counts about two magnitudes below the average.   The unmistakeable drop in quasar counts between Mabs -23.5 and -23.0 in the extreme right column is replicated several times at progressively higher magnitudes as we move to higher red shift tables.   There is a statistical reason why that happens; it has to do with a very rigorous selection of quasars in the 2QZ survey which is explained <A HREF="QSOTable5.html">in Table 5</A>.
<p>
<IMG SRC=pixelshim.gif width=36 height=7 ALIGN=left>Last, be sure to look at the number counts at the bottom and notice that the 2QZ survey has more than doubled the number of quasars listed in the 9th edition of the Verons' quasar catalogue on  the right half of the table.   Also notice in Table 2A that the 2QZ standard deviation <font face=symbol>s</font> is about 0.6 (compared to about 1.0 for quasars in the Veron 10th) and there's a reason for that too, which is explained <a href="QSOApp4sigma.html#2QZ">in QSO Appendix 4</A>.
