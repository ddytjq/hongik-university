										<h3><a href="../m_uni.html"><span class="hdline3">Foundations of the Big Bang theory</span></a></h3>

										<dl>

											<dt><a href="uni_101bb1.html">Theoretical pillars</a>

											<dd>General Relativity and the &#147;Cosmological Principle&#148; give rise to the Big Bang theory.

											<dt><a href="uni_101bb2.html">Concepts of the Big Bang theory</a>

											<dd>The Big Bang theory makes definite predictions for the structure and evolution of the universe that depend on the nature and amount of matter in the universe.

										</dl>

										<a href="uni_101bbtest.html"><span class="hdline3">Observational tests of the Big Bang theory</span></a>

										<dl>

											<dt><a href="uni_101bbtest1.html">Expansion of the universe</a>

											<dd>Edwin Hubble's 1929 observation that galaxies were generally receding from us provided the first clue that the Big Bang theory might be right.

											<dt><a href="uni_101bbtest2.html">Abundance of the light elements H, He, Li</a>

											<dd>The Big Bang theory predicts that these light elements should have been fused from protons and neutrons in the first few minutes after the Big Bang.

											<dt><a href="uni_101bbtest3.html">The cosmic microwave background (CMB) radiation</a>

											<dd>The early universe should have been very hot. The cosmic microwave background radiation is the remnant heat leftover from the Big Bang.

										</dl>

										<h3><a href="uni_101bblimit.html"><span class="hdline3">Limitations and extensions of the Big Bang theory</span></a></h3>

										<dl>

											<dt><a href="uni_101structures.html">Structure in the universe</a>

											<dd>The Big Bang theory makes no attempt to explain how structures like stars and galaxies came to exist in the universe.

											<dt><a href="uni_101Flucts.html">Fluctuations in the cosmic microwave background (CMB) radiation</a>

											<dd>The temperature of the CMB is observed to vary slightly across the sky. What produced these fluctuations and how do they relate to stars and galaxies?

											<dt><a href="uni_101tuning.html">The &#147;fine tuning&#148; of the universe</a>

											<dd>The universe we live in appears to be very &#147;unlikely&#148; in the context of the standard Big Bang theory. Is there some mechanism that forces it to be that way?

											<dt><a href="uni_101inflation.html">The inflationary universe</a>

											<dd>A very short, but especially rapid burst of growth in the <i>very</i> early universe (&#147;inflation&#148;) provides an elegant, yet untested, explanation of the above puzzles.

										</dl>

										<h3><a href="uni_101ouruni.html"><span class="hdline3">Our universe</span></a></h3>

										<dl>

											<dt><a href="uni_101matter.html">Matter in the universe</a>

											<dd>The amount and type of matter in the universe is fundamental in determining the properties of our universe. How much matter is there?
