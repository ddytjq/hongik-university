<p> <font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">In the last few 
  decades, the planets and moons with which we share our cosmic neighborhood have 
  stopped being mere points of light in the sky and have been revealed as worlds 
  that we (or at least our robot representatives) can explore up close and in 
  detail. Images and data from the surface of Mars or from orbit around the moons 
  of Jupiter have profoundly transformed our understanding of the solar system. 
  As a result, the number of books and articles being written about the exploration 
  of the solar system has become - well - astronomical. Below, we list a very 
  small selection of our favorite solar system readings and web sites to get armchair 
  explorers, teachers, and students started.</font></p>

<p> <font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2"><a name="1"><font color="#6666CC"><b>General 
  Books on the Solar System</b></font></a> </font></p>
<p> </p>
<blockquote> 
  <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2"> Beatty, J. Kelly, 
    et al., eds. <b>The New Solar System</b>, 4th ed. 1999, Sky Publishing/Cambridge 
    U.Press. The definitive introduction to our current knowledge, with articles 
    by top planetary scientists. Can be a bit technical in places. <br>
    </font></p>
  <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">Fraknoi, Andrew; 
    Morrison, David; and Wolff, Sidney. <b>Voyages To the Planets</b>. 2000, Saunders 
    College Publishing/ Harcourt Brace. A non-technical introductory textbook, 
    with analogies and background material, about our solar system and planets 
    around other stars. <br>
    </font></p>
  <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">Kluger, Jeffrey 
    <b>Journey Beyond Selene</b>. 1999, Simon & Schuster. A journalist chronicles 
    several lunar and planetary exploration missions, including Galileo. <br>
    </font></p>
  <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">Lewis, John <b>Worlds 
    Without End: The Exploration of Planets Known and Unknown</b>. 1998, Perseus. 
    A noted astronomer discusses how planets form and how the current appearance 
    of the solar system (and other planetary systems) reflects their origins. 
    <br>
    </font></p>
  <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">McNab, David 
    & Younger, James<b> The Planets</b>. 1999, Yale U. Press. Overview of the 
    solar system; accompanies a TV program. <br>
    </font></p>
  <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">Miller, Ron and 
    Hartmann, William <b>The Grand Tour: A Traveler' s Guide to the Solar System</b>, 
    2nd ed. 1993, Workman. Lavishly illustrated beginners' primer. <br>
    </font></p>
  <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">Morrison, David 
    <b>Exploring Planetary Worlds</b>. 1993, Scientific American Library/W. H. 
    Freeman. Clear, up-to-date, non-technical survey. <br>
    </font></p>
  <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">Sagan, Carl <b>Pale 
    Blue Dot</b>. 1994, Random House. An eloquent and impressive introduction 
    to planetary exploration. <br>
    </font></p>
  <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">Taylor, Stuart 
    <b>Destiny or Chance: Our Solar System and Its Place in the Cosmos</b>. 1998, 
    Cambridge U. Press. A modern overview of how our system formed. </font></p>
</blockquote>
