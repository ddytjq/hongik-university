<p class="articlesubheadline">How biotechnology in a
seed can enhance global food security.</p>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<br>

<p>The world’s population more than
doubled in the last half century and topped 6 billion in 1999.<sup>1</sup>
Each year, it is adding about 73 million people &mdash; a population nearly the size
of Vietnam’s. By 2030, it is projected to reach around 8 billion, and nearly
all of that increase is expected to occur in developing countries<sup>2</sup>,
which are also expected to see higher incomes and rapid urbanization. </p>

<p>At the same time, the world’s
hungry and chronically malnourished remain at about 840 million people, despite
global pledges and national efforts to improve food security.</p>

<p>These trends mean the world will
have to double its food production and also improve food distribution over the
next quarter century.<sup>3</sup>
These pose staggering challenges for the world’s farmers:  Much of the world’s land suitable for
farming is already cultivated and natural resources are under pressure. Soil
degradation is widespread, agriculture has already razed 20 to 30 percent of
the world’s forest areas<sup>4</sup>
and water tables in many areas are falling. Agriculture consumes about 70
percent of the fresh water people use every year and, at the current
consumption rate, two out of three people will live in water-stressed
conditions by 2025. <sup>5</sup> </p>

<p>By 2050, some 4.2 billion people
may not have their daily basic needs met.<sup>6</sup>
</p>

<p>These projections and complex
challenges facing the world’s future food supply are prompting international
food and agricultural experts and policymakers &mdash; including the U.N. Food and
Agriculture Organization and the World Health Organization &mdash; to call plant
biotechnology a critical tool to help feed a growing population in the 21<sup>st</sup>
century. </p>

<p>Governments need to develop
policies to ensure greater investment in research and regulatory oversight
that’s needed to manage the health, environmental and socioeconomic issues
associated with biotechnology, according to the Human Development Report 2001,
an annual report commissioned by the U.N. Development Programme.<sup>7</sup></p>

<p class="articlepullquote">Biotechnology: An eco-efficient option</p>

<p>World crop productivity could
increase by as much as 25 percent<sup>8</sup>
through the use of biotechnology to grow plants that resist pests and diseases,
tolerate harsh growing conditions and delay ripening to reduce spoilage,
according to the Consultative Group on International Agricultural Research
(CGIAR). All this could be achieved on existing farmland and customized to meet
local needs. </p>

<p>Biotechnology also offers the
possibility for scientists to design “farming systems that are responsive to
local needs and reflect sustainability requirements,” said Calestous Juma,
director of the Science, Technology and Innovation Program at the Center for
International Development and senior research associate at the Belfer Center
for Science and International Affairs, both at Harvard University.<sup>9</sup></p>

<p>Scientists are developing crops
that resist diseases, pests, viruses, bacteria and fungi, all of which reduce
global production by more than 35 percent at a cost estimated at more than $200
billion a year.<sup>10</sup> For
instance, test fields in Kenya are growing <a href="http://allafrica.com/stories/200008210136.html" onclick="javascript:window.open('index.asp?redirect=http%3A%2F%2Fallafrica%2Ecom%2Fstories%2F200008210136%2Ehtml','','').focus();return false;">sweet potato<ximg src="html/images/spacer.gif" border="0" width="4"><ximg src="html/images/ealink.gif" border="0" width="10"></a>
varieties that are resistant to a complex set of viruses that can wipe out
three-fourths of Kenyan farmers’ harvest. </p>

<p>In the United States, crops with
built-in insect protection and that tolerate a specific herbicide have helped
farmers improve yields and reduce costs. In 2000, direct benefit to growers of
insect resistant corn, cotton and potatoes exceeded $300 million, according to
the <a href="http://www.epa.gov/pesticides/biopesticides/otherdocs/bt_brad2/1%20overview.pdf" onclick="javascript:window.open('index.asp?redirect=http%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Eepa%2Egov%2Fpesticides%2Fbiopesticides%2Fotherdocs%2Fbt%5Fbrad2%2F1%2520overview%2Epdf','','').focus();return false;">Environmental
Protection Agency<img src="html/images/spacer.gif" border="0" width="4"><img src="html/images/pdf.gif" border="0" width="20"></a>. <sup>11</sup> </p>

<p>In a study to be released in
2002, the National Center for Food and Agricultural Policy quantified
biotechnology’s benefits for U.S. farmers through 44 case studies that covered
30 different crops, including papaya, citrus, soybeans and tomatoes. For
instance, it found that herbicide tolerant soybeans helped farmers reduce their
annual production costs by $15 an acre, which totals $735 million across 49 million
acres. Virus-resistant papaya is credited with saving Hawaii’s papaya industry,
which produces 53 million pounds of the fruit valued at $17 million a year.<sup>12</sup></p>

<p class="articlepullquote">Biotechnology: Getting the most from poor growing conditions</p>

<p>Scientists are developing crops
that can tolerate extreme conditions, such as drought, flood and harsh soil.
For instance, researchers are working on a rice that can survive long periods
under water <sup>13</sup> as well
as rice and corn that can tolerate aluminum in soil. <sup>14</sup> </p>

<p>A tomato plant has been
developed to grow in salty water that is 50 times higher in salt content than
conventional plants can tolerate and nearly half as salty as seawater.<sup>15</sup>
About a third of the world’s irrigated land has become useless to farmers
because of high levels of accumulated salt.</p>

<p>Biotech crops “could
significantly reduce malnutrition, which still affects more than 800 million
people worldwide, and would be especially valuable for poor farmers working
marginal lands in sub-Saharan Africa,” the Human Development Report stated. </p>

<p class="articlepullquote">Technology in a seed</p>

<p>While the Green Revolution kept
mass starvation at bay and saw global cereal production double as a result of
improved crop varieties, fertilizers, pesticides and irrigation, its benefits
bypassed such regions as sub-Saharan Africa. The new hybrids needed irrigation
and chemical inputs that farmers there couldn’t afford. </p>

<p>In contrast, the benefits of
biotechnology are passed on through a seed or plant cutting, so that farmers
anywhere around the world can easily adopt the technology. That’s why
biotechnology is particularly attractive to scientists and rural development
experts in poor countries where most of the people farm for a living. </p>

<p>Biotech crops are “tailor-made
for Africa’s farmers, because the new technology is packaged in the seed, which
all farmers know how to handle,&quot; said Florence Wambugu, a Kenyan plant
scientist who helped develop a virus-resistant sweet potato.<sup>16</sup>
</p>

<p>Agreeing with Wambugu, the
International Society of African Scientists issued a <a href="http://www.monsantoafrica.com/reports/ISAS/ISAS.html" onclick="javascript:window.open('index.asp?redirect=http%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Emonsantoafrica%2Ecom%2Freports%2FISAS%2FISAS%2Ehtml','','').focus();return false;">statement<ximg src="html/images/spacer.gif" border="0" width="4"><ximg src="html/images/ealink.gif" border="0" width="10"></a> in
October 2001 calling plant biotechnology a “major opportunity to enhance the
production of food crops.”<sup>17</sup></p>
