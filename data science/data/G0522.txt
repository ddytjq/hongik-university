<b>
<p>
Although space is very empty and the stars in the Milky Way are very far apart,
the space between the stars contains a very diffuse medium of gas and dust
astronomers call the <i>interstellar medium (ISM)</i>.  This medium consists of
neutral hydrogen gas (HI), molecular gas (mostly H<sub>2</sub>), ionized gas 
(HII), and dust grains.  Although the interstellar medium is, by several orders
of magnitude, a better vacuum than any physicists can create in the laboratory
there is still about of 5-10 billion 
M<sub><img src="./images/doodads/odot_sm.gif"></sub> of gas and dust out there,
comprising approximately 5% of the mass of visible stars in the Galaxy.
<p>
<a name="HI"><font size=+2 color="burgundy">
<u>Neutral Hydrogen Gas</u></font></a>
<p>
The Milky Way Galaxy is filled with a very diffuse distribution of neutral
<a href="http://antwrp.gsfc.nasa.gov/apod/ap961218.html">hydrogen gas</a> 
which has a typical density of about 1 atom/cm<sup>3</sup> 
(10<sup>-24</sup>g/cm<sup>3</sup>).
The interstellar medium is far too cool to excite the UV or optical
transitions of hydrogen, but there is a feature at 21 cm wavelength in the 
radio produced by the spins (magnetic fields) of the hydrogen atom's
nuclear proton and orbiting electron.  Because the proton and electron are 
spinning distributions of electric charge they create minute magnetic fields
which interact, creating a small energy difference between the state in which 
the poles are aligned versus counter-aligned.  This energy difference 
corresponds to the energy of radio waves at 21-centimeters.  Every once in a 
while (about once per 500 years) hydrogen atoms will collide, exciting an atom 
into the higher energy spin-aligned configuration.  It will take as long as 
30 million years for the atom to jump back to the lower energy state via
a <i>spin-flip</i>, emitting 21 cm radio emission.
<p>
<center>
<img src="./images/spin_flip.gif"><br>
</b>(Diagram courtesy of Dr. Terry Herter, Cornell University)<b>
</center>
<p>
The neutral hydrogen is distributed in clumpy fashion with cool, denser 
regions that astronomers call "clouds" but which are more like filaments.
These regions have a typical temperature of about 100K and a density between
10--100 atoms/cm<sup>3</sup>.  Surrounding the clouds is a warmer lower 
density medium with about 0.1 atom/cm<sup>3</sup> and T ~ 1000K.
<p>
<a name="GMC"><font size=+2 color="burgundy">
<u>Molecular Clouds</u></font></a>
<p>
<Table>
<td><b>
Comparatively dense (n<sub>H<sub>2</sub></sub> > 
1000 molecules/cm<sup>3</sup>), cold (T ~ 10K) clouds of molecular hydrogen
and dust, known as 
<a href="http://cfa-www.harvard.edu/cfa/oir/Research/swas/html/theories/cores/cores.html">
molecular clouds</a> or dark clouds are the birthplaces of
stars.  We do not detect molecular hydrogen directly, but infer its
characteristics from other molecules, most often CO.  Over 50 other
molecules have been detected including NH<sub>3</sub>, CH, OH, CS and
molecules as complex as ethyl alcohol (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>OH - the
stuff in whisky) have been found in Milky Way molecular clouds. The
Horsehead Nebula 
(<a href="http://seds.lpl.arizona.edu/messier/xtra/ngc/b33.html">Messier 
Nebulae</a>, <a href="http://www.seds.org/billa/twn/b33x.html">Web Nebulae</a>)
to the right is produced by the incursion of a plume of
dust from a molecular cloud, covering the lower half of the image, into a
region of ionized hydrogen. A Giant Molecular Cloud (GMC) may have a
mass of 10<sup>6</sup>M<sub> <img src="./images/doodads/odot_sm.gif"></sub> 
and a diameter of 150 l.y.  Within the GMCs are warm dense corse of order 
2-3 l.y. in diameter, with T~100K and densities as high as
n~10<sup>7</sup>-10<sup>9</sup> molecules/cm<sup>3</sup>.  It is in these
regions where the star-formation process begins.  There are thousands of
GMCs in the Milky Way, mostly on the Spiral Arms and concentrated toward
the Galactic Center.  The total mass of molecular gas is estimated to be
about equal to, or perhaps somewhat less (~25%) than, the mass of HI gas.

<p>
<a name="HII"><font size=+2 color="burgundy">
<u>Ionized Hydrogen Regions</u></font></a>
<p>
Material left over from the formation of young, hot stars represents the most 
spectacular component of the ISM, the ionized hydrogen or HII regions like
the Orion Nebula (<a href="http://seds.lpl.arizona.edu/messier/m/m042.html">
Messier Database</a>, <a href="http://www.seds.org/billa/twn/n1976x.html">
Web Nebulae</a>), shown below in this 
<a href="http://antwrp.gsfc.nasa.gov/apod/ap970511.html"> HST mosaic</a>. 
Here is the
<a href="http://antwrp.gsfc.nasa.gov/apod/ap990202.html"> Near Infrared 
view</a> of the Orion Nebula.

<p>
<center>
<a href="http://antwrp.gsfc.nasa.gov/apod/ap970511.html">
<img src="./images/Orion_hst.jpg"></a></center>
<p>
Massive O and B stars, recently formed in molecular clouds (remember - massive 
stars live fast &amp; die young!) ionize the gas left over from their 
formation heating it to a temperature, T ~ 10,000K and causing it to fluoresce
producing an <i>emission-line spectrum</i>.
<p>
<img src="./images/h2_region.gif">
<p>
Ultraviolet photons from four massive stars called the
<a href="http://antwrp.gsfc.nasa.gov/apod/ap971118.html">Trapezium</a>
in the nebula have sufficient energy to strip the electrons completely away 
from - ionize - hydrogen atoms.  This requires a photon of energy greater 
than 13.6 eV or wavelength less than 912&Aring; in the ultraviolet.  If a 
hydrogen atom absorbs a photon with wavelength less than 912&Aring; the atom 
is ionized with the "extra" energy going into the <i>kinetic energy</i> of 
the electron.  Collisions between electrons "thermalize" this energy heating 
the nebular gas to a temperature of about 10,000K.  Collisions between 
electrons and ions in the gas excite the ions to higher energy levels 
producing emission features of O<sup>+</sup>,O<sup>++</sup>,N<sup>+</sup>,
S<sup>+</sup>, etc. as shown in the above spectrum.  Electrons recombining to 
upper level in hydrogen and helium cascade through many energy levels down to 
the ground state producing the emission features of H &amp; He.
<p>
The Orion Nebula is a bubble on the side of a much larger 
<a href="http://cfa-www.harvard.edu/cfa/hotimage/worion.html">Giant Molecular 
Cloud</a> complex.  The GMC contains a lage cluster of 
<a href="http://www.seds.org/hst/97-13.html">newly formed stars</a>
<p>
Scroll down this page to see a selection of 
<a href="http://www.cita.utoronto.ca/~johnston/orion.html">HST images of 
Orion</a> showing circumstellar disks surrounding pre-main sequence stars 
of solar mass.

<p>
<a name="dust">
<font size=+2 color="burgundy">
<u>Interstellar  Dust</u></font></a>
<p>
About 1% of the mass of the ISM is in the form of tiny grains of
<a href="http://www.astronomynotes.com/ismnotes/ismglxya.htm
#A1.1">dust</a> about the size of particles of cigarette smoke.  We have 
already described how this dust obscures the plane of the Milky Way from
our view.  We know something about the characteristics of this dust from
the way that it scatters visible and ultraviolet photons.  The effect of
dust is to dim the light from distant objects (<i>interstellar
extinction</i>) in the Galaxy and redden the colors (<i>interstellar
reddening</i>) because red light is not scattered as efficiently as blue
light.

<p>
A graphical exhibition of the effects of dust at different wavelengths is shown
by the visible and infrared images of the constellation Orion above.  Dust 
scatters and obscures visible wavelengths where stars emit most of their light
(note Betelgeuse the bright red giant at Orion's left shoulder).  Dust is 
largely transparent in the infrared, but at temperatures of about 40K, emits
strongly at wavelengths between 50-100<sub><img src="./images/doodads/mu.gif">
</sub>m.
<p>
We know that dust grains are elongated, perhaps needle shaped, with sizes of 
about 1000&Aring;, about the wavelength of light that the grains scatter most 
efficiently.  Dust characteristics vary somewhat from place to place in 
the Galaxy, but a typical grain is believed to be composed of carbon in a 
graphite-like crystal structure, mixed with silicates (eg MgSi0<sub>3</sub>
like olivine).  Nearly all of the elements like Carbon and Silicon in the ISM 
are tied up in dust.  In molecular clouds the grains appear to be coated with 
a water-ice shell.  Ned Wright at UCLA has developed a
<a href="http://antwrp.gsfc.nasa.gov/apod/ap961119.html">fractal model for 
interstellar dust grains</a> shown in this APOD.

<a name="sn"><font size=+2 color="burgundy">
<u>Reflection Nebulae</u></font></a>
<p>
Dust is responsible for the blue haze around the
<a href="http://antwrp.gsfc.nasa.gov/apod/ap971018.html">Pleiades</a>
star cluster 
(<a href="http://seds.lpl.arizona.edu/messier/m/m045.html">Messier 
Database</a>, (<a href="http://www.seds.org/billa/twn/m45x.html">Web 
Nebulae</a>); this nebulosity is called a <i>reflection nebula</i>
resulting from blue light from the hot 
<a href="http://seds.lpl.arizona.edu/messier/more/m045_tab.html">B-stars</a>
being scattered toward us from dust surrounding the cluster stars.
