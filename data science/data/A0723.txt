				<p><b><font color="#3399CC">CAT standards sealed with a KISS</font></b> 
				<p>CAT standards are meant to allow investors to KISS complicated investment jargon goodbye. All companies offering Individual Savings Accounts have pledged to Keep It Simple and Straightforward. The CAT initials stand for Cost, Access and Terms. They mean the providers of CAT standard ISAs must be committed to treating their customers fairly using plain English and keeping rules simple, clear and fair.</p>
				<p><b>Article Overview</b></p>
					<ul>
						<li><a href="cat.htm#1">The mark of the CAT: Your entitlements</a></li>

			<b><font color="#3399CC">The mark of the CAT</font></b>
			<p><b>When you buy an ISA you should know that:</b></p>
				<ol>
					<li>CAT standard ISAs must be available for sale on their own. Customers should not be obliged to buy some other product. </li>
					<li> Providers of CAT standard ISAs must be committed to continuing to provide the products they offer to their existing customers. </li>
					<li> If a provider decides to discontinue or merge CAT standard ISAs, it should give existing customers at least three months' notice and try to arrange easy transfers on the same or better terms. </li>
					<li> Transfers should entail no charges. </li>
				</ol>

            <span class="text">              
			<b><font color="#3399CC">CAT standards come in threes</font></b><br><br>
			There are three different types of ISAs - cash, stock and shares and insurance and CAT standards vary in detail to cater for their different natures. You should understand your rights for each category. 
                                <a name="2a"></a><p></p>
                              <p> <b>CAT standard for cash ISAs <br></b> 
                              <p><b>Charges: </b>There should be no one-off or regular charges of any kind but duplicate statements and lost cards may incur fees. <b><br>Access: </b>You should be able to deal in sums as little as £10, withdraw cash when you want and get your money within seven working days. 
                                <br>
                                <b> Terms: </b>The interest rate should be no lower than 2 percentage points below base rate. Upward interest rate changes should reflect base rate movements within a calendar month although downward changes may be slower. <a name="2b"></a> 
                              </p>
                              <p></p>
                              <p><b>CAT standard for insurance ISAs </b></p>
                              <p> 
                              <blockquote><span class="text"><b>Charges:</b> The annual charge should be no more than 3% of the value of your fund. The charge quoted should be the maximum annual charge, accrued daily. There should be no other charges. The ISA structure should ensure that there are no double charges and that the ISA avoids explicit surrender penalties but providers can allocate lower bonuses in the early years of the contract. <br>
                                <b>Access:</b> Minimum premium should be no greater than a £250 lump sum a year or £25 monthly. <br>
                                <b>Terms: </b>Surrender values should reflect, over time, the value of the underlying assets of the fund. After three years surrender values should at least return the premiums paid in. A CAT standard insurance ISA is free to take lump sums, regular savings or intermittent savings or any combination of the three. 
                                <a name="2c"></a>
                                </span></blockquote><span class="text">
                              <p></p>
                              <p><b>CAT standard for stocks and shares ISAs </b></p>
                              <p> 
                              <blockquote><span class="text"><b>Charges:</b> Total charges should be no more than 1% of net asset value (nav) per year with no other charges to be paid by the saver. Charges must represent annual rates, accrued daily in accordance with the rules enforced by the regulator, the Financial Services Commission. The saver must pay nothing extra to the annual charge.<br>
                                <b>Access:</b> The minimum saving allowed should be no more than £500 lump sum a year or £50 a month. <br>
                                <b>Terms:</b> The ISA should invest in an authorised unit trust, Oeic (open-ended investment company) or investment trust. It should have at least 50% invested in shares and securities that are listed on European Union stock exchanges. The units and shares should have one price and this rule bars funds that have one price for buyers and one price for sellers. A stocks and shares ISA may take lump sum savings, regular savings, intermittent savings, or any combination of these three. There are some special rules for investment trust companies. There is a limit on what the investment trust can borrow to invest and it cannot offer more than one class of share such as one that pays dividends and another that is designed to increase the sum of capital invested.]
                                </span></blockquote><span class="text">
                              <div align="right"><a name="3"></a><a href="cat.htm#top" class="top">Back to top</a></div>

                          <span class="text">
                              <b><font color="#3399CC">Points to remember</font></b>
                              <p> 
                              <ol>
                                <li> The mark of the CAT on an ISA is designed to reassure investors that fees will be reasonable and easy to understand. </li>
                                <li> The word CAT stands for Cost, Access and Terms and ISA providers have to commit themselves to meeting, or providing better than, these minimum required standards before they can have the CAT mark on their product. </li>
                                <li> The ISA is a government-introduced savings vehicle through which investors can save and invest without paying income or capital gains tax on the proceeds. It is not an investment in itself. </li>
                                <li> The CAT mark is not a guarantee of investment 
                                  performance. </li>
                                <li> There are three different types of ISAs - cash, stock and shares and insurance and CAT standards vary in detail to cater for their different natures. Investors need to understand their rights for each of the three investment categories. </li>
                              </ol>
