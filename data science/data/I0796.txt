<P><B>Dangers of stagnation</B><BR>Mr Aigner, who warned last December of the dangers of the world's premier club competition "stagnating", was pleased that the Champions League is to revert to a single group stage from the 2003/04 season. He described the decision by UEFA's Executive Committee as&nbsp;"a chance to put things right". </P>
<P><B>Key factors</B><BR>Following today's meeting at UEFA headquarters in Nyon, the UEFA CEO told uefa.com: "There were various key factors that prompted the Executive Committee to modify the present format. First of all, in the sporting aspect, we have indications that the intensity of matches, parallel to the national leagues, is disturbing to the public. The players playing to a very high rhythm led to a system at certain clubs called rotation, using a player in a European context but not in a national context. </P>
<P><B>Number of matches played </B><BR>"Then there is the total number of matches being played. We are talking about the best players in the world, playing also for their national team, whose clubs go very far in Europe and who are playing for their national title. They are always performing at the highest level, and we cannot expect that these days. </P>
<P><B>'Load was too heavy'</B><BR>"Obviously the Champions League load was too heavy, and [following these changes] the players will be fresher, not only physically but mentally. Players need time to prepare themselves mentally for new challenges, especially at the highest level, and a break between two European matches, which was traditionally two weeks, is ideal for that. </P>
<P><B>Saturation level</B><BR>"The same applies to the public. They also need to prepare for the next challenge, and if we offer too much good material at too high a density, then their appetite will go down. We also have difficulties in solving collision problems with national league fixtures. Commercially too conditions are not favourable for generating more income, and we know there is a certain saturation when it comes to the transmission of football matches on television. This concerns less the Champions League and more the UEFA Cup, whose image we also would like to find a method to improve."</P>
<P><B>'We will have a mixed reaction'</B><BR>Asked whether he felt it would be a popular change, Mr Aigner said: "I think we will have a mixed reaction. There will be much positive reaction, but in the present climate where clubs have to expect less income from the market irrespective of the changes we are making, such an additional move may not be welcomed by clubs already at the limit of their financial possibilities."</P>
<P><B>'What the public wants'<BR></B>For his part, Mr Johansson told the assembled press: "We have made a very thorough investigation about the development of the UEFA Champions League. We have met with hundreds of clubs, and with the Club Committee and Club Forum. We have heard your points of view in the media, and we have found that we should give priority to sporting aspects, to what the public wants, and what is interesting for the viewers.</P>
<P><B>Second stage the main objection</B><BR>"There have been changes on the way, and you have to be flexible. You learn, during the competition what went right and what didn't go so well, and finally we found out that the main objection came about the second stage. We have to listen to market needs, and the ideas that come from different sources, among them from the clubs concerned. </P>
<P><B>World Cup example</B><BR>"They themselves have found now that the number of matches have to be reduced, the medical experts have pointed out that in the [FIFA] World Cup there were too many injuries just because [the players] were worn out, and it has also been shown that people cannot digest every match that has been shown on television.</P>
<P><B>'Room for players to recover'</B><BR>"You shouldn't overdo it. There are too many matches. You should have some room for the players to recover. All of this together made us take the decision. In the end, we have to do something which we believe is for the benefit of the majority."<BR></P> </TD></TR></TABLE><TABLE CELLPADDING="0" CELLSPACING="5" BORDER="0" WIDTH="355" xmlns:msxsl="urn:schemas-microsoft-com:xslt" xmlns:user="http://mycompany.com/mynamespace"><TR><TD class="NewsBody"><a target="_blank" href="&#xA;					/news/newsId=27973,printer.htmx&#xA;				"><img SRC="/images/All/buttons/printer_friendly.gif" border="0" width="130" height="9" vspace="10" /></a></TD><TD align="right" class="NewsBody"><a href="javascript:void(0)" onclick="window.open('/news/sendmail.htmx?newsId=27973','SendMail','width=355,height=516')"><img SRC="/images/All/buttons/mail_friend.gif" border="0" width="99" height="9" vspace="10" /></a></TD></TR></TABLE><TABLE CELLPADDING="0" CELLSPACING="5" BORDER="0" WIDTH="355" xmlns:msxsl="urn:schemas-microsoft-com:xslt" xmlns:user="http://mycompany.com/mynamespace"><TR><TD class="NewsBody">©uefa.com 1998-2002.
