<TITLE> Information analysis of Fis binding sites</TITLE>

<B>ABSTRACT</B><abstext>
<P>
<B>Originally discovered in the bacteriophage Mu DNA inversion system </B><B><I>gin</I></B><B>,
Fis (Factor for Inversion Stimulation) regulates many genetic systems. To
determine the base frequency conservation required for Fis to locate its
binding sites, we collected a set of 60 experimentally defined wild-type
Fis DNA binding sequences. The sequence logo for Fis binding sites showed
the significance and likely kinds of base contacts, and these are consistent
with available experimental data. Scanning with an information theory based
weight matrix within </B><B><I>fis</I></B><B>, </B><B><I>nrd</I></B><B>,
</B><B><I>tgt</I></B><B>/</B><B><I>sec</I></B><B> and </B><B><I>gin</I></B><B>
revealed Fis sites not previously identified, but for which there are published
footprinting and biochemical data. DNA mobility shift experiments showed
that a site predicted to be 11 bases from the proximal </B><B><I>Salmonella
typhimurium</I></B><B><I>hin</I></B><B> site and a site predicted to be
7 bases from the proximal P1 </B><B><I>cin</I></B><B> site are bound by
Fis </B><B><I>in vitro</I></B><B>. Two predicted sites separated by 11 bp
found within the </B><B><I>nrd</I></B><B> promoter region, and one in the
</B><B><I>tgt</I></B><B>/</B><B><I>sec</I></B><B> promoter, were also confirmed
by gel shift analysis. A sequence in </B><B><I>aldB</I></B> previously reported
to be a Fis site, for which information theory predicts no site, did not
shift. These results demonstrate that information analysis is useful for
predicting Fis DNA binding.</abstext>
</ABS></B>

<B>
<H3><ST><A HREF="#top" NAME="hd0"><B>INTRODUCTION</B></A></ST></H3>
</B>
<P>
Fis is a pleiotropic DNA-bending protein that enhances site-specific recombination,
controls DNA replication, and regulates transcription of a number of genes
in <I>Escherichia coli</I> and <I>Salmonella typhimurium</I> (<A NAME=bbr1_1
HREF=#bb1>1 </A>-<A NAME=bbr4_1 HREF=#bb4>4 </A>). Fis is composed of two
98 amino acid polypeptides, with each polypeptide having four [alpha] helices,
A-D. Homodimers of Fis bind to and deflect DNA from 40&deg; to 90&deg; (<A
NAME=bbr3_1 HREF=#bb3>3 </A>,<A NAME=bbr5_1 HREF=#bb5>5 </A>-<A NAME=bbr7_1
HREF=#bb7>7 </A>). Mutational analyses suggest that the N-terminal portion
of the Fis monomer containing the A helix is necessary for recombination,
while the C-terminal portion containing the D helix is thought to be involved
in DNA binding (<A NAME=bbr8_1 HREF=#bb8>8 </A>,<A NAME=bbr9_1 HREF=#bb9>9
</A>). However, X-ray crystal structures of Fis reveal that the D helices
appear to be too close together for Fis to fit into two successive major
grooves on straight B-form DNA, suggesting that the DNA bends to accommodate
Fis (<A NAME=bbr6_1 HREF=#bb6>6 </A>,<A NAME=bbr10_1 HREF=#bb10>10 </A>,<A
NAME=bbr11_1 HREF=#bb11>11 </A>), that Fis is flexible, or that Fis binds
in a completely unanticipated manner.
<P>
Although Fis binds to precise sequences according to footprint data, it
is often noted that the Fis binding site has a poorly defined consensus
sequence (<A NAME=bbr3_2 HREF=#bb3>3 </A>,<A NAME=bbr6_2 HREF=#bb6>6 </A>,<A
NAME=bbr8_2 HREF=#bb8>8 </A>-<A NAME=bbr25_1 HREF=#bb25>25 </A>). Consensus
sequences are often used to locate binding sites, but it is widely known
that this does not work well, especially in the case of Fis (<A NAME=bbr24_1
HREF=#bb24>24 </A>-<A NAME=bbr26_1 HREF=#bb26>26 </A>). Since a consensus
is constructed by selecting the most frequent base or bases at every position
across a binding site, creating a consensus sequence throws out important
information about the observed frequency of bases in the binding site.
<P>
In contrast to consensus sequences, information theory provides quantitative
models for binding sites. These models are represented by the sequence logo,
a graphical method that retains most of the subtleties in sequence data
(<A NAME=bbr27_1 HREF=#bb27>27 </A>-<A NAME=bbr30_1 HREF=#bb30>30 </A>).
Even a glance at a sequence logo often reveals the possible nature of specific
base contacts, which side of a base pair is likely to face the protein,
and whether or not the DNA is distorted away from B-form (<A NAME=bbr30_2
HREF=#bb30>30 </A>,<A NAME=bbr31_1 HREF=#bb31>31 </A>).
<P>
Because of its importance in a variety of genetic systems and because many
binding sites were already well defined, interaction of Fis with DNA was
an attractive candidate for a thorough information analysis. In addition
to the information content measure (<A NAME=bbr32_1 HREF=#bb32>32 </A>)
and the sequence logo (<A NAME=bbr29_1 HREF=#bb29>29 </A>), we used a new
method, `individual information' (<I>R<SUB>i</SUB></I>), that defines the
information content of individual binding sites (<A NAME=bbr33_1 HREF=#bb33>33
</A>) and displays the results graphically as a `sequence walker' (<A NAME=bbr34_1
HREF=#bb34>34 </A>). These methods have an advantage over other methods
in that training is not required to obtain a quantitative binding site model,
and only examples of functional sites are used to construct the model. Using
well-defined biochemical data helps to ensure that the models are realistic.
Our analysis of Fis binding sites and their surrounding sequences revealed
many previously unidentified sites adjacent to known ones, and experiments
demonstrated that some of the predicted sequences are indeed bound by Fis
<I>in vitro</I>.
</SEC>

<H3><ST><A HREF="#top" NAME="hd2"><B>Nucleotide sequences and binding sites</B></A></ST></H3>
</B>
<P>
Fis sites identified by footprinting, gel shift or mutational data were
gathered from the GenBank accession numbers, coordinates and orientations
shown in Figure <A NAME=figr1_1 HREF=#fig1>1 </A>. The exact alignment of
the sites was confirmed by maximizing the information content (<A NAME=bbr54_1
HREF=#bb54>54 </A>).

<P>
A few of the sites previously footprinted were not included. Site II at
coordinate 238 of the <I>aldB</I> promoter has an information content of
-5.3 bits, which strongly implies that it is not a binding site. It was
noted in Xu and Johnson (<A NAME=bbr53_1 HREF=#bb53>53 </A>) that this site
is the weakest of all sites and that it gives effects on the DNase I footprint
only at the highest concentration of Fis. Since, according to the model
built from the 60 sites listed in Figure <A NAME=figr1_2 HREF=#fig1>1 </A>,
the probability that site II is a naturally occurring Fis binding site is
&#60;4.6 &times; 10<SUP>-6</SUP>, and there were no better nearby sites,
we did not include it in our model. The last <I>cin</I> site at 336 was
listed in Finkel and Johnson (cin site #4) (<A NAME=bbr53_2 HREF=#bb53>53
</A>4) as being at coordinate 348 on X01828. This had a -0.9 bit site which
was on the edge of the DNase I footprint shown in figure 5 of ref. <A NAME=bbr49_1
HREF=#bb49>49 </A>. Since there is a 4.4 bit site at coordinate 336 which
fits the DNase I footprint exactly, we used it instead.
<P>
In the regions around the first 46 sites (Fig. <A NAME=figr1_3 HREF=#fig1>1
</A>) the information theory weight matrix model consistently revealed Fis
binding sites for which experimental footprinting data already exist, but
which had been missed by searches using a consensus sequence. At this point
of our collection, we began using the first 46 sites to help locate the
remaining sites (<A NAME=bbr33_2 HREF=#bb33>33 </A>). We examined the R6K
[gamma] origin with our current model and found multiple overlapping Fis
sites, only some of which correspond to the available footprint data (<A
NAME=bbr26_2 HREF=#bb26>26 </A>). We believe that further experimental analysis
of these sites is warranted before they are included in our model. We used
coordinate +48 instead of +51 for <I>ndh</I> site III because it was the
closest match (<A NAME=bbr55_1 HREF=#bb55>55 </A>). The model revealed seven
sites that correspond to the DNase I hypersensitive locations on <I>hns</I>
footprints, so we used these (<A NAME=bbr56_1 HREF=#bb56>56 </A>).

<H3><ST><A HREF="#top" NAME="hd4"><B>Design of Fis binding experiments</B></A></ST></H3>
<P>
In designing the sequences of Figure <A NAME=figr6_1 HREF=#fig6>6 </A> we
chose the <I>hin </I>site of <I>S.typhimurium</I> (<A NAME=bbr12_1 HREF=#bb12>12
</A>) because it is well characterized and the binding site prediction is
clear. We chose 32 bases of the <I>hin</I> sequence because according to
the information-theory based search (Fig. <A NAME=figr5_1 HREF=#fig5>5 </A>)
this region contains two overlapping Fis sites, one of which is the Fis
site proximal to the recombination junction <I>hixL</I> (<A NAME=bbr12_2
HREF=#bb12>12 </A>). We added five bases of natural DNA sequence on each
end-half a twist of DNA-to be sure we were not missing important components,
although this region does not show up significantly in the sequence logo.
Beyond these ends we added <I>Eco</I>RI and <I>Hin</I>dIII overhangs. We
created three other sequences using the anti-consensus of the Fis weight
matrix to destroy the proximal site, the newly identified `medial' site,
or both sites. The anti-consensus sequence is the sequence that should bind
Fis the worst (<A NAME=bbr33_3 HREF=#bb33>33 </A>). It is predicted from
the number of bases at each position or the <I>R<SUB>iw</SUB></I>(<I>b</I>,<I>l</I>)
matrix by noting which bases appear least frequently at each position of
the site or which give the lowest weight. In ambiguous cases we chose C
or G when possible because these appear rarely in the logo (Fig. <A NAME=figr2_1
HREF=#fig2>2 </A>). (Note: the anti-consensus sequence in the early model
we used had C at -5 rather than G.)
<P>

ALIGN=RIGHT HSPACE=18 VSPACE=10></A>

<P>
These sequences and their complements were synthesized (The Midland Certified
Reagent Co., Midland, TX, USA) so that when annealed they provide sticky
<I>Eco</I>RI and <I>Hin</I>dIII ends. Annealed oligos were ligated into
plasmid pTS385 (<A NAME=bbr59_1 HREF=#bb59>59 </A>) which had been digested
with <I>Eco</I>RI and <I>Hin</I>dIII, and transformed into <I>E.coli</I>
DH5[alpha] (<A NAME=bbr60_1 HREF=#bb60>60 </A>) as previously described
(<A NAME=bbr61_1 HREF=#bb61>61 </A>). Transformants were selected on LB
media containing 50 &micro;g/ml kanamycin and 50 &micro;g/ml of ampicillin.
When necessary, we transformed <I>E.coli</I> BL21/DE3 (<A NAME=bbr62_1 HREF=#bb62>62
</A>) and selected them on the same media containing 1 mM IPTG. We knew
from previous experiments that the parental plasmid pTS385 is conditionally
lethal to this strain because a strong T7 promoter is positioned between
the <I>Eco</I>RI and <I>Hin</I>dIII sites (<A NAME=bbr59_2 HREF=#bb59>59
</A>). Induction of the chromosomally imbedded T7 RNA polymerase gene with
IPTG thus provided a strong selection for recombinant plasmids containing
the intended insert DNA, eliminating all but a few wild-type pTS385 plasmids
from the lot of transformants in BL21/DE3. The resultant plasmids were screened
by restriction analysis and PCR amplification using primers flanking the
inserted DNA pTS37f1 5'-ACATTTCCCCGAAAAGTGC-3' and pTS37r1 5'-CGGAACACGTAGAAAGCCA-3'.
When recombinants were identified, plasmid DNA was transformed into and
maintained within <I>E.coli</I> DH5[alpha]. The sequence between the <I>Eco</I>RI
and <I>Hin</I>dIII sites was then confirmed by dideoxy sequencing with an
ABI model 373A automated sequencer (<A NAME=bbr63_1 HREF=#bb63>63 </A>).
<P>
For gel mobility shifts (<A NAME=bbr64_1 HREF=#bb64>64 </A>,<A NAME=bbr65_1
HREF=#bb65>65 </A>), we used Fis protein cloned and purified from <I>E.coli</I>
obtained as a gift from R.Johnson (<A NAME=bbr66_1 HREF=#bb66>66 </A>).
Plasmid DNA from the four <I>hin</I> clones was purified by the method of
Birnboim and Doly (<A NAME=bbr67_1 HREF=#bb67>67 </A>) or Hengen (<A NAME=bbr68_1
HREF=#bb68>68 </A>), linearized with <I>Eco</I>RI, end-filled with biotin-16-dUTP
using the Klenow fragment of <I>E.coli</I> DNA polymerase I, and digested
with <I>Bgl</I>II, which cleaves 368 bp from the <I>Eco</I>RI site. The
smaller fragment was purified away from the vector by electrophoresis and
then digested with <I>Hin</I>dIII.
<P>
Binding assays were accomplished by incubating DNA at ~1 nM with various
concentrations of Fis protein as 2-fold serial dilutions beginning with
1000 nM, and at room temperature for 15 min in 25 mM Tris-HCl pH 7.6, 80
mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, 2 mM DTT, 100 &micro;g/ml acetylated bovine serum albumin
and 125 &micro;g/ml calf thymus DNA (<A NAME=bbr12_3 HREF=#bb12>12 </A>).
Gel shift analysis was done by separation of the different species on an
8.0% polyacrylamide gel in 1&times; TBE. The DNA was electro-transferred
onto Tropilon-Plus<SUP>TM</SUP> positively charged nylon membrane and detected
using a Southern-Light<SUP>TM</SUP> chemiluminescent kit (<A NAME=bbr69_1
HREF=#bb69>69 </A>) using the CSPD<SUP>&reg;</SUP> substrate (CAS = 142849-53-4,
Tropix, Inc., Bedford, MA, USA).
<P>
For experiments designed to confirm Fis binding, chosen Fis sites were engineered
as single-stranded DNAs such that the DNA loops back onto itself by forming
a hairpin turn structure centered at 5'-GCGAAGC-3' (<A NAME=bbr70_1 HREF=#bb70>70
</A>). The natural Fis sequence from -10 to +10 was used and an <I>Eco</I>RI
restriction site added on the open end. The DNA sequence was further checked
for any extraneous Fis sites above 0 bits by using walkers (<A NAME=bbr34_3
HREF=#bb34>34 </A>).
<P>
DNA oligos for the <I>cin</I> external site (5'-GGAATTCTATGTACGCGTATCAACAAATGCGAAGCATTTGTTGATACGCGTACATAGAATTCC-3'),
<I>aldB</I>II (5'-GGAATTCGCTGTCGTAAAGC-TGTTACCGGCGAAGCCGGTAACAGCTTTACGACAGCGA-ATTCC-3'),
<I>tgt</I>/<I>sec</I> at -73 (5'-GGAATTCTGAGCTAAAAAATT-CATCGATGCGAAGCATCGATGAATTTTTTAGCTCAGAATTCC-3'),
<I>nrd</I>F1 (5'-GGAATTCTTCGCTTATATATTGACCACAG-CGAAGCTGTGGTCAATATATAAGCGAAGAATTCC-3'),
and <I>nrd</I>F2 (5'-GGAATTCATTGACCACAACTGATACATCGCGA-AGCGATGTATCAGTTGTGGTCAATGAATTCC-3')
were synthesized with biotin on the 5' end and gel purified. Gel shift assays
were performed essentially as before, except that the DNA was diluted to
1 nM in Fis binding buffer, heated to 90&deg;C for 10 min, and slowly cooled
to room temperature. The self-annealing property obviated the need for further
manipulation of the DNA.

<H3><ST><A HREF="#top" NAME="hd5">RESULTS AND DISCUSSION</A></ST></H3>
<SEC>
<B>
<H3><ST><A HREF="#top" NAME="hd6"><B>Interpretation of the Fis sequence
logo</B></A></ST></H3>
</B>
<P>
To create a model for Fis binding, 60 Fis binding sites for which DNA footprinting
or mutational data are available were collected (Fig. <A NAME=figr1_4 HREF=#fig1>1
</A>). Each footprint was compared to the prediction from information scans
and walkers to be sure that the location of the binding sites matched the
final model. By this method, the locations of several sites given in previous
compilations were corrected (see Materials and Methods).
<P>
Since <I>E.coli </I>and <I>S.typhimurium</I> Fis proteins have identical
amino acid sequences (<A NAME=bbr3_3 HREF=#bb3>3 </A>,<A NAME=bbr24_2 HREF=#bb24>24
</A>) and no modifications that we are aware of, we used binding sites from
both species. This would be inappropriate if <I>in vivo</I> DNA binding
conditions are different between the two species. Because Fis binds as a
dimer (<A NAME=bbr6_3 HREF=#bb6>6 </A>,<A NAME=bbr10_2 HREF=#bb10>10 </A>),
the sequences and their complements were aligned to produce a sequence logo
(<A NAME=bbr29_3 HREF=#bb29>29 </A>). Analysis of such logos can reveal
interaction details that are otherwise obscured by a consensus (<A NAME=bbr31_2
HREF=#bb31>31 </A>). From the sequences, the sequence logo (Fig. <A NAME=figr2_2
HREF=#fig2>2 </A>a) the structure of DNA base pairs (Fig. <A NAME=figr2_3
HREF=#fig2>2 </A>b), and molecular modeling, the following observations
were made:
<P>
(i) The correlation between sequence conservation peaks at &plusmn;7 and
&plusmn;3 and a 10.6 base spacing (shown by the sine wave in the figure)
suggests that Fis makes contacts in two consecutive major grooves (<A NAME=bbr31_3
HREF=#bb31>31 </A>). Further, the information content at &plusmn;7 is above
one bit, which also suggests major groove binding (<A NAME=bbr30_3 HREF=#bb30>30
</A>,<A NAME=bbr35_1 HREF=#bb35>35 </A>). This is consistent with protection
data showing that the methylation of the major groove N7 of G at &plusmn;7
interferes with Fis binding (<A NAME=bbr12_4 HREF=#bb12>12 </A>), with Fis
binding that protects against DMS methylation at &plusmn;7 (<A NAME=bbr36_1
HREF=#bb36>36 </A>), and with hydroxyl radical footprints (<A NAME=bbr37_1
HREF=#bb37>37 </A>).
<P>
(ii) As seen on the logo, if an A is substituted for the majority G at -7
(or the complementary G at +7), a possible G-O6 contact would be lost while
a G-N7 contact could be retained as A-N7. On the other hand, if T is substituted,
a G-N7 contact would be lost but a G-O6 contact could be replaced by T-O4.
This is consistent with the observed frequency of bases at position -7 (and
it's complement at +7) for which G>A~T>C. That the frequency of As and Ts
are nearly the same at this position suggests this A-N7 contact is energetically
equivalent to a T-O4 contact. Similar contacts appear at &plusmn;15 in OxyR
binding sites (<A NAME=bbr31_4 HREF=#bb31>31 </A>) and at +6/-7 in CRP (<A
NAME=bbr30_4 HREF=#bb30>30 </A>). The conservation can be explained by direct
contacts or indirect through-water bonds.
<P>
(iii) At position &plusmn;6 there is no observed sequence conservation,
yet methylation of a G in the major groove at that position interferes with
Fis binding (<A NAME=bbr12_5 HREF=#bb12>12 </A>). This suggests that Fis
passes close to the base in that region but does not make a specific contact.
<P>
(iv) At -4 and +3 the logo shows conservation of Cs or Ts, while at positions
-3 and +4 the logo shows the complementary As or Gs. Because N7 is the only
contact common to both A and G in the major groove (Fig. <A NAME=figr2_4
HREF=#fig2>2 </A>b), this observation suggests that all four positions have
N7 contacts (<A NAME=bbr38_1 HREF=#bb38>38 </A>). These contacts are consistent
with DMS interference experiments (<A NAME=bbr12_6 HREF=#bb12>12 </A>).
The relative heights of the letters reveal a 4.8-fold preference for A over
G at -3 (T over C at +3), suggesting other direct contacts in the major
groove or DNA bending effects.
<P>
(v) At positions 0, &plusmn;1 and &plusmn;2 there is an A-T region where
Fis most likely faces the minor groove. Since A is as frequent as T but
C and G are allowed at low frequency, this preference could be caused by
a series of protein probes that sterically interfere with the N2 of G in
the minor groove (Fig. <A NAME=figr2_5 HREF=#fig2>2 </A>b) (<A NAME=bbr30_5
HREF=#bb30>30 </A>). Consistent with this, methylation of A at N3 in the
minor groove at positions 0 and &plusmn;1 interferes with Fis binding (<A
NAME=bbr12_7 HREF=#bb12>12 </A>,<A NAME=bbr39_1 HREF=#bb39>39 </A>).
<P>
(vi) The -4 to +4 central region of the Fis logo can be interpreted in a
different way. We constructed a three-dimensional model of Fis-DNA binding
predicted from the logo and probable contact points (see <A HREF="http://www-lmmb.ncifcrf.gov/~toms/fismodels/">http://www-lmmb.ncifcrf.gov/~toms/fismodels/</A>
for details). Mutations at Arg 85 and Lys 91 of Fis alter its ability to
bind DNA (<A NAME=bbr8_3 HREF=#bb8>8 </A>,<A NAME=bbr9_2 HREF=#bb9>9 </A>),
and molecular dynamics docking of Fis with DNA supports the notion that
these residues contact the DNA (<A NAME=bbr40_1 HREF=#bb40>40 </A>). When
we compressed a Fis binding site in the minor groove from -2 to +2 to account
for the A-T region, kinked the DNA at &plusmn;3.5 and &plusmn;7.5 to create
a bend at pyrimidine-purine pairs, and aligned Fis so that Arg 85 contacts
G &plusmn;7 and Lys 91 contacts phosphate &plusmn;1.5, an unavoidable gap
appeared that prevents direct contact between Fis and bases -4 to +4. Because
our detailed model incorporates all the features observed in the sequence
logo, and shows the same gap observed by others (<A NAME=bbr5_2 HREF=#bb5>5
</A>,<A NAME=bbr6_4 HREF=#bb6>6 </A>,<A NAME=bbr10_3 HREF=#bb10>10 </A>,<A
NAME=bbr11_2 HREF=#bb11>11 </A>,<A NAME=bbr40_2 HREF=#bb40>40 </A>), the
entire conservation from -4 to +4 might be accounted for by indirect contacts
instead of direct contacts. Direct contacts represent physical contact between
the protein and the DNA, while indirect `contacts' are those in which there
is no direct contact but instead the structure of the DNA is distorted,
indirectly leading to sequence conservation. Since molecular modeling is
not entirely reliable, both the direct and the indirect binding modes are
plausible and further experimental work would be required to distinguish
between them. However, these two binding modes are not exclusive since it
is possible that Fis can flex enough to bind to straight DNA using direct
contacts to all of the bases. Subsequently, the DNA could bend using the
bending properties of the central bases.
<P>
(vii) The sequence logo shows that Fis sites easily accommodate the Dam
methylase site 5'-GATC-3' at &plusmn;4 through &plusmn;7, suggesting that
under some circumstances Fis binding may be controlled by methylation. A
Fis site in Tn<I>5</I> is only bound when overlapping GATCs are unmethylated
(<A NAME=bbr41_1 HREF=#bb41>41 </A>). The only other occurrence of GATC
within a Fis site in our list (Fig. <A NAME=figr1_5 HREF=#fig1>1 </A>) is
at 0 to +3 of <I>oriC</I> Fis 283, so there may be a connection between
Fis and this feature of the origin of DNA replication, as suggested previously
(<A NAME=bbr42_1 HREF=#bb42>42 </A>).
<P>
(viii) It is possible that the bases at one position of a Fis site are correlated
to those in another position. For example, an A at -3 might only appear
when there is an A at -2, but not when there is a T at -2. This would make
the sequence logo an incomplete model because these are not displayed. The
Diana program (<A NAME=bbr43_2 HREF=#bb43>43 </A>) shows only faint correlations
between -20 and -19 (0.14 bits; <I>P</I> &#60; 1 &times; 10<SUP>-7</SUP>
given the background of correlations from -20 to +20 of -0.02 &plusmn; 0.03
bits) and between -2 and -1 (0.12 bits; <I>P</I> &#60; 1 &times; 10<SUP>-6</SUP>)
and their complements. As these values are within the error of the total
sequence conservation (&plusmn;0.27 bits), there is little or no missing
sequence conservation in the sequence logo model.
<P>

<H3><ST><A HREF="#top" NAME="hd7"><B>Searching specific sequences with the
Fis individual information weight matrix model</B></A></ST></H3>
<P>
To model the base preferences of Fis, we computed a weight matrix from:

<TABLE WIDTH=100% BORDER=0>
<TR>
<TD VALIGN=TOP ALIGN=LEFT><I>R<SUB>iw</SUB></I>(<I>b</I>,<I>l</I>) = 2 +
log<SUB>2</SUB> <I>f</I>(<I>b</I>,<I>l</I>) - <I>e</I>(<I>n</I>) (bits per
base)</TD>
<TD VALIGN=TOP ALIGN=LEFT><B>1</B></TD>
</TR>
</TABLE>
<P>
where <I>f</I>(<I>b</I>,<I>l</I>) is the frequency of each base <I>b</I>
at position <I>l</I> in the aligned binding site sequences and their complements,
and <I>e</I>(<I>n</I>) is a sample size correction factor for the <I>n</I>
= 120 sequences used to create <I>f</I>(<I>b</I>,<I>l</I>) (<A NAME=bbr33_4
HREF=#bb33>33 </A>). <I>R<SUB>iw</SUB></I>(<I>b</I>,<I>l</I>) values range
between -[infinity] and 2 bits. To evaluate a DNA sequence, the bases of
the sequence are aligned with the matrix entries and the <I>R<SUB>iw</SUB></I>(<I>b</I>,<I>l</I>)
values corresponding to each base are added together to produce the total
<I>R<SUB>i</SUB></I> value. This measure has several advantages over other
methods. First, the scale is in bits, which are easy units to think about
and which allow direct comparison to many other systems. Second, by adding
the weights together for various positions in a particular binding site,
we get the total `individual information' (<I>R<SUB>i</SUB></I>) for that
site. Third, the average <I>R<SUB>i</SUB></I> for all of the binding sites
used to create the <I>R<SUB>iw</SUB></I>(<I>b</I>,<I>l</I>) matrix is the
average information content, <I>R<SUB>sequence</SUB></I> (<A NAME=bbr32_3
HREF=#bb32>32 </A>). This is the same as the area under the sequence logo.
Fourth, unlike a neural network that needs to be cyclically trained and
requires both sites and non-sites, the matrix can be created immediately
using only proven sites as examples. This avoids the danger of training
against unknown functional sites, and therefore was critical for obtaining
the results presented here. Fifth, functional binding sites have positive
<I>R<SUB>i</SUB></I> values, within the error of the method, allowing one
to make predictions. Finally, unlike consensus sequences which destroy the
available sequence data by arbitrarily rounding the frequencies up or down,
the individual information method uses the base frequences directly and
so it preserves subtleties in the data.
</SEC>
<SEC>
<H3><ST><A HREF="#top" NAME="hd8"><B>Validation of the individual information
scanning method</B></A></ST></H3>
<P>
We used individual information (<A NAME=bbr33_5 HREF=#bb33>33 </A>) to study
Fis binding sites throughout the <I>E.coli</I> genome and at several specific
loci. Although the theoretical cutoff for distinguishing sites from non-sites
is 0 bits, we often used a conservative 2 bit cutoff to define Fis sites
because our previous experience showed that sites between 0 and 2 bits can
bind Fis (data not shown). When comparing the output from the Scan, DNAplot,
MakeWalker and Lister programs to previously reported footprinting data,
we consistently found sites which were seen as DNase I protected regions.
<P>
For example, by using a degenerate consensus pattern, previous workers found
five Fis binding sites upstream of the transcriptional start site of the
<I>nrd</I> operon of <I>E.coli</I> (<A NAME=bbr44_1 HREF=#bb44>44 </A>).
When we scanned for potential Fis binding sites, several more sites were
identified (Fig. <A NAME=figr3_1 HREF=#fig3>3 </A>). These were confirmed
to be bona fide sites since Cu-phenanthroline footprinting of this region
had already been done by Augustin <I>et al</I>. Their data (Fig. <A NAME=figr3_2
HREF=#fig3>3 </A>, lanes 4 and 5) correspond well with our predictions even
though none of these additional sites were used in the <I>R<SUB>iw</SUB></I>(<I>b</I>,<I>l</I>)
model. In another case, in addition to the site found at position -58 of
the <I>tgt</I>/<I>sec</I> promoter (<A NAME=bbr45_1 HREF=#bb45>45 </A>),
an information scan shows a second strong site at -73 (<A NAME=bbr34_4 HREF=#bb34>34
</A>). Both <I>tgt</I>/<I>sec</I> sites were included in our model because
they are supported by footprinting, gel shift and <I>in vivo</I> transcriptional
assays. Although Fis has a poor consensus sequence, theoretically it can
bind precisely (<A NAME=bbr46_1 HREF=#bb46>46 </A>,<A NAME=bbr47_1 HREF=#bb47>47
</A>), and indeed footprints reveal concise binding on well separated sites.
Complex footprints appear to be imprecise binding if one uses a consensus
sequence. Often the protected genetic regions can be dissected into their
components by using individual information tools, so that the data is interpreted
as representing overlapping sites.

<H3><ST><A HREF="#top" NAME="hd9"><B>Using sequence conservation to infer
the number of Fis sites in </B><B><I>E.coli</I></B></A></ST></H3>
<P>
The total number of Fis sites in the <I>E.coli</I> genome is not known,
so the information needed to locate those sites (<I>R<SUB>frequency</SUB></I>)
cannot be calculated (<A NAME=bbr32_4 HREF=#bb32>32 </A>). However, the
total sequence conservation at the binding sites is 7.86 &plusmn; 0.27 bits
(Fig. <A NAME=figr2_6 HREF=#fig2>2 </A>), which suggests that there is one
site roughly every 2<SUP>7.86&plusmn;0.27</SUP> = 232 &plusmn; 43 bases
or an average of 4.7 &plusmn; 0.9 sites at each of the ~4289 genes of the
entire 4 638 858 bp <I>E.coli</I> genome (GenBank accession no. U00096,
version of 16-JAN-1997). It also implies that ~20 000 &plusmn; 3700 Fis
molecules would be needed to fill all Fis sites on a single chromosome.
Using the method of individual information we scanned the genome and found
68 552 Fis sites with >2 bits of sequence conservation. These estimates
are comparable to the number of Fis molecules per cell, which ranges from
close to zero in stationary cells to between 25 000 and 50 000 Fis dimers
per cell during the transition to exponential growth or an increase in nutrients
(<A NAME=bbr18_1 HREF=#bb18>18 </A>). Thus, almost every Fis site could
be filled by one Fis dimer under those growth conditions.
</SEC>

<H3><ST><A HREF="#top" NAME="hd11"><B>Fis sites at recombinational enhancers</B></A></ST></H3>
<P>
Fis sites have been identified on recombinational enhancers (<A NAME=bbr3_4
HREF=#bb3>3 </A>,<A NAME=bbr4_2 HREF=#bb4>4 </A>,<A NAME=bbr12_8 HREF=#bb12>12
</A>,<A NAME=bbr13_1 HREF=#bb13>13 </A>,<A NAME=bbr49_2 HREF=#bb49>49 </A>,<A
NAME=bbr50_1 HREF=#bb50>50 </A>). In the <I>S.typhimurium</I> <I>hin</I>
region there are two Fis sites that are proximal and distal to the <I>hix</I>L
recombination site. An information scan of this region shows a third potential
Fis site located 11 bp [~1 helical turn of DNA, 10.6 bp (<A NAME=bbr51_1
HREF=#bb51>51 </A>,<A NAME=bbr52_1 HREF=#bb52>52 </A>)] to the right of
and overlapping the proximal site (Fig. <A NAME=figr5_2 HREF=#fig5>5 </A>,
top left). We call this site the `medial' site; it is 37 bp (~3.5 helical
turns) to the left of the distal site. The same structure is found in bacteriophage
Mu <I>gin </I>and p15B <I>min</I> enhancers (Fig. <A NAME=figr5_3 HREF=#fig5>5
</A>, left three graphs).
<P>
In the bacteriophage P1 <I>cin</I>, bacteriophage P7 <I>cin</I>, and <I>E.coli
e14</I> <I>pin</I> enhancers, a potential overlapping site occurs 7 bp (~1/2
helical turn) to the left of the previously identified proximal site (Fig.
<A NAME=figr5_4 HREF=#fig5>5 </A>, right three graphs). Since this potential
site is outside the region between the proximal and distal sites, we named
it the `external' site.
<P>
At recombinational enhancer proximal Fis sites, when a potential new Fis
site is found on the right, it is 11 bases away while when a potential new
Fis site is found on the left, it is 7 bases away. It is not known whether
this correlation is coincidental. We also observed that potential Fis sites
corresponding in location to site III in <I>gin</I> (<A NAME=bbr9_3 HREF=#bb9>9
</A>) appear in all other enhancers scanned except <I>hin</I> and that in
three cases a weaker potential site falls exactly between the distal site
and the one corresponding to site III with spacings of 11-12 bp.
</SEC>
<SEC>
<H3><ST><A HREF="#top" NAME="hd12"><B>Predicted Fis sites are bound </B><B><I>in
vitro</I></B></A></ST></H3>
<P>
Scanning the Fis <I>R<SUB>iw</SUB></I>(<I>b</I>,<I>l</I>) model across DNA
inversion regions reveals pairs of Fis sites spaced either 7 or 11 bases
apart (Fig. <A NAME=figr5_5 HREF=#fig5>5 </A>). Three of the sites are the
footprinted proximal sites. In addition, the medial site for <I>gin</I>
is supported by footprint data, but it was not identified as a Fis site
(<A NAME=bbr9_4 HREF=#bb9>9 </A>). To test whether the proposed medial site
exists at <I>hin</I> we performed gel shift experiments on DNAs in which
we presumably had knocked out neither, one, or both of the sites. The DNA
design is shown in Figure <A NAME=figr6_2 HREF=#fig6>6 </A> using sequence
walkers, a graphical representation of the individual information content
at specific binding sites (<A NAME=bbr34_5 HREF=#bb34>34 </A>). Characters
representing the sequence are either oriented normally and placed above
a line indicating favorable contact, or upside-down and placed below the
line indicating unfavorable contact. Functional sites therefore have most
letters pointing upwards, while those we have destroyed have many letters
pointing downwards. The walkers also show that we did not inadvertently
create any other Fis sites. The results of shifting the <I>hin</I> sequences
of Figure <A NAME=figr6_3 HREF=#fig6>6 </A> are shown in Figure <A NAME=figr7_1
HREF=#fig7>7 </A>.
<P>
Under our experimental conditions <I>hin</I> does have a second site as
predicted, since the knockout of the stronger proximal site still allowed
the DNA to shift (Fig. <A NAME=figr7_2 HREF=#fig7>7 </A>, HL). However,
more Fis protein was required to shift an equivalent amount of DNA than
for the wild-type proximal site, indicating that Fis binds weakly to the
medial site. This is consistent with the weaker sequence conservation of
the medial site (<I>R<SUB>i</SUB></I> = 6.9 bits) compared to the proximal
site (<I>R<SUB>i</SUB></I> = 8.3 bits).
<P>
To further investigate the predictive ability of our individual information
model, we synthesized five oligonucleotides representing various interesting
sites:
<P>
<DIR>(i) We were curious as to whether the predicted <I>cin</I> external
site (7 bases from the proximal site, <I>R<SUB>i</SUB></I> = 2.8 bits),
could bind Fis, even though it is so close to the proximal site (Fig. <A
NAME=figr5_6 HREF=#fig5>5 </A>).</DIR>
<DIR>(ii) Four lines of evidence indicate that a site predicted to be at
-73 of the <I>tgt</I>/<I>sec</I> promoter (<I>R<SUB>i</SUB></I> = 10.8 bits,
Fig. <A NAME=figr1_6 HREF=#fig1>1 </A>, # 23) should bind Fis (see fig.
2 of ref. 34). We decided to test this prediction directly.</DIR>
<DIR>(iii) Footprinting data covers two overlapping sites spaced 11 bases
apart within the <I>nrd</I> promoter at -283 (<I>nrd</I>F1, <I>R<SUB>i</SUB></I>
= 14.6 bits) and -272 (<I>nrd</I>F2, <I>R<SUB>i</SUB></I> = 5.0 bits, Fig.
<A NAME=figr1_7 HREF=#fig1>1 </A>, #19) relative to the start of transcription
(Fig. <A NAME=figr3_3 HREF=#fig3>3 </A>), but only one site had been identified
(<A NAME=bbr44_2 HREF=#bb44>44 </A>). We decided to test both.</DIR>
<DIR>(iv) The site previously identified as Fis site II at 238 in the <I>aldB</I>
promoter (<A NAME=bbr53_3 HREF=#bb53>53 </A>) has a negative <I>R<SUB>i</SUB></I>
value (-5.3 bits) and therefore should not bind Fis.</DIR>
<P>

<P>
Figure <A NAME=figr8_1 HREF=#fig8>8 </A> shows that all four sites having
positive <I>R<SUB>i</SUB></I> values are able to bind Fis as predicted (<A
NAME=bbr33_6 HREF=#bb33>33 </A>). Although the gel is not quantitative,
the band intensities correlate well with information content:
<P>
<DIR>(i) The <I>cin</I> external site was bound weakly, suggesting that
it might be involved in site-specific inversion.</DIR>
<DIR>(ii) The site within the <I>tgt</I>/<I>sec</I> promoter region upstream
from the start site of the <I>queA</I> gene (<A NAME=bbr34_6 HREF=#bb34>34
</A>) had been previously footprinted (<A NAME=bbr45_2 HREF=#bb45>45 </A>),
however, that footprint extended up to and included a DNase I hypersensitive
region at -79. In addition, a secondary shift product was observed when
that DNA was used in a gel shift experiment. There are actually two adjacent
Fis sites in that region since we were able to shift the one at -73.</DIR>
<DIR>(iii) The two sites within the <I>nrd</I> promoter were also previously

separated they are individually able to bind Fis. These two sites are likely
to be responsible for the single protected region seen on the published
footprint from -268 to -285.</DIR>
<DIR>(iv) Although it had been observed as a protected region on a footprint
(<A NAME=bbr53_4 HREF=#bb53>53 </A>), the site II at coordinate 238 of the
<I>aldB</I> promoter has an information content of -5.3 bits and, as expected,
it did not shift. This justifies excluding it from the list of known sites
(see Materials and Methods). We propose that the DNase I protection observed
could be an artifact due to secondary structures formed by a DNA-Fis complex
when Fis binds to other surrounding sites. A requirement for binding multiple
Fis molecules could explain why a high concentration of Fis is required
for protection (<A NAME=bbr53_5 HREF=#bb53>53 </A>). Alternatively, it could
represent a special binding mode of Fis.</DIR>
<P>
In summary, we have shown that information theory can be used to predict
Fis binding sites, and we have confirmed some of those sites experimentally.
Furthermore, information theory can also predict when a sequence is unlikely
to be a binding site. The information theory models can be applied to any
nucleic acid binding interaction, so they provide a general tool for researchers
to identify and characterize binding sites.
