<a name="summary"></a>
<span class="xxlarge">Summary of Results</span>
<!----------------------------------------------------------------------------->
<p>
<a name="summary_psi"></a>
<a href="#cone_angle_variation"><span class="large">Variation of Precession Cone Angle</span></a>
<ul>
	<li><a href="#cone_angle_variation">Three cases</a> are shown for precession 
        cone angles (i.e., nominal Sun angles) of 35, 40, and 45 degrees.  
        Larger than 45 degrees runs into trouble with shield size (hardware 
        complexity and cost, in addition to increase of perturbations).  Smaller 
        than 35 degrees and we won't meet the (most likely definition of the) 
        mission requirements.
        <p>
    <li>In general, the mission astrometric errors degrade as the nominal Sun angle decreases.
        <p>
	<li>We do very well in <a href="#latitude_histo">latitude errors</a>: histogram 
        peaks are around 26-28 uas, and 100 percent of the sky is better than 
        37 uas even for the worst case.  Hence, with respect to latitude errors, 
        mission requirements are not affected by nominal Sun angle in the range 35-45 degrees.
        <p>
	<li>Nearly 100 percent of the sky is 50 uas or better in both 
        <a href="#longitude_histo">longitude</a> (99.99%) and 
        <a href="#parallax_histo">parallax</a> (98.1%) for the 45 degree case.  
        Going to 40 degrees costs us about 18 percent of the sky at 50 uas in 
        parallax, and it costs about 14 percent of the sky at 50 uas in position 
        in longitude.  At 35 degrees nominal Sun angle the requirement of 90 percent
        of the sky at 50 uas or better is not met. 
        <p>
	<li>However, even at 35 degrees, the worst <a href="#parallax_histo">parallax</a> error 
        is only 69 uas and the worst <a href="#longitude_histo">longitude</a> error is only 67 uas.
        <p>
	<li><a href="#vlong_histo">Proper motion in longitude</a> significantly fails 
        the 90 percent of the sky, &lt;50 uas/yr target for all three precession 
        cone angles.  
        <p>
    <li>The parallax requirement of 90 percent of the sky better than 50 uas restricts
        the precession cone angle to greater than or equal to 43 degrees.
        <p>
	<li>Ratios of errors in the ecliptic region to those in the polar regions get 
        substantially worse for longitude and parallax as you decrease the nominal 
        Sun angle from 45 degrees to 35 degrees (see the plot of median values by 
        latitude, shown in <a href="#medians">Figure 12</a>).  The corresponding 
        ratios get slightly better for errors in latitude, but the difference is 
        so small as to not matter.
        <p>
	<li>The <a href="#counts_histo">observation count</a> is roughly in the range
        600-1000 for most areas of the sky.  The dominant histogram peak shifts 
        towards lower counts as the precession cone angle decreases.
        <p>
	<li>A test was run during which normal points were simulated whenever more than
        one CCD was encountered during a focal plane crossing.  In such cases, the
        weight of the observation was proportional to the number of CCDs in the
        given column.  There was almost no difference in the resulting astrometric error
        distributions.
        <p>
	<li>The <a href="#allsky_counts">FAME observation density distribution</a> is 
        much more uniform than that of <a href="#allsky_counts_HIPPARCOS">HIPPARCOS</a>, 
        especially with a <a href="#allsky_counts_5yr">5-year extended mission</a>.  
        It is, however, affected by <a href="#allsky_counts_Tphi">changes in the precession period</a>.
        <p>

<p>
<a name="summary_Tphi"></a>
<a href="#precession_variation"><span class="large">Variation of Precession Period</span></a>
<ul>
    <li>The structure of the <a href="#allsky_counts_Tphi">observation density distribution</a>
        on the sky is sensitive to the precession period.  Coverage is more uniform
        for smaller precession periods.
        <p>
	<li>However, there is almost no difference in the mission astrometric errors for precession 
        periods in the range 15 to 30 days.
        <p>
    <li>Results begin to deteriorate around 30 days, which therefore probably represents
        a reasonable upper bound on the precession period.
</ul>
<p>
<a name="summary_5yr"></a>
<a href="#5yr"><span class="large">5-Year Extended Mission</span></a>
<ul>
    <li>The observation density is smoother -- longitudinal ribbing effect
        is lessened (compare the observation density all-sky image of the
        <a href="#allsky_counts">2.5-year</a> case to that of the 
        <a href="#allsky_counts_5yr">5-year</a> case).
        <p>
    <li>All errors fall <i>well</i> within the 50 uas or uas/yr mission requirements.
        <p>
    <li>Position in latitude and both proper motion components are entirely
        less than 25 uas or uas/yr.
        <p>
    <li>The polar cap parallax feature is at about 21 uas.

<a name="sim_details"></a>
<span class="xxlarge">Simulation Details</span>
<!----------------------------------------------------------------------------->
<ul>
	<li>Introduction and geometry are discussed in the Technical Memorandum,
        <img src="graphics/PDFsmall.gif" align="top" width=15 height=16 alt="PDF document"><a href="../../Papers/ObservationDensity/Geometry.pdf">The 
        Observation Density and Scan Angle Distributions of a Spin-Stabilized, 
        Precessing, Scanning Satellite Instrument: I. Geometry</a> (FTM2000-16).
        <p>
	<li>1-<span class="sym">s</span> scan-direction single-measurement error = 580 uas.  This sets
        the units for the results: uas for parallax and position, and uas/yr for 
        proper motions.
        <p>
	<li>grid = [341,171], evenly spaced in [longitude, sin latitude]
        <p>
	<li><span class="sym">D</span>t = amount of time to scan across one grid cell at equator 
        in longitude direction
        <ul>
            <li type="disc">determined by the grid size specification and the spin period
        </ul>
        <p>
    <li>spin periods: 35, 40, and 45 minutes
        <p>
    <li>precession periods: 15, 20, and 30 days
        <p>
    <li>precession cone angles: 35, 40, and 45 deg
        <p>
	<li>simulation times: 2.5 years and 5 years
        <p>
    <li>2 viewports
        <p>
    <li>basic angle = 81.5 deg
        <p>
	<li>Observations were performed by between 1 and 4 detectors per viewport.  
        The number of detectors corresponds to the number of astrometric CCDs in one of the
        9 CCD columns on the focal plane.  The column was chosen randomly for each
        focal plane crossing of a grid cell.
        <p>
    <li>astrometric CCD count by column: 123242321
        <p>
    <li>An option for calculating normal points was available for multiple CCD encounters
        per focal plane crossing.
        <p>
    <li>Least squares solutions for the astrometric parameters and their errors were
        performed for each grid cell.  A detailed description of the method is 
        available in the Technical Memorandum,
        <img src="graphics/PDFsmall.gif" align="top" width=15 height=16 alt="PDF document"><a href="../../Papers/ObservationDensity/ParameterEstimation.pdf">Astrometric 
        Parameter Estimation Suitable for Simulations</a> (FTM2000-17).
</ul>

<a name="histo_behavior"></a>
<span class="xlarge">Histogram Characteristics and Behavior</span>
<p>
<img src="zones.gif" width="450" height="608" alt="" border="0"><br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;">Figure 5</span>&nbsp;&nbsp;Illustration of 
contributions to the histograms of the ecliptic band,
the "trough" regions, and the polar cap regions. (45-degree case used for
this illustration.)  The trough regions span 6 degrees in latitude, centered on the
high-density latitude <span class="sym">|b| = p/2 - y</span>, where <span class="sym">y</span> 
is the precession cone angle (nominal Sun angle).  The polar cap regions are the areas of the sky
of higher latitude than the troughs, and the ecliptic band is the region of the 
sky lower in latitude than the troughs.  For smaller precession cone angle, the
troughs move to higher latitude, the polar cap area shrinks, and the ecliptic
band grows.  Therefore, we expect the following characteristics and behavior
of histograms of the astrometric errors as the precession cone angle decreases:
<ul>
    <li><span style="font-weight: bold;">parallax</span>
    <ul>
        <li>In general, the best parallaxes are obtained in the polar cap regions.  The closer
            <span class="sym">|b|</span> approaches 90 degrees, the more homogeneous 
            the scan angle distribution and the more circular the parallactic ellipse.
            In addition, the observation density is generally higher in the polar regions than
            in the ecliptic regions.  These conspire to yield better (i.e., smaller) 
            parallax errors in the polar cap regions.
        <li>The peak of the distribution moves left toward better accuracy and shrinks in area.
        <li>The low-accuracy tail moves right toward poorer accuracy and grows in area.
        <li>The high-accuracy trough "bump" moves left toward better accuracy.
        <li>The ratio of median errors near the ecliptic to median errors near the poles
            increases.
    </ul>
    <li><span style="font-weight: bold;">longitude</span>
    <ul>
        <li>Because of (1) the cone-shaped distribution of scan angle at the ecliptic 
            and (2) the lower observation density near the ecliptic,
            longitude errors are at their worst there.
        <li>The high-accuracy peak moves left toward better accuracy and shrinks in area.
        <li>The low-accuracy peak moves right toward poorer accuracy and grows in area.
        <li>The ratio of median errors near the ecliptic to median errors near the poles
            increases.
        <li>The peaks due to the high-accuracy trough contribution move left toward better accuracy.
    </ul>
    <li><span style="font-weight: bold;">latitude</span>
    <ul>
        <li>The latitude errors are largely unaffected by the cone-shaped scan angle distribution 
            (the cone is oriented along local ecliptic meridians), so the contributions from
            the three regions overlap, forming a single histogram peak of relatively high
            accuracy (compared to longitude).  
        <li>The histogram peak moves slightly left toward better accuracy.
    </ul>
    <li><span style="font-weight: bold;">proper motion in longitude</span>
    <ul>
        <li>Same as position in longitude.
    </ul>
    <li><span style="font-weight: bold;">proper motion in latitude</span>
    <ul>
        <li>Same as position in latitude.
    </ul>
</ul>
