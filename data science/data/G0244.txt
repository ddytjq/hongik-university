<FONT COLOR="#660000">Unless a body is acted upon by an outside influence

<FONT COLOR="#993300">F = ma</FONT></H3></CENTER>

<UL>
<LI>

<LI>
<I>Force </I>is the outside influence (dynes - like our Chancellor - in

<LI>
<I>Acceleration</I> is the change in motion; this can be speeding up, slowing

<H3>
<A HREF="http://dept.physics.upenn.edu/~www/gladney/mathphys/java/sect5/subsection5_1_1.html">Gravity</A> - The First Fundamental Force</H3>

Newton knew from his Third Law that the Force of Gravity must be reciprocal 
between the two masses involved.  By applying his newly developed mathematical
tool called <i>fluctions</i> (<i>i.e.</i> calculus), to the orbit of the Moon 
around the Earth, he was able to determine that the force must depend upon the 
<i>inverse square</i> of the distance between the Earth and the Moon.
The gravitational force between the Earth and the Moon, or any two 
masses, is given by
<p><Center><Table>
<td align="left" valign="center"><img src="./images/physics/earth.gif"></td>
<td align="right" valign="center">
<img src="./images/doodads/to_r.gif"></td>
<td align="right" valign="center">
<img src="./images/doodads/clearsp.gif" width=20>
<img src="./images/physics/F_grav.gif">
<img src="./images/doodads/clearsp.gif" width=20></td>
<td align="right" valign="center">
<img valign="center" src="./images/doodads/to_bw.gif"></td>
<td align="right" valign="center">
<img src="./images/physics/moon.gif"></td></Table></Center>
<p>
where<SUB><IMG SRC="./images/physics/m_earth.gif"></SUB>is the mass of the 
Earth, and<SUB> <IMG SRC="./images/physics/m_moon.gif"></SUB> is 
the mass of the Moon, <b>d</b> is the distance between the Earth and the Moon, 
and <b>G</b> is Newton's Gravitational constant
(<b>G</b> = 6.67 x 10<sup>-8</sup>dyne<sup>.</sup>cm<sup>2</sup>/g<sup>2</sup>)
<A HREF="http://www.astronomynotes.com/gravappl/gravappla.htm">
Gravity</A> is such a weak force that Newton's constant could not be measured 
at that time.  The first to estimate the value of <b>G</b> was astronomer 
<a href="http://www-groups.dcs.st-and.ac.uk/~history/Mathematicians/Maskelyne.html">Nevil Maskelyne</a>. He used the deflection of a plumbline on the 
slopes of Mt. Schiehallion in Scotland to determine the gravitational 
attraction between the plumbob and the mountain in 1774.  From Schiehallion's
very regular shape, Maskelyne was able to estimate the mountain's mass and 
thus determine the value of <b>G</b>.
<a href="http://www.astro.virginia.edu/~eww6n/bios/Cavendish.html"> Henry 
Cavendish</a> was the first to measure <b>G</b> in the laboratory 
<p>

Despite the fact that Gravity is the weakest of the fundamental forces, 
because it is a purely attractive, long-range force, we will see that Gravity
dominates many areas of astronomy. These include the orbits of planets,stars,
and galaxies, the life cycles of stars, and the evolution of the Universe.
<p>

The form of the equation for electrical force is the same as that for gravity,
but there are some key differences:
<ul>
<li> There is only one kind of mass whereas there are two kinds of electrical 
charge - positive and negative - which behave in opposite fashion.
<li> Gravitation is purely attractive, but the electrical force can be either 
attractive or repulsive - like charges repel, opposite charges attract.

</Table></Center><BR>
<li> Moving charges produce and respond to the <i>magnetic force</i>. 
It turns out that the electric and magnetic forces are different  
manifestations of the same thing, due to Special Relativity. For this 
reason, astronomers &amp; physicists speak of the "Electromagnetic Force".
</ul>

Although the Electromagnetic force is of order 10<sup><FONT SIZE=+1>39
</FONT></sup> times stronger than Gravity, it is not as apparent because of 
its dual (attractive/repulsive) nature. This causes most regions of space to 
be electrically neutral.  The forces which prevent you from falling through 
the floor or your chair, the forces that are exerted when you push an object,
(friction, etc.) are all examples of the electromagnetic force in action.<p>

<font size=+1 color="blue"><u>The Weak Force</u></font>
<br><br>
The Weak Force is about 10<sup>-13</sup> times as strong as the 
Electromagnetic force, with a range of only 10<sup>-16</sup> centimeters.
The Weak Force moderates certain kinds of nuclear decays such as the neutron 
decay shown below. The most common particle which interacts only via the Weak
Force is the <i>neutrino</i>, symbol Greek "nu" below.  We shall hear more 
about the neutrino when we talk about Nuclear Reactions &amp; Stellar 
Evolution and Cosmology.<p>

<Center><img src="./images/physics/n_decay_blue.gif"></Center>

<p>
We now know that the Weak Force is an aspect of a 
<a href="http://www.cern.ch/Public/SCIENCE/unitingForces.html">"unified 
force"</a> called the <i>"Electro-Weak Force"</i> which combines the 
properties of the Weak Force and the Electromagnetic 
Force.  Many physicists believe that all of the forces are really different 
aspects of a single "Grand Unified Force."  Progress is being made toward 
combining the Strong Nuclear Force with the Electroweak Force, but as yet how 
to include Gravity remains a problem.
<p>

<font size=+1 color="blue"><u>The Strong (Nuclear) Force</u></font>
<br>

The Strong Nuclear Force (also called the "nuclear force" or the "strong
force") holds the atomic nucleus together. To do this, it must overcome the 
tremendous repulsion that arises when the positively charged protons 
are packed together into the small space of the nucleus. While the nuclear
force is the strongest of the fundamental forces, it has a very short range, 
effectively only the scale of the atomic nucleus ~ 10<sup><FONT SIZE=+1>-13
</FONT></sup>cm. So,
the strong force can only overcome the electrical repulsion when the protons 
are close enough to be "touching". Because neutrons interact with protons via
the Strong Force, but have no charge, more and more neutrons are necessary to
"dilute" the repulsive forces and hold heavy nuclei together.<p>

<a name="light"><font size=+2>
<A HREF="http://imagine.gsfc.nasa.gov/docs/science/know_l1/emspectrum.html">
THE ELECTROMAGNETIC SPECTRUM</A></font></a>
<p>
For this part of the tutorial we will treat light in terms of its 
<A HREF="http://csep10.phys.utk.edu/astr162/lect/light/waves.html">wave</A>
properties. (We will see in the tutorial on <a href="./Planck.html">Atomic 
Structure</a> that light 
also behaves as a "particle" called a <I>photon</I>).<BR> 
<CENTER><IMG SRC="./images/physics/wave.gif"></CENTER>
<P>
We characterize a wave by its: 
<ul>
<li><FONT COLOR=BLUE>frequency</FONT> 
(<img src="./images/doodads/nu_low.gif">; - Greek "nu"): 
the number of wavecrests per second (units: Hz or 1/s).
<li><FONT COLOR=BLUE>wavelength</FONT> 
(<img src="./images/doodads/lambda_low.gif"> - Greek 
"lambda"): distance between wavecrests (units: cm or &Aring;  
&Aring;ngstroms = 
10<sup>-8</sup>cm; you will also see nm = 10<sup>-9</sup>m = 
10<sup>-7</sup>cm = 10&Aring;)
<li><FONT COLOR=BLUE>speed</FONT>, c = 3 x 10<SUP>10</SUP>cm/s in vacuum. 
</ul>
If  <img src="./images/doodads/nu_low.gif"> wavecrests pass a point 
each second, 
each separated by <img src="./images/doodads/lambda_low.gif"> cm, 
then the speed must be:
<p><center><FONT COLOR=BLUE>speed</FONT> = c = 
<img src="./images/doodads/nu_low.gif"> x 
<img src="./images/doodads/lambda_low.gif"> =<FONT COLOR=BLUE>
 frequency</FONT> x <FONT COLOR=BLUE>wavelength</FONT>
</center><p>
The wave can also be characterized by its <FONT COLOR=BLUE>energy</FONT>,
 E:<p>
<center> Energy = h <sup>.</sup> <img src="./images/doodads/nu_low.gif">
</center>
<p>
where <i>h</i> = 6.625 x 10<sup>-27</sup>erg-sec is <i>Planck's Constant</i>.
For example KGB radio, 101.5 FM, has a frequency = 101.5
megahertz = 101,500,000 cycles/sec.  This must correspond to a wavelength 
of about 295cm between peaks in the electric and magnetic fields of the wave.
The energy of this radio photon is 6.7 x 10<sup>-19</sup>erg.  Considering 
that an erg is about the energy that a fly expends taking off from a wall,
it's apparent that radio waves don't carry much energy.  (Considering the
long wavelengths of radio waves, it also doesn't make much sense to talk 
about radio photons!)
<P>
Visible light, radio waves, microwaves, x-rays; they are all <FONT COLOR=BLUE>
electromagnetic radiation</FONT>. They each have a certain range of 
wavelengths and energies, and they represent a region in the Electromagnetic
Spectrum:<BR><BR>

<LI>

<h3>Atmospheric Transmission</h3>

Not all wavelengths of light are transmitted through the Earth's atmosphere
(fortunately for us).  The Earth's 
<a href="http://csep10.phys.utk.edu/astr162/lect/light/windows.html">
atmospheric windows</a> are shown below.
<p><center><table><td align=center>
<img src="./images/emsurface.gif"><br><font color="blue"><b>
Transmission of the Earth's atmosphere; the white region indicates the 
altitudes where light<br> 
from the different spectral region does not penetrate.
<i>From NASA's</i></font>
<a href="http://imagine.gsfc.nasa.gov/docs/introduction/emspectrum.html">
Imagine</a></td></table>
</center>
<p>

<i>Atoms</i> and <i>ions</i> in the Earth's upper atmosphere absorb
gamma-rays, x-rays, and ultraviolet light; the atmosphere is opaque to
these three forms of radiation.  (The UV which is responsible for sunburn
and skin cancer is "very-violet" visible light by our astronomical
definition.)  The atmosphere is transparent to visible light.  <i>Molecules</i>
(H<sub>2</sub>O, CO<sub>2</sub>, CH<sub>4</sub>...)  absorb most wavelengths 
of infrared light; there are windows at 1.25&micro;m, 1.65&micro;m,
2.0-2.5&micro;m, 3-4&micro;m, 4.5-5.5&micro;m,8-12&micro;m, and
17-25&micro;m.  Then the atmosphere is largely opaque out to millimeter 
wavelengths.  The atmosphere is transparent through the Radio to meter 
wavelengths, where the ionisphere reflects radio waves.
<p>
<font size=+1><a href="http://www.astronomynotes.com/telescop/telescpc.htm">
Atmospheric Seeing</a></font>

The Earth's atmosphere has another negative effect on astronomical research.
Turbulence in the Earth's atmosphere produces the twinkling of the stars
which astronomers call seeing.  Turbulent cells in the Earth's atmosphere act 
like small lenses, refracting light rays as they pass through and blurring an 
image.  This site has a graphic demonstration of the effects of 
<a href="http://astrosun.tn.cornell.edu/courses/astro201/seeing.htm">
seeing</a> on photographs, and here is an 
<a href="./images/anim/betelgeuse.mpg">
mpeg animation</a> of Betelgeuse, from the 4-m Herschel Telescope in the 
Canary Islands,courtesy of the <a href="http://op.ph.ic.ac.uk/">Applied Optics 
Group</a>, Imperial College, London.
