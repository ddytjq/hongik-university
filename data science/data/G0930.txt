<u>First, there are probably some questions on your mind:</u></font>
<p>
<ol>
<Font size=+1 color="burgundy">
<li>If the Universe is expanding, what is it expanding into?</Font>
<ul>
<li>Ask <a href="http://www.drscience.com/vault/960215.htm">Dr. Science</a>.
<li><i>Nothing</i>, the very fabric of space and time are expanding.
<li>For a more complete &amp; satisfactory answer ask
<a href="http://www.astro.ucla.edu/~wright/cosmology_faq.html#XIN">Dr. Ned 
Wright</a> at UCLA.
</ul><br>
<Font size=+1 color="burgundy">
<li>What happened before the Big Bang?</Font>
<ul>
<li><i>We don't know. Quite probably we can't know.</i> Before about 
	10<sup>-43</sup>s our current laws
	of physics are inadequate to describe the Universe.  For example,
	we would need a theory of Gravity that is consistent with Quantum
	Mechanics, which we don't yet have.  Even as our understanding of
	Physics improves, we may get closer and closer in time to the Big 
	Bang, but may be prohibited from understanding the "moment of creation"
	or what came before.	
<li>Dr. Bruce Margon &amp; Dr. Craig Hogan at the Univ. of Washington have a
<a href="http://www1.cac.washington.edu/alumni/columns/march96/universe1.html">
good answer</a>.
<li><a href="http://www.astro.ucla.edu/~wright/cosmology_faq.html#XIN">Dr. Ned 
Wright</a> has an answer for this one too.
</ul><br>
<Font size=+1 color="burgundy">
<li>If everything is moving away from us, 
doesn't that put us at the center of the Universe?</Font>
<ul>
<li>No, everything is moving away from everything else; everyplace looks like 
the center, but there really is no center.
<li>Ned Wright's <a href="http://www.astro.ucla.edu/~wright/Balloon.html">
Balloon Analogy</a> helps.
</ul>
</ol><p>
<font size=+2 color="burgundy">
<u>The Geometry of the Universe</u></font>
<p>
General Relativity allows for three geometries of Spacetime in the Universe 
depending on the density of the Universe and the Universal fate:
<table><td>
<img src="./images/sp_geom.gif"></td><td align=top><font color="limegreen">
<b>
Hyperbolic (Open) Geometry<br></font></b>
<ul><b>
<li>Parallel lines diverge.

<li>V<sub>sphere</sub> > 4/3 <img src="./images/greek/pi.gif">R<sup>3</sup>.
<li>Universe is unbound (expands forever).
<li><img src="./images/greek/Omega.gif"> < 1 (<sub>
<img src="./images/greek/rho.gif"></sub> < 10<sup>-29</sup>g/cm<sup>3</sup>).
</ul>
<hr>
<p>
Flat (Euclidean) Geometry<br>
<ul>
<li>Parallel lines remain at a constant distance.

<li>V<sub>sphere</sub> = 4/3 <img src="./images/greek/pi.gif">R<sup>3</sup>
<li>Universe continuously slows down.
<li><img src="./images/greek/Omega.gif"> = 1 (<sub>
<img src="./images/greek/rho.gif"></sub> = 10<sup>-29</sup>g/cm<sup>3</sup>).
</ul>
<hr>
<p><font color="red">
Spherical (Closed) Geometry</font><br>
<ul>
<li>Parallel lines converge.

<li>V<sub>sphere</sub> < 4/3 <img src="./images/greek/pi.gif">R<sup>3</sup>
<li>Universe is bound (will collapse back)
<li><img src="./images/greek/Omega.gif"> > 1 
(<sub><img src="./images/greek/rho.gif"></sub> > 
10<sup>-29</sup>g/cm<sup>3</sup>).
</ul>
</td></table>

<u>We Live in a Curved Space</u></font>
<p>

<p><table><td><b>Before you go off thinking that this is all to wierd to try 
to grasp, remember that we live on a spherically curved surface, called Earth. 
Lines of longitude, <i>parallel</i> at the equator, follow the surface of the 
Earth, converging at the poles. <p>
Also, circles may not be what they seemed in high school geometry.  Suppose 
you started drawing circles around 
the North Pole, keeping the pole at the center.  Measuring the relation between
Circumference and radius, you would find that for small circles: 
<br><br><center>
C = 2<img src="./images/greek/pi.gif">R 
</center><br>
(Space always seems flat on small scales; that's why  there are still people 
who believe the Earth is flat!).  But continuing to draw ever increasing 
circles (lines of latitude) you will finally draw the biggest circle that 
you can on the earth -
around the equator.  Now the circumference of the circle is the circumference 
of the Earth.  The radius, <i>measured along the curved surface from the pole 
to the equator</i> is 1/4 of the circumference of the earth:
<br><br><center>
C = 4R < 2<img src="./images/greek/pi.gif">R 
</center><br></td><td><img src="./images/gr8crcl.gif"><br><br><center>
Diagram from Ned Wright's 
<a href="http://www.astro.ucla.edu/~wright/cosmolog.htm">
Cosmology Tutorial</a> <br> 
&copy; Edward L. Wright (UCLA), used with permission.</center></td></table>

<u>Cosmological Tests</u></font>
<p>
<ol>
<li>Measure the density of matter directly.  Well we can't do that; the best 
we can do is to measure the density of luminous matter in the Universe -
count the number of galaxies in a region of space, then multiply by the mass
of a galaxy.  When we do this, we find that the density of luminous matter in
the Universe is about 1% of the critical density 
(<img src="./images/greek/Omega.gif"><sub>lum</sub> ~ 0.01 or 
<sub><img src="./images/greek/rho.gif"></sub> ~ 
10<sup>-31</sup>g/cm<sup>3</sup>).  But 
what mass do we use for a galaxy?  That is what about Dark Matter?  If we 
correct for the amount of known dark matter, say by the factor of 10 required
to bind galaxy clusters we are at about 10% of the critical density.  But
<u><i>we really don't know how much dark matter is out there</u></i>.  It is 
entirely plausible that
