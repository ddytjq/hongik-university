          29, session of Center Conversations at the In- ternational Motor
          Racing Research Center at Watkins Glen.</p>

          competition and other series have changed over the years. Oliver has

      <h3><!--mstheme--><font face="Arial, helvetica, geneva" color="#000080">Sandra Leitzinger to be Featured in Exhibit&nbsp;<!--mstheme--></font></h3>
      <p>The art of Sandra Leitzinger will be featured in a special exhibit
      hosted by the International Motor Racing Research Center at Watkins Glen
      June 21-23 during Watkins Glen International's Sports Car Grand Prix
      weekend.&nbsp;</p>
      <p>The exhibit honors the memory of motorsports artist Leitzinger, who
      died Jan. 9 at home in State College, Pa. She was 65.&nbsp;</p>
      <p>Leitzinger was the wife of Bob Leitzinger, long-time IMSA and SCCA
      sports car racer, and mother of road-racing star Butch Leitzinger. Her
      children Chuck and Karen Kurtz also have been involved in motorsports. The
      art show is presented in cooperation with Leitzinger's family. The Center
      has works by Leitzinger in its collection, including a print of her
      signature piece, &quot;Drivers Meeting.&quot;&nbsp;</p>
      <p>The Center was honored when Leitzinger's family designated it as a
      recipient for memorials in her name. The Center is working with her family
      to determine an appropriate use for the donated funds. The Center will
      have additional Sunday hours of 9 a.m. to 5 p.m. for the art show.</p>
      <h3><!--mstheme--><font face="Arial, helvetica, geneva" color="#000080"><a name="Rand" target="main">Michael Rand</a> Named Executive Director of
      International Motor Racing Research Center<!--mstheme--></font></h3>
      <p>WATKINS GLEN — The International Motor Racing Research Center at
      Watkins Glen has named Michael M. Rand as Executive Director.</p>
      <p>The Research Center, which opened its doors in 1999, is dedicated to
      preserving the heritage of international motor sports in all of its
      dimensions and variety. The impressive and ever-growing archival
      collection includes not only road racing and oval-track racing, but
      preserves materials encompassing the history of the entire spectrum of
      motor racing.</p>
      <p>Mr. Rand, 54, joins the Research Center from a distinguished career in
      motor racing that has included positions in marketing, management,
      business development and operations management. He was most recently Vice
      President and General Manager of Virginia International Raceway, where he
      oversaw the rebirth of that facility after 25 years of closure.
      Previously, he was Vice President and General Manager of Lime Rock race
      circuit in Connecticut.</p>
      <p>Mr. Rand’s background includes management of sanctioning bodies and
      race series. He was General Manager of the International Motor Sports
      Association and of the Sportscar Vintage Racing Association. An
      accomplished race driver himself, Mr. Rand was manager of the Skip Barber
      Racing School and General Manager of the Barber Saab professional racing
      series.</p>
      <p>“Today marks an important step in the history of this one-of-kind
      facility,” said John Bishop, President of the Racing Research Center
      Council. “Mike Rand brings great experience and leadership that will
      continue the growth and recognition of the Center as a repository for
      international motor sports archives and as a resource for journalists,
      historians and researchers.”</p>
      <p>Racing legend Sir Jackie Stewart, a member of the Center’s Drivers
      Council, helped identify the unique position the Center occupies in his
      remarks upon presiding over the opening of the facility.</p>
      <p>“I am very excited with what has been created at Watkins Glen. The
      International Motor Racing Research Center is an extraordinary resource
      and treasure for our sport,” Stewart said. “There are wonderful racing
      museums all over the world, but at Watkins Glen you have created a
      facility and staff dedicated to preserving the archival heritage of motor
      racing and making it available to both the serious historian as well as
      the more casual racing enthusiast.”</p>
      <p>“I cannot imagine a more interesting and worthwhile undertaking
      within our industry,” Rand said. “As a lifelong enthusiast for all
      types of motor sport, I am extremely excited with the challenges and
      opportunities presented by this impressive and significant facility.”</p>
      <p>At Watkins Glen, Rand will head a staff that includes librarians,
      archivists and historians. His responsibility will include day-to-day
      management of the Research Center as well as overall planning and
      direction and development of an endowment fund.</p>
      <h3><!--mstheme--><font face="Arial, helvetica, geneva" color="#000080">Historic 'Alligator' at Research Center<!--mstheme--></font></h3>
      <p>WATKINS GLEN - The car that Miles Collier drove to victory in the 1949
      Watkins Glen Grand Prix is now on display at the International Motor
      Racing Research Center at Watkins Glen. Known as the &quot;Ardent
      Alligator&quot; the car is a 1929 Brooklands Riley modified by the Collier
      brothers and fitted with a 3.9-liter Mercury Flathead engine and
      drivetrain.&nbsp;</p>
      <p>The car's history is as colorful as its bright red body and yellow
      wheels. Owner Pete McManus of Thornton, Pa., will discuss that history at
      the monthly Center Conversations on Saturday, March 16. The free talk will
      be at 1 p.m.&nbsp;</p>
      <p align="left"><img border="0" src="images/sitephotos/alligator.jpg" lowsrc="alligator" alt="alligator" align="left" width="400" height="273">Miles
      and Sam Collier brought the Alligator to the United States in 1934 and
      raced it with its original Riley engine for the Automobile Racing Club of
      America, predecessor to today's Sports Car Club of America. They exchanged
      the engine and mechanical components for a 1939 Mercury flathead V-8
      engine and drivetrain shortly before World War II.&nbsp;</p>
      <p>In post-war racing, the car's most significant victory was at Watkins
      Glen in 1949, when Miles Collier passed Briggs Cunningham on the last lap.
      Cunningham was at the wheel of a Type 166 Ferrari Spyder, the first
      Ferrari to race in the U.S. The car's nickname, &quot;Ardent
      Alligator,&quot; was painted on its hood for the 1949 race. The name was a
      reference to the Colliers' home area in south Florida, west of the
      Everglades. Cameron Argetsinger of Burdett, founder of the Watkins Glen
      races, owned the Alligator for a while in the early 1950s.&nbsp;</p>
      <p>The Alligator continued to be raced until 1955. It was later destroyed
      when a building collapsed on it during a fire. A full restoration
      followed. The Alligator now competes in vintage sports car racing.&nbsp;</p>
      <p>The Alligator is will be on display until April.</p>
