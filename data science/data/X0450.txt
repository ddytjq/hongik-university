	<td width="75%">
		<b>Ottawa hot under collar over soldering gun                                                          </b><br>
		<font size=2>Author: Lorne Gunter                                       <br>
		Source: Windsor Star                                                                                                                                          </font>
	</td>

	<td align=right VALIGN=TOP>		
				Mon.           April 8, 2002
	</td>
	</tr>
	<tr>
		<td colspan=2><br>
		<br>
				<P align=justify>Stop, I've got a soldering gun and I'm not afraid to use it! </P>
<P align=justify>To be honest, I don't own a soldering gun. If I did, as with most power tools, I wouldn't have a clue what to do with it. </P>
<P align=justify>But Brian Buckley has a soldering gun. I imagine he's pretty handy with it, too. </P>
<P align=justify>Buckley owns an auto body shop in Oak Lake, Man., a community of about 500 along the TransCanada highway between Virden and Brandon. </P>
<P align=justify>Buckley is also a recreational gun owner opposed to Ottawa's firearms registry. </P>
<P align=justify>He has a valid licence to possess firearms. As required by the Firearms Act, more than a year ago he went to all the trouble and expense of registering himself as an owner of firearms. </P>
<P align=justify>He filled out the complicated and intrusive licensing forms, submitted his life to scrutiny by federal bureaucrats and police and did so within the deadline stipulated in the law. </P>
<P align=justify>He didn't want to, but he complied anyway with the legal requirement that all Canadians who wish to own firearms obtain a possession licence from Ottawa. </P>
<P align=justify>In other words, he put himself through all the hoops imposed by the Canadian Firearms Program to certify that he is fit to own guns. </P>
<P align=justify>But when around Christmas, Buckley received in the mail the forms for registering his guns (gun owners had until the end of 2000 to register themselves and to the end of this year to register their guns), he decided to have a bit of fun with the federal Liberals and their gun registry. </P>
<P align=justify>He decided to see whether he could register his Black &amp; Decker heat gun and his Weller soldering gun, neither of which is obviously a firearm. </P>
<P align=justify>Heck, I own neither power tools nor firearms and even I know a soldering gun isn't a firearm. </P>
<P align=justify>I guess if a robber had a long enough extension cord, he could take his soldering gun to a convenience store and threaten to give the clerk a nasty burn if he/she didn't fork over the contents of the till. Or perhaps a robber could plug one in at a bank and then menacingly warn the tellers, "Hey, when this baby warms up in about 15 or 20 minutes, I'm gonna hold this place up like you won't believe!" </P>
<P align=justify>Buckley's registration certificate is hilarious. On the line that reads "Make:" it declares "Black &amp; Decker/Weller." </P>
<P align=justify><STRONG>"Type: HEATGUN/SOLDERING GUN."</STRONG> </P>
<P align=justify><EM>"Action: 110 VOLT AC."</EM> </P>
<P align=justify>It's very clear that human beings are not processing registration applications. </P>
<P align=justify><STRONG>Processed by computer</STRONG> </P>
<P align=justify>The forms are, apparently, being received and scanned into a computer. If they contain information on specific guns the computer has been programmed to reject, one presumes the application is denied. (But we can only presume that, the whole system is such a mess, it's entirely possible prohibited weapons are making it past this system, too.) </P>
<P align=justify>About a year ago, senior officers inside the RCMP admitted to Alliance MP Garry Breitkreuz that the Mounties had been ordered by the Department of Justice Canada to stop verifying the information on registration forms and just push through whatever came in. Buckley's soldering-gun registration proves that is exactly what's happening. </P>
<P align=justify>If the registry cannot even kick back an application to register hardware, how could it possibly detect a fraudulent application to register a machine gun as a hunting rifle or a short-barrelled, concealed pistol as a legal handgun? </P>
<P align=justify>The answer is, it can't. The registration of guns is a sham. It is based entirely on the honour system. Whatever you tell it you own, that is what it registers. The only rejections are likely of applications that contain honest mistakes, not deliberate misinformation. </P>
<P align=justify>Criminals aren't going to register -- honourably -- their sawed-off shotguns and machine pistols, their oversized magazines and the Saturday Night Specials they buy from smugglers. </P>
<P align=justify>In just the last year, the central registry has lost track of nearly 40,000 licensed owners, issued duplicate licences to at least 1,000 more, printed out hundreds of licences with the wrong names or photos on them and sent 57 registration certificates to one owner for the 16 guns in his possession -- and those are just the cases known to <BREITKREUZ>. </P>
<P align=justify>Buckley's act of insolence has made a mockery of the Canadian Firearms Program and of Justice Canada and they are not amused. </P>
<P align=justify>While the Criminal Code makes it an offence to use false information to register a gun, that provision was designed to keep criminals from lying to obtain a licence and to prevent prohibited weapons from remaining in circulation. Yet, Justice Canada has asked the RCMP to see whether Buckley can be charged. </P>
<P align=justify>Nothing Buckley claimed on his application was false. He did not mislead. The fault is entirely with the registry's slapdash methods. </P>
<P align=justify>But having been shown to have no clothes, the emperor is now enraged. Apparently, it is as great an offence to ridicule the federal government as it is to commit a heist with a gun. </P>
<P align=justify><EM>This article was published in the Editorial section of the <A href="http://www.southam.com/windsorstar/" target=_blank>Windsor Star</A> on April 4, 2002</EM></P>
