          <td height="549"> 
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The 
              currently accepted and experimentally well-tested theory of electromagnetic 
              and weak interactions is called the Standard Model. The Standard 
              Model is based on <b>relativistic quantum 
              gauge field theory</b>. When physicists in the 1920s tried 
              to combine quantum mechanics of Heisenberg and Schrodinger with 
              the special relativity of Einstein, they unleashed a can of worms 
              that was only closed mathematically with the development of <b>relativistic 
              quantum field theory</b>. <br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The application of <b>relativistic 
              quantum field theory</b> to the classical electromagnetism 
              of Maxwell opened a new can of worms. The Maxwell equations possess 
              a special local symmetry called <b>gauge 
              invariance</b> whereby the photon field, also called the 
              vector potential, transforms as</font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="gifs/GaugeTrans.gif" width="186" height="28" alt="Gauge transformation"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"> 
              This transformation leaves the field strength</font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="gifs/FieldStrength.gif" width="138" height="28" alt="Electromagnetic field strength"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">action</font></p>
            <p align="center"><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099"><img src="gifs/EMAction.gif" width="152" height="46" alt="Electromagnetic action"></font></p>
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">and 
              all physical observables unchanged. In the case of electromagnetism, 
              the transformations that leave the action unchanged form a <b>gauge 
              group</b> known as the unitary group <b>U(1)</b>. 
              <br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;An understanding of the quantum aspects of gauge 
              invariance led to the development of <b>relativistic 
              quantum gauge field theory</b>. Gauge invariance is a powerful 
              symmetry that tames uncontrollable infinities in quantum amplitudes 
              and encodes the rich symmetry structure of conserved charges observed 
              in elementary particle physics.<br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Today, three of the observed forces in Nature 
              have been successfully described as theories of quantum gauge symmetry, 
              and it turns out that these three forces can be described in terms 
              of <b>unitary groups</b> of different 
              dimensions. Physicists write this combination of gauge groups as 
              <b>SU(3)xSU(2)xU(1)</b>. <br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;In the quantum gauge theory described by the group 
              <b>SU(N)</b>, there end up being 
              N<sup>2</sup>-1 gauge bosons.The group <b>SU(3)</b> 
              is the gauge group of the theory of the strong interactions known 
              as <b>QCD</b>. The massless gauge 
              field of this theory is known as the gluon. The group <b>SU(3)</b> 
              has eight generators, and this turns out to mean that there are 
              eight types of gluons predicted by the theory. <br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The <b>SU(2)xU(1)</b> 
              part that remains is a bit more complicated. One might expect that 
              the <b>U(1)</b> refers to electromagnetism, 
              with its single massless gauge boson, known to everyone as the photon. 
              So the <b>SU(2)</b> must refer to 
              the weak interaction. The group <b>SU(2)</b> 
              has three generators of gauge symmetry, and that would give three 
              massless gauge bosons to mediate the weak nuclear force.<br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;But that's wrong.<br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The weak nuclear force is a short range force, 
              behaving as if the gauge bosons are very heavy. In order to make 
              a gauge invariant theory work for the weak nuclear force, theorists 
              had to come up with a way to make heavy gauge bosons in a way that 
              wouldn't destroy the consistency of the quantum theory.<br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The method they came up with is called <b>spontaneous 
              symmetry breaking</b>, where massless gauge bosons acquire 
              mass by interacting with a scalar field called the Higgs field. 
              The resulting theory has massive gauge bosons but still retains 
              the nice properties of a fully gauge invariant theory where the 
              gauge bosons would normally be massless.<br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;In the end, the successful theory is called <b>electroweak 
              theory</b>, because electromagnetism and the weak nuclear 
              force start out being mixed together in an overall <b>SU(2)xU(1)</b> 
              gauge symmetry. The scalar field interactions mix up the four massless 
              gauge bosons, and out of the mixture, there winds up being three 
              massive gauge bosons, now called the <b>W<sup>+</sup>, 
              W<sup>-</sup> and Z</b>, and one massless gauge boson, the 
              <b>photon</b>, the carrier of the 
              electromagnetic force. The only explicit remaining gauge symmetry 
              is the <b>U(1)</b> of electromagnetism.<br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Particle physicists describe this as saying that 
              the symmetry of <b>SU(3)xSU(2)xU(1)</b> 
              is spontaneously broken down to <b>SU(3)xU(1)</b> 
              at the electroweak scale of about 100GeV.</font></p>

          <td> 
            <p><font face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2" color="#000099">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Particle 
              experiments in the fifties and sixties produced copious numbers 
              of <b>mesons</b> and <b>baryons</b> 
              named after letters in the Greek alphabet. Physicists literate in 
              group theory, most notably Murray Gell-Mann, were able to see that 
              the patterns of symmetries in that teeming zoo suggested that the 
              huge number of mesons and baryons being discovered could be neatly 
              organized by the principle of group theory, and that the resulting 
              patterns could be explained in terms of a <b>quark 
              model</b> of particles with fractional electric charge, carrying 
              some other type of charge that physicists now call <b>color</b>. 
              <br>
              &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The existence of quarks inside the mesons and 
              baryons had to be deduced mathematically because free quarks have 
              never been observed by particle physics. It is believed that in 
              the theory of QCD, the <b>color charge 
              is confined</b> so that the only particle states that can 
              be made from quarks are those with zero total color charge. A free 
              quark would carry some nonzero color charge and would not be allowed 
              under the principle of confinement.</font></p>
