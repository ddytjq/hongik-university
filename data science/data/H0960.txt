      <td width="560" height="550" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" valign="top" rowspan="4">

        <p align="left">As evidence against using a flow splitter it is often
        heard that the ratio of the split changes with the changing viscosity of
        the mobile phase during the HPLC derived organic gradient.&nbsp; One
        might imagine that this would mean that the flow rate through the column
        would decrease with decreasing viscosity as the fluid would be less
        likely to be pushed through the high pressure (column) arm of the
        split.&nbsp; But this really does not make any sense because as the
        viscosity changes the mobile phase also meets lower resistance through
        the column and thus the split ratio remains the same.&nbsp; The physical
        data in the graph below demonstrates this principle.</p>

        <p align="center"><img border="0" src="art/fluidics.gif" width="406" height="335"></p>

        <blockquote>
          <blockquote>

        <p align="left"><b>Figure 6:</b> Time points were taken at 5 minute
        intervals.&nbsp; At each interval volume was measured, pressure was
        recorded in bars, and %B was noted.&nbsp; The flow rate essentially does
        not change, pink line, over this steep organic gradient.&nbsp; <u>A</u>
        solvent was water/ 0.05%TFA, and the <u>B</u> solvent was acetonitrile/0.05%
        TFA.&nbsp; The column was a C18 RP column 0.32 X 150 mm, 300Å, 5µm.</p>

        <p align="left">&nbsp;</p>

          </blockquote>
        </blockquote>
        <p align="left">Figure 6 above demonstrates that as the viscosity
        changes over the organic gradient as evidenced by the decreasing system
        pressure, red line, the flow measured through the capillary column
        remains stable, pink line.&nbsp; Note that the system pressure decreases
        nearly 30% from 70 to 50 bar.&nbsp; This experiment demonstrates that
        the changing viscosity does not affect the split ratio.&nbsp; The only
        thing that can change the ratio is a change in resistance through either
        arm of the splitter.&nbsp; It is conceivable that changing organic
        content could cause the stationary phase to swell or shrink thus
        changing the resistance on the column.&nbsp; This does not seem to be
        the case in the real experimental data shown above.&nbsp; Theory and
        conjecture is often the starting point for a good experiment however the
        buck stops with completion of a well designed experiment.</p>

        <p align="left">One last nugget.&nbsp; One might expect that you would
        need to change the configuration of the splitter when changing to a
        column of a different diameter.&nbsp; This only seems to make
        sense.&nbsp; Our experience has been that columns are rather self
        regulating, within reason.&nbsp; Larger diameter columns let more mobile
        phase through and smaller columns let less mobile phase through as would
        be the demand.&nbsp;&nbsp; For example the standard splitter used for
        running 320 µm ID columns uses a 50 µm ID X 50 cm resistor with a
        pre-split flow rate of about 200 µL/min..&nbsp; We have found that when
        changing to 500 µm ID columns we can use the same resistor and merely
        start out at a lower flow rate and adjust up until a suitable flow rate
        is achieved, often the pre-split flow rate is not much different.&nbsp;
        To choose a suitable flow rate for a column of given diameter visit the <a href="http://www.ionsource.com/Card/linvelocity/linvol.htm" target="_blank">linear
        velocity page</a>. Take note that columns can be run at lower and faster
        than recommended flow rates and that the chromatographic and scientific
        goal should dictate the exact flow rate through the column.&nbsp;</p>
