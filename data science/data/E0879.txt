    <td bgcolor="#DDFFDD">
      <p align="left"><font face="Verdana">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; All of the books
      in the 1 C Plus Plus Street 'recommended readings' are best-of-breed tomes.&nbsp;
      However, I am not exagerating in any way when I say that this book blows
      every other book I've read in the past 2 years completely away.</font></p>
      <p align="left"><font face="Verdana">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Let me
      first warn you that this book is NOT a programming book--it is an OO book.&nbsp;
      So if you still get confused when you hear the word 'method' instead of
      'sub' or 'function',&nbsp; or if you think 'inheritance' only refers to
      how much you can expect to receive from your favorite aunt when she dies,
      you are probably not ready for this one yet.&nbsp; However, if you have a
      solid understanding of OO concepts and the experience to match, then this
      book can take your object designs, frameworks and implentations from the
      advanced level into the rarified air of the top 10% of all OO system
      designers and builders.</font></p>
      <p align="left"><font face="Verdana">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Am I
      gushing about this book?&nbsp; I probably am, but I feel it deserves it.&nbsp;
      A reviewer from Amazon.com echoes my sentiment when he says, <em><small>&quot;</small>
      When it comes to actually designing and implementing object technology,
      the second volume of Ambler's trilogy is undoubtedly the best book so far
      in the software development business.&quot;</em></font></p>
      <p align="left"><font face="Verdana">&nbsp;&nbsp; If you're a frequent
      reader of OO texts, you'll find that most are more like religious
      treatises on the benefits of OO, rather than pragmatic instruction on how
      to build real-world systems.&nbsp; This book strikes a defiant stand
      against that trend.&nbsp; From topics like 'How to build an A-class object
      framework' to 'Constructing superior diagrams', to &quot;How to persist
      objects', this book delivers invaluable information that would take a new
      OO developer years to discover on his/her own.</font></p>
      <p align="left"><font face="Verdana">&nbsp;&nbsp; If there is anything bad
      to say about this book, it is that occasionally the reader is forced to
      endure a little bit more of Ambler's ego than would be desired.&nbsp;
      (Let's put it this way...the guy created his own modeling notation and
      named it after himself)&nbsp; However, in a genre where egos seem to run
      more rampant than rednecks do in the country-side, Ambler is hardly the
      worst offender (...right away, Booch comes to mind right away as being
      much worse...).</font></p>
      <p align="left"><font face="Verdana">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; So to
      summarize, this book is fantastic, and I heartily recommend it.&nbsp; It
