<h1>A Dale of two drivers: Jarrett wins, Earnhardt loses</h1>

DAYTONA BEACH, Fla. -- Dale Jarrett held off a hard-charging Dale Earnhardt over
the final 20 laps Sunday to capture his second Daytona 500 victory at
Daytona International Speedway.
<p> 

Jarrett, who grabbed last week's Busch Clash, showed the
muscle in his Robert Yates-owned Ford Thunderbird by holding off
the powerful Chevrolet Monte Carlos of Earnhardt and third place
Ken Schrader. <p>

All three were among the leaders throughout the afternoon,
taking advantage of tremendous vehicle power and finding the
right draft on the 2½-mile superspeedway to stay at the front
of the field. <p>

Jarrett scooted past Earnhardt
on Lap 177 of the 200-lap race, then stayed out front the rest of
the way to hold off Earnhardt for the second time in four years.<p>
	   Earnhardt, who has finished second four times in the 500,
including each of the past two years and three of the last four
years, is an agonizing 0-18 in the only major stock car event he
never has won.<p>
	   &quot;That's the Daytona 500,&quot; Earnhardt said. &quot;We finished second
again. That's OK. We just didn't have anything to match those
Fords.<p>
	   &quot;I was trying to get a run on (Jarrett), but he had a real
strong race car.&quot;<p>

Jarrett found a fast groove in the low-center area of the turns
and couldn't be touched on the straightaways or the hump-shaped
front straightaway at Daytona. <p>

During the final lap, Earnhardt frantically tried to find a
weakness in Jarrett's position, moving from the high-line to the
low-line to find a spot and claim his first Daytona victory.<p> 

	   It appeared that the Chevrolets of Earnhardt and Schrader might be able to work together and draft past Jarrett's
Thunderbird, but it didn't happen.<p>
	   Over the last 10 laps, Earnhardt kept moving up and down the
track, apparently feeling out Jarrett for a weakness. Finally, on
the last trip around the high-banked oval, Earnhardt gave
it a major effort.<p>
	   On the backstretch, Earnhardt drove his car low but, just as
Jarrett did in 1993 when he won his first Daytona race, he went
right with Earnhardt and blocked his path.<p>
	   For a brief moment, Schrader drew alongside Earnhardt, taking
away momentum from both Chevys.<p>
	   They got back in single file and Earnhardt made three more moves
coming off the final corner, diving low again, then high, then low
once more. But he could not catch Jarrett, son of two-time Winston
Cup champion and TV broadcaster Ned Jarrett.<p>
	   &quot;They didn't have a Robert and Doug Yates engine,&quot; Jarrett
said of Earnhardt and Schrader. &quot;That was the difference. I was
worried about them getting a run, but they just didn't have enough
for that engine.&quot;<p>
	   Jarrett crossed the finish line 0.12 seconds -- about 2½
car-lengths -- ahead of Earnhardt, who was just in front of
Schrader.<p>
	   The 39-year-old Jarrett, who has five career victories, averaged
154.308 mph on the way to a victory that netted him $362,775 from
the total purse of just over $3.6 million.<p>
	   &quot;On one of our early stops, we made an air pressure adjustment
and it just messed things up real bad,'' Jarrett explained. &quot;I
fell back quite a ways. But we finally got the car so it could just
fly. I could go anywhere and do anything I wanted out there. That
was the key.&quot;<p>

Schrader, the gutsy racer who had nothing but bad luck last
year, was the only of Henrick Motorsports' three entries with a
shot at the checkered flag. <p>

&quot;I didn't think we could have got Dale (Jarrett) anyway,&quot;
Schrader said. <p>

Schrader suffered through a horrible wreck at Talledega, several
blown motors and drove a Sunday race despite losing most of a
thumb in the middle of a week last season.<p> 

	   Mark Martin's Ford finished fourth, followed by new teammate
Jeff Burton and Wally Dallenbach Jr.<p>
	   Sterling Marlin's bid to become the first driver to win the
Daytona 500 three straight times came to an abrupt end on lap 80,
just four laps after he had moved into the lead for the first time.
	   &quot;That's the same motor we won with here last year, but it
didn't quite make it this time,&quot; Marlin said.<p>
	   Another of the favorites, Ernie Irvan, also was out of
contention early, getting caught on lap 28 in an incident sparked
by Earnhardt as a big pack sped along the main straightaway.<p>
	   Earnhardt slowed suddenly with an ignition problem. Irvan
checked up right behind him and Dallenbach got into the rear of
Irvan's car. Irvan, whose racing comeback from near-fatal head and
chest injuries in August 1994 began last September, slapped the
wall hard, damaging the left front of his car.<p>
	   &quot;It was like (Earnhardt) shut the switch off,&quot; Irvan said. &quot;I
hit him and then somebody hit me from behind. Oh well, you've got
to worry about things you can change.&quot;<p>
	   During the ensuing caution period, everybody pitted and
Earnhardt's crew was able to switch to a second ignition box
already installed on his car without losing a lap.<p>

The race was a wild shootout, with the lead changing hands 33
times during the afternoon. Fourteen cars were out of the
running by race end, largely due to six wrecks. Among those
knocked out in the early running were pre-race favorites 
Irvan of the Robert Yates team, and defending Winston Cup champion Jeff
Gordon of the Hendrick squad. <p>

Engine compression limits and restrictor plate rules imposed by
NASCAR on superspeedways kept the field fairly even, with fewer
cars dropping off the lead lap. <p>

There were several surprises at the front of the field. Wally
Dallenbach Jr. driving the Bud Moore entry that had just
one race sponsor prior to the race, climbed as high as second
late in the race and finished sixth. <p>

Michael Waltrip, in his first ride in the Wood Brothers-owned
Ford, ran a strong race despite a damaged front end from an
early accident. Waltrip faded late in the race and finished
10th. <p>

Terry Labonte, the third of the Hendrick entries, led for a
race-high 44 laps. Engine problems forced Labonte to fade deep
in the field after a strong performance in the first 80 laps.<p> 

Sterling Marlin, who was seeking a record-setting third consecutive
Daytona 500 championship, was leading in the 79th lap when he
developed engine problems, knocking him out of the race. <p>
