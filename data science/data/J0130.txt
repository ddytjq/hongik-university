function isEmpty(field){

        if(field.charAt(i)!=' ') return false;

    return true;
}

}

          <td colspan="2" class="texte10" width="532">
      <p>&nbsp;</p>
      <p><span class="leaguetitle">Grand Prix Grip</span></p>
          <hr>
      <font color="3333CC"><b>Michelin is the dominant force in the world&#146;s
      fastest motorcycle racing discipline - 500cc Grand Prix. The French tyre
      giant has come to totally dominate 500s, winning the last nine World Championships
      and remaining unbeaten in 38 GPs. <br>
      This year Michelin celebrates its 20th 500 cc World Championship success,
      confirmation that it reigns technically supreme in this most demanding of
      motorsports.</b></font>
      <hr>
      <p><span class="leaguesstitle"><br>
        A</span>nd yet Michelin&#146;s history of success extends far beyond 500
        GPs. Over the past 27 years the French tyre giant has won more than 80
        World Championships in every class going. In Grand Prix racing, Michelin
        has won 125, 250 and 500 titles, as well as crowns in the now extinct
        50, 80 and 350 classes. In four-stroke competition, Michelin has also
        dominated, taking multiple successes in World Superbike, World Endurance
        and TT Formula One.<br>
        <br>
        Michelin&#146;s engineers and technicians have worked ceaselessly to achieve
        this amazing run of results, dedicating themselves to perfecting the traction,
        feel, handling, steering and longevity offered by its products. The company
        believes no one knows more about motorcycle tyre design, and history would
        seem to endorse that belief.<br>
        <br>
        <img src="http://michsportw2.mdeo.com/html/gp/2001/league/intro/img/pz2.jpg" align="left"> It all started in 1973 when Michelin
        introduced its PZ range. These were road tyres available in any shop but,
        like many Michelin products, they made a radical difference on the racetrack;
        more rounded in profile, they allowed riders to put more rubber on the
        road for extra grip.<br>
        <br>
        One year later, Kent Andersson took Michelin&#146;s first World Championship,
        winning the 1974 125 title. Over the next few seasons Michelin began supplying
        special race tyres and assumed total dominance, <font color="#3333CC"><b>Barry
        Sheene</b></font> winning the company&#146;s first 500 title in 1976.
        The following year Michelin won the &#145;grand slam&#146; for the first
        time with success in the 500, 350, 250, 125 and 50 classes. Since then
        the brand has achieved a full house of Grand Prix titles on no less than
        seven occasions.<br>
        <br>
        Racing tyre design would never be the same again after the mid-seventies.
        As horsepower figures escalated, slicks replaced traditional treaded tyres
        and once again Michelin was at the forefront of a revolution. In 1984
        the company introduced the first radial construction race tyres, winning
        five back-to-back 500 titles from &#145;85 to &#145;89 with <font color="#3333CC"><b>Freddie
        Spencer</b></font>, <font color="#3333CC"><b>Eddie Lawson</b></font> and
        <b><font color="#3333CC">Wayne Gardner</font></b>, as well as the inaugural
        &#145;88 World Superbike series with Fred Merkel.<br>
        <br>
        <img src="http://michsportw2.mdeo.com/html/gp/2001/league/intro/img/radial.jpg" width="125" height="125" align="right">Radials
        made a huge difference for racers, reducing heat build-up and thus prolonging
        performance, and improving handling and steering through a reduction in
        weight, compared to cross-plies. Radials also offered a crucial improvement
        in feel for rear-wheel steering - by the mid-eighties riding techniques
        had progressed to the point where most riders were sliding the rear tyre
        out of corners.<br>
        <br>
        Michelin men like <font color="#3333CC"><b>Wayne Rainey</b></font>, <font color="#3333CC"><b>Kevin
        Schwantz</b></font> and <b><font color="#3333CC">Mick Doohan</font></b>
        would soon take the next step as they dominated the nineties - sliding
        the front tyre, an even more magical manifestation of riding talent. Doohan&#146;s
        dazzlingly fast mid-corner speed was the crucial factor in his domination
        from &#145;94 to &#145;98 and took Michelin tyre development to the next
        level. While Australians and Americans had dominated from the early eighties,
        using their rear-wheel steering skills learned on dirt tracks back home,
        Doohan&#146;s input brought the class back to artful Europeans, whose
        style relied upon corner speed, learned in 125 and 250 racing. In 1999
        Alex Crivill&eacute; became the first European since &#145;82 to win the
        500 crown, ten years after he won the 125 title on Michelin.<br>
        <br>
        Des pilotes Michelin comme <font color="#3333CC"><b>Wayne Rainey</b></font>,
        <font color="#3333CC"><b>Kevin Schwantz</b></font> et <font color="#3333CC"><b>Mick
        Doohan</b></font> ne tardent pas &agrave; aller encore plus loin et se
        mettent &agrave; glisser aussi de la roue avant. Grande r&eacute;v&eacute;lation
        du talent quasi magique de ces champions, cette technique permet &agrave;
        la nouvelle g&eacute;n&eacute;ration de marquer les ann&eacute;es 90 de
        son empreinte. En effet, la vitesse &eacute;poustouflante de Doohan en
        mi-courbe a jou&eacute; un r&ocirc;le d&eacute;terminant dans sa domination
        de la discipline de 1994 &agrave; 1998 et a amen&eacute; Michelin &agrave;
        franchir une nouvelle &eacute;tape technologique.<br>
        <br>
        <img src="http://michsportw2.mdeo.com/html/gp/2001/league/intro/img/pilot.jpg" width="125" height="125" align="left">The
        battle for the first 500 World Championship of the new millennium was
        a breathtaking affair, with the closest, fastest action ever seen in 500
        GPs. Once again, Michelin dominated totally, its tyres giving every rider
        the grip and feel that&#146;s vital for victory. Equipping all riders
        with the tyres to suit their differing riding styles is a huge task, and
        yet Michelin did just that, because during 2000 there have been more different
        race winners than ever before. The man who came out on top was son of
        a legend : <b><font color="#3333CC">Kenny Roberts Jnr</font></b> (Telefonica
        Movistar Suzuki-Michelin) but these men also won races* - Garry McCoy
        (Red Bull Yamaha WCM-Michelin), Alex Barros (Emerson Honda Pons-Michelin),
        Alex Crivill&eacute; (Repsol YPF Honda-Michelin), Loris Capirossi (Emerson
        Honda Pons-Michelin), Valentino Rossi (Nastro Azzurro Honda-Michelin),
        Max Biaggi (Marlboro Yamaha Team-Michelin) and Norick Abe (Antena 3 Yamaha-D&#146;Antin-Michelin).<br>
        <br>
        Like his many rivals, Roberts knows his tyres provide the final, crucial
        interface between rider and racetrack. He works closely with Michelin
        engineers 12 months a year to ensure he has championship-winning grip.
        This knowledge helps Michelin advance its racing tyres and is then applied
        to street tyres.<br>
        <br>
        The Michelin competitions department currently provides tyres for the
        entire 500 grid and its aim is the same as ever: not only to offer riders
        maximum grip, but also to provide them with the feel they need to confidently
        and safely exploit the awesome power of a 500 GP bike. That is one reason
        why 500 racing is now closer, faster and more thrilling than ever before.<br>
        <br>
        <span class="texte9">*Results up to and including the 2000 Rio GP.</span></p>
      </td>

          <td colspan="2" class="texte10" width="532">
      <p>&nbsp;</p>
      <p><span class="leaguetitle">Michelin&#146;s winning chemistry</span></p>
          <hr>
      <font color="3333CC"><b>Michelin&#146;s record in bike racing&#146;s premier
      class speaks volumes - the French tyre brand has totally dominated 500 Grand
      Prix racing in recent years, winning the past nine World Championships and
      118 of 130 races during this period. In other words, Michelin knows more
      about building the ultimate motorcycle tyre than anyone else in the world.</b></font>
      <hr>
      <br>
      <p><span class="leaguesstitle"><img src="http://michsportw2.mdeo.com/html/gp/2001/league/intro/img/team.jpg" width="188" height="125" align="right">T</span>he
        company sends 14 tyre designers, engineers, chemists and technicians to
        each round of the 500 series, where they are responsible for ongoing development,
        as well as working closely with riders and teams, ensuring that every
        rider selects the best tyres for the conditions.<br>
        <br>
        This isn&#146;t easy - 500 GP bikes are unforgiving beasts, with a power-to-weight
        ratio similar to that of Formula 1 cars and a rubber-to-tarmac contact
        patch 13 times smaller! Those two simple statistics should give you some
        idea of the breathtaking skill of today&#146;s 500 riders and the huge
        demands placed on 500 tyres.<br>
        <br>
        <img src="http://michsportw2.mdeo.com/html/gp/2001/league/intro/img/simulation.jpg" width="125" height="125" align="left">To
        make matters even more complex, the available contact patch changes constantly,
        as the combined rider/machine mass is thrown rearward under acceleration
        and forward under braking. During cornering, when lean angles can exceed
        50 degrees, both tyres provide contact patches of around 45 square centimetres
        (about the size of a credit card), even though fronts are substantially
        narrower than rears (120mm, against 180mm). With no F1-style down-force,
        g-forces aren&#146;t huge in bike racing, but a 500 can pull a lateral
        1.4g during cornering and a longitudinal 1.3g during ultra-heavy braking.
        That may not seem much compared to F1, but GP riders aren&#146;t strapped
        inside a cockpit, rather they hold on for dear life with hands, feet and
        backside.<br>
        <br>
        A 500&#146;s braking power is awesome but perhaps the defining moment
        of 500 racing is acceleration, when riders unleash the full 190 horsepower
        available, deftly controlling wheelspin by adjusting throttle opening
        and shifting body weight. This is serious high-octane brinkmanship, and
        Michelin&#146;s rear slicks offer a very special kind of traction to give
        riders the confidence to spin the tyre, using wheelspin to help turn the
        bike, while maintaining good forward drive. Such treatment gives tyres
        a hard time - Michelin rear slicks operate at around 90 to 120 degrees
        Celsius, while fronts work at 80 to 100 degrees.<br>
        <br>
        <img src="http://michsportw2.mdeo.com/html/gp/2001/league/intro/img/slick.jpg" width="125" height="125" align="right">GP
        riders have an apparently bewildering array of Michelin tyres to choose
        from at each race, with the company&#146;s engineers often producing specific
        tyres to suit each circuit. Usually there are four or five front slicks
        available, in three different compounds, two alternative constructions
        and one or two different profiles. At least six rears are on offer at
        most tracks, in four compounds and a couple of different constructions
        and profiles. This year has seen Michelin&#146;s 16.5in rear slick return
        to vogue, over the traditionally more popular 17in slick. First used successfully
        by Kevin Schwantz in the mid-nineties, the 16.5 uses a radical profile
        to give a larger contact patch at maximum lean for more grip, cooler running
        and longer life. Then, of course, there are Michelin&#146;s famed dual-compound
        rear slicks for tracks that present highly contrasting demands for different
        sides of the tyre.<br>
        <br>
        And when the weather turns bad there are Michelin wets and intermediates.
        Only one kind of rain tyre is deemed necessary for most circuits, with
        two intermediates usually on offer, but the ultimate choice is almost
        limitless since Michelin engineers can cut grooves into slicks and intermediates
        to ensure riders have the perfect tyre for any conditions.<br>
        <br>
        Providing such a myriad choice requires Michelin to transport around 2500
        tyres to each Grand Prix. Top-level 500 riders use up to 40 tyres during
        a GP weekend, testing each choice for several laps as they work their
        way toward finding the ideal race-day compromise between grip and endurance.
        Of course, only one rider can win on race day. But it&#146;s a safe bet
        he&#146;s on Michelin.<br>
