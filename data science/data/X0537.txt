	<td width="75%">
		<b>Animal rights bill will hurt                                                                        </b><br>
		<font size=2>Author: Robert D. Sopuck                                   <br>
		Source: National Post                                                                                                                                         </font>
	</td>

	<td align=right VALIGN=TOP>		
				Mon.           April 29, 2002
	</td>
	</tr>
	<tr>
		<td colspan=2><br>
		<br>
				<P align=justify>The use of animals for human purposes is vital to the well-being and safety of all Canadians, but if Bill C-15B passes through Parliament, the ultimate goal of animal rights activists, namely the elimination of all animal use, even as pets, by people is much closer to reality. In Tuesday's National Post, Andy Lamey makes that extreme agenda sound perfectly reasonable, but there is a darker side, especially for rural Canada. </P>
<P align=justify>Bill C-15B, Canada's new proposed animal cruelty law, could seriously affect established animal uses in agriculture, medical research and recreation among others. Let's look at the issues. </P>
<P align=justify>Bill C-15B will rely on existing codes of practice, but these codes are open to debate. Animals will be moved out of the property section even though livestock, for example, are often used as collateral for bank loans. This is a radical departure and is the first step in granting rights to animals. </P>
<P align=justify>The new definition of animal as: "Any vertebrate, other than a human being, and any animal that can feel pain" is far too broad and will prove to be problematic in the courts. This will open animal husbandry, bio-medical research, fish farming and angling to frivolous legal challenges, especially since scientists cannot agree on which animals actually feel pain, or at what level. </P>
<P align=justify>No upfront protection is granted to farmers, hunters, trappers, medical researchers and others of the so-called "animal use" communities while simply doing their jobs. The Crown will have less to prove before an accused is forced to provide defence evidence. </P>
<P align=justify>Bill C-15B opens the door for "authorized organizations," such as humane societies, to challenge legitimate animal uses. The charge would only proceed to our already overburdened court system with the approval of an attorney general, but the person charged would incur the expense of having to hire a lawyer to prove why the charge should not proceed. In the meantime, the person against whom the frivolous charge was laid will no doubt see their name appear in the media as having been charged with an offence under the "Sexual Offences, Public Morals and Disorderly Conduct" statute, where Bill C-15B resides. </P>
<P align=justify>Persons convicted under Bill C-15B could be required to pay costs incurred during investigations and in caring for rescued animals. As Shelagh MacDonald of the Canadian Federation of Humane Societies explains, this is exactly their goal. She notes that many of the roughly 250 humane societies across the country are financially hard-pressed and need more funds. The kindly people who donate money to humane societies seem to be unaware that these organizations are increasingly dominated by animal rights crusaders. </P>
<P align=justify>Humane Societies have often been delegated the legal power to enforce animal cruelty laws. In Bill C-15B, we are creating a mechanism whereby the "animal police" will get to keep any fines they generate. The potential for corruption and abuse is obvious. It's as if local police stations kept the money they generated from speeding tickets. Think about it. </P>
<P align=justify>Most animal rights groups want the complete elimination of all animal use by people, and the extension of human rights to animals. "The life of an ant and that of my child should be granted equal consideration" and "A rat is a pig is a dog is a boy" are two of the more outrageous statements made by leaders of animal rights organizations. That radical view has found legal expression in Bill C15-B. </P>
<P align=justify>Animal activists have openly stated that legal test cases will be launched if Bill C-15B is passed in its present form. If promising avenues of medical research are abandoned due to fear of lawsuits, the health consequences for Canadians will be not just significant, but disastrous. Bill C-15B will expose those involved in animal use, such as medical research, to new legal risks. Justice officials are trying to convince animal use groups, such as farmers, that C-15B will have no effect. But as one farm leader said to them, "If it has no effect, why do it?" Why, indeed. </P>
<P align=justify>A favoured tactic of animal activists goes like this: "If you can't win in the court of public opinion, get the laws changed so that you just might win in a court of law." A sympathetic judge, a test case, and voila, no more fishing or certain vital medical research or -- well, you fill in the blank. Using law to drive an animal-rights agenda won't help either mistreated animals or people. </P>
<P align=justify>If this law is passed all Canadians will feel the pain. Canada's animal cruelty should reflect Canadian reality. Our existing animal welfare laws should be retained. </P>
<P align=justify>NOTE: Robert D. Sopuck is Director of the Rural RenaissanceProject at the Frontier Centre for Public Policy, a Winnipeg-based public policy think tank. <A href="http://www.fcpp.org/">www.fcpp.org</A></P>
