organelle. The chemical complexity of a unit's DNA is also called its genome size.			<li>There are more different sequences in 3 pg animal nuclear DNA than in 0.004 pg of bacterial DNA. Conversely, in 12 pg of DNA there are many more copies of a genome if the genome is bacterial than if the genome is vertebrate. The animal DNA is more <a href="MG112282.html">complex</a>.			<li><a href="MG112282.html">Kinetic</a> complexity is defined as the amount of DNA (in base pairs) required so that every sequence of a DNA is represented just once.			<li>For the calculation of complexity, <i>E. coli</i> DNA is used as a standard because it is readily available.<br>			C<sub>o</sub>t <sub>1/2</sub> of X<br>			------------------ x 4.2 x 10<sup>6</sup> bp = complexity of X<br>			C<sub>o</sub>t<sub>1/2</sub> of <i>E.coli</i>			<li>Repetition frequency, f, is defined as:<br>			f = (chemical complexity/ kinetic complexity )		</ul>		<center>			<p>			<table border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0">				<tr>					<td><strong>Nucleic Acid</strong></td>					<td><strong>Kinetic Complexity</strong></td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td>AAAAAAAAA</td>					<td align="center">1 bp</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td>ATCGAATCGAATCGAATCGA</td>					<td align="center">5 bp</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td><font face="Symbol">F</font>X 174 RF DNA</td>					<td align="center">5836 bp</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td>CaMV DNA</td>					<td align="center">8000 bp</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td>bacteriophage T4</td>					<td align="center">1.7 x 10<sup>5</sup> bp</td>				</tr>				<tr>					<td><i>E. coli</i></td>					<td align="center">4.2 x 10<sup>6</sup> bp</td>				</tr>			</table>			</p>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a></p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Interp"></a>Interpretations</h4>		<ul>			<li><a href="MG11233.html">Genomes</a> vary widely in size. Thus , the amount of DNA per cell or haploid nucleus also varies widely.			<li>Larger genomes reassociate at higher C<sub>o</sub>t<sub>1/2</sub> values than do smaller ones		</ul>		<center>			<p><a href="#Facts">Facts</a> | <a href="#Interp">Interpretations</a> | <a href="#Info">Further Info.</a> | <a href="#bottom">Other Pages</a><br>			</p>		</center>		<h4><a name="Info"></a>Further information</h4>		<ul>			<li>C<sub>o</sub>t <sub>1/2</sub> <a href="MG112282.html">values</a> used for complexity calculations are obtained from the rate constant, correcting for the contribution of that component to the total genome. For example, consider a component that is 45% of total and has an apparent C<sub>o</sub>t <sub>1/2</sub> of 630. If it constituted 100% of the DNA, it would be 100/45 fold more concentrated. Thus, 0.45 x 630 = 283 (C<sub>o</sub>t <sub>1/2</sub> corrected). <img src="nav4.gif" width="69" height="71" align="right" usemap="#nav4B1F889C9"><map name=""></map>			<li>For many organisms, the repetition frequency calculated for the kinetically identified unique DNA class is close to 1.0, indicating that these DNAs are truly unique in the genome.		</ul>		<center>			<p><a name="bottom"></a><a href="MG112282.html">Last</a> | <a href="MG1129.html">Vocabulary</a> | <a href="MG11228.html">Overview</a> | <a href="#Facts">Top</a> | <a href="MG112284.html">Next</a><br>			<br>			</p>		</center>		<p>		<hr>		</p>		<p><img height="72" width="108" src="divlogo2.gif" align="right">This is page 112283 of <a href="../MG01.html">Molecular Genetics</a> by <a href="http://opbs.okstate.edu/~melcher/UM.html">Ulrich Melcher</a>, &copy; 1997-2000<br>		<hr>		</p>		<address>E-mail inquiries to <a href="mailto:umelcher@biochem.okstate.edu">U.
