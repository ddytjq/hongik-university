<p class=MsoBodyText style='margin-top:.25in'><span style='font-size:18.0pt;
font-family:Times'>LISA: Laser Interferometer Space Antenna<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoBodyText style='margin-top:.25in'><span style='font-size:18.0pt;
font-family:Times'>Technology Development Summary<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<h1 style='margin-top:.25in'><span style='font-size:14.0pt;font-family:Times'>30
April 2002<o:p></o:p></span></h1>

<p class=Section><span style='font-family:Times'>Introduction<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=Body>The Laser Interferometer Space Antenna (LISA) is a joint ESA-NASA
mission that will provide a new window on the universe through the study of
gravitational waves.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>LISA consists
of three spacecraft, orbiting the Sun, in a triangular configuration with a
baseline of 5 million kilometers between spacecraft. Gravitational waves are
detected by measuring changes in the distance between proof masses housed in
the spacecraft with very high precision (10 picometers) using laser metrology.
Sources of gravitational waves include compact binaries in our own Galaxy, the
merger of supermassive black holes in the cores of distant galaxies, inspirals
of white dwarfs, neutron stars, and stellar-mass black holes into supermassive
black holes, and violent mass density fluctuations in the early universe.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span></p>

<p class=Body>LISA has been the subject of a Phase A study by ESA and is
currently in pre-formulation within NASA.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>The science and performance requirements given here are those of the
Phase A study.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Small changes to
those science requirements have recently been drafted by the LISA International
Science Team, but the reference design has not yet been amended.</p>

<p class=Section><span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;</span>(1) What are the
Level 1 and Level 2 science requirements for the proposed mission, and how do
these motivate the candidate technologies which will be investigated?<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Explain the connections between the
science requirements and the engineering requirements on those technologies. </p>

<p class=Body>LISA seeks to detect gravitational waves from a variety of
astrophysical scenarios in the 10<sup>-4</sup> to 0.1 Hz band.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>These observations will address
fundamental issues in astrophysics, fundamental physics, relativity and
cosmology.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Among these are:</p>

<p class=BulletedList><![if !supportLists]><span style='font-family:Symbol'>&middot;<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><![endif]>LISA
will study the role of massive black holes (MBH) in galaxy evolution through
the detection and characterization of mergers of massive black holes.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Measurement objective: Detect
individual MBH mergers and/or constrain the rate of such mergers out to high
redshift (z&nbsp;&gt;&nbsp;10)</p>

<p class=BulletedList><![if !supportLists]><span style='font-family:Symbol'>&middot;<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><![endif]>LISA
will be able to make precision tests of General Relativity, including the
&quot;no-hair&quot; theorem through mapping of the space-time geometry in the
vicinity of a massive black hole during compact star inspiral into the black
hole.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Measurement objective: Map
the space-time of an MBH and determine its metric by observing the inspiral
trajectory of a stellar-mass black hole or other compact object.</p>

<p class=BulletedList><![if !supportLists]><span style='font-family:Symbol'>&middot;<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><![endif]><span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;</span>LISA will study the population of
ultra-compact binaries in our Galaxy through the identification of thousands of
individual compact white dwarf binaries. In doing so, LISA will contribute
important new knowledge to the study of binary evolution and also to the
understanding of white dwarf structure.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>Measurement objective: Detect the population of individual compact
binaries at high frequencies (&gt;&nbsp;3&nbsp;mHz) and the diffuse galactic
background from the compact binary population at low frequencies
(&lt;&nbsp;3&nbsp;mHz).</p>

<p class=BulletedList><![if !supportLists]><span style='font-family:Symbol'>&middot;<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><![endif]>LISA
will search for gravitational wave emission from the early universe, probing
energy and length scales that were characteristic of the universe 10<sup>-15</sup>&nbsp;s
after the big bang. This is potentially the most fundamental observation that
LISA could make.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Measurement
objective:<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Measure or constrain
the amplitude of the stochastic isotropic GW background at frequencies between
10<sup>-4</sup> and 10<sup>-1</sup>&nbsp; Hz.</p>

<p class=Body>A gravitational wave detector must have an array of proof masses,
free to respond to passing waves and shielded from disturbances, and a
measurement system that can sense changes in the separation of those
masses.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>A detector can be roughly
characterized by its measurement sensitivity, the residual disturbance level
experienced by the proof masses and its baseline.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>One chooses an integration time compatible with the signal characteristics
of the science targets.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Since LISA
has multiple science targets with different amplitudes and frequency bands,
these four key parameters must be chosen to encompass them all.</p>

<p class=Body>The science requirements and the associated measurement
requirements for LISA are illustrated together in Figure 1.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The colored regions show the range of
amplitudes and frequencies that each source type is expected to occupy.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The lower boundary of the colored
regions, between 10<sup>-4</sup> and 1 Hz, reflects the anticipated sensitivity
of the present LISA reference design.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>The disturbance level and the baseline set the performance below 3 mHz
and the measurement sensitivity, the baseline and the instrument response
function set the sensitivity above.</p>

<p class=FigureCaption>Figure 1. The strain amplitude of gravitational waves
from sources that LISA expects to see.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>These values represent the amplitude that LISA would see with a
signal-to-noise of 5 after a one year integration, averaged over the entire sky
and both polarizations.</p>

<p class=Body>Figure 1 shows how the anticipated science signals drive the key
performance requirements, disturbance reduction and measurement
sensitivity.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>These two performance
requirements dominate the technology needs of LISA.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span></p>

<p class=Body>In the LISA reference design, disturbances are controlled through
a &#8220;disturbance reduction system&#8221; wherein the proof masses are
allowed to fall freely (at least along the measurement degree-of-freedom), and
the enclosing spacecraft is controlled to follow the orbit of the proof
mass.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The spacecraft shelters the
proof mass from environmental disturbances, and is designed to minimize any
residual forces between itself and the proof mass.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>The disturbance requirement is an acceleration noise on a
single proof mass below 3x10<sup>-15</sup> m/s<sup>2</sup>/&#8730;Hz, 10<sup>-4</sup>
to 3x10<sup>-3</sup> Hz.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>This
requirement constitutes the greatest technological challenge of LISA.</p>

<p class=Body>Changes in proof mass separation are measured with
interferometry.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The measurement
sensitivity requirement for the change in the difference of the round-trip
optical path between two arms is 40 pm/&#8730;Hz, 10<sup>-3</sup> to 1 Hz.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span></p>

<p class=Section>(2) What are the significant technological challenges for
these technologies --- i.e., which technical capabilities have not already been
demonstrated? </p>

<p class=Body>The LISA Project is currently engaged in producing a Technology
Development Plan.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>That plan
focuses on the disturbance reduction system, the interferometry measurement
system and system verification.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span></p>

<p class=Body><i>The Disturbance Reduction System (DRS)<o:p></o:p></i></p>

<p class=Body>The goal of the DRS is to minimize forces on the proof masses,
other than the gravitational attraction of the Sun.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>This generally involves a drag-free control system to reduce
environmental disturbances (e.g. solar radiation pressure) and careful design
of the proof mass and spacecraft to minimize residual interactions between the
two.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>A simple
&#8220;drag-free&#8221; system comprises (1) a freely falling proof mass, (2)
an enclosing spacecraft whose position relative to the proof mass can be
sensed, (3) thrusters suitable for orienting and positioning the spacecraft with
respect to the proof mass and (4) control laws which convert the relative
position signal to thruster commands.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>The proof mass with its immediate enclosure and sensing apparatus is
called the Gravitational Sensor. The LISA DRS is somewhat more complicated in
that it involves two Gravitational Sensors in a single spacecraft, each of
which is free to move in its associated measurement direction, and an
articulation between them.</p>

<p class=Body>Disturbance reduction systems have a long heritage in space
flight, including the Triad, Champ, GRACE and GP-B missions.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>However, the LISA disturbance
requirement exceeds the demonstrated performance by about five orders of
magnitude.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The technological
challenges are:</p>

<p class=BulletedList><![if !supportLists]><span style='font-family:Symbol'>&middot;<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><![endif]>Gravitational
sensor performance: low residual acceleration, relative position sensing, proof
mass control, charge control</p>

<p class=BulletedList><![if !supportLists]><span style='font-family:Symbol'>&middot;<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><![endif]>Thruster
system: micro-newton thrust, thrust noise, lifetime, contamination </p>

<p class=BulletedList><![if !supportLists]><span style='font-family:Symbol'>&middot;<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><![endif]>Control
laws: multiple degree-of-freedom control strategy, stability, robustness</p>

<p class=Body><i>The Interferometry Measurement System<o:p></o:p></i></p>

<p class=Body>The interferometry measurement system comprises a laser, laser

<p class=Body>Many of the components needed in the LISA measurement system have

frequency control: frequency stabilization, frequency reference,

<p class=Section>(4) What kinds of demonstrations are required to validate
technical readiness?<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Will ground
testing be sufficient, or are there technical capabilities which can only be
demonstrated in space? </p>

<p class=Body><i>The Disturbance Reduction System (DRS)<o:p></o:p></i></p>

<p class=Body>The requirements of this technology strongly militate for a
flight demonstration.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>Gravitational sensors need an environment where the ambient disturbance
level is ~10<sup>-16</sup> g.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>This
is only achievable in carefully selected orbits.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>The common demonstration concept involves flying two sensors
close together, closing a drag-free loop around one and monitoring their
separation with an interferometer.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span></p>

<p class=Body>Within ESA, the Smart 2 mission has been selected to demonstrate
LISA technology.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>NASA&#8217;s New
Millennium Program recently selected a DRS test appropriate to LISA for its ST7
mission.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The ESA LISA Test Package
and the ST7/DRS payload will have coordinated design differences, will both fly
on Smart 2 and will engage in coordinated tests.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>These tests will not only measure the critical disturbance
and drag-free performance requirements, but they will also explore noise models
using controlled, exaggerated stimuli.</p>

<p class=Body>Substantial work on thruster systems can be carried out in the
laboratory.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Some testing of
gravitational sensors is possible on torsional pendulums, and some disturbance
processes (e.g., patch fields) can be investigated with specially designed
laboratory apparatus.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The lab work
will be complementary to the flight demonstrations.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>Drag-free controllers will be tested with gravitational
sensor and control system simulators.</p>

<p class=Body><i>The Interferometry Measurement System<o:p></o:p></i></p>

<p class=Body>With the test beds being developed for system verification, the
interferometry technologies can be validated in a laboratory setting.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>There have already been several
laboratory demonstrations of proof-of-principle for these technologies (e.g.,
laser power stabilization, laser frequency stabilization, laser frequency
control, phase measurement).<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The
interferometry technologies can be validated by applying these techniques in
various combinations.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span></p>

<p class=Body><i>System Verification<o:p></o:p></i></p>

<p class=Body>The validation of the test beds, the demonstration flight, the
modeling and the special test equipment will be accomplished by their
intercomparison.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Together, they
constitute a web of evidence workable designs based on an correct understanding
of the phenomena being validated.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>Exaggerated, controlled stimuli are another tool for validating these
verification technologies.</p>

<p class=Section>(6) Is the required capability a reasonable extension of the
current capability or does it require a significant advancement or new
approach? </p>

<p class=Body><i>The Disturbance Reduction System (DRS)<o:p></o:p></i></p>

<p class=Body>The LISA gravitational sensor design is derived from a line of
space accelerometers developed at ONERA in France over two decades.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Relatively small design changes have
been made. </p>

<p class=Body>The FEEP thrusters have been developed over 2-3 decades in
Europe.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Only minor changes in the
Italian Cs design for the LISA mission.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>The Austrian In design is a small modification to charge control system
flown on CLUSTER.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The American
colloidal thrusters have less heritage.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>Generally, the thruster systems are scaled to satisfy LISA&#8217;s
thrust requirement, and a neutralizer must be integrated into the thruster
systems.</p>

<p class=Body>The drag-free controller does not require extraordinary
performance.</p>

<p class=Body>Needless to say, the DRS flight demonstration aims to advance the
demonstrated capabilities of a DRS several orders of magnitude beyond that
which has been demonstrated in flight.</p>

<p class=Body><i>The Interferometry Measurement System<o:p></o:p></i></p>

<p class=Body>Flight qualified laser systems close to meeting LISA&#8217;s
specifications exist.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Only small
changes and qualification for longer lifetimes are needed.</p>

<p class=Body>Several laboratory schemes exist for laser frequency
control.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The main issues are their
adaptation to the flight environment.</p>

<p class=Body>The optical system is a new application of mostly flight
qualified components.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The design
is novel, with little precedent in space flight, though the techniques are used
in laboratory settings.</p>

<p class=Body>The phase measurement system applies well-known time and
frequency measurement techniques to a lower frequency regime than is commonly
done in standards laboratories.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>The laser and clock frequency noise correction algorithms have parallels
in ground-based gravitational wave detection, but are used in LISA to an
extraordinary extent.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>This is a
significant advancement, but not a new approach.</p>

<p class=Body>While the beam pointing system is novel, neither the sensing nor
the actuation rely on extensions of current commercial or laboratory technology.</p>

<p class=Body><i>System Verification<o:p></o:p></i></p>

<p class=Body>The system verification technologies, with the exception of the
demonstration flight, are all reasonable extensions of current capability.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span></p>

imaging a nearby star.<o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=Body><i>The Interferometry Measurement System<o:p></o:p></i></p>

<p class=Body>LISA&#8217;s interferometry measurement system is probably unique
to gravitational wave detection.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>However, there are many current mission concepts that call for metrology
interferometers that involve components or techniques similar to those found in
the LISA.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Examples are:</p>

<p class=BulletedList><![if !supportLists]><span style='font-family:Symbol'>&middot;<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><![endif]>Laser
components: lasers, laser stabilization schemes, </p>

<p class=BulletedList><![if !supportLists]><span style='font-family:Symbol'>&middot;<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><![endif]>Optical
components: beamsplitters, polarizers, fiber components, ultra-stable telescope,
</p>

<p class=BulletedList><![if !supportLists]><span style='font-family:Symbol'>&middot;<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><![endif]>Opto-mechanical
components: mounts, actuators, </p>

<p class=BulletedList><![if !supportLists]><span style='font-family:Symbol'>&middot;<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><![endif]>Electro-optic
components: phase modulators, frequency modulators, </p>

<p class=BulletedList><![if !supportLists]><span style='font-family:Symbol'>&middot;<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><![endif]>Phase
measurement sub-systems: phase meters, GPS-like receivers.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span></p>

<p class=Body>Most of the most missions based on imaging or stellar interferometers
(e.g., SIM, Starlight, TPF, MAXIM, SI) have metrology interferometers with
measurement resolutions near that of LISA.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>Optical ranging and communications missions may also share
components or subsystems.<o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=Body><i>System Verification<o:p></o:p></i></p>

<p class=Body>The test bed and test equipment technologies have some common heritage
with other missions.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The interferometry
test beds were inspired by the SIM technology development program.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The gravitational sensor simulator was
adapted from the GP-B gyroscope simulator.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>Other system verification technologies, such as torsion
pendulums and the micro-radian phase control of laser beams, may prove useful
in other missions that push the envelope of measurement science.</p>

<p class=Body>The integrated modeling effort builds on similar enterprises for advanced
missions having complex interactions between optical, mechanical, thermal and
control designs.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Integrated
modeling software traces back to early interferometric missions like SIM and
continues to be a major aspect of the NGST development.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The LISA Project is taking a more
aggressive end-to-end approach to integrated modeling than other projects have
and this experience may prove valuable to other projects that follow.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The LISA modeling is also drawing on
other large scale, computer-aided design efforts within NASA, such as the
Re-usable Space Transportation System (RSTS).</p>
