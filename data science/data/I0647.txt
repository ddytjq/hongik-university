<TD width=12><IMG height=1 src='http://www.thefa.com/static/framework/skins/thefa_skin/images/spacer.gif' width=1 border=0></TD>

<TD width=12><IMG height=1 src='http://www.thefa.com/static/framework/skins/thefa_skin/images/spacer.gif' width=1 border=0></TD>
<TD vAlign=top align=left><P><SPAN class=body12>07 May 2002</SPAN><BR><SPAN class=editorialtitle>The F.A. Abroad</SPAN></P></TD>
<TD width=12><IMG height=1 src='http://www.thefa.com/static/framework/skins/thefa_skin/images/spacer.gif' width=1 border=0></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD colspan=3>&nbsp;</TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD width=12><IMG height=1 src='http://www.thefa.com/static/framework/skins/thefa_skin/images/spacer.gif' width=1 border=0></TD>
<TD>
<TABLE height=108 cellSpacing=0 cellPadding=0 align=left border=0>
<TBODY>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>The Football Association's international relations vision, as set out by Chairman Geoff Thompson, is; firstly, to develop an international perspective for The F.A.'s principle of "using the power of football to build a better future", revolving around a long-term commitment to using England's resources and knowledge to aiding less-privileged regions of the world in developing their football infrastructure; and secondly, to lead and shape the debate at UEFA and FIFA level by playing a full and proactive role within world football. 
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>As Geoff Thompson explained, the philosophy behind the vision is a simple one: "Football is truly a global sport, and The F.A. is committed to playing a role, extending a helping hand and building solid links around the world." 
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>Two years on, the International Relations team of Jane Bateman and Kim Fisher, led by F.A. Executive Director David Davies, can claim an impressive range of achievements in both fields. The first pillar, centred around The F.A.'s International Development Programme (IDP), has seen projects initiated in all regions of the world, ranging from goalkeeping courses to marketing, sports medicine and administration workshops, each targeted at the specific needs of the country or countries involved. The F.A. also holds annual international courses for fitness trainers, treatment and management of injuries, referees,  and the F.A. International Coaching Licence.

</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>Africa has been the beneficiary of several of these initiatives. The F.A. is recognised by UEFA for its leading role in the UEFA-CAF Meridian Project, providing assistance to partner countries Botswana, Lesotho and Malawi in the areas of technical and administrative support, referee development, IT training and the supply of kit and equipment. A girls' football scheme in Kenya has also received F.A. funding, while Cameroon, Nigeria, Uganda and South Africa have or will receive support or assistance in various fields.  Four CAF workshops will be held in 2002-3 for all CAF members in South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya and Cameroon, respectively in the areas of Administration, Coaching, Referee Instruction and Marketing, as agreed by F.A. Chairman Geoff Thompson and CAF President Issa Hayatou at the 2002 CAF Congress in Mali.
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>Assistance for Asia has generally involved the provision of technical expertise and know-how. Goalkeeping and youth coaching courses have been conducted by members of The F.A.'s coaching staff, while aiding AFC with the formulation of their Strategic Development Plan for the region. 
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>Workshops for youth coaches and sports medicine specialists have been held by F.A. experts Dick Bate and Alan Hodson, for members of the Oceania Football Confederation.
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>The F.A. has also been sharing English experience in marketing & sponsorship and strategic planning for coaches through the organisation of workshops for members of North, Central America and the Caribbean confederation, CONCACAF. The first, entitled "Marketing the Beautiful Game" was headed by Paul Barber, the F.A.'s Director of Marketing, and Peter Gandolfi, Head of Sports Marketing for England sponsor Nationwide, whilst the second was conducted by F.A. coaches Les Reed and Martin Hunter. CONCACAF members will also benefit from The F.A. International Coaching Licence course, a Referee Instructors course and a follow-up Marketing & Administration workshop later in 2002.
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>In South America The F.A. has focused its attention on aiding and advising the Paraguay FA during their period of restructuring, putting in place a new administrative framework which has yielded encouraging results. An Administration workshop for all CONMEBOL members is to be arranged later in 2002.
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>These projects would of course be impossible to realise without solid partnerships with other organisations. Aside from football bodies such as UEFA and FIFA, the International Relations team have developed ties with the Foreign and Commonwealth Office, the British Council, UK Sport, Sport England and a number of volunteer organisations and international charities, including FIFA's long-standing partner SOS Children's Villages. Over the last year, English football representatives have visited Villages from Thailand to Lesotho to Paraguay. In addition, commercial partners such as Mitre and Umbro provided logistical support in the form of kit and equipment. 
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>The second key objective for The F.A.s International Relations team was to gain a stronger voice for English football at European and world level, and to play a full part in the direction and future of the universal game. Building on the contacts developed during England's 2006 World Cup bid, the international profile and reputation of The F.A. has arguably never been higher. While development projects are of course an integral part of building bridges around the world, it must be backed up by consistent dialogue and debate. 
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>England has an unprecedented level of representation within UEFA committees, with Chairman Geoff Thompson a key and well-respected member of the Executive Committee. Seventeen venues in the North East, Yorkshire and Lincolnshire played host to a successful European Under-16 Championship in April/May, culminating in 31,000 fans flocking to Sunderland's Stadium of Light for the final between France and Spain. The tournament provided a reminder of the passion for football in England and the country's ability to organise international competitions.
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>International fixtures have allowed The F.A.'s international relations team to strengthen relationships with national associations across Europe, while the introduction of F.A. Ambassadors Goodwill trips has also been a notable success. As Executive Director David Davies explained: "These visits are about more than just handing out t-shirts: they are about demonstrating The F.A.'s long-term commitment to helping less-privileged countries around the world." 
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>The first such trip saw an England Ambassadors team travel to Moscow last November to play an equivalent Russian team as part of the celebrations surrounding Konstantin Beskov's birthday and the anniversary of Dinamo Moscow's first visit to Britain. This was followed by the visit of F.A. Goodwill Ambassadors Ian Wright and Mark Bright to Tirana ahead of England's World Cup qualifier against Albania, an experience described by Wright as "like going from colour to black and white". The two-day stay took in youth coaching courses and a UNICEF Child Friendly Spaces project, and left an indelible impression on the participants. 
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>Another team travelled to South Africa in June 2001 to play a charity match for the Ellis Park Disaster Fund and Nelson Mandela Children's Fund, and also featured visits to township football projects in Soweto and Durban. A highly entertaining match in front of a vibrant, 20,000-strong crowd ended in a 2-2 draw. South African Football Association CEO Danny Jordaan emphasised how the two countries had come together in the wake of unsuccessful 2006 World Cup bids: "The English F.A. has made a huge contribution to heal the wounds of the past and to walk hand in hand in solidarity into a better and brighter future, not only for football but for the people of South Africa and England. The two nations share one passion - football - and we can use that to cross social, racial, cultural and economic considerations, and in that respect this goodwill trip has been a huge success." 
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>In February 2002, at the request of the Ministry of Defence, The F.A.'s David Davies led a delegation to Afghanistan in support of the symbolic "Game of Unity" held at the Ghazi Olympic Stadium in Kabul. F.A. Ambassadors Lawrie McMenemy and Gary Mabbutt managed the Kabul United and ISAF (International Security Assistance Force) teams and Peter Jones, Andy Martin, Keith Hill and Peter Eastwood were match officials. On behalf of FIFA David Davies and F.A. International Relations Officer Kim Fisher met with the Afghanistan Football Federation to assess immediate needs to rebuild football in the country and to officially welcome them back into the football family.
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>A quarterly international newsletter, Communiqu√©, published in English, French and Spanish, keeps the international and domestic football community up to date with what The F.A. is doing around the world. Expanded from four pages to eight, its increasing volume is testament to the ever-growing list of projects and initiatives.
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>Head of International Relations Jane Bateman is excited about the future: "Over the next year we will be building on the work accomplished so far, looking to initiate relations and projects with more countries while continuing to develop our existing relationships. The response that we have received from around the world has been very encouraging, and confirmed our belief that English football needs to play a leading role in the world game." 
</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>&nbsp;</P></TD>
</TR>
<TR>
<TD><P class=body08>For more information contact Jane Bateman, Kim Fisher or Morag Taylor from the International Relations Team on 0207 745 4545. 
</P></TD>
</TR>
