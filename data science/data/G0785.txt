    <H1><CENTER>Anticipating the Perseids</CENTER></H1>

    <H4><CENTER>The 2001 Perseid meteor shower peaks on August 12th.
    Will it be an extraordinary sky show like last year -- or a moonlit
    disappointment? Wake up early and find out.</CENTER></H4>
</TD>

    <P><A HREF="http://antwrp.gsfc.nasa.gov/apod/ap000821.html"><B><IMG 
    SRC="../images/perseids2001/perseidaurora_westlake_med.jpg" 
    WIDTH="223" HEIGHT="251" ALIGN="RIGHT" BORDER="1" NATURALSIZEFLAG="3"
    ALT="see caption"></B></A><B>July 31, 2001:</B> Summer nights
    and shooting stars. They're an unbeatable combination. That's
    why northern sky watchers look forward so to the annual Perseid
    meteor shower in August. During the shower's peak, which happens
    this year on August 12th, dozens and sometimes hundreds of meteors
    shoot across the sky every hour. Dark-sky observers who stay
    out all night sometimes count more than a thousand!</P>

    <P>The much-anticipated Perseids, as reliable as clockwork, rarely
    fail to please. Around this time last year, however, enthusiasts
    were bracing for something unusual: Perseid disappointment. On
    the night of the shower's 2000 maximum, the Moon was to be nearly
    Full. It would outshine all but the brightest shooting stars.</P>

    <P><B><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Right</FONT></B><FONT SIZE="-1"
     FACE="Arial">: Jimmy Westlake captured this photo of a Perseid
    meteor streaking through Northern Lights on Aug. 12, 2000. In
    the foreground is Hahn's Peak, an extinct volcano in Colorado.
    [<A HREF="http://spacescience.com/headlines/y2000/ast14aug_1.htm">more</A>]</FONT></P>

    <P>Undaunted, sky watchers went outdoors in great numbers. Perhaps
    it was simply habit. After all, the Perseids have been putting
    on their annual show for more than two thousand years. Veteran
    meteor watchers call the shower &quot;Old Faithful&quot; and
    many figured it might be worth watching in spite of the Moon's
    glare.</P>

    </TABLE>They were right. Indeed, it was a night that no sky watcher
    was likely to forget -- ever!</P>

    <P>Just as hopeful observers were settling in for a long spell
    of meteor watching, <A HREF="http://science.msfc.nasa.gov/headlines/y2000/ast14aug%5F1.htm">the
    sky erupted</A> in color. A billion-ton cloud of electrified
    gas from the Sun (a &quot;coronal mass ejection&quot;) had crashed
    into Earth's magnetic field and ignited widespread auroras. All
    across North America sheets of red and green and purple light
    danced across the sky, stunning onlookers.</P>

    <P>That's when the Perseids showed up.</P>

    <P>&quot;I couldn't believe my eyes,&quot; said an observer from
    Bishop, CA. Bright Perseid meteors, plentiful after all, were
    streaking through seldom-seen Northern Lights. &quot;The moonlight
    hardly mattered,&quot; he added.</P>

    <P>It just goes to show, you never know what might happen during
    a night under the stars!</P>

    <P><CENTER><A HREF="../images/perseids2001/schmutz1.jpg"><IMG 
    SRC="../images/perseids2001/schmutz1_strip.jpg" WIDTH="450" 
    HEIGHT="108" ALIGN="BOTTOM" BORDER="1" NATURALSIZEFLAG="3" ALT="see caption"></A></CENTER></P>

    <P><B><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Above</FONT></B><FONT SIZE="-1"
     FACE="Arial">: Douglas Schmutz captured this image of a faint
    Perseid meteor (visible in the lower middle of an enlarged image)
    and pink aurora borealis over Hamblin Valley, Utah. [<A HREF="http://spacescience.com/headlines/y2000/ast14aug_1.htm">more</A>]</FONT></P>

    <P>This year experts are again predicting a meager display of
    Perseids, and the reason sounds familiar. A swollen quarter Moon,
    bright and shining, will rise at midnight on Sunday, August 12th.
    By dawn -- just when the shower should be at its best -- the
    Moon will lie high in the sky. Bright moonlight will likely reduce
    apparent meteor rates to no more than 20 or 30 per hour.</P>

    <P>&quot;That's still a wonderful meteor shower and I recommend
    trying to see it,&quot; says astronomy professor George Lebo,
    a Summer Faculty Fellow at the NASA Marshall Space Flight Center.
    &quot;No matter where you live, the best time to watch will be
    between local midnight and dawn on Sunday morning, August 12th.&quot;
    (Local midnight means midnight in your time zone.) &quot;The
    Perseids are primarily a northern hemisphere shower,&quot; he
    added. Sky watchers south of the equator won't see nearly as
    many shooting stars as their northern cousins.</P>

    <P><CENTER><IMG SRC="../images/perseids2001/skymap.gif" WIDTH="400"
    HEIGHT="395" ALIGN="BOTTOM" BORDER="1" NATURALSIZEFLAG="3" ALT="see caption"></CENTER></P>

    <P><B><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Above</FONT></B><FONT SIZE="-1"
     FACE="Arial">: The sky as seen by an observer at mid-Northern
    latitudes looking east at 4:00 am local time on August 12, 2001.
    The red dot denotes the Perseid radiant. Even if you don't see
    many meteors, the dazzling trio of planets -- Jupiter, Venus,
    and Saturn -- will make the trip outside worthwhile.</FONT></P>

    <P>Watching for Perseids is easy. Simply find a dark observing
    site, away from city lights if possible, and look up toward an
    unobstructed part of the sky. Meteors will streak overhead. If
    you trace their tails backwards, most will lead to the constellation
    <A HREF="http://www.astronomical.org/constellations/per.html">Perseus</A>,
    which contains a point in the sky called &quot;the Perseid radiant.&quot;</P>

    <P>The Perseid radiant lies near the northeastern horizon at
    midnight, but it will climb higher as the night wears on. By
    dawn it will lie about 70 degrees above the northeastern horizon.
    The Moon -- bright and beautiful, but a real nuisance -- will
    be in the neighboring constellation Taurus. Like an unwanted
    guest, the Moon will follow Perseus as it ascends the night sky.</P>

    <P><A HREF="http://antwrp.gsfc.nasa.gov/apod/ap000812.html"><IMG 
    SRC="../images/perseids2001/perseid3_sk_med.jpg" WIDTH="275"
    HEIGHT="166" ALIGN="RIGHT" BORDER="1" NATURALSIZEFLAG="3" ALT="see caption"></A>&quot;Don't
    look in the direction of the Moon,&quot; advises Lebo. Instead,
    face somewhat away from the Perseid radiant with the Moon at
    your back. Perseid meteors travel far across the sky, so you
    won't need to look directly toward Perseus to see them. An old
    meteor watcher's trick is to view a moonlit shower from the shadow
    of a tall building. The building will block some of the sky,
    but it will also reduce the Moon's glare.</P>

    <P><B><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Above</FONT></B><FONT SIZE="-1"
     FACE="Arial">: A colorful Perseid meteor. Credit &amp; Copyright:
    S. Kohle &amp; B. Koch</FONT></P>

    <P>Typical Perseid meteors are about as bright as the stars in
    the Big Dipper -- and many are considerably brighter. If you
    stay out all night on August 12th, don't be surprised to see
    several colorful fireballs rivaling the brilliant planet Venus.
    Such meteors can be seen even from urban areas with light pollution.</P>

    <P>Every Perseid meteor that you spot this month is a tiny piece
    of periodic comet Swift-Tuttle. The comet swings by the Sun at
    135 year intervals and leaves behind a trail of dusty debris.
    Its dust particles strike Earth's atmosphere traveling 59 km/s
    (132,000 mph). Their extreme speed is the reason that tiny comet
    particles, most no larger than grains of sand, can produce such
    dazzling streaks of light. Typical dust specks burn up entirely
    about 100 km above our planet's surface.</P>

    <P><A HREF="http://stardust.jpl.nasa.gov/science/sd-particle.html"><IMG 
    SRC="../images/perseids2001/idp.gif" WIDTH="164" HEIGHT="143"
    ALIGN="LEFT" BORDER="1" NATURALSIZEFLAG="3" ALT="see caption"></A><B><FONT
     SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Left</FONT></B><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">:
    Tiny comet flakes like this are at the heart of fiery-looking
    Perseid meteors. This one is only 10 microns across. [<A HREF="http://stardust.jpl.nasa.gov/science/sd-particle.html">more
    information</A>]</FONT></P>

    <P>Earth entered the outskirts of Swift-Tuttle's debris stream
    in late July, and already sky watchers are spotting 3 to 5 Perseids
    per hour in the predawn sky. Meteor rates will gently climb in
    the days ahead as Earth approaches the heart of the debris stream.
    Sometime on August 12th we'll encounter a concentrated filament
    of comet dust and meteor rates will soar. The shower that everyone
    has been waiting for will finally be underway.</P>

    <P>But will it be worth the wait? Will the 2001 Perseids rival
    last year's extraordinary show? Or will the Perseids, out of
    character, disappoint? The best way to find out is to be outdoors
    that Sunday morning and see for yourself!</P>
