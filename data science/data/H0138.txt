<h2>Evaluation of E Test for Detection of Beta Lactam Resistance

in Streptococcus pneumoniae</h2>



<p><b>Presented at CACMID, October 1997<br>

L. McKercher*, M. Lovgren, T. Ng, and G.J. Tyrrell</b></p>





<p>Following is a study undertaken at the National Centre for Streptococcus to evaluate the E Test for the detection of high level beta lactam resistance in <i>Streptococcus pneumoniae</i>.  The objective of the study was to test isolates with penicillin susceptibilities at or around the resistant breakpoint of 2.0 mg/L.  The evaluation compared E Test minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) values to those obtained by Broth Microdilution MIC.</p>



<p>One hundred and thirty-six isolates were selected that had penicillin Broth Microdilution MIC&#146;s performed previously and were found to be &gt;0.5 mg/L.  All isolates were stored in 20% skim milk at &#150;70<FONT SIZE="-1"><SUP>o</SUP></FONT>C.  Before testing, all isolates were subcultured onto BAPs and incubated overnight in CO<FONT SIZE="-1"><SUB>2</SUB></FONT> twice before testing.  Broth Microdilution MIC trays were prepared in-house for all three antibiotics.   Both the Broth Microdilution trays and the commercial AB BIODISK E Test strips were stored at &#150;20<FONT SIZE="-1"><SUP>o</SUP></FONT>C until use. 



<p>E Test was performed according to manufacturer recommendations.  The 0.5 McFarland suspension was streaked onto Mueller Hinton agar with 5% sheep blood and the plates were incubated at 35<FONT SIZE="-1"><SUP>o</SUP></FONT>C in 5 &#150; 7% CO<FONT SIZE="-1"><SUB>2</SUB></FONT> for 20 to 24 hours.  Using the same 0.5 McFarland suspension, Broth Microdilution MIC trays were set up according to NCCLS recommendations.  The trays were prepared with cation adjusted Mueller Hinton broth with 3% lysed horse blood and incubated at 35<FONT SIZE="-1"><SUP>o</SUP></FONT>C in ambient air for 20 to 24 hours.</p>



<p>The results were analysed using the Broth Microdilution MIC as the Gold standard method errors and were classified according to the following criteria:

<ul>

<li>A dilution error was categorized as any difference of greater than 1 log<FONT SIZE="-1"><SUB>2</SUB></FONT> dilution between the two methods.

<li> Minor errors were described as any result giving a single category change in the interpretation.  (S-I, I-S, I-R, R-I)

<li> Major errors were described as the E Test method giving a resistant MIC and the Broth Microdilution MIC being susceptible.  The sample chosen didn&#146;t include any penicillin sensitive strains, therefore this type of error was not possible for penicillin.

<li>Very major errors were described as the E Test method giving a susceptible MIC and the Broth Microdilution method a resistant MIC.

</ul>

<p>Table 1 shows a summary of the errors.  The low number of dilution errors indicates that the precision of the E Test is satisfactory.  Note that penicillin produced the largest number of minor errors with 37 out of 136 (or  27.2%) of the isolates giving an error.</p>

<p>A scatter plot of the results for penicillin testing follows.  The sample selected is clustered at the category breakpoint between intermediate and resistant.  When the Broth Microdilution MIC is 1.0 mg/L, the correlation is acceptable with 45/51 or 88% of the isolates tested showing an identical result with both methods.  At 2.0 mg/L only 33/66 or 50% of the results are identical and 50% gave a minor error.  Examining the minor errors, 36 out of the 37 or 97% of them result from an E Test result that is in the intermediate category and the Broth Microdilution result is in the resistant category. </p>

<p>In conclusion, we are recommending that if the E Test is being used for Penicillin susceptibility testing and a MIC of 1.0 mg/L is observed, it should be confirmed with Broth Microdilution testing because it may be a resistant isolate.  This has now become standard practice both in the National Centre for Streptococcus and recently in our routine diagnostic laboratory at the University of Alberta Hospitals.  </p>

<p>Similarly, although the evidence is not quite as convincing as it is for penicillin, cefotaxime E Tests of 0.5 mg/L should be confirmed with the Broth Microdilution method to eliminate the possibility of a falsely susceptible result.  </p>



<p>At this time, our data suggests that for ceftriaxone, the E Test is reliable for distinguishing between intermediate and susceptible isolates of Streptococcus pneumoniae.  For labs using the E Test, Ceftriaxone may be a better choice than cefotaxime for testing susceptibility against 3rd generation cephalosporins for pneumococcus isolates.  </p>
