				<td width="100%" class="tableTitleRow"><p class="indent25X10"><font class="news"><b>Hussain talking a good game</b></font></p></td>
				<td rowspan="2" valign="top" width="196">
				<img src="/images/news/1543.jpg" width="196" height="146"><!--  --><br><img src="/1.gif" width="1" height="5"><br>

					<td><font class="smallwhite">Hussain: Skipper is taking it easy<br><br>© ALLSPORT</font></td>

				<td><p class="indent25X10"><img src="/1.gif" width="1" height="4"><br>
				<font class="newsdate"> Monday, January 14, 2002 17:35 (GMT)</font><br>
				<img src="/1.gif" width="1" height="18"><br>
				<font class="news">Just three weeks after departing from India with his head held high - despite the loss in the Test series - Nasser Hussain is back in the country for the series of six one-day internationals and has again displayed his knack of saying the right thing without hurting any sentiments.<br><br>The England skipper has made it clear that England hope to take full advantage of India's tendency to buckle under pressure.<br><br>"India gets to a lot of finals and loses. Our strategy is to keep India under pressure," Hussain told reporters shortly after his team&#8217;s arrival in Kolkata last week.<br><br>If his tactics in last month&#8217;s Test series is any indication, there is little doubt that Hussain will keep up the pressure in such a way that the Indians make the maximum mistakes.<br><br>The Indian-born English captain has already earned rave reviews for his leadership qualities, being hailed in some circles as England&#8217;s best captain since Mike Brearley &#8211; which is to say, in the last 20 years. <br><br>Even Indian supporters, who initially termed his tactics at curbing the Indian batsmen with a packed leg-side and having left-arm spinner Ashley Giles bowling outside the leg stump as &#8220;shamelessly negative&#8221;, are now pay grudging tribute to Hussain, while admitting that he was the captain of a weaker side.<br><br>And among his admirers he can count on former Test cricketers Erapalli Prasanna and Woorkheri Raman, who have showered praise on his shrewd, strategic planning. He has been hailed as a thinking captain.<br><br>If anything, Hussain will continue to carry this kind of deep analysis into the one-day game, although the format may not allow him too much leeway in repeating the tactics he resorted to in the Test series. But one must not forget that England are a pretty handy limited overs side and have been strengthened by the return of Darren Gough, Andy Caddick and Graham Thorpe. <br><br>Hussain in fact fired the first salvo off the field when at the press conference, he said  &#8220;when the pressure is there, India can collapse&#8221;.<br><br>He was referring to India's record of nine consecutive defeats in finals of one-day tournaments involving three or more countries, stretching back to 1998. <br><br>While conceding that India was superior, he emphasized the point that his side could pull off a win if they raise their performances. The England captain said his team would have to perform with the same verve they showed to sweep a five-match one-day series in Zimbabwe last year to recover from 11 consecutive one-day losses. &#8220;Then, we showed the right attitude towards one-day cricket. India is obviously not Zimbabwe, we have to up our game by at least 20 percent if we've got to beat them." <br><br>Hussain has been an instant hit with the Indian media since he has always given `good copy&#8217;. He answers questions in a straightforward, honest manner, complete with his by now trademark smile and impish sense of humor. Asked about Sourav Ganguly's poor Test batting form, Hussain felt the Indian skipper would still be a threat. "Saurav is a fine player. I am sure his form will return," he said and then added in a lighter vein, "But I hope it doesn't in the next six matches." <br><br>But then Hussain&#8217;s hallmark has also been his ability not to be drawn into anything that smells of controversy. For example, he refused to be drawn into remarks by former Indian captain Sunil Gavaskar, who in his syndicated column described England's Test performance as "boring".<br><br>Hussain&#8217;s admirable response was "Gavaskar is a legend as a player. I will listen to what the great man has to say. He has his own opinion, we have ours. I have no problems with that and I am not going to give something back." <br><br>Ultimately, with all his planning and strategy, Hussain can take heart from the fact that England has a pretty good record in this country against India.<br><br>While they lost the three-match series in 1981-82 by 2-1, they took the five-match series three years later by an emphatic 4-1 margin. And on their last tour nine years ago, the six-match series ended level at 3-3.<br><br>In addition, England won the World Cup semifinal in 1987, but lost in the MRF World Series game two years later. <br><br>- <i>Partab Ramchand</i></font><br>
