<h1>Question 2: Significant Technological Challenges</h1>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>I believe that the most difficult challenge for SAFIR will
be the cooling to ~4K of the entire telescope structure, the secondary mirror assembly,
and the optics train in the instrument cryostat.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>Given that SAFIR will be a long duration mission, stored
cryogens are not an attractive option, so a closed-cycle cooler capable of
providing perhaps 100 mW of cooling at 4K.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>This requirement is close to the goals of the ACTDP
(Advanced Cryocooler Technology Development Program), which call for 7.5mW at
6K flight-ready by 2005.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>An
extension of this development program might yield twice this power at 4K a few
years beyond that, if funding is made available.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>This cooler would have to have high reliability, long
lifetime, and feature high enough efficiency to avoid taxing the spacecraft
power bus substantially.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>Additionally, coolers for ~0.1K for detectors will be necessary.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>The second most difficult challenge is likely to be the
development of large format detector arrays for the far-IR wavelengths.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>In the US, groups at GSFC, JPL, and at
several universities are making progress on direct detectors.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Groups at JPL and at universities are
working on heterodyne instruments.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>There are challenges in improving sensitivity, scalability to large
arrays, and (for heterodyne systems) local oscillators and backend electronics.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>There is no substantial program for
investing in far-IR detector development for space purposes; this seems to be
mission-driven and consequently not well-focused on a mission like SAFIR.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>A third challenge is the development of a deployable,
cryogenic telescope in a ~10m diameter format with surface sufficient to
achieve diffraction-limited imaging at 40&micro;m.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>It might be safe to assume that NGST will bear the brunt of
this development, and that SAFIR will be able to piggyback on this effort.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>If SAFIR looks substantially different
from NGST, this may not be the case.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>A fourth challenge &#8211; though no less necessary than the
other three &#8211; is in the development of testing facilities for large
aperture cryogenic telescope systems.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>It is technically very difficult to simulate a space-like environment to
enable ground-based testing of a deployable, cryogenic telescope.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<h1>Question 3: Metrics for Success:</h1>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>In the case of cryocoolers, it is simple to envision a
second ACTDP opportunity to develop technology for extension to 4K; higher
powers can be achieved by larger monolithic or paralleled systems.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Technical readiness will depend on the
demonstration of a single system capable of meeting the SAFIR design
requirements at a reduced scale.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>Furthermore, demonstration of the systems engineering trades for the
design of a cooling system for a large aperture telescope must be done.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Some metrics are base temperature,
power, efficiency, reliability, system complexity, and scalability.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>Detectors must be demonstrated that can meet the sensitivity
requirement (e.g., NEP of ~10<sup>&#8211;19</sup>W/&#8730;Hz for low spectral
resolution instrument), and that are fabricated in an architecture that permits
scaling to larger array formats.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>There are two separate thrusts for investigation: sensitivity and architecture.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Some metrics are sensitivity, architecture,
and system complexity.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>The optical system can be demonstrated with metrics of
aperture size, figure, emissivity, and complexity of fabrication, deployment
and operation.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<h1>Question 4: Demonstrations:</h1>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>For cryocoolers, most of the performance requirements can be
met on the ground in limited testing.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>Flight demonstrations would be a good idea, and may be possible on
technology spacecraft or the Shuttle.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>Detectors can also be tested on the ground, but very low
background detectors (e.g., moderate resolution direct detection spectrometers)
can probably not be tested other than in a laboratory environment.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>A precursor space mission to SAFIR is
probably the only space flight demonstration possible.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>The optical demonstration will be tricky if deployment is
considered, but NGST will pave the way for this.<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>Testing the optical quality at cryogenic temperatures will
be a real challenge, but again NGST may teach us what to do.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<h1>Question 5: Status of metrics:</h1>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>Briefly, cryocoolers cannot meet the temperature/power
specification without outrageous demands on spacecraft bus power
(&gt;10kW).<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>This may change within
two years, since the ACTDP is pushing this limit.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>Detectors have been made that approach the desired
sensitivity for continuum instruments, but no architecture for kilopixel arrays
has been proven.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Sensitivity for
high resolution instruments is still a few years in the future, provided
technology development funding is allocated to this.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>It is a bit too early to tell if NGST will demonstrate the
optical technology SAFIR needs.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>After this summer&#8217;s selection of NGST technology, this may become
more obvious.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<h1>Question 6: Can current technology meet requirements?</h1>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>For cryocoolers, there are many potential low temperature
technologies (e.g., Stirling+JT, pulse tube, turbo-Brayton, sorption coolers,
magnetic coolers).<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>Alone or in
combination, it is possible to achieve the requirements with these
technologies, but it will be necessary to demonstrate sufficient cooling power
and high enough efficiency to be practical.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>Detectors planned for near future flight missions such as
SIRTF and Herschel do not yet meet the SAFIR requirements for sensitivity and
wavelength coverage.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>It is likely
to be necessary to adopt new detector technologies such as superconducting TES
bolometers or tunnel junctions with RF-SET readouts in order to reach the
goals.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>The technologies required for a deployable, cryogenic,
diffraction-limited telescope have not been fully developed, but the resources
currently directed to this research will probably make this a
low-technology-risk area within a few years.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<h1>Question 7: Will other missions develop similar technologies?</h1>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>Cryocoolers are under development for many missions.<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>The SEU Technology Subcommittee will
hear from CMBPOL and several high energy missions that would benefit from 4K
cryocoolers.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>Detectors of the superconducting TES bolometer type would be
candidate detectors for CMBPOL, and very similar detectors are being readied
for Constellation-X as microcalorimeters.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>The common interest between the far-IR and the X-ray regime in these
detectors makes them a very attractive technology.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>NGST is developing the first large, deployable, cryogenic
telescope.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>With NGST as a
predecessor, there could be a strong synergy between NGST and SAFIR in that
SAFIR could, quite probably, use an identical telescope designs scaled up to
~10m aperture.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class=MsoNormal>Returning to the issue of testing, it is easy to draw the
conclusion that any missions that invest in similar technologies will likely
develop similar testing facilities.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>Whatever giant test setup NGST uses could probably accommodate SAFIR
with some modification.<b><o:p></o:p></b></p>
