      <table width="430" border="0" cellspacing="0" cellpadding="0">
        <tr valign="top">
          <td width="130" class="bodyBold">Advance</td>
          <td width="300">The term given to the mortgage loan .</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td colspan="2">&nbsp;</td>
        </tr>
        <tr valign="top">
          <td width="130" class="bodyBold">Annuity mortgage</td>
          <td width="300">See capital and interest mortgage.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td colspan="2">&nbsp;</td>
        </tr>
        <tr valign="top">
          <td width="130" class="bodyBold">APR</td>
          <td width="300">The APR represents the total amount of interest paid
            on a loan, added to any other charges which the borrower has to meet.
            This total is divided by the number of years the mortgage will last
            for to give the amount paid each year. The amount is then converted
            into a percentage of the average loan over the mortgage period, to
            give the annual percentage rate. </td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td colspan="2">&nbsp;</td>
        </tr>
        <tr valign="top">
          <td width="130" class="bodyBold">Arrangement fee</td>
          <td width="300">The fee for applying for a mortgage. This is usually
            non-refundable, even if the bank rejects your application.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td colspan="2">&nbsp;</td>
        </tr>
        <tr valign="top">
          <td width="130" class="bodyBold">Base Rate</td>
          <td width="300">The Base Rate set by the Bank of England acts as the
            benchmark for the interest rates of high street banks.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td colspan="2">&nbsp;</td>
        </tr>
        <tr valign="top">
          <td width="130" class="bodyBold">Bridging Loan</td>
          <td width="300">Short-term loan to bridge the gap between the purchase
            of a new property and the sale of the old.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td colspan="2">&nbsp;</td>
        </tr>
        <tr valign="top">
          <td width="130" class="bodyBold">Buyer's report</td>
          <td width="300">Surveyor's report on a property - less detailed than
            a structural survey, but covering more than the lender's valuation.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td colspan="2">&nbsp;</td>
        </tr>
        <tr valign="top">
          <td width="130" class="bodyBold">Capital</td>
          <td width="300">The amount of the loan on which interest is calculated.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td colspan="2">&nbsp;</td>
        </tr>
        <tr valign="top">
          <td width="130" class="bodyBold">Capital & Interest Mortgage</td>
          <td width="300">With this type of mortgage the monthly repayment includes
            an element of the capital sum borrowed in addition to the interest
            charged. In the early years of the mortgage the majority of the monthly
            repayment consists of interest with only a small part repaying the
            capital. However, as the debt gradually reduces the element of capital
            increases and the interest element reduces, so although the monthly
            repayment stays the same (assuming interest rate doesn't change) the
            debt starts to reduce more quickly as the term of the mortgage progresses.
            On a 25 year term mortgage it would not be unusual to still owe over
            50% of the original debt after the first 15 years. Providing the correct
            monthly repayments are made on their due dates this mortgage will
            guarantee to repay the total mortgage debt at the end of the mortgage
            term. </td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td colspan="2">&nbsp;</td>
        </tr>
        <tr valign="top">
          <td width="130" class="bodyBold">Capital Gain</td>
          <td width="300">An increase in the value of a capital asset (either
            investment or real estate). The gain is not realized until the asset
            is sold for more than the original purchase price. A capital gain
            may be short-term (one year or less) or long-term (more than one year)
            and must be claimed on income taxes.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td colspan="2">&nbsp;</td>
        </tr>
        <tr valign="top">
          <td width="130" class="bodyBold">Chain</td>
          <td width="300">This occurs when the seller needs the sale of their
            house to go through before they can complete the purchase of another
            property. The same situation may exist for others in the chain. As
            a result, the whole chain can collapse if one link breaks.</td>
        </tr>
