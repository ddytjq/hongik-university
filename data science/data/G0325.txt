    <H1><CENTER><FONT COLOR="#000000">In Search of E.T.'s Breath</FONT></CENTER></H1>

    <H4><CENTER><FONT COLOR="#000000">Advanced space telescopes might
    soon probe far-off worlds for the chemical signatures of alien
    life.</FONT></CENTER></H4>
</TD>
    <TD WIDTH="16%">
    <P><CENTER><A HREF="http://www.nasa.gov/"><IMG SRC="../images/msfcmeatballsm.gif"

    <P><A HREF="http://extrasolar.spaceart.org/image.cgi?hd177830.jpg"><B><IMG 
    SRC="images/exo-atmospheres/_hd177830_sm.jpg" ALT="see caption"
    hspace="5" BORDER="1" ALIGN="RIGHT" NATURALSIZEFLAG="3" WIDTH="227"
    HEIGHT="237"></B></A><B><FONT COLOR="#000000">January 10, 2002:</FONT></B><FONT
     COLOR="#000000"> &nbsp;If &quot;E.T.&quot; is out there, whether
    in the form of intelligent beings or much simpler organisms,
    we may soon be hot on its trail. For the first time in history,
    the dream of searching for signs of life in other solar systems
    belongs not only on the philosopher's wish list, but on the list
    of doable and planned human endeavors.<BR>
<BR>
    Momentum is gaining rapidly. Only 6 years ago, the first planet
    around another Sun-like star was discovered by scientists using
    </FONT><A HREF="http://exoplanets.org/doppframe.html">Doppler
    Detection</A><FONT COLOR="#000000"> -- a method that reveals
    Saturn-sized (or larger) planets close to their parent suns.
    Today, we know of more than 80 candidates for such worlds, and
    more are being found all the time.</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">Scientists crossed </FONT>a new frontier<FONT
     COLOR="#000000"> in &quot;exo-planet&quot; research just last
    year when, using the Hubble Space Telescope, they </FONT><A 
    HREF="http://science.nasa.gov/headlines/y2001/ast27nov_1.htm">detected
    sodium</A><FONT COLOR="#000000"> in the atmosphere of a large
    alien world orbiting the star HD 209458. The Hubble data not
    only revealed that exo-planets have atmospheres, but also that
    we can analyze them from a distance. For the first time, humans
    are discovering <I>and exploring</I> worlds outside the solar
    system. </FONT></P>

    <P><TABLE WIDTH="128" BORDER="0" CELLSPACING="0" CELLPADDING="3"
    ALIGN="RIGHT">
      <TR>
        <TD WIDTH="100%">
        <TABLE WIDTH="115" ALIGN="LEFT" BORDER="1" CELLPADDING="5" CELLSPACING="0">

            WIDTH="89" HEIGHT="86" ALIGN="BOTTOM" BORDER="0" NATURALSIZEFLAG="3"
            ALT="subscription image"><BR>
            <FONT SIZE="-2" FACE="Arial,Helvetica,san-serif">Sign up for
            EXPRESS SCIENCE NEWS delivery</FONT></A></CENTER></TD>

      </TR>
    </TABLE><B><FONT COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Above:</FONT></B><FONT
     COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"> &nbsp;An artist's concept
    of alien worlds. Copyright </FONT><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"><A
    HREF="http://extrasolar.spaceart.org/extrasol.html">Lynette Cook</A></FONT><FONT
     COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">, all rights reserved.</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">So far, all known extra-solar planets
    are gas giants -- unlikely abodes for life as we know it. In
    fact, some are so large that they might not be planets at all,
    but a kind of failed star called a &quot;</FONT>brown dwarf<FONT
     COLOR="#000000">.&quot; Of greater interest are Earth-size planets,
    which are </FONT>too small<FONT COLOR="#000000"> for even the
    Hubble Space Telescope to detect. Nevertheless, astronomers believe
    they exist.<BR>
<BR>
    Enter </FONT><A HREF="http://www.kepler.arc.nasa.gov/">Kepler</A><FONT
     COLOR="#000000">, a space telescope </FONT>approved recently
    by NASA<FONT COLOR="#000000">.</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">Beginning in 2006, Kepler will monitor
    about </FONT>100,000 nearby stars<FONT COLOR="#000000">, searching
    for the slight dimming that occurs when an orbiting planet blocks
    some of the parent star's light.&nbsp;Because Kepler will be
    sensitive enough to detect planets as small as Earth, this celestial
    survey will give scientists an idea of how common Earth-like
    planets are -- and identify candidates for further study.<BR>
<BR>
    <IMG SRC="images/exo-atmospheres/new-nulling2.gif" ALT="see caption"
    WIDTH="96" HEIGHT="95" hspace="5" BORDER="0" ALIGN="LEFT" NATURALSIZEFLAG="0">
    If all goes as planned, an important new tool for exploring such
    planets will be operating by the end of this decade. Called the
    Terrestrial Planet Finder (</FONT><A HREF="http://tpf.jpl.nasa.gov/">TPF</A><FONT
     COLOR="#000000">), this space telescope will use a technique
    called &quot;</FONT>interferometry<FONT COLOR="#000000">&quot;
    to dramatically reduce the obscuring glare from the planet's
    parent star, allowing scientists to see the planet itself. The
    Web site for the European Space Agency's similar </FONT><A HREF="http://sci.esa.int/home/darwin/">Darwin</A><FONT
     COLOR="#000000"> project notes that, &quot;L</FONT>ooking for
    planets around nearby stars is like trying to discern, from a
    vantage point 1000 km away, the feeble light from a candle next
    to a lighthouse.&quot;</P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">It is indeed a daunting challenge, but
    worth the effort. The goal is nothing short of finding worlds
    that could support life -- and perhaps some that do.</FONT></P>

    <P><B><FONT COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Above:</FONT></B><FONT
     COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"> &nbsp;By taking advantage
    of the wave nature of light, </FONT><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"><A
    HREF="http://science.msfc.nasa.gov/headlines/y2001/ast15mar_1.htm">interferometry</A></FONT><FONT
     COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"> can cancel out some
    of the light coming from a star, enabling the telescope to image
    the much dimmer planets in orbit around it. Image courtesy </FONT><FONT
     SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"><A HREF="http://tpf.jpl.nasa.gov/">NASA's
    Terrestrial Planet Finder</A></FONT><FONT COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1"
     FACE="Arial"> mission.</FONT></P>

    <P><B><FONT COLOR="#000000" SIZE="+1">Seeing E.T.'s &quot;breath&quot;</FONT></B><FONT
     COLOR="#000000"><BR>
<BR>
    Planets circling other stars are many light years away. (A light-year
    is the distance that light travels in a year -- about 9.5 trillion
    km.) Even with the TPF's advanced optics, Earth-like worlds would
    appear as a single pixel of light. How, then, will it be possible
    to learn much about them?<BR>
<BR>
    </FONT><A HREF="images/exo-atmospheres/spectral_library.jpg"><IMG 
    SRC="images/exo-atmospheres/spectra_sm.jpg" ALT="see caption"
    WIDTH="266" HEIGHT="291" hspace="5" BORDER="1" ALIGN="RIGHT"
    NATURALSIZEFLAG="0"></A><FONT COLOR="#000000">Amazingly, that
    tiny speck of light can speak volumes about the planet from which
    it came. Embedded in that light are the &quot;fingerprints&quot;
    of the chemicals that have touched it, including the gases in
    the planet's atmosphere. By splitting the light into its component
    frequencies -- which for visible light creates the familiar rainbow
    of colors -- scientists can reveal these &quot;fingerprints&quot;
    and learn about the chemistry of the planet's atmosphere.</FONT></P>

    <P><B><FONT COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Right:</FONT></B><FONT
     COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"> &nbsp;If an alien astronomer
    on a distant planet were exploring our solar system, they might
    guess that something special is happening on Earth. The spectra
    of Venus and Mars show the mark of a carbon dioxide-rich atmosphere,
    but only Earth's spectrum has strong ozone and water vapor fingerprints
    as well. Scientists will use clues such as these to search for
    the chemical signs of life on planets around other stars. Click
    on the image for a </FONT><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"><A HREF="images/exo-atmospheres/spectral_library.jpg">graphic</A></FONT><FONT
     COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"> showing the spectral
    fingerprints of several important chemicals for astrobiology.
    Image courtesy NASA's Terrestrial Planet Finder mission.</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">If life is widespread on a planet, its
    atmosphere should show signs of life's presence. Just as the
    air you exhale has more carbon dioxide and less oxygen than the
    air you inhale, the combined &quot;breathing&quot; of all the
    life on a planet will change the chemistry of its atmosphere.
    If life is plentiful on the planet, these changes may be large
    enough to notice.<BR>
<BR>
    A simple premise -- but what would E.T.'s breath look like? Which
    gases should we search for? We know the answers for our own world,
    but predicting how an alien biology might interact with its atmosphere
    is no simple matter.<BR>
<BR>
    &quot;As astrobiologists we've got to be sure that we're not
    too Earth-centric,&quot; says Michael Meyer, senior scientist
    for astrobiology at NASA Headquarters in Washington, D.C. <BR>
<BR>
    The possibility that life elsewhere has a biology that's radically
    different from our own is perhaps the most exciting and challenging
    part of astrobiology (not to mention a ubiquitous theme of science
    fiction). If life evolves by random mutations and natural selection,
    why should we expect alien life forms to be even remotely similar
    to Earthly life?<BR>
<BR>
    </FONT><A HREF="http://www.divediscover.whoi.edu/"><IMG SRC="../images/vents/ss010408_photo9.jpg"
    ALT="see caption" WIDTH="296" HEIGHT="209" hspace="5" BORDER="1"
    ALIGN="LEFT" NATURALSIZEFLAG="0"></A><FONT COLOR="#000000">&quot;We
    have to be very careful about how foreign biology might be different
    from our own, especially when you get to the bigger molecules&quot;
    such as DNA, says David Des Marais, principal investigator for
    the Ames Research Center team of NASA's Astrobiology Institute.
<BR>
<BR>
    For example, people have speculated that silicon, a primary component
    of sand and a close cousin to carbon, could form the basis of
    an extraterrestrial biology. Alien life might forgo sunlight
    and depend instead on the geothermal energy in hydrogen and sulfur
    compounds emitted from the planet's interior, much like the deep-sea
    vent ecosystems here on Earth. Or maybe the chemistry of alien
    life will be utterly different and unimaginable.</FONT></P>

    <P><B><FONT COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Above:</FONT></B><FONT
     COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"> &nbsp;Even life on Earth
    can surprise scientists. The strange deep-sea creatures in this
    ecosystem, discovered in 1977, thrive on chemical energy rather
    than sunlight. [</FONT><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"><A HREF="http://science.nasa.gov/headlines/y2001/ast13apr%5F1.htm">more</A></FONT><FONT
     COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">]</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">Fortunately, the chemical constraints
    within which life must function make it likely that simple molecules
    such as oxygen and carbon dioxide will play the same roles in
    an extraterrestrial biology as they do on Earth.</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">&quot;Suppose,&quot; says Meyer, &quot;that
    there is silicon-based life. [It might be] photosynthetic, and
    you would still end up with oxygen in the atmosphere. You could
    go there and the life could be completely different, but some
    of the chemistry could still be the same [as on Earth].&quot;</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">&quot;The small molecules are going
    to be more universal,&quot; agrees Des Marais. &quot;Large molecules
    like DNA and chlorophyll represent later, highly significant
    innovations of life on Earth, but also the ones that may have
    differed elsewhere.&quot;<BR>
<BR>
    For this and other reasons, the exploration of distant Earth-like
    planets with TPF will focus on simple gases such as oxygen, ozone,
    carbon dioxide, methane, and, of course, water vapor.</FONT></P>

    <P><CENTER><A HREF="images/exo-atmospheres/ATM_Time_Earths.jpg"><IMG 
    SRC="images/exo-atmospheres/ATM_Time_Earths_sm2.jpg" ALT="see caption"
    WIDTH="410" HEIGHT="250" BORDER="1" NATURALSIZEFLAG="0" ALIGN="BOTTOM"></A></CENTER></P>

    <P><B><FONT COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Above:</FONT></B><FONT
     COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"> Oxygen gas is a good
    indicator of life. But, scientists caution, oxygen has been abundant
    on Earth only since the advent of photosynthesis -- probably
    a billion years or more <I>after</I> life began! To have the
    greatest chance of spotting extraterrestrial life, astrobiologists
    must consider how life looked on the young Earth, too, when the
    atmosphere was different. Image courtesy David Des Marais and
    Kevin Zahnle.</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">Oxygen and its tag-along cousin ozone
    will top the list of target molecules.</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">Without life, oxygen should be rare
    on rocky worlds. A small amount of it can be created without
    life by ultraviolet radiation that splits water vapor into hydrogen
    and oxygen. But that oxygen would be readily consumed by rocks
    and minerals on the planet's surface in the &quot;oxidizing&quot;
    reactions that produce, for example, rust. Volcanic gases also
    react with oxygen and remove it from the atmosphere. Geological
    processes alone usually work against the accumulation of oxygen.</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">An oxygen-rich atmosphere is, therefore,
    out of chemical equilibrium, suggesting that some active agent
    -- namely photosynthetic life -- is constantly replenishing the
    supply. As Carl Sagan noted in a 1997 <I>Scientific American</I>
    </FONT><A HREF="http://www.sciam.com/explorations/010697sagan/010697sagan3.html">article</A><FONT
     COLOR="#000000">, &quot;the great concentration of oxygen (20
    percent) in Earth's dense atmosphere is very hard to explain
    by [any means other than life.]&quot; The same would be true
    of planets around other stars.</FONT></P>

    <P><B><FONT COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Below</FONT></B><FONT
     COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">: This planet, which
    harbors abundant life, has an oxygen-rich atmosphere (with a
    dash of methane) and telltale signs of chlorophyll on the ground.</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000"><IMG SRC="images/exo-atmospheres/earth_1_apollo17_med.gif"
    WIDTH="225" HEIGHT="223" ALIGN="RIGHT" BORDER="0" NATURALSIZEFLAG="3"
    ALT="see caption">Next on the list of target molecules is methane.
    Scientists suspect that for roughly the first billion years of
    its history, life on Earth had not yet evolved oxygen-producing
    photosynthesis. Instead, the microorganisms that then dominated
    the planet tapped the energy in gases leaking out of the Earth's
    interior, with some microbes creating methane as a byproduct.</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">On a planet with a similar geology to
    Earth, methane levels greater than about 100 parts per million
    would suggest the presence of life. But methane would be a more
    ambiguous discovery than oxygen, because planets of a different
    geological make-up might produce abundant methane without life.</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">Other details about these planets --
    such as their size, their distance from the parent star, their
    carbon dioxide and water vapor levels, and their reflectivity
    -- will help scientists accurately interpret a methane or oxygen
    discovery. These other details can also be measured (or at least
    estimated) using TPF and other telescopes.</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">Some of these ideas have already been
    tested on a planet known to support life -- Earth.</FONT></P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">In 1990, the Galileo spacecraft </FONT><A
    HREF="http://science.nasa.gov/newhome/headlines/ast21may99_1.htm">made
    a flyby of our planet</A><FONT COLOR="#000000"> on its circuitous
    journey to Jupiter. As it passed, Galileo's sensors detected
    high levels of oxygen and methane in Earth's atmosphere and signs
    of chlorophyll on the ground. Chlorophyll, which absorbs light
    at the far-red end of the visible spectrum, is a &quot;red flag&quot;
    for life. The TPF won't be sensitive to chlorophyll on a planet's
    surface because atmospheric water vapor, which is opaque in the
    mid-infrared frequency range that TPF will observe, will hide
    the surface below. Even without chlorophyll, signs of oxygen
    and methane -- which TPF <I>can</I> detect -- would make a persuasive
    case for life.</FONT></P>

    <P><A HREF="images/exo-atmospheres/formation-flyer-beams.jpg"><B><IMG 
    SRC="images/exo-atmospheres/formation-flyer-beams_sm.jpg" ALT="see caption"
    WIDTH="279" HEIGHT="209" hspace="5" BORDER="1" ALIGN="LEFT" 
    NATURALSIZEFLAG="0"></B></A><FONT COLOR="#000000">If the TPF
    finds a habitable planet with lots of oxygen and some methane
    in its atmosphere, it would be a momentous discovery. But would
    such data really <I>prove</I> life is there? &quot;Proof&quot;
    is always a tall order in science, especially when discussing
    extraterrestrial life. </FONT>Nevertheless, say astrobiologists,
    such evidence would be &quot;very compelling.&quot; </P>

    <P><B><FONT COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">Above:</FONT></B><FONT
     COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"> &nbsp;This </FONT><FONT
     SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"><A HREF="http://olbin.jpl.nasa.gov/news/tpf/Dec2001.html">proposed
    design</A></FONT><FONT COLOR="#000000" SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">
    for NASA's Terrestrial Planet Finder mission will combine the
    light from four powerful telescopes to make Earth-size planets
    orbiting other stars visible. [</FONT><FONT SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial"><A
    HREF="http://tpf.jpl.nasa.gov/">more</A></FONT><FONT COLOR="#000000"
     SIZE="-1" FACE="Arial">]</FONT></P>

    <P>One day it might happen ... and after catching its first whiff
    of E.T.'s breath, humanity won't likely give up the chase. The
    next step would be an even larger space telescope that could
    see the planet as more than one pixel, allowing scientists to
    see surface features such as continents and to observe the changing
    of the planet's seasons. And perhaps by the end of the next decade
    it would be possible to send a probe across interstellar space
    to take a close-up look, which could finally provide incontrovertible
    evidence.</P>

    <P><FONT COLOR="#000000">Proof will be for the patient: Even
    using </FONT><A HREF="http://science.nasa.gov/BeyondRocketry.htm">advanced
    propulsion</A><FONT COLOR="#000000"> technologies, a probe might
    take decades to reach a neighboring star. But to answer a profound
    question that's been asked by humanity for centuries, perhaps
    that isn't too long to wait.</FONT></P>
