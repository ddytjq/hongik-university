<b>
<p>

We may consider three principal types of 
<a href="http://csep10.phys.utk.edu/astr162/lect/light/absorption.html">
spectra</a> which appear when the light from an object is broken up into its 
component wavelengths or <i>"dispersed"</i>: 
<ul>
<li>a continuous spectrum or <i>continuum</i>; the emission of a thermal 
spectrum is one type of continuum.
<li>an <i>absorption spectrum</i> or sometimes an absorption-line spectrum.
<li>an <i>emission spectrum</i> or emission-line spectrum.
</ul>
<p>
<center><img src="./images/physics/em_abs.gif"></center>
<p>
An 
<a href="http://www.lightlink.com/sergey/java/java/atomphoton/index.html">
absorption</a> spectrum is produced when a continuum passes through "cooler" 
gas.  Photons of the appropriate energies are absorbed by the atoms in the gas.
Although the photons may be re-emitted, they are effectively removed from the 
beam of light, resulting in a dark or <i>absorption</i> feature.  The 
atmospheres of stars act as a cooler blanket around the hotter interior of a 
star so that typical stellar spectra are absorption spectra.<p>
<img align=center src="./images/physics/abs_spect.gif">
<p>

<p>

and the understanding that such
<a href="http://lheawww.gsfc.nasa.gov/users/allen/spectral_classification.html">spectral classification</a> is essentially a sequence in atmospheric 
temperature was due to <a href="http://www.netsrq.com/~dbois/cannon.html">
Annie J. Cannon</a> at Harvard.

<h3><u>
The Stellar Spectral Sequence as a Temperature Sequence</u></h3>
<p>
The temperature of the stellar photosphere determines the rate and severity 
of collisions between molecules, atoms and ions which in turn determines: 
<ol>
<li><i>the molecular equilibrium</i> - if the star is too hot, fragile 
molecular 
bonds will be broken apart. Most molecules such as TiO are seen only in 
spectra of the coolest stars ( T = 3000 -- 4000K). Strong
molecules such as CH and CN can be seen in somewhat hotter stars like the sun. 
           
<li><i>the ionization equilibrium</i> - the hotter the temperature, the 
higher the
ionization state of the atoms in the stellar atmosphere will be. Atoms are 
ionized (or partially ionized) when they lose or gain an
electron. In cool stars most atoms will be neutral. At higher Temperature, 
easily ionized atoms such as Na, Ca, etc, will be ionized; above T = 10,000K 
hydrogen becomes ionized and above about 15,000K helium becomes ionized. 
           
<li><i>the number of atoms in excited states</i>. At low T, almost no H atoms 
are in the n=2 orbit, capable of absorbing "Balmer" photons, but as T 
increases the n=2 population increases and we see the hydrogen features, 
reaching a maximum in stars with T = 10,000. 
</ol><p>
       
Among the other things that we may determine from the absorption spectrum are: 
density, chemical composition, magnetic field strength, and radial velocity. 
These are all secondary effects compared to temperature.
