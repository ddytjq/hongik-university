				<font class="newsdate"> Monday, February 4, 2002 18:08 (GMT)</font><br>
				<img src="/1.gif" width="1" height="18"><br>
				<font class="news">When they arrived in India for the Test series in November last, England were written off as no hopers. A 3-0 whitewash was predicted for the home team against a side bereft of star names and weakened by the absence of Alec Stewart, Andy Caddick and Darren Gough.<br><br>Well, we all know what happened. Though India took the First Test, England had the better of two drawn Tests in the second and third games and returned home for a well-earned Christmas break with their heads high even in defeat. <br><br>Four weeks later, on the eve of the six match one-day international series, the pundits again predicted an easy win for India, despite having been proved wrong earlier and in spite of the fact that England were strengthened by the inclusion of Caddick and Gough.<br><br>Some critics in fact predicted that India would win the series by the tennis-like score of 6-0. It was pointed out that in the last couple of years, India had scored victories over New Zealand, Zimbabwe and South Africa and had lost to world champions Australia only by a narrow 3-2 margin. <br><br>And yet when the series ended at the Wankhede stadium in Mumbai on a humid Sunday night, the score line read: India 3, England 3.<br><br>The fluctuating fortunes and the narrow victories brought back vivid memories of the last contest in India between the two teams, nine years ago, which also ended with a similar scoreline.<br><br>The roles however were reversed. In 1993, India down 1-3, fought back to level the series, while this time India led 3-1 only for England to show remarkable resilience in taking the last two matches. <br><br>England&#8217;s showing was a supreme example of what fired-up team spirit and inspiring captaincy could achieve. Certainly history and form were against them and man for man, the Indian team looked stronger.<br><br>But matches can also be won in dressing rooms and hotel corridors and lobbies and not necessarily on the field. England proved this cricketing adage in spades by turning out a performance that could not fail to win the hearts of even the most diehard Indian cricket supporter.<br><br>They were the underdogs and for a team dismissed as no hopers, their leveling the series in the manner they did should, by any yardstick, rank as one of the most outstanding feats by any visiting team in India. <br><br>England displayed commendable fighting spirit and clearly had no intention of throwing in the towel even after they were down 1-3, or even when India looked to be in a winning position in the last two games.<br><br>If the batting lacked consistency, they could always depend on one or two making a sizeable score. Marcus Trescothick was a revelation and there is no doubt that the tour will do him a lot of good.<br><br>Hussain himself came up with a number of useful contributions. Nick Knight took some time to find his bearings but his century at the Kotla was a classy effort. The middle order remained a problem but Andrew Flintoff made up for this with a few breezy knocks. <br><br>However, the bowling covered up for any loopholes in the batting. The seamers were quite outstanding and Gough, Caddick, Flintoff and Matthew Hoggard served the side admirably.<br><br>By the end of the tour, Flintoff had emerged as a genuine match winner. And Hussain got an unexpected bonus with spinners Snape and Giles striking when it was most needed.<br><br>Giles&#8217; second spell at the Kotla was one of the highlights of the series. It takes guts for a bowler to even think of bowling again after he has conceded 32 runs in his first four overs and when confronted by an inform Sourav Ganguly who treats spinners with disdain.<br><br>But Giles in his second spell ripped the Indian innings apart with some incisive bowling and his five wickets in five overs proved decisive. <br><br>England were also well served by young Jamie Foster who, with encouragement, could have a long and fruitful career behind the stumps.<br><br>The fielding, not generally known to be England&#8217;s strong point, was a revelation. Led by their skipper, the players threw themselves at the ball, dived and leapt to take catches and displayed an efficiency that almost matched the South Africans at their best.<br><br>Hussain, as he had done in the Test series, again led by both personal example and tactical acumen. A lot of thought had been given to plotting a particular batsman&#8217;s downfall and in matters of strategy, Hussain certainly stole a march over Ganguly. <br><br>It must have been galling for India not to win a series that seemed to be theirs on more than one occasion. But then they have to take the blame for throwing opportunities away.<br><br>The inexperienced middle-order was perhaps the main reason for their capitulation. They were hurt by the absence of Rahul Dravid, who had to miss the series after undergoing treatment to a bothersome shoulder, and Venkatsai Laxman - dropped midway through following a string of low scores.<br><br>Dinesh Mongia, Hemang Badani and Md Kaif quite often could not give the run-rate the required impetus after the electrifying start given by Sachin Tendulkar, Virender Sehwag and Ganguly.<br><br>The absence of an all-rounder again told in the end and Ajit Agarkar did not exactly rise to the occasion. Despite a couple of timely contributions with bat and ball, he remains an enigma.<br><br>Overall, however the bowling was seen in better light than the batting though the gamble of playing Sarandeep Singh instead of Harbhajan Singh at New Delhi proved costly.<br><br>India however did unearth one true find in Ajay Ratra who could finally solve the vexed wicketkeeping problem. <br><br>- <i>Partab Ramchand</i></font><br>
