                              protons and neutrons held together by the
                              <I>Strong Force</I>. The  electrons "orbit"
                              the nucleus, attracted by the 
                              <I>Electrical Force</I>.

<DT><DD><IMG SRC="./images/doodads/pdot_sm.gif">
<u><i>Atomic Nuclues</i></u> - ~10<sup>-13</sup>cm.
<BR>
<BR>
<DT><DD><IMG SRC="./images/doodads/pdot_sm.gif">
<u><i>Size of Atom</i></u> - the radius of the first electron "orbit" in 
                                  the Hydrogen atom is 
                                  1/2&Aring; = 0.5 x 10<sup>-8</sup>cm.<BR>

The atom is about 100,000 times bigger than its nucleus. If the atomic 
nucleus were a pingpong ball (r ~ 1cm), its electrons would be
in orbits about a kilometer away.<p>

<DT><DD><IMG SRC="./images/doodads/pdot_sm.gif">
<u><b>Molecules</b></u> - Atoms build themselves into molecules 
                              (<i>e.g.</i> H<sub>2</sub>O) with sizes ranging 
                              from a few &Aring; up to 10<sup-3</sup> cm for
                              the largest molecules, such as DNA.
<p>
<DT><DD><IMG SRC="./images/doodads/pdot_sm.gif">
<u><b>Cells</b></u> - 10<sup>-2</sup> cm.<p>

<DT><DD><IMG SRC="./images/doodads/pdot_sm.gif">
<u><b>Humans</b></u> - The largest humans who don't play basketball are 
                           about 2 meters ( = 200 cm) in size; Professor 
                           Smith is 186 cm in height.<p>

<DT><DD><IMG SRC="./images/doodads/pdot_sm.gif">
<u><b>The Earth</b></u> - The Earth is about 25,000 miles around, which
                              makes its radius about 4000 miles. More 
                              precisely, the radius at the equator, R<sub>E
                              </sub> = 6378 km = 6.378 x 10<sup>8</sup> cm.
                              In other words, it would take about 7 million 
                              people to span the distance from San Diego to 
                              China through the center of the Earth.<p>
<DT><DD><IMG SRC="./images/doodads/pdot_sm.gif">
<u><b>The Sun</b></u> - The Sun, our nearest star is 93 million miles = 
                        150 million km = 1.5 x 10<sup>13</sup>cm away.  
                        Its radius , R<sub>S</sub> = 7 x 10<sup>10</sup>cm. 
<p>
If the sun were a large grapefruit (r~ 7 cm), the Earth would be 
a pinhead 15 meters away.<p>
<Center>
<Img src="./images/solar_system/sol_sys.gif" Width=100%><br>
<Font color=blue>The Solar System;  Sizes are to scale, but distances are not.
</Font></Center><p>

<DT><DD><IMG SRC="./images/doodads/pdot_sm.gif">
<u><b>The Stars</b></u> - The nearest star, Alpha Centauri, is a triple 
                              star system in the constellation Centaurus. It
                              is only visible from the Southern Hemisphere.
                              The distance to Alpha Centauri is 4.34 light-
                              years = 4 x 10<sup>18</sup>cm.<p>

A light-year is the distance that light travels in one year:<BR><BR>
<CENTER><IMG SRC="./images/doodads/liteyr_sm.gif"></CENTER>
<BR>
<BR>If the Sun were a grapefruit in San Diego, the nearest grapefruit (star)
would be 2500 miles away.
<p>

<DT><DD><IMG SRC="./images/doodads/pdot_sm.gif">
<u><b>The Galaxy</b></u> - The Sun is one of the 500,000,000,000 stars in
                               the Milky Way Galaxy. The Milky Way is about 
                               100,000 light-years = 10<sup>23</sup>cm across.<p>

<DT><DD><IMG SRC="./images/doodads/pdot_sm.gif">
<u><b>Other Galaxies</b></u> - The Andromeda Galaxy, our nearest sizeable
                                   neighbor, is about 2 million light-years
                                   = 2 x 10<sup>24</sup>cm away.<p>

<DT><DD><IMG SRC="./images/doodads/pdot_sm.gif">
<u><b>The Universe</b></u> - 10<sup>27</sup>cm: There may not be a "size" 
                                 to the Universe; it may be infinite. But, a 
                                 crude estimate of the scale of the Universe
                                 can be made from looking back to the 
                                 "beginning". Astronomers believe that the 
                                 Universe began with the <i>Big Bang</i>,some
                                 10 - 20 billion years ago. So, the most 
                                 distant objects that we can see are about 10
                                 - 20 billion light-years (= 10<sup>27</sup>
                                 cm) away.
</ul><p>
</DD></DT></DL>
From the size of the atomic nucleus,important for the fusion energy of the 
stars, at 10<sup>-13</sup>cm, to the gigantic extent  of the
Universe as a whole ~ 10<sup>27</sup>cm, the distance scales of
interest in Astronomy span a factor of about 10<sup>40</sup> -- <i>40 orders 
of magnitude!</i>
