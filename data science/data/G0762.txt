		<td class="hdline1"><b>How Did Structure Form in the Universe?</b></td>
		<td></td>
		<td><img src="../ContentMedia/GalaxyIcon.jpg" width="128" height="91" border="0" alt=" "></td>
	</tr>
</table>
							</td>

						<tr>
<td class="bodytext">
<div class="linkstyle1">
<br>
The <a href="../m_uni/uni_101bb1.html">Big Bang</a> theory is widely considered to be a successful theory of cosmology, but the theory is incomplete. Astronomers observe considerable structure in the universe, from stars to galaxies to clusters and superclusters of galaxies. The famous &quot;Deep Field Image&quot; taken by the Hubble Space Telescope, shown below, provides a stunning view of such structure. How did these structures form? Most cosmologists believe that the galaxies that we observe today grew gravitationally out of small fluctuations in the nearly-uniform density of the early universe. These fluctuations leave an imprint in the <a href="../m_uni/uni_101bbtest3.html">cosmic microwave background radiation</a> in the form of temperature <a href="../m_uni/uni_101Flucts.html">fluctuations</a> from point to point across the sky. The MAP satellite will measure these small fluctuations in the temperature of the cosmic microwave background radiation and in turn probe the early stages of structure formation. </div>
								<div class="linkstyle1" align="center">
									<p><b>Hubble Deep Field Image:</b><br>
									</p>
									<p><a href="../ContentMedia/HDFWF3.jpg"><img border="0" src="../ContentMedia/HDFWF3_s.gif" alt="Hubble Deep Field Image-ancient galaxies" width="507" height="128"></a><br>
										<a href="http://oposite.stsci.edu/pubinfo/PR/96/01/PR9601.txt" target="new"><span class="caption">Text Link </span></a><span class="caption">to the HST press release describing this image</span><br>
									</p>
								</div>
								<div class="linkstyle1">
									<p>In its simplest form, the Big Bang theory assumes that matter and radiation are uniformly distributed throughout the universe and that general relativity is universally valid. While this can account for the existence of the <a href="../m_uni/uni_101bbtest3.html">cosmic microwave background radiation</a> and explain <a href="../m_uni/uni_101bbtest2.html">the origin of the light elements</a>, it does not explain the existence of galaxies and large-scale structure. The solution of the structure problem must be built into the framework of the Big Bang theory.</p>
									<p><span class="hdline3"><b>Gravitational Formation of Structure</b></span></p>
									<p>Most cosmologists believe that the galaxies that we observe today grew gravitationally out of small fluctuations in the density of the universe through the following sequence of events:</p>
									<ul>
										<li>When the universe was one thousandth its present size (roughly 500,000 years after the Big Bang), the density of matter in the region of space that now contains the Milky Way, our home galaxy, was perhaps 0.5% higher than in adjacent regions. Because its density was higher, this region of space expanded more slowly than surrounding regions. 
										<li>As a result of this slower expansion, its relative over-density grew. When the universe was one hundredth its present size (roughly 15 million years after the Big Bang), our region of space was probably 5% denser than the surrounding regions.

										<li>This gradual growth continued as the universe expanded. When the universe was one fifth its present size (roughly 1.2 billion years after the Big Bang), our region of space was probably twice as dense as neighboring regions. Cosmologists speculate that the inner portions of our Galaxy (and similar galaxies) were assembled at this time. The stars in the outer regions of our Galaxy were probably assembled in the more recent past. Some cosmologists suspect that some of the objects recently detected by the Hubble Space Telescope may be galaxies in formation. 
									</ul>
								</div>
								<div class="linkstyle1" align="center">
									<p><b>HST Images of Galaxies in Formation?<br>
											<br>
										</b><img border="0" src="../ContentMedia/GalaxEvC.jpg" alt="Hubble Deep Field Images- Galaxies: Snapshots in time. Elliptical and spiral galaxies at 2,5,9, and 14 billion years after the Big bang" width="495" height="429"></p>
								</div>
								<div class="linkstyle1">
									<center>
										<p></p>
									</center>
									<p><span class="hdline3"><b>Observing These Small Fluctuations</b></span></p>
									<p>Tiny variations in the density of matter in the early universe leave an imprint in the <a href="../m_uni/uni_101bbtest3.html">cosmic microwave background radiation</a> in the form of temperature <a href="../m_uni/uni_101Flucts.html">fluctuations</a> from point to point across the sky. The MAP satellite will measure these small fluctuations in the temperature of the cosmic microwave   background radiation. These temperature fluctuations are minute: one part of the sky might have a temperature of 2.7251 Kelvin (degrees above absolute zero), while another part might have a   temperature of 2.7249 Kelvin. NASA's Cosmic Background Explorer <a href="http://space.gsfc.nasa.gov/astro/cobe/cobe_home.html" target="new">(COBE)</a> satellite, has detected these tiny fluctuations on large angular scales. MAP will re-measure the fluctuations with both higher <a href="../m_help/h_glossary.html">angular resolution</a> and <a href="../m_help/h_glossary.html">sensitivity</a>. The <a href="../aboutmap.html">mission summary</a> page offers a quick introduction to how MAP achieves this sensitivity - more details are available on the <a href="ob_tech1.html">technical information</a> page.</p>
									<p><span class="hdline3"><b>What Made These Small Fluctuations?</b></span></p>
									<p>While gravity can enhance the tiny fluctuations seen in the early universe, it can not produce these fluctuations. Cosmologists speculate about the new physics needed to produce the primordial fluctuations that formed galaxies. Two popular ideas are:</p>
									<ul>
										<li><a href="../m_uni/uni_101inflation.html">Inflation</a>
										<li>Topological Defects 
									</ul>
									<p>These different theories make very different predictions about the properties of the <a href="../m_uni/uni_101Flucts.html">cosmic microwave background fluctuations</a>. For example, the inflationary theory predicts that the largest temperature fluctuations should have an angular scale of one degree, while the defect models predict a smaller characteristic scale. MAP, with its superb sensitivity, should be able to easily distinguish between the two theories, or rule them out entirely.</p>
