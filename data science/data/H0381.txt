These same modalities need to be addressedin the face as well, but sensory input from the face does notenter the spinal cord.  In fact, it all enters the brainstem viathe <B>trigeminal nerve</B>.  Just as in the spinal cord, thesethree modalities have different receptors, travel along differenttracts, and have different targets in the brainstem.  Once thepathways synapse in the brainstem, they join the pathways fromthe body on their way up to the thalamus.<BR></FONT><P><B><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino" COLOR=#CC00CC>A.  The trigeminal nucleus complex:</FONT></B><P><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino">The collection of cells in the brainstem that canbe called the trigeminal nucleus is huge - it stretches from midbrainto medulla.  If we could see it in a transparent brainstem, itwould look something like this:<BR></FONT><P><CENTER><IMG SRC="face1.gif"><BR></CENTER><P><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino">Most of the sensory fibers enter the <B>trigeminalganglion</B>, regardless of which trigeminal division they arecoming from.  Their cell bodies, like those of all somatosensoryneurons, lie outside the CNS in the ganglion, and their proximalprocesses enter the brainstem in the mid-pons.  From there theyfan out to their different targets.  Each modality will be describedseparately below.<BR></FONT><P><B><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino" COLOR=#CC00CC>B.  Discriminative touch:</FONT></B><P><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino">The large diameter (A</FONT><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Symbol">b</FONT><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino">)fibers enter directly into the <B>main sensory nucleus of thetrigeminal</B> (V), also called the <B>principal nucleus</B>. Just like the somatosensory neurons of the body, they SYNAPSE,then CROSS.  The secondary afferents can then join the <B>mediallemniscus</B> on its way to the thalamus.<BR></FONT><P><CENTER><IMG SRC="face2.gif"><BR></CENTER><P><B><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino" COLOR=#CC00CC>C.  Pain and temperature:</FONT></B><P><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino">The small diameter fibers carrying pain and temperatureenter at mid pons, and then do something unusual - they turn <U>down</U>the brainstem.  They travel down the pons and medulla until theyreach the caudal medulla, which is where they finally synapseand cross. </FONT><P><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino">The tract that the descending axons travel in iscalled the <B>spinal tract of V</B>, and the long tail of a nucleusthat they finally synapse in is called the <B>spinal nucleus ofV</B>.  These names come from the fact that they actually reachas far down as the upper cervical spinal cord.  The spinal nucleusof V can be divided into three regions along its length; the regionclosest to the mouth is called <B>subnucleus oralis</B>, the middleregion is called <B>subnucleus interpolaris</B>, and the regionclosest to the tail is called <B>subnucleus caudalis</B>.  Thepain fibers actually synapse in subnucleus caudalis, so you mayhear that term used instead of the spinal nucleus of V.<BR></FONT><P><CENTER><IMG SRC="face3.gif"><BR></CENTER><P><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino">The secondary afferents from subnucleus caudaliscross to the opposite side, and join the <B>spinothalamic tract</B>on its way to the thalamus.<BR></FONT><P><B><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino" COLOR=#CC00CC>D.  Proprioception:</FONT></B><P><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino">The proprioceptive axons in the trigeminal nerveare the stretch and tendon receptors from the <B>muscles of mastication</B>. (Recall that all of the muscles of facial expression are controlledby the facial nerve. )  These axons coming from the face havea strange characteristic unique among primary somatosensory neurons: their cell bodies are <U>inside the CNS</U>.  They are the onlyexception to the rule.  Although their cells look similar to cellsin the dorsal root ganglion (the cell body does not come betweenthe distal and proximal axon processes), they are located insidethe brainstem in a nucleus called the <B>mesencephalic nucleus</B>. The mesencephalic nucleus is essentially a dorsal root ganglionthat has been pushed into the CNS, so there are no synapses withinit.  The fibers enter the brainstem via a small branch of thetrigeminal that bypasses the trigeminal ganglion, turn up towardsthe mesencephalic nucleus, pass by the cell body, and leave thenucleus immediately.  Most then synapse in the nearby <B>motornucleus</B> where they can initiate the stretch reflexes for themuscles of mastication.  The stretch reflex in the face behavesexactly like that in the body, and tapping on the tendon of themasseter (for example) will produce a twitch.<BR></FONT><P><CENTER><IMG SRC="face4.gif"><BR></CENTER><P><B><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino" COLOR=#CC00CC>E.  Motor innervation:</FONT></B><P><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino">Motor or efferent control is not considered a sensorymodality, but it is the fourth component of the extensive trigeminalcomplex.  The <B>motor nucleus of V</B> lies just medial to themain sensory nucleus, and in it reside the </FONT><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Symbol">a</FONT><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino">-motorneurons that control the muscles of mastication.  The two principalmuscles involved are the <B>masseter</B> (in your cheek) and the<B>temporalis</B> (over your temple), both of which tighten whenyou clench your teeth.  The motor axons leave the mid-pons andbypass the trigeminal ganglion, and reach their targets via themandibular division of the trigeminal nerve. <BR></FONT><P><CENTER><IMG SRC="face5.gif"><BR></CENTER><P><B><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino" COLOR=#CC00CC>F.  On to the thalamus:</FONT></B><P><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino">The somatosensory information from the face joinsthat from the body and enters the thalamus with it.  However,face information actually enters a different nucleus in the thalamus. Recall that information from the body enters the ventroposteriorlateral nucleus (VPL).  Information from the face actually entersthe <B>ventroposterior medial nucleus</B> (VPM).  The thalamocorticalafferents take all of the signals, whether from VPL or VPM, toprimary somatosensory cortex.  Once there, it is distributed ina somatotopic (body-mapped) fashion, with the legs representedmedially, at the top of the head, and the face represented laterally.<BR></FONT><P><B><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Palatino" COLOR=#CC00CC>G.
