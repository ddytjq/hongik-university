(©empics)</td></tr></table><table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" border="0" width="355" xmlns:msxsl="urn:schemas-microsoft-com:xslt" xmlns:user="http://mycompany.com/mynamespace"><tr><td align="center"><img src="/images/all/black_spacer.gif" width="351" height="1" /></td></tr></table><TABLE CELLPADDING="0" CELLSPACING="5" BORDER="0" WIDTH="355" xmlns:msxsl="urn:schemas-microsoft-com:xslt" xmlns:user="http://mycompany.com/mynamespace"><TR><TD class="NewsTitleBig">Heading the ball<BR /><SPAN class="NewsDate">Wednesday 29 May 2002</SPAN></TD></TR></TABLE><TABLE CELLPADDING="0" CELLSPACING="5" BORDER="0" WIDTH="355" xmlns:msxsl="urn:schemas-microsoft-com:xslt" xmlns:user="http://mycompany.com/mynamespace"><TR><TD class="NewsBody"><P>Learning to head the ball is a gradual process that begins at an early footballing age – and there are plenty of fun ways in which youngsters can practice as they go along. </P>
<P><B>Begin learning</B><BR>You have&nbsp;all seen the great strikers rise to plant a firm header past a despairing goalkeeper, or a top-class defender using his power to head a dangerous cross out of the penalty area. The top players who are good at heading the ball began learning this skill well before their teens. </P>
<P><B>Series of tips</B><BR>UEFA technical director Andy Roxburgh gave us a series of tips for youngsters on how to learn to become a good header of the ball. </P>
<P><B>Use a light ball</B><BR>“First of all, children have to start off using a light ball,” he says. “They really shouldn’t be using a heavier ball when they’re very young, for obvious physical reasons.” </P>
<P><B>Alone or with a partner</B><BR>As with learning to kick or pass a ball, there are a variety of exercises&nbsp;which youngsters can enjoy as they look to make progress heading the ball. “You can start by yourself - trying to juggle the ball up and down in the air with your head,” says Andy Roxburgh. “Then you can go on to practice with a partner, who throws the ball at you, and you head it back.” </P>
<P><B>Dangling ball<BR></B>“There’s even a type of practice exercise where you have a ball dangling from a rope, and you run in and head the ball, or jump to head the dangling ball.” </P>
<P><B>Middle of the forehead <BR></B>Heading the ball properly means trying to meet the ball with the middle of the forehead, “You mustn’t shut your eyes when you head the ball,” explains Andy Roxburgh. “That’s when you can hurt yourself. For example, it can be painful if you shut your eyes, drop your head and the ball hits you on top of the head. </P>
<P><B>Striking cleanly</B><BR>“Such simple practices are designed to give the youngster an opportunity to get used to heading the ball properly with the forehead, with the correct movement of the neck and body so that the ball is being struck cleanly.” </P>
<P><B>Head tennis<BR></B>Head tennis is another enjoyable game for youngsters to play while learning. “One of the best examples I saw of training to head the ball was with ten-year-old players at the Dutch club AFC Ajax,” Andy Roxburgh remembers. “They played two against two, with a net the height of a badminton net. The players either headed the ball to their partner to set up a header over the net, or headed the ball over the net themselves in a tennis-style game.” </P>
<P><B>Jumping to the ball</B><BR>Heading the ball also involves learning to jump to meet the ball. An optimum way to jump and head the ball when you are&nbsp;on the run, for example, is to jump off one foot. You can learn to hang in the air to meet the ball by making full use of your arms, which should be bent out slightly to give you balance. </P>
<P><B>Diving headers</B><BR>Children can also practice spectacular diving headers. “Kids love diving headers,” says Andy Roxburgh. “You can teach them first by throwing the ball straight to them, and they throw themselves to head the ball, or you can then make this slightly more difficult, by throwing the ball across them for a diving header. One thing that has to be remembered is that you should obviously practice diving headers with children on a soft surface. </P>
<P><B>About timing </B><BR>“Heading is like volleying a ball,” explains the UEFA technical director. “It’s all about timing, which is the hardest thing to teach – arriving at the same time that the ball arrives at a certain spot.” </P>
<P><B>Not tall? No problem!</B><BR>Lack of height is not&nbsp;a disadvantage in heading the ball if you have the right timing, the ability to hang in the air, and sufficient power. “Look at England’s Michael Owen for an example of someone who is not particularly tall, but who heads the ball well,” says Andy Roxburgh. “When I coached Scotland, one of the smaller players in the squad, David Speedie, was a brilliant header of the ball,&nbsp;because his timing and ability to hover in the air was so good.</P>
<P><B>Gradual learning</B><BR>“A young footballer learns gradually to head the ball. Up to the&nbsp;age of about ten, they should try some of the simpler exercises, and they can start to move on to more advanced practice after this.”</P><B>
