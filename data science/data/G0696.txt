<p><font size=+2>Activity: Time Travel to the Edge of the Universe and Back!</font></p></center>

<p><a href="#objectives">Instructional Objectives</a><br>
<a href="#background">Background Information</a><br>
<a href="#time">Time Needed for Activity</a><br>
<a href="#target">Target Grade Level</a><br>

<p><hr><p>
<b><a name="objectives">Instructional Objectives:</a></b>
<blockquote>
<p> Students will -
<ol>
<li>  study the size of the universe,
<li>  calculate the time to travel to near and distant destinations,
<li>  construct a time scale model of the universe by using time travel
distances.</ol></blockquote>
<p><hr><p>
<b><a name="background">Background Information:</a></b>
<blockquote>
<p>When we observe the night sky, the planets, stars and galaxies appear to be close together.  
We know that some objects are closer to the Earth than others and that enormous distances separate us from other objects in the universe. As we gather information from the farthest reaches of the universe, we can learn about the history of the universe.</p>

<p>Distances to objects in the universe are measured indirectly and take
advantage of the parallax effect.  When an object is being measured
against a stationary background, it appears to move when observed from
two different points.  The amount that an object appears to shift is
determined by the distance to the object being observed and the distance
between the two points of observation (the baseline).  Because we have two 
eyes, we use parallax constantly to judge distances to objects.</p>

<p>To learn more about parallax, try the activities in Project Earth
Science: Astronomy, P. Sean Smith, NSTA Publications, 1992.</p></blockquote>
<p><hr><p>

<b><a name="time">Time Needed for Activity:</a></b>
<blockquote>
<p>45-50 minutes<p></blockquote>
<p><hr><p>

<b><a name="target">Target Grade Level: </a></b>
<blockquote>
<p>High School (Note: Middle school extensions and advanced
high school extensions are provided below.)</p></blockquote>
<p><hr><p>

<b><a name="materials">Materials: </a></b>
<ul>
<li>Calculators</li>
<li>Astronomy reference books</li>
<li>String or clothesline</li>
<li>4 x 6 inch index cards</li>
<li>Paper hole punch</li>
<li>Paper clips</li></ul>
<hr><p>

<b><a name="procedures">Procedures:</a></b>
<blockquote>
<p>Distances to stars and celestial objects are immense. Cars travel at an
average of 60 miles/hour, jets fly at about 600 miles/hour, and rockets travel
through space at 25,000 miles/hour. Imagine that with new technolgy you can build a time machine that travels at light speed, or 186,000 miles/second.  The unit used to express stellar distance is the light-year, which is the
distance that light travels in a year (5.8 trillion miles or
9.46 x 10<sup><font size=-2>12 </font></sup>kilometers).  With the unit of light-years, we can begin to
make sense of the very great distances between objects in the universe
and begin our time travel exploration. </p>

<p>1.  Using the chart below,  calculate the travel time from the Earth to the
destinations given.</p>

<p>2.  Suspend the string or clothesline from the ceiling in your classroom or stretch it along the length of the hallway.  Determine an appropriate scale, depending
on whether you want to show the edge of the universe, approximately <br>15
billion light-years away, or the objects closer to the Earth, for which you will use a scale of 3 million light-years.  Mark the scale
on the string or clothesline.</p>

<p>3.  Give each student a card.  Have them record the name of the object
that is their destination and the time distance to travel there.  Use paper clips to attach the cards to the string at the appropriate distances
on your scale.  (Extension:  Use the images from the Hubble Space
Telescope and add artwork to each card.  Select additional
locations for students to add to their time line from the program and
from the Hubble Space Telescope observations.)</p>

<p>4.  The chart and travel time line represent a glimpse into the past. 
In a sense, our time travel is like a time machine.  When we look at
the history of the universe, what can we learn?  What evidence have
scientists gathered with new technologies and the Hubble Deep Field
Project?</p>

<p>5.  Because of the finite speed of light, we are restricted to studying
our observable universe.  Discuss with your class the question, 
"Are there other universes?"  What hypotheses can they make?</p>
</blockquote>

<p><hr><p>
<b><a name="extensions">Extensions:</a></b>

<ul>
<li>Middle School Extensions -- Give students the light-year calculation and have them
construct the time line by using the known distances.  Have students use an
astronomical globe that shows the position of the stars and the planets in
relation to Earth.</li></p>
<p><li>Advanced High School Students -- Have students design and conduct experiments to study the correlation between a star's brightness and its distance.  Use light meters to
perform experiments and make measurements.  Discuss how the brightness
of a star is determined by how large it is, how hot it is, and how far away it
is.</li></p>
<p><li>Extension for All Students -- Find out about NASA's Discovery Program called STARDUST, which plans to sample a comet and an interstellar dust stream and return the
material to Earth.  The samples may provide a window to the distant
past.</li></ul></p>
