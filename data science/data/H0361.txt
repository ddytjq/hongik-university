<H1>DOE-Funded Microbial Genomes:<BR>  Completed and Ongoing Projects</H1>



         <h3><b>Sequencing Microbial Genomes to Uncover Potential Applications Relevant 
  to DOE Missions</b></h3>
  
<p><sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup>Draft 
  sequence by the DOE Joint Genome Institute.<br>


 <sup><img src="sym2.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="&sect;"></sup>New microbes being sequenced by JGI.<br>

  <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong></em>Completed 
  and published (see <a href="http://www.tigr.org/tdb">www.tigr.org/tdb</a>).<br>
        <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong></em>Completed, 
        not published (as of May 2002).</p>


<p><b>Carbon Sequestration</b></p>
      
<ul>
  <sup><img src="sym2.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="&sect;"></sup><em>Azotobacter 
  vinelandii AvOP</em> (4.5 Mb)<br>
  <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong>Chlorobium 
  tepidum</em> (bacteria, 2.1 Mb): Photosynthetic; may play important role in 
  earth's overall carbon cycle.<br>
  <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Chloroflexus 
  aurantiacus J-10-fl</em> (bacteria, 3 Mb): Modern version of organism that needs 
  no oxygen for photosynthesis. Uses unique pathway to fix carbon dioxide.<br>
  <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong>Nitrosomonas 
  europaea ATCC25978</em> (bacteria, 2.2 Mb): Important in soil nitrogen cycling 
  and ammonia oxidation; degrades chlorinated hydrocarbons; aids incorporation 
  of carbon dioxide into biomass.<br>
  <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Nostoc 
  punctiforme ATCC229133</em> (bacteria, 10 Mb): Fixes carbon dioxide and nitrogen; 
  produces hydrogen; survives acidic, anaerobic, and low-temperature conditions.<br>
  <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong>Prochlorococcus 
  marinus MED4 </em>(bacteria, 1.7 Mb) and <i>Prochlorococcus marinus MIT9313</i> 
  (bacteria, 2.4 Mb): Both abundant in temperate and tropical oceans; important 
  in ocean carbon cycling; absorb blue light efficiently. <i>MIT9313</i> is adapted 
  to lower-light conditions (lower ocean depths).<br>
  <strong><em><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></em></strong><em>Rhodopseudomonas 
  palustris CGA009</em> (bacteria, 5.5 Mb): Fixes carbon dioxide; produces hydrogen; 
  biodegrades organic pollutants under both aerobic and anaerobic conditions.<br>
  <sup><img src="sym2.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="&sect;"></sup><em>Rhodospirillum 
  rubrum ATCC11170</em> 3.4 Mb)<br>
  <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong>Synechococcus 
  WH8102</em> (bacteria, 2.4 Mb): Photosynthetic; important to ocean carbon fixation; 
  genetically tractable.<br>
  <sup><img src="sym2.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="&sect;"></sup><em>Thalassiosira 
  pseudonana</em> (eukarya, ~25 Mb): Ocean diatom, major participant in biological 
  &quot;pumping&quot; of carbon to ocean depths.<br>
  <sup><img src="sym2.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="&sect;"></sup><em>Trichodesmium 
  erythraeum IMS101</em> 6.5 Mb)<br>
</ul>


<p><b>Energy Production</b></p>
<ul>
  <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong>Methanobacterium 
    thermoautotrophicum delta H</em> (archaea, 1.7 Mb): Produces methane; plays role in 
    earth's overall carbon cycle.<br>
  <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong>Methanococcus 
  jannaschii DSM2661</em> (archaea extremophile, 1.7 Mb): May identify high-temperature, 
  high-pressure enzymes; produces methane.<br>

  <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Methanosarcina 
    barkeri fusaro</em> (archaea, 2.8 Mb): Lives in cattle rumen; digests cellulose 
    and other polysaccharides to produce methane. Very oxygen sensitive. Grows 
    in variety of substrates. <br>

  <em>Methylococcus capsulatus</em> (bacteria, 4.6 Mb): Uses methane as single 
    carbon and energy source; generates pollutant-oxidizing enzymes; used commercially 
    to produce biomass and other proteins.<br>
</ul>


<p><b>Bioremediation</b></p>
      <ul>

<em>Acidithiobacillus ferrooxidans</em> (bacteria, 2.9 Mb): Used in mining industry to sequester iron and sulfide.<br>

        <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Burkholderia 
          LB400</em> (bacteria, 8 Mb): Outstanding degrader of polychlorinated 
          biphenyls (PCBs).<br>

        <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong>Caulobacter 
          crescentus</em> (bacteria, 4.01 Mb): Potential for heavy-metal remediation 
          in waste-treatment plant wastewater.<br>

   <sup><img src="sym2.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="&sect;"></sup><em>Dechloromonas RCB</em> (2 Mb)<br>

        <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong>Dehalococcoides 
          ethenogenes</em> (bacteria, 1.5 Mb): Degrades dangerous solvent 
          trichloroethene to benign products.<br>

        <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong>Deinococcus 
          radiodurans R1</em> (bacteria, 3 Mb): Survives extremely high levels of 
          radiation; possesses DNA-repair capabilities for radioactive waste cleanup.<br>

        <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Desulfitobacterium 
          hafniense DCB-2</em> (bacteria, 4.6 Mb): Degrades pollutants such as chlorinated 
          organic compounds that include some pesticides. <br>


   <sup><img src="sym2.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="&sect;"></sup><em>Desulfovibrio desulfuricans G20</em> (3.1 Mb)<br>

        <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong>Desulfovibrio 
          vulgaris</em> (bacteria, 3.2 Mb): High potential for bioremediation 
          through metal and sulfate reduction and sulfate utilization.<br>


   <sup><img src="sym2.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="&sect;"></sup><em>Desulfuromonas acetoxidans</em> (4.1 Mb)<br>

        <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Ferroplasma 
          acidarmanus fer1</em> (archaea, ~2.8 Mb): Lives in most acidic conditions 
          on earth. Oxidizes iron. Transforms sulfide in metal ores to sulfuric 
          acid, leading to contamination of mining sites. <br>


   <sup><img src="sym2.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="&sect;"></sup><em>Geobacter metallireducens</em> (6.8 Mb)<br>

        <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong>Geobacter 
          sulfurreducens</em> (bacteria, 2.5 Mb): Reduces a variety of metals, 
          including iron and uranium.<br>


        <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Magnetospirillum 
          magnetotacticum MS-1 ATCC31632</em> (bacteria, 4.5 Mb): Requires limited oxygen. 
          Reduces iron, produces magnetite. Possible model for biomineralization 
          and evolutionary responses. May serve as a geomagnetic tracer.<br>


   <sup><img src="sym2.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="&sect;"></sup><em>Mesorhizobium BNC1</em> (5 Mb)<br>


   <sup><img src="sym2.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="&sect;"></sup><em>Methanococcoides burtonii DSM6242</em> (3 Mb)<br>

   <sup><img src="sym2.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="&sect;"></sup><em>Microbulbifer 2-40</em> (6 Mb)<br>


        <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Novosphingobium aromaticivorans F199</em> (bacteria, 3.8 Mb): 
Degrades aromatic compounds in soil, including toluene, xylene, naphthalene, and fluorine.
        <br>


        <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Pseudomonas 
          fluorescens PFO-1</em> (bacteria, 5.5 Mb): Metabolically diverse. Degrades 
          pollutants such as styrene, TNT, and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. 
          Useful in applications requiring bacteria release and survival in soil. 
        <br>

        <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong>Pseudomonas 
          putida</em> (bacteria, 6.1 Mb): High potential for bioremediation by reducing 
          metal and pollutants.<br>

        <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Ralstonia 
          metallidurans CH34</em> (bacteria, 5 Mb): Contains two &quot;mega&quot; plasmids. 
          Resistant to wide variety of heavy metals, which accumulate on the cell 
          surface. Strong potential for bioremediation of metals. <br>

        <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Rhodobacter 
          sphaeroides 2.4.1</em> (bacteria, 4.4 Mb): Metabolically diverse, grows 
          in wide variety of conditions. Photosynthetic. Provides fundamental 
          insights into light-driven, renewable-energy production. Can detoxify 
          metal oxides, useful in bioremediation.<br>

        <em><strong><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"><img src="star.gif" width="12" height="12" alt="*" border="0"></strong><i>Shewanella 
          oneidensis MR-1</i></em> (bacteria, 4.5 Mb): May degrade organic 
          wastes and reduce or sequester a range of toxic metals.<br>

      </ul>
<p><b>Cellulose Degradation</b></p>
      <ul>
        <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Clostridium 
          thermocellum ATCC27405</em> (bacteria, ~5 Mb): Thermophilic; degrades 
          cellulose via multienzyme complexes (&quot;cellulosomes&quot;).<br>

        <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Cytophaga 
          hutchinsonii ATCC33406</em> (bacteria, 4 Mb): Very abundant in nature; decomposes 
          cellulose, lacks cellulosomes.<br>

        <em><sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup>Phanerochaete 
          chrysosporium</em> (eukarya, ~20 to 30Mb): &quot;White rot&quot; fungus; 
          aerobic and degrades both celluloses and lignins; can also degrade polyaromatic 
          hydrocarbons.<br>

        <sup><img src="sym.gif" width="7" height="11" alt="$"></sup><em>Thermobifida 
          fusca YX</em> (bacteria, 3.6 Mb): 
          Major degrader of organic materials, responsible for Farmer's Lung disease.<br>
      </ul>
