<TD><FONT FACE="Arial,Helvetica,Helv" SIZE="2">
<BR>
<HR WIDTH="100%" SIZE=7 ALIGN=left COLOR="0000ff"><B><FONT SIZE=4 COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Ocean Studies Board Member Biographies</FONT></B><BR>
<HR WIDTH="100%" SIZE=7 ALIGN=left COLOR="0000ff"><BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Nancy Rabalais</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial"> </FONT><B><I><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">(Chair)</FONT></I></B><B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> </FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">received her Ph.D. in zoology from the University of Texasat Austin in 1983.  She is currently a Professor at the Louisiana Universities Marine Consortium.  Her research interests include hypoxia, eutrophication, estuarine and benthic ecology, and continental shelf ecosystems.  Her research and public outreach efforts in the realm of nutrient overenrichment and Gulf of Mexico hypoxia garnered a 1999 NOAA Environmental Hero Award, the 1999 Blasker Award for Science and Engineering, shared with Gene Turner of Louisiana State University, and the 12th Bostwick H. Ketchum Award from the Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution.  In addition, Dr. Rabalais is an AAAS Fellow, an Aldo Leopold Leadership Fellow, and a Past President of the Estuarine Research Federation.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Arthur Baggeroer</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> received his Sc.D. from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 1968.  He is currently the Ford Professor of Engineering and holds the Secretary of the Navy/Chief of Naval Operations Chair for Ocean Science in the Department of Ocean and Electrical Engineering at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.  His areas of expertise are in acoustics and sonar.  Dr. Baggeroer has been a member of the National Academy of Engineering since 1995.  </FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">James Coleman</FONT></B><B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> </FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">received his Ph.D. in geology from Louisiana State University in 1966.  He is currently Boyd Professor for the Coastal Studies Institute of Louisiana State University and Agricultural and Mechanical College.  Dr. Coleman's research interests focus on relationships between process, form, and sedimentary characteristics, especially in deltaic and offshore regions.  He is the former chairman of the Marine Board, a member of the National Academy of Engineering since 1990, a recently elected member of the Russian Academy of Natural Sciences, and awarded the Kapitsa Medal of Honor for his contributions to the field of Petroleum Sciences. Dr. Coleman is a member of the U.S. Commission on Ocean Policy.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Larry B. Crowder</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> is currently the Stephen Toth Professor of Marine Ecology at the Nicholas School of the Environment and Earth Sciences at Duke University.  Dr. Crowder earned his Ph.D. in zoology in 1978 from the Michigan State University, East Lansing.  His research focuses on fisheries  ecology, species interactions, and community ecology of aquatic systems; predation and food web interactions; mechanisms underlying recruitment variation in fishes; and population modeling in conservation biology.  Dr. Crowder is in residence at Duke University Marine Lab, Beaufort, NC.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">G. Brent Dalrymple</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> received his Ph.D. in geology from the University of California, Berkeley in 1963 and a D.Sc. from Occidental College in 1993.  He is retired and now Dean &amp; Professor of Oceanography Emeritus in the College of Oceanic and Atmospheric Sciences, Oregon State University.  His expertise is in the areas of isotope geology, geochronology, the origin of ocean island chains, and the age of the Earth and Solar System. Dr. Dalrymple is a Fellow and Past President of the American Geophysical Union, and a Fellow of the American Academy of Arts &amp; Sciences.  He has been a member of the National Academy of Sciences since 1993.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Richard B. Deriso</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> received his Ph.D. in Quantitative Ecology from the University of Washington.  Dr. Deriso is currently the Associate Adjunct Professor of Biological Oceanography at the Scripps Institution of Oceanography and the Chief Scientist, Tuna-Billfish Program, at the Inter-American Tropical Tuna Commission (IATTC).  His current interests are population dynamics, quantitative ecology, and fishery stock assessment.  Dr. Deriso has served as a Co-chair on the NRC Committee on Fish Stock Assessment Methods and has served as a member of the Committee to Review Atlantic Bluefin Tuna.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Earl Doyle</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> earned a M.S. in ocean engineering from the University of Rhode Island in 1968. He recently retired from Shell Oil where he worked in the area of ocean engineering. He is currently an engineering consultant specializing in the integration</FONT><BR>
<FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">of marine geology, geophysics and geotechnical engineering.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Robert A. Duce</FONT></B><B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> </FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">is a Professor of Oceanography and of Atmospheric Sciences at Texas A&amp;M University. He earned his Ph.D. in inorganic and nuclear chemistry in 1964 from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. His research focuses on atmospheric and marine chemistry, including the global cycling of trace elements, the role of air/sea exchange processes in this cycling, and the impact of atmospheric substances (particularly iron and nitrogen) on ocean productivity.  He is a Fellow of the AGU, AMS and AAAS.  He is past president of The Oceanography Society and the International Association of Meteorology and Atmospheric Sciences.</FONT><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> He is also the president of the Scientific Committee on Oceanic Research (SCOR).</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Wayne R. Geyer</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> </FONT><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">received his Ph.D. in Physical Oceanography from the University of Washington in 1985.  Dr. Geyer is currently a Senior Scientist and Chair of the Applied Ocean Physics and Engineering Department at the Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution.  His research interests include estuarine and coastal hydrodynamics, dispersion, sediment transport, and morphodynamics. Dr. Geyer has served as a member of the NRC Panel on Environmental Processes: Source, Fate, and Transport.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">D. Jay Grimes</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> received his Ph.D. in microbiology from Colorado State University in 1971.  He is currently a Professor and Dean of the College of Marine Sciences and Director of the Gulf Coast Research Laboratory at the University of Southern Mississippi.  His research interests include the microbiology of waste disposal and environmental contaminants and the microbiological quality of water resources. Dr. Grimes is a AAAS Fellow and a Fellow of the American Academy of Microbiology.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Miriam Kastner</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> is currently a professor in the Graduate Department of Scripps Institution of Oceanography at the University of California, San Diego.  Dr. Kastner earned her Ph.D. in geology in 1970 from Harvard University.  Her expertise is in marine geochemistry and her research focuses on chemical paleoceanography; the role and fluxes of fluids in convergent plate margins; the origin, environmental implications, and diagenesis of primarily marine authigenic minerals (i.e. phosphates, sulfates, silicates, carbonates); gas hydrates in continental margins; and hydrothermal deposits in the submarine environment.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Cindy Lee</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> earned her Ph.D. in oceanography from Scripps Institution of Oceanography in 1975.  She is currently a Professor in the Marine Sciences Research Center at the State University of New York (SUNY) at Stony Brook and a Fellow of the American Geophysical Union.  Her expertise is in the area of marine geochemistry of organic compounds. </FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Ralph S. Lewis</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> </FONT><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">received his M.S. in Geology from Franklin and Marshall College in 1974.  Currently, he is the State Geologist and Supervisor of the State Geological and Natural History Survey within Connecticut's Department of Environmental Protection.  He is responsible for overseeing the activities of the State Survey which develops and conducts geological and biological programs involving basic research, data collection, inventories, monitoring and analysis, habitat and species protection, resource evaluation, technical assistance, and outreach in order to ensure the viability of scientific, economic and educational natural resource information for Connecticut. He is also an adjunct assistant professor of geology, and the Director of the Long Island Sound Resource Center at the University of Connecticut/Avery Point. His research concentration has been the geologic framework and sediment system of Long Island Sound.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Bonnie McCay</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> earned a Ph.D. in anthropology from Columbia University in 1976.  Dr. McCay is Board of Governors Distinguished Service Professor at Rutgers University, New Jersey, in the Department of Human Ecology and the graduate faculties of Anthropology and Ecology and Evolution.  Her expertise is on the social, cultural, and economic dimensions of managing fisheries and other natural resources and on the intersections of science, lay knowledge and participation, and environmental policy. Dr. McCay is an AAAS Fellow, past-president of the International Association for the Study of Common Property, and president of the Anthropology and Environment Section of  the American Anthropological Association.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Julian McCreary, Jr.</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> received his Ph.D. in Oceanography from Scripps Institution of Oceanography, University of California, San Diego in 1977.  Dr. McCreary is currently the Director of the International Pacific Research Center (IPRC) at the School of Ocean and Earth Science and Technology at the University of Hawaii, Honolulu.  The IPRC Mission is &quot;to provide an international, state-of-the-art research environment to improve understanding of the nature and predictability of climate variability in the Asia-Pacific sector, including regional aspects of global environmental change.&quot;  Dr. McCreary's research interests include: equatorial ocean dynamics, coastal ocean dynamics, ocean circulation, coupled ocean-atmosphere models of climate dynamics and ecosystem modeling. </FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Jacqueline Michel</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> received her Ph.D. in Geochemistry from the University of South Carolina in 1980.  Currently, Dr. Michel is the President of Research Planning, Inc.  Dr. Michel is an expert in oil and chemical response and natural resource damage assessment. She has been the program manager providing scientific support to NOAA's</FONT><BR>
<FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">Hazardous Materials Response and Assessment Division since 1978.  Dr. Michel chaired the NRC Committee on Spills of Emulsified Fuels, is a member of the Committee on Oil in the Sea, and was a member of the Committee on Marine Transportation of Heavy Oil. Dr. Michel is also a member of the Science Advisory Panel to the U.S. Commission on Ocean Policy.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Ram Mohan</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> holds a Ph.D. in ocean engineering from Texas A&amp;M  University and is a P.E. in Maryland.  He is Principal Engineer &amp; Director, Coastal Engineering at Blasland, Bouck &amp; Lee, (BBL) Inc., an ENR top 100 ranked environmental consulting company. Dr. Mohan has more than 12 years of experience in dredging systems, port and harbor planning, river/channel hydraulics, coastal modeling, contaminated sediments, and environmental planning.  He also serves on the Board of Directors for the Association of Coastal Engineers and the Western Dredging Association, and is the Chair, Marine Technology Society's Physical Oceanography Committee.  He also serves as adjunct faculty at the Johns Hopkins Whiting School of Engineering, and Texas A&amp;M University's Center for Dredging Studies.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Scott Nixon</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> received his Ph.D. in botany from the University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill in 1970.  He is currently a Professor of Oceanography at the University of Rhode Island.  In 1992 he received the Ketchum Award for excellence in coastal research.  He serves as co-editor in chief of the journal, </FONT><I><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">Estuaries</FONT></I><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">.  His research interests include ecological systems and nutrient cycling. </FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Jon G. Sutinen</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">  is currently a professor in the Department of Environmental and Natural Resource Economics at the University of Rhode Island.  Dr. Sutinen earned his Ph.D. in economics in 1973 from the University of Washington.  His expertise is economics and his primary research interests are fisheries management and regulation with an emphasis on compliance and enforcement. He has extensive experience in international fisheries, including Latin America, Africa, Southeast Asia, and Europe. Most of this experience involved conducting research and supplying advice on fisheries policy.' His current research focuses on several bioeconomic aspects of New England marine fisheries and the Northeast Large Marine Ecosystem. Dr. Sutinen was the founding editor of the journal Marine Resource Economics and served in that role for over a decade.</FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Nancy M. Targett</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial"> </FONT><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">is a professor of marine biology-biochemistry at the Graduate College of Marine Studies at the University of Delaware. Dr. Targett earned her Ph.D. in oceanography in 1979 from the University of Maine. Her expertise is in biological oceanography and her research focuses on marine chemical ecology/organismal interactions mediated by naturally occurring metabolites, including: plant/herbivore interactions, predator/prey interactions, detoxification of allelochemicals, chemoattraction, and biofouling. She is an associate editor for the Journal of Chemical Ecology and an Aldo Leopold Leadership Program Fellow.  From 1994-2000 she held an appointment to the Mid Atlantic Fisheries Management Council where she chaired several of their committees.  </FONT><BR>
<BR>
<B><FONT COLOR="000080" FACE="Arial">Paul Tobin</FONT></B><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> </FONT><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">retired from the Navy in 1998 after 35 years of service rising to the rank of Rear Admiral.  He was a shipboard officer who spent 14 years at sea on most of the world's major oceans.  His last Navy assignment  was Oceanographer of the Navy, and after retiring,  he was affiliated with the Armed Forces Communications and Electronics Association for three years.  He is now the </FONT><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">Executive Director</FONT><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial"> for </FONT><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">AFCEA Education Foundation</FONT><FONT COLOR="800000" FACE="Arial">. His technical background is in Shipboard  Engineering and Computer Science. He has a great interest in Operational Oceanography and Marine Biology. His work has involved education and training and he has visited and coordinated with many of our foremost Universities.</FONT><BR>
