    <p align="left" style="margin-top: 1"><font size="2" face="Arial">Poor USA TV ratings are severely
    impacting CART's ability to attract new sponsors, but worse yet, they are
    hurting CART's image.&nbsp; As flawed as the Nielson TV rating system is,
    it's the only thing we have in the USA, and it's what all the marketing and
    advertising folks hang their hat on. However, CART is much more than just a
    USA racing series.&nbsp; Don't we want to know how many people watch CART
    races worldwide?</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">About two months ago I spoke to
    CART's TV exec, Keith Allo, about the lack of good TV data outside the
    USA.&nbsp; We talked about the studies that were done back in the late 90's
    and why we did not have any more recent data.&nbsp; Keith informed me that
    there was no budget item set aside to research the worldwide TV data the
    last two or three years, and hence he had nothing new to offer me.&nbsp; He
    was as frustrated as I over the lack of data.&nbsp; How an entertainment
    business such as CART did not budget to gather TV data that would be useful to
    potential sponsors is unfathomable.&nbsp; The good news is that we are told
    they will undertake a new study soon.</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">The last two years the study was
    done, CART's annual worldwide TV audience was right around 1 billion (987
    million in 1998), an impressive number indeed.&nbsp; However, critics
    pointed to numbers that seemed high in some areas with no good background
    data to support them.&nbsp; I have in front of me the 1998 study done by SRi/ISL
    Marketing. China was listed as having 428 of the 987 million cumulative
    viewers (no, 1/3 of the China population does not watch CART races, that
    number is a cumulative number for the season).&nbsp; Although China has a
    population of 1.2 billion people, how could China have almost half the
    viewers when CART doesn't even race in China?&nbsp; Those sort of questions
    immediately made the study suspect.</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">Keith Allow did tell me he
    thought the actual cumulative number was closer to 2 billion per year, but
    they purposely reported it low because they knew they would be criticized
    for not being able to substantiate some of the numbers.&nbsp; As you might
    imagine, if the USA, as sophisticated as it is, can't perfect its TV
    ratings, what can we expect from other countries, especially ones like
    China?</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">To get very reliable worldwide
    data is expensive, but can CART afford not to make the investment?&nbsp; The
    fact that CART gets far more worldwide TV viewers than NASCAR, is totally
    lost because of lack of data.&nbsp; While NASCAR enjoys tremendous success
    and is flush with sponsor dollars, CART is floundering trying to convince
    sponsors it really has a lot to offer.&nbsp; As many marketing execs will
    tell you, 'it's the TV stupid.'&nbsp; CART continues to get beat up in the
    USA TV ratings war, yet in an area where it is strong (internationally) it
    fails to deliver the goods.</font></p>

    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">Because of CART's lack of good
    international TV data, EuroSpeedway Lausitz decided to do it themselves.&nbsp; Leave
    it up to the German's to be thorough.&nbsp; EuroSpeedway Lausitz hired Germany's leading TV and market research
    company, IFM, to compute the official international TV data for the
    inaugural German American Memorial 500.&nbsp; EuroSpeedway Lausitz's President, Hans-Jörg Fischer,
    was kind enough to provide me with some data from that study.</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">For the first time a CART race promoter
    has provided us with some insight into the promotional campaign that goes
    into making a successful race, and the media coverage that resulted.&nbsp;
    We were most interested in the TV impact.&nbsp;</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">In reviewing the table below,
    there are a few key things to note.&nbsp;&nbsp;In 39 hours of total TV
    broadcasting over the weekend of the event, approximately
    158 million people worldwide either watched the race itself, or tuned into
    race highlight programs.&nbsp; That is a lot of people that saw at least a
    portion of the race.&nbsp;&nbsp;</font></p>

    <p align="left" style="margin-top: 1"><font size="2" face="Arial">A couple

    far the two biggest numbers and are driven by the fact that the race was
    either rebroadcast many times, or there were many pre and post race shows
    and news reports.&nbsp; The tragic Alex Zanardi accident likely drove the

    amount.</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">The data we were provided does

    race, as there is a conglomeration of the numbers between full race
    broadcasts and news highlights.&nbsp; Of the 76 million people in Italy for
    example, we don't know how many watched the full race broadcast vs. just
    news highlights.&nbsp; It does, however, point out just how hard it is to
    measure precisely how much reach the international TV broadcasts have.</font></p>

    for potential sponsors and partners to become involved with the German 500 event in the future. We are,
    therefore, quite optimistic to attract solid and&nbsp;long lasting partnerships from 2002 onwards.&nbsp;
    In close cooperation with CART we are at the moment evaluating and discussing a very attractive TV partnership in Germany from 2002 onwards. We
    firmly believe in the success of this fascinating series and will further contribute with our still new event "German 500" to the future international
    success of CART.&nbsp; The &quot;German 500&quot; is now firmly positioned
    to be the No. 1 challenge for the European F1 Grand Prix's.&quot;</font></p>
    <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">In addition to the race being a
    success at the gate with an event attendance of 149,000 spectators, and a
    race day attendance of 88,000, it seems the race received a lot of worldwide
    TV exposure which certainly brought more value to the race and team
    sponsors.
    </font></p>
