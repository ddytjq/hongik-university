        <h1 align="center"><b><font size="+3"> Capillary&nbsp; HPLC UV Detection</font></b></h1>
      </td>
      <td width="121" height="9" align="center" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFCC">

    <tr>
      <td width="560" height="9" align="center" colspan="3" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF">&nbsp;</td>

    <tr>
      <td width="560" height="9" align="center" colspan="3" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF">
        <p align="left">Typical capillary HPLC flow rates present a special challenge
for UV peak detection. Standard HPLCs have UV flow cells with internal
volumes ranging from 8 to 12 µLs.&nbsp; The large volume of the standard flow
cell makes it unsuitable for flow rates below 100 µL/min.&nbsp; A 12 µL volume
represents roughly 2 minutes of volume at a typical capillary flow rate,
so capillary HPLC peaks separated chromatographically by 2 min. are unresolved
in the standard flow cell.&nbsp; In addition UV detectors are concentration dependent
detectors and therefore the gains made in peak concentration by the capillary column would be negated by the dilution effect in the larger
flow cell.</td>

    <tr>
      <td width="560" height="9" align="center" colspan="3" bordercolor="#FFFFFF" bgcolor="#FFFFFF" rowspan="2">

<P align="left">Capillary flow cells have traditionally been made by etching
a UV transparent window in fused silica tubing, see
Figure 4.&nbsp; Needless to say, Beer's law applies to capillary flow cells.
The sensitivity of the flow cell will be determined by the path length and
it's optical characteristics.&nbsp; There have basically been two ways to create
a capillary flow cell from fused silica.&nbsp; Early capillary flow cells were
made by etching a window in the fused silica capillary and placing the window
between the light source and the detector.&nbsp; While this cell design is simple
it lacks a significant path length. The path length in this cell is essentially
the diameter of the fused silica tubing (typically 100 microns).
This cell works and can be found on many capillary and CE systems.&nbsp; This
simple cell, &quot;on-column cell&quot;, has an ability to to detect analytes down
to around 5 pmol when used in capillary HPLC.&nbsp; Manufacturers of capillary flow cells attempted to increase
the sensitivity of the cell by increasing the path length.&nbsp; A cell was introduced
in which a larger window was etched in the fused silica and the tubing was
bent to create a longer cell path length (5 to 8 mm). This cell offers greater sensitivity
with no increase in flow cell dead volume.&nbsp; The cell does suffer from poor
optical characteristics because the light scatters as it traverses the cell.&nbsp;
Due to the poor optical characteristics the response of this cell is generally
not linear making it unsuitable for those tasks that require quantification.&nbsp;
However, when the ultimate in sensitivity is required this cell works well.&nbsp;
Detection down to 250 fmol can be routinely achieved with the U or Z shaped cell.</P>

    <tr>
      <td width="617" height="1" align="center" valign="top" colspan="3">

<P align="left"> The
difference in sensitivity between the on-column and U shaped cell is not
phenomenal. The apparent non-linearity of&nbsp; the longer FSC cell design is due to the fact that
while the U or Z shaped cell
does have an increased path length it suffers from poor optical characteristics
in comparison to the simpler on-column cell.</P>

<P align="left">The Z or U shaped cell with the increased path length can
be readily purchased for most UV detectors.&nbsp; The Z or U shaped cell is more
difficult to construct but is useful when exploring
at very low levels.&nbsp; We choose to use this cell for proteome investigations
or when collecting rare proteins for N-terminal sequence analysis.&nbsp; The on-column
cell is easy to make and can be used for amounts above 5 pmol.&nbsp; Peaks
tend to be sharper with the on-column cell, and linearity of response is achievable.</P>
