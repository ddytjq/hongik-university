<p class=roman>They Say Defense Wins Championships
<hr size=2 color=#000000>
by Dean Oliver, founder of JoBS, <a href="http://www.tsoft.com/~deano/index.html">Journal of Basketball Studies</a>
<p>
<b>The proverbial "they" say that Defense Wins Championships.</b>
</p></center>
<p>I really don't know who exactly "they" are that claim this, but "they" are wrong.  Or at least "they" should step forward and
tell me what "they" mean.  Because I really can't tell. </p>
<p>Growing up and playing on a few teams (basketball, football, softball, and, of course, dodge ball), I saw lots of games
and lots of teams.  I won a few championships and played on a few bad teams, too.  But in most of the cases where I saw the best
teams, it was offense -- not defense -- that made the teams as good as they were.</p>
<p>There was the basketball team that had a guy who could dunk, who could blow by just about any defender, and who ultimately played
college ball.  They won our league title because this guy could score. <em>They</em> didn't say that defense wins championships.  Only
"they" did.</p>
<blockquote><b><I> ... Who Gets Recruited? ... </i></b></blockquote>
<p>On top of that: Who are the players that get recruited for the next level?  It sure ain't the guy who sticks to his man like glue,
but can't buy a wide open ten footer.  It's more likely to be the guy who can break down any defense and has a breakdown about
playing defense. </p>
<blockquote><b><I> ... The King of the Court ... </i></b></blockquote>
<p>If you think Michael Jordan is called the greatest basketball player ever because of his defense, then you're probably also wondering what all the fuss about
Elvis' death was about since you just saw him at the grocery store handing out free samples of Yoohoo.  Nike, McDonalds,
Gatorade, and <a href="http://www.sportsline.com/u/basketball/nba/index.html">Sportsline</a> are paying Jordan because he can hang in the air, do his
taxes, then dunk on Shaquille O'Neal -- not because he can shut down Steve Smith. </p>
<blockquote><b><i> ... The Fifty Greatest Players ... </i></b></blockquote>
<p>You want more?   How about this:  Of the Fifty Greatest Players in NBA History, only three had career scoring averages
under 15 ppg.  Of all these players, it is fair to say that only Bill Russell, Dave DeBusschere, and maybe Nate Thurmond
made their reputation primarily on defense. </p>
<blockquote><b><i> ... The 1997 Chicago Bulls ... </i></b></blockquote>
<p>But I have heard <cite>Defense Wins Championships</cite> as much as anyone and I have at least been partially brainwashed by it.   I thought I saw the Bulls
winning the '97 NBA Title because they turned up the defensive intensity. I <em>know</em> I saw that.  Didn't you see it?</p>
<table align=left width=200 cellpadding=10>
<tr><td align=center>
<p class=bblack>
"I remember Jordan making shot after shot, but I don't remember a single defensive play.
</td></tr>
</table>

<p>But I also saw Jordan bury his <i>%@!#&!</i> 23rd game winning jumper at the buzzer.  And I saw Jordan score 38 points when
no defense -- not even the flu -- could stop him from winning Game 5 of the Finals.  Funny, I remember Jordan making shot
after shot, but I don't remember a single defensive play. </p>
<p>As I remembered Jordan's shots, I thought I could be missing something, so I decided to look at this question in more detail.
I really tried to find some evidence that Defense Wins Championships. </p>
<blockquote><b><I> ... The Basic Study ... </i></b></blockquote>
<p>My first thought was to evaluate the number of times the NBA's best defensive team won the title and compare it to the
number of times the NBA's best offensive team won the title. If you consider the <b>best defensive team</b> to be the one that
allowed the fewest points per game, then this almost never happens, happening only <b>four</b> times since the Celtic dominance
of the '60's.  On the other hand, the league's <b>highest scoring team</b> has won <b>five</b> times since the Celtic era.</p>
<p>Those who know my work, however, are trying to buzz in and remind me that I typically define the league's best
offense and defense by the number of points they score and allow per 100 possessions. I call them offensive and
defensive ratings.  I also call them a pain to calculate, but I did it all the way back through the 1986 season.</p>
<p>For more detailed descriptions, definitions, and explanation of calculating "ratings", <br>see <a href="theywin2.html">
JoBS Methods Descriptions for the Scientific Study of Basketball</a></p>
<blockquote><b><I> ... The Champs Were Most Efficient How Often? ... </i></b></blockquote>
<p>Since the 1986 season, the most efficient <b>defensive</b> team has won the title <b>three</b> times.  The most efficient
<b>offensive</b> team has won the title <b>four</b> times.</p>
<p>If Defense Wins Championships, it certainly helps to have good offense.</p>
<blockquote><b><I> ... If They'd Only Apply Themselves ... </i></b></blockquote>
<p>
Still trying to prove myself stupid, I figured I'd go back to
the method I outlined in <a href="aa052097.htm">Toying With 'Em</a>.
In short, this method says that good teams slack off
in a lot of games that they have won by the third
quarter, so their numbers aren't as good as they
would be if they competed throughout games.  As your
mom used to tell you, "You could do so much better
if you just applied yourself!!"  Well, teams <em>do</em>
apply themselves in the playoffs, so it seems relevant
to look at this measure, which just adjusts teams'
offensive and defensive efficiencies by evaluating
how much they slacked off in the regular season.
</p>
<p>
Before I do, let me provide some context.  Going back through
all the playoff series since 1986,  I find that the <b>better
defensive team</b> won their playoff series a not-so-whopping
<b>63%</b> of the time.  The <b>better offensive team</b> won their playoff
series <b>66%</b> of the time.  This still shows no support
for the Defense (not Offense) Wins Championship phrase
that "they" cling to.
</p>
<table align=right width=200 cellpadding=10>
<tr><td align=center class=black>
<b>Since 1986, the better defensive team has won 71% of
their playoff series.  The better offensive team has
won 75% of their playoff series.
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>
Now when I determined how much teams slacked off, which
I could only do for 5 of the past 12 seasons because I didn't
have game by game scores, I saw something of a change.
The better defensive team won 71% of their series, an
improvement giving evidence to support the notion that
teams do turn it up defensively in the playoffs.  But, the
better offensive team won 75% of their series,
which implies that teams also turn it up offensively
in the playoffs.
</p>
<blockquote>
<b><I> ... Really, They Only Got Lazy Defensively ... </i></b>
</blockquote>
<p>
Given the rather dim light this placed on "their" mantra,
I pulled out one last desperate trick to try to prove
"them" right.  What my previous method
did (I really need a name for it) was <em>assume</em> that teams
slacked off equally on defense and on offense.  Heck,
I don't know if that is right, but I had no basis to
think otherwise... until now.  Since "they" say
Defense Wins Championships, I figured that it must be
only the defense that teams slack off on during the regular
season, turning it on for the postseason.</p>
<p>
And -- <b><i>whaddya know!!</i></b> -- Defense Does Win
Championships!!!  With this adjustment, the better defensive
team won 76% of their series and the better offensive
team won just 70%.
</p>
<p>
Still, I think I'll take Michael Jordan
over Dikembe Mutombo.
</p>
