class="NewsBody"><P>With the FIFA World Cup behind us, Europe's football attention is beginning to focus on the coming season's action, and in particular the EURO 2004™ qualifying competition, which gets under way in the early autumn. </P>
<P><B>Down to business</B><BR>Europe's national associations are starting to attend to the business of getting their national squads ready for the start of the competition, which will culminate in the final round in Portugal in two summers' time. </P>
<P><B>Series of casualties</B><BR>Yet for some countries, things will not be the same as before. The gruelling World Cup campaign has left a series of playing and coaching casualties along the way – with a number of prominent European coaches among the bloody and bruised victims. </P>
<P><B>Considerable list</B><BR>The list is considerable. Roger Lemerre (France), José Antonio Camacho (Spain), António Oliveira (Portugal), Robert Waseige (Belgium), Oleg Romantsev (Russia), Jerzy Engel (Poland) and Srecko Katanec (Slovenia) will no longer be in charge of their country’s national teams for the EURO 2004™ qualifiers. They have either left their posts or been dismissed in the wake of the World Cup. </P>
<P><B>Taxing job<BR></B>Roy Hodgson, the English coach who has experienced euphoric highs and bitter lows during a long and distinguished career in the game, says that the World Cup will have left some of the participating coaches wholeheartedly endorsing the view that leading a national football team is one of the toughest jobs in the world. </P>
<P><B>Bad times</B><BR>"World Cups are a bad time for coaches," says the current United Arab Emirates national coach Hodgson, who led Switzerland to the World Cup finals in 1994. "Normally after a World Cup, a coach who for months has been a complete hero in a country, who has been the saviour, and who has brought his European team to the finals, ends up losing his job because the European team hasn't won the final." </P>
<P><B>No longer heroes</B><BR>"Quite a few of the European teams will be going into the EURO qualifiers with new coaches. Coaches who were heroes one month ago are being lambasted because their team hasn’t won the tournament," he adds. </P>
<P><B>Back to a European context<BR></B>Hodgson says that in tactical and playing terms, those coaches who are still at the national-team helm will actually feel relief at leaving the world stage behind and returning to a European context. </P>
<P><B>Recognised styles</B><BR>"It will also be a new start in terms of opponents who are better known," explains Hodgson. "The thing about the World Cup is that, as a coach of a European team, you're thrown up against styles of play and types of players that you don't meet very often in your European experiences. A lot of the coaches will be quite happy to get back to styles of football that they recognise a little bit more." </P>
<P><B>New and old</B><BR>For all of the coaches, new and old, the coming EURO 2004™ adventure will be an opportunity to consolidate World Cup achievements or banish nightmare thoughts of what went wrong in Japan/Korea. </P>
<P><B>Looking to build</B><BR>"After a break, many teams will be looking to build upon what they've done in this World Cup, in particular Turkey and Germany, who will be looking to build on an outstanding World Cup and carry that through into the qualifiers," says Hodgson. "For other teams, such as France and Portugal, this will be the opportunity for them to put matters right." </P>
<P><B>Taking a break</B><BR>But first of all, many of the men who have gone through the month-long pressure tunnel in the glare of the world’s spotlight will be profiting from the European summer to take a break from the stress of it all and ponder the future. </P>
<P><B>Winners recover quickly<BR></B>"It's easy to recover when you're a winner," says Rinus Michels, the Dutchman who led the Netherlands to the 1974 World Cup runners-up spot and 1988 UEFA European Championship title. </P>
<P><B>Mutual confidence</B><BR>"If there's a base of mutual confidence between the players and the coach, and if results at the World Cup have not been too disappointing, then it shouldn't take long to recover," adds Michels, "especially as there are new qualifiers to prepare soon." </P> </TD></TR></TABLE><TABLE CELLPADDING="0" CELLSPACING="5" BORDER="0" WIDTH="355" xmlns:msxsl="urn:schemas-microsoft-com:xslt" xmlns:user="http://mycompany.com/mynamespace"><TR><TD class="NewsBody"><a target="_blank" href="&#xA;					/news/newsId=27927,printer.htmx&#xA;				"><img SRC="/images/All/buttons/printer_friendly.gif" border="0" width="130" height="9" vspace="10" /></a></TD><TD align="right" class="NewsBody"><a href="javascript:void(0)" onclick="window.open('/news/sendmail.htmx?newsId=27927','SendMail','width=355,height=516')"><img SRC="/images/All/buttons/mail_friend.gif" border="0" width="99" height="9" vspace="10" /></a></TD></TR></TABLE><TABLE CELLPADDING="0" CELLSPACING="5" BORDER="0" WIDTH="355" xmlns:msxsl="urn:schemas-microsoft-com:xslt" xmlns:user="http://mycompany.com/mynamespace"><TR><TD class="NewsBody">©uefa.com 1998-2002.
