				<font class="newsdate"> Tuesday, January 1, 2002 00:35 (GMT)</font><br>
				<img src="/1.gif" width="1" height="18"><br>
				<font class="news">It was another of those mixed years for Indian cricket, marked by triumphs at home and disasters abroad. But the fact that the balance sheet also contains arguably the greatest victory ever in a home series gives the profit page an edge over the loss section.  <br><br>As the year started, the portents were encouraging. In the last couple of months of 2000, India had unexpectedly made the final of the ICC KnockOut championship at Nairobi, had beaten Bangladesh by nine wickets in four days in the inaugural Test at Dhaka and had registered a series victory at home over Zimbabwe.<br><br>Sourav Ganguly was quite firmly in the saddle and there was a new coach in John Wright, the first foreigner appointed to the post. The mood was one of cautious optimism.<br><br>The big test came early in the form of a series against Australia. Few gave the Indians any chance against the unofficial world champions, despite the obvious home advantage and the fact that Australia had not won a Test series in this country since 1969.<br><br>Australia came to India with a run of 15 consecutive victories and they duly made it 16 by winning the First Test at Mumbai inside three days. They then enforced the follow on the Second Test at Kolkata and things seemed to be moving according to predictions.<br><br>Midway through the game however, there was a sudden transformation. VVS Laxman came up with a record Indian score of 281, Rahul Dravid scored 180 - the two sharing a 376-run partnership for the fifth wicket after batting throughout the fourth day&#8217;s play - and suddenly the hunter had become the hunted.<br><br>Harbhajan Singh underlined the storybook finish by bowling out the Australians and for only the third time in the 124-year-old history of Test cricket, a team had come back to win after following on.<br><br>The notable triumph set the stage for the final Test at Chennai and in keeping with the great cricket played throughout the series, the decider too was a fluctuating, well-contested match before India emerged victors by two wickets in a nail-biting finish on a sunny March day at the MA Chidambaram stadium.<br><br>Harbhajan was named man of the series with his bag of 32 wickets, including 28 in the last two games, while Ganguly was given due credit for being an aggressive, no-nonsense captain. The victory was all the more notable as it was achieved without the services of Anil Kumble, still nursing the injury sustained at Sharjah in October last year. <br><br>The euphoria following this great triumph lasted quite a while, but soon the team and the fans were brought down to Earth with a dull thud. A series of reverses followed abroad, which, given India&#8217;s poor away record, should not have come as a surprise.<br><br>A shared series in Zimbabwe, a loss to Sri Lanka and another defeat in South Africa were all sore disappointments. Even granting that Sachin Tendulkar, injured in Zimbabwe, could not make the trip to Sri Lanka and that Kumble did not return until the South Africa series, the successive setbacks only served to show that the Indians were indeed tigers at home and lambs abroad.<br><br>To make matters worse, the irritating habit of going down at the last hurdle in one-day competitions continued, and it was a rather chastened Indian team that took on England at home towards the end of the year.<br><br>But the epitaph `tigers at home, lambs abroad&#8217; remained accurate, even if the home team huffed and puffed to overcome an England side which was rated as arguably the weakest from that country to visit India. <br><br>A positive aspect, however, was that by the end of the year, some of the problems that plagued Indian cricket at the start of it seemed to have been solved.<br><br>The seam attack had a promising look about it. With the burgeoning of young pace bowling hopefuls &#8211; symbolized by the debut of three new ball bowlers for the Mohali Test against England &#8211; there seemed to be an embarrassment of riches.<br><br>The fact that Javagal Srinath, at 32, still retains a lot of fire, that Zaheer Khan and Ashish Nehra are still around and that a new discovery has been made in Tinu Yohannan, are all signs that augur well for Indian cricket.<br><br>The spin department is in good hands with Harbhajan and Anil Kumble bowling in tandem, and with Sarandeep Singh showing at Bangalore that he was not far behind.<br><br>There remains the problem with finding a suitable man behind the stumps, despite Deep Dasgupta having proved his worth with the bat. Perhaps the time has come for young Ajay Ratra to get the big break.<br><br>One more of Indian cricket&#8217;s perennial problems, the opening batting, had hopefully been solved. Indeed, there suddenly seemed to be a problem of plenty with Shiv Sundar Das, Dasgupta, Connor Williams and Sadagopan Ramesh being the contenders.<br><br>The middle-order batting remains the main strength of the side, though admittedly the protagonists should perform in keeping with their reputation.<br><br>The discovery of Virender Sehwag was a major plus and towards the fag end of the year, Kumble gave cricket fans something to cheer by taking his 300th wicket. As for Tendulkar, crossing landmarks are routine, but becoming the first to cross 10,000 runs in one-day internationals was indeed something special, even for him. <br><br>However, looking to the immediate future, it is difficult to be very optimistic. The New Year starts with the series of one-day internationals against England and the Test series against Zimbabwe.<br><br>Since both the contests are at home, there should not be any problems, but thereafter the course gets tougher. First, there is a tour to the West Indies followed by a trip to England in the summer.<br><br>Given our record in these two countries &#8211; and our woeful away record overall &#8211; it is easy to see why the mood at the end of the year is rather downbeat. <br><br>-<i> Partab Ramchand</i></font><br>
