	<td width="75%">
		<b>Huge fish new threat to Great Lakes                                                                 </b><br>
		<font size=2>Author: Philip Mascoll                                     <br>
		Source: The Toronto Star                                                                                                                                      </font>
	</td>

	<td align=right VALIGN=TOP>		
				Fri.           July 12, 2002
	</td>
	</tr>
	<tr>
		<td colspan=2><br>
		<br>
				<P align=justify>It can grow to be 50 kilograms, has a face like ... well, a fish, and could change the way we work and frolic on the Great Lakes, scientists warn. </P>
<P align=justify>The dreaded Asian carp, which escaped nearly a decade ago into the southern Mississippi River in Arkansas, is now within 40 kilometres of Lake Michigan and could have a devastating effect on the Great Lakes, far beyond the damage caused by the sea lamprey or zebra mussels, according to a warning issued yesterday by the International Joint Commission. </P>
<P align=justify>"One has been found in a fountain in Toronto, but that one we believe was released there," said Jim Houston, environmental administrator of the IJC, an organization that helps Canada and the United States manage their shared lakes and rivers. </P>
<P align=justify>But that was the only humorous thing Houston had to say about the Asian carp. </P>
<P align=justify>The fish, which comes in two varieties, the big head and the silver Asian carp, is a plankton feeder, he explained. </P>
<P align=justify>But it has such a voracious appetite - growing to more than a metre long and 50 kg - it leaves little behind for smaller varieties that also feed on plankton. These fish at the bottom of the food chain feed the carnivorous varieties that fuel the commercial and sport fishing industry on the Great Lakes. </P>
<P align=justify>The added danger with the carp is their size - they would beat out salmon for the title of largest fish in the lakes - and the fact they can jump, Houston said. </P>
<P align=justify>"A 110-pound fish hitting a water skier or a person on a jet ski, or jumping into a boat and hitting someone could cause serious injury." </P>
<P align=justify>The fish are not yet in the Great Lakes, but large numbers have been found in the Illinois River and the Chicago Ship and Sanitary Canal connected to the Lake Michigan near Chicago. </P>
<P align=justify>That puts them only 40 km from the lake and, based on their current rate of dispersal, they could reach the lake this year. </P>
<P align=justify>Nicholas Mandrak, a research scientist with Canada's Department of Oceans and Fisheries, said the carp are a real danger. </P>
<P align=justify>"If they become established, they will vacuum up all the food in the Great Lakes," he said. </P>
<P align=justify>In the Mississippi River, "they are just so abundant, it is impossible to eradicate them," he added. </P>
<P align=justify>The fish were introduced to the States in Arkansas in the 1970s to manage snails and algae in the aquaculture industry. </P>
<P align=justify>But they escaped into the Mississippi through flooding and other accidental release, in 1993 it is believed, and have been making their way north ever since, Houston said. </P>
<P align=justify>"They reproduce in large numbers and are very active," he said. "They can take over very quickly." </P>
<P align=justify>In a July 5 letter to Canada's foreign affairs minister, Bill Graham, and U.S. Secretary of State Colin Powell, the IJC calls on the American government to stop Asian carp from entering the Great Lakes. </P>
<P align=justify>In addition to continuing the operation of an electrical barrier in the Chicago River, the IJC suggests a second, more permanent, barrier be installed. </P>
<P align=justify>The IJC also wants both governments to: </P>
<P align=justify>Educate the public about the threat of Asian carp to the Great Lakes ecosystem. </P>
<P align=justify>Investigate other chemical and physical alternatives to prevent the movement of aquatic nuisance species to and from the Great Lakes. </P>
<P align=justify>Consider implementing regulatory controls to prevent transfer of aquatic nuisance species via other pathways, such as the food- and bait-fish industries and aquaculture. </P>
<P align=justify>The barriers cost $350,000 to install and about $350,000 a year to maintain. </P>
<P align=justify>But they are the most viable solution, Mandrak said. </P>
<P align=justify>"The other option is to close the Chicago Ship and Sanitary Canal connected to the Great Lakes near Chicago, Illinois, and cut off the river from our Great Lakes. But that wouldn't be economically viable." </P>
<P align=justify>This article appeared in the <A href="http://www.thestar.com/" target=_blank>Toronto Star</A> on July 12, 2002.</P>
